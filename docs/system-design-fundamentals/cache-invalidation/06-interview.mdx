## Question Types

Cache invalidation questions in system design interviews fall into four categories.

### Category 1: Design a Cache for [Service]

The interviewer asks you to design a caching layer for a specific service: a social media feed, an e-commerce catalog, a real-time analytics dashboard, or a content delivery system.

What they are evaluating: your ability to design a complete caching solution, including invalidation strategy. They want to see you consider the trade-offs between different invalidation approaches and justify your choices.

**Example.** "Design the caching layer for a social media news feed that shows posts from friends. Posts are frequently updated with likes and comments. How do you ensure users see fresh content?"

### Category 2: Solve a Stale Data Incident

The interviewer describes a production incident where stale data was served to users. You must diagnose the root cause and propose a fix.

What they are evaluating: your debugging skills, your understanding of invalidation failure modes, and your ability to design reliable systems.

**Example.** "Users are reporting that their profile pictures are not updating after they change them. Other users see the old picture for up to 5 minutes. The cache has a 60-second TTL. What is going on?"

### Category 3: Compare Invalidation Strategies

The interviewer asks you to compare two or more invalidation strategies for a specific use case.

What they are evaluating: your depth of understanding of each strategy, including edge cases and trade-offs.

**Example.** "Compare write-invalidate with write-update for caching an e-commerce product page. Which would you choose and why?"

### Category 4: Design an Invalidation System for a Distributed Cache

The interviewer asks you to design the invalidation system itself: how events flow, how keys are resolved, how consistency is maintained across nodes.

What they are evaluating: your distributed systems knowledge, your understanding of CAP trade-offs, and your ability to design for reliability at scale.

**Example.** "Design a cache invalidation system that works across 20 cache nodes in 3 regions. Every write must invalidate the cache within 500ms."

## Interviewer Evaluation Criteria

Interviewers evaluate your answer on these dimensions:

| Criterion | What They Look For |
|---|---|
| **Scope** | Do you identify the data characteristics (read/write ratio, change frequency, staleness tolerance) before choosing a strategy? |
| **Trade-off articulation** | Can you explain why you chose one invalidation strategy over another, including the downsides of your choice? |
| **Failure mode awareness** | Do you discuss what happens when invalidation fails? Do you have a fallback? |
| **Scalability reasoning** | Does your design work at the scale specified (or do you ask about scale)? |
| **System design** | Can you sketch the components (CDC, event bus, invalidation manager, cache) and explain how they interact? |
| **Operational maturity** | Do you discuss monitoring, alerting, recovery, and testing? |

## Level Expectations

The depth of your answer should match the level you are targeting.

### L3 / Junior Engineer

**Expectation.** Understand the basic problem of stale data. Know that TTL is a solution. Be able to describe a simple cache-aside pattern with TTL.

**What to say.** "I would use a cache-aside pattern with a TTL. When data is read, check the cache first. If the cache has the data and it has not expired, return it. If the cache is missing or expired, query the database, populate the cache with a TTL, and return the data."

**What to avoid.** Do not propose complex event pipelines or distributed invalidation protocols. Focus on the basics.

### L4 / Mid-Level Engineer

**Expectation.** Know multiple invalidation strategies. Understand the trade-off between staleness and complexity. Be able to design a TTL + explicit purge system.

**What to say.** "For this e-commerce product page, I would use a combination of TTL and explicit purge. The product details would have a 5-minute TTL. When a product is updated, the product service explicitly deletes the cache key. If the delete fails, the TTL ensures the data is refreshed within 5 minutes."

**What to avoid.** Do not ignore failure modes. Always mention what happens when the explicit purge fails and how TTL covers that gap.

### L5 / Senior Engineer

**Expectation.** Deep understanding of invalidation failure modes. Ability to design event-driven invalidation with CDC. Knowledge of tag-based invalidation and multi-key transactions.

**What to say.** "I would implement an event-driven invalidation pipeline using CDC from the primary database. The CDC connector would publish invalidation events to Kafka. A consumer group processes these events, resolves cache keys using a tag registry, and sends targeted deletes to the cache cluster. For multi-key atomicity, I would batch related invalidations into a single transaction. TTL of 60 seconds would be the safety net."

**What to avoid.** Do not propose an event-driven pipeline without addressing its failure modes (consumer lag, event loss, partition). Discuss how you monitor and recover from each failure.

### L6 / Staff Engineer

**Expectation.** Ability to design invalidation across multi-region deployments. Knowledge of gossip protocols, consistency models, and CAP trade-offs. Ability to design for 10M+ DAU.

**What to say.** "I would design a multi-region invalidation system with a global event bus. Each region has a local invalidation consumer that processes events from the bus. Cache entries are version-stamped, and a gossip protocol exchanges invalidation state between regions. The system is AP with bounded staleness: readers always get a response, even if it is slightly stale. Staleness is monitored per region, and regions with high staleness are flagged for investigation."

**What to avoid.** Do not claim strong consistency across regions. Understand and explain why cross-region invalidation is inherently eventually consistent.

### L7 / Principal Engineer

**Expectation.** Ability to define the invalidation strategy for the entire organization. Knowledge of when not to cache. Ability to design for zero operational toil.

**What to say.** "I would define a company-wide invalidation standard: all mutable data uses event-driven invalidation with CDC, tag-based key resolution, and TTL safety nets. Immutable data and append-only logs use TTL only. The invalidation pipeline is treated as critical infrastructure, with SLOs, SLIs, and error budgets. Self-healing mechanisms (consistency checkers, automatic replay) minimize operational toil."

**What to avoid.** Do not present a one-size-fits-all solution. Show that you understand when different invalidation strategies are appropriate and that you can tailor the design to each service's requirements.

## FAANG Interview Framework

Use this framework to structure your answer to any cache invalidation question.

### Step 1: Clarify Requirements (2 minutes)

Before proposing a solution, understand the constraints:

- **Data characteristics.** What is the read-to-write ratio? How frequently does the data change? Is it mutable or append-only?
- **Freshness requirements.** What is the acceptable staleness? Seconds? Minutes? Near-instant?
- **Scale.** How many users? How many cache nodes? How many writes per second?
- **Infrastructure.** Do you have an event bus? CDC pipeline? Multi-region deployment?

**Good question to ask.** "What is the acceptable staleness for this use case? Can users tolerate seeing data that is up to 30 seconds old?"

### Step 2: Identify the Invalidation Strategy (3 minutes)

Based on the requirements, choose the primary invalidation strategy:

| Requirement | Recommended Strategy |
|---|---|
| Staleness under 1 second | Event-driven invalidation with CDC |
| Staleness under 1 minute | Explicit purge + TTL safety net |
| Staleness under 1 hour | TTL only, but tune TTL |
| Data changes at known intervals | TTL only, set TTL = interval |
| Write-heavy (over 10% of traffic) | Write-update (reduces miss storms) |
| Multi-region | Event-driven + gossip + shorter TTL in remote regions |
| Immutable/append-only | TTL only (or no invalidation needed) |

### Step 3: Design the Architecture (5 minutes)

Sketch the components:

1. **Data source.** Database, file system, or external API.
2. **Change capture.** CDC (Debezium, Maxwell), database trigger, or application-level event.
3. **Event bus.** Kafka, RabbitMQ, or internal queue.
4. **Invalidation manager.** Consumer that resolves keys and dispatches operations.
5. **Cache.** Redis, memcached, or CDN.
6. **Consistency checker.** Background process that verifies cache entries.

Use the drawing conventions from this course: `[services]`, `((databases))`, `{caches}`, and `<queues>`.

### Step 4: Address Failure Modes (3 minutes)

Discuss what happens when things go wrong:

- **Invalidation event lost.** TTL safety net catches it.
- **Cache node partitioned.** Consistency checker detects and repairs.
- **Consumer crashes.** Consumer group rebalances; events are replayed from committed offsets.
- **Backlog grows.** Monitor consumer lag; auto-scale consumers; rate-limit event production if needed.

### Step 5: Discuss Monitoring and Operations (2 minutes)

End with how you operate the system:

- **SLIs.** Staleness window, invalidation latency, purge queue depth, failed invalidations.
- **SLOs.** p99 staleness under 1 second, zero failed invalidations.
- **Alerting.** Pager when staleness exceeds target or when failed invalidations exceed threshold.
- **On-call runbook.** Steps for handling invalidation storms, consumer crashes, and consistency checker alerts.

## Phrase Bank

Use these phrases to demonstrate depth during your interview.

**On strategy selection.** "The choice of invalidation strategy depends on how much staleness the business can tolerate. If the business can tolerate 5 minutes of staleness, TTL alone is sufficient. If the business needs near-instant freshness, we need an event-driven pipeline."

**On TTL as safety net.** "I always include a TTL even when using event-driven invalidation. The TTL bounds the maximum staleness if the event pipeline fails. It turns an unbounded staleness problem into a bounded one."

**On invalidation storms.** "Invalidation storms happen when a single change invalidates many cache entries, and the re-population overloads the database. I mitigate this by staggering the re-population, rate-limiting deletes, or using write-update for bulk changes."

**On tag-based invalidation.** "Tags are an elegant approach to selective invalidation. Instead of the writer knowing the exact cache keys, it only needs to know the tags. The tag registry maintains the mapping. This decouples the writer from the cache topology."

**On partial invalidation.** "Partial invalidation is dangerous because it leaves the cache in an inconsistent state. If a database transaction updates two rows, the invalidation must also be atomic across both cache keys. I use batch invalidation with all-or-nothing semantics."

**On write-update vs write-invalidate.** "Write-invalidate is simpler and uses less bandwidth, but it causes a cache miss on the next read. Write-update avoids the miss but requires sending the full value. I use write-invalidate for large values and write-update for small, frequently-read values."

**On multi-region consistency.** "Cache invalidation across regions is inherently limited by the speed of light. I design for eventual consistency and make sure the product experience tolerates bounded staleness. For critical data, I route writes to the primary region and accept the latency penalty."

**On monitoring staleness.** "I instrument every cache read with the staleness value and track the distribution. If p99 staleness exceeds 1 second, I want to know immediately. Over time, I use this data to tune TTLs and optimize the invalidation pipeline."

**On the CAP trade-off.** "For most cache invalidation systems, I choose AP: serve stale data rather than fail the request. Availability is more important than perfect freshness for read-heavy workloads. The invalidation pipeline converges the cache to fresh data asynchronously."

## Red Flags

Avoid these mistakes in your interview answer.

**Red flag 1: Claiming TTL guarantees freshness.** TTL guarantees staleness, not freshness. The maximum staleness is bounded by TTL, but there is no guarantee the data is fresh within the TTL window.

**Red flag 2: Ignoring failure modes.** Proposing an event-driven invalidation pipeline without discussing what happens when the event bus is down or the consumer crashes suggests inexperience with production systems.

**Red flag 3: Proposing synchronous invalidation at scale.** Waiting for all cache nodes to acknowledge invalidation before responding to the write adds latency proportional to the slowest node. This does not scale.

**Red flag 4: Claiming strong consistency in multi-region caches.** Cross-region invalidation is subject to network latency and partition risks. Claiming strong consistency across regions is unrealistic.

**Red flag 5: Forgetting the TTL safety net.** Even with the most sophisticated event-driven invalidation, a TTL safety net is essential. Forgetting to mention it suggests you have not operated this system in production.

**Red flag 6: Not asking about data characteristics.** Jumping into a solution without asking about read/write ratio, change frequency, or staleness tolerance suggests a one-size-fits-all approach.

**Red flag 7: Designing for the wrong scale.** Proposing a complex event pipeline for a system with 1,000 users is over-engineering. Proposing TTL-only for a system with 10 million users is under-engineering. Ask about scale first.

**Red flag 8: Ignoring operational cost.** An event-driven pipeline requires ongoing maintenance: monitoring consumer lag, managing Kafka topics, handling schema evolution. Acknowledge this cost and discuss how you manage it.

## Practice Drills

### Drill 1: The E-Commerce Product Page

Design the invalidation strategy for an e-commerce product page cache. The page shows product name, price, description, images, inventory status, and reviews. Price changes weekly. Inventory changes every few seconds. Reviews change hourly.

Work through: What invalidation strategy do you use for each data element? Do you use one cache entry or multiple? How do you handle the inventory hot-spot problem (popular products being read and written frequently)?

### Drill 2: The Social Media Feed

Design the invalidation strategy for a social media feed. Each user's feed is a list of posts from people they follow. Posts are created, edited, and deleted. The feed must show new posts within 10 seconds.

Work through: Do you pre-compute feeds or compute them on read? How do you invalidate a feed when a followed user posts? What happens when a post is edited or deleted?

### Drill 3: The Real-Time Dashboard

Design the invalidation strategy for a real-time analytics dashboard. The dashboard shows metrics that are updated every 5 seconds from a stream processing pipeline. Users should see data within 5 seconds of the source event.

Work through: Is cache invalidation the right approach here? Or should you use a different data delivery mechanism? What is the staleness budget?

### Drill 4: The Configuration Service

Design the invalidation strategy for a distributed configuration service. Configuration values are updated by administrators and should propagate to all services within 30 seconds. Configuration changes are rare (once per day on average).

Work through: Would you use polling or push-based invalidation? What is the cost of each approach? How do you handle the case where a configuration change breaks a service?

### Drill 5: The Multi-Region Cache

Design the invalidation strategy for a multi-region cache. Your application runs in US-East, EU-West, and AP-Southeast. Writes go to a primary database in US-East and replicate asynchronously to the other regions. Cache must be consistent within 2 seconds globally.

Work through: How does the writing strategy affect the invalidation strategy? Should you have a global event bus or region-specific ones? How do you handle the replication lag between regions during invalidation? What happens if a user in AP-Southeast writes to the primary region (500ms latency) and expects to see their write reflected immediately?
