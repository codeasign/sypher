# Scalability Trade-offs

## The Core Tension

Every scalability decision is a bet against an unknown future.

Scale too early and you burn money on infrastructure you do not need. Your team spends weeks building sharding logic instead of shipping features. Your 4-server deployment costs $3,000/month when one $100 server would suffice.

Scale too late and your application collapses under load. Users see 30-second page loads. Error rates hit 15%. Your competitors run ads on "that slow app" while you scramble to add capacity at 3 AM.

The right approach: Monitor relentlessly. Scale when you have evidence, not when you have anxiety. Premature scaling is the root of most unnecessary complexity in system design.

## Decision Matrix

| Situation | Scale vertically | Scale horizontally | Do nothing |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|------------|
| 500 users on a single server | No (waste of $) | No (overkill) | **Best** |
| 5,000 users, CPU at 60% | **Maybe** (quick fix) | No (too complex) | Maybe (monitor) |
| 50,000 users, DB at 85% | **Yes** (buy time) | Plan for it | No (risk) |
| 500,000 users, DB at 50% | No (band-aid) | **Yes** (design for it now) | No (growth coming) |
| Traffic spikes 5x during events | No (overpay at baseline) | **Yes** (auto-scaling) | No (will fail) |
| Read-heavy workload (10:1 ratio) | No (replicas better) | **Yes** (add read replicas) | No (DB will saturate) |
| Write-heavy workload (1:1 ratio) | **Yes** (shard later) | Plan to shard | No (writes bottleneck) |
| Global user base (multi-continent) | No (physics) | **Yes** (multi-region) | No (latency unacceptable) |
| Real-time vs batch workloads | Optimize real-time path | **Yes** (separate batch cluster) | No (mixing kills both) |
| Synchronous vs async replication | **Yes** (strong consistency) | **Yes** (higher throughput) | No (data loss risk) |
| In-memory vs disk-based cache | **Yes** (for hot data) | Disk for cold data (tiered) | **Maybe** (working set fits RAM) |
| Monolithic vs microservice | No (band-aid on monolith) | **Yes** (independent scaling) | **Best** (if fewer than 20 engineers) |
| SQL vs NoSQL | **Maybe** (ACID needed) | **Yes** (at very large scale) | **Best** (if schema stable) |

## CAP Positioning for Scalability

Scalability forces trade-offs in the CAP theorem. You cannot have all three — consistency, availability, and partition tolerance.

| Scaling strategy | Consistency | Availability | Partition tolerance |
|-----------------|-------------|--------------|-------------------|
| Single database (ACID) | Strong | Low (single point) | None |
| Read replicas (async) | Eventual | High | High |
| Synchronous replicas | Strong | Medium (write blocked if replica down) | Medium |
| Sharded database | Per-shard strong | High (shard-level) | High |
| Multi-region (active-active) | Eventual (LWW) | Very high | Very high |
| Cache (Redis) | Eventually consistent | High | High (cache miss = DB fallback) |

**Practical guidance:**

- Banking and payments: Optimize for consistency. Use synchronous replication. Accept lower write throughput.
- Social feeds and content: Optimize for availability. Use eventual consistency. A delayed post is better than no post.
- Analytics: Optimize for partition tolerance. Batch processing with delayed consistency is standard.

## Cost of Scaling

Every scaling decision has a concrete price tag. Understanding these costs helps you make informed trade-offs.

**Real dollar breakdown:**

- Read replicas: +$500/month per node on AWS RDS (db.r6g.large). Three replicas = $1,500/month.
- Redis cluster (3 nodes): +$1,000-$3,000/month on ElastiCache (cache.r6g.large). Each node adds capacity but also cost.
- Database sharding setup: 2-6 weeks of engineering time for a senior engineer at $150/hour. That is $12,000-$36,000 in engineering cost alone, before infrastructure.
- Multi-region deployment: approximately 2x your current infrastructure bill. If you spend $5K/month in us-east-1, expect $10K/month for us-east-1 + eu-west-1 + ap-southeast-1.
- Full-text search (Elasticsearch, 3 nodes): +$800-$2,500/month depending on instance size.
- CDN (CloudFront or Cloudflare): +$100-$2,000/month depending on data transfer volume.
- Queue system (SQS): approximately $0.40 per million requests. At 10M messages/day, that is roughly $120/month.
- Microservices migration: 3-12 months of a 4-6 person team. At average engineering cost, that is $200K-$1M before you see any benefit.

**When each option makes sense:**

- **Vertical upgrade** is your play when you need a 2-4x capacity boost in under an hour and your application is single-threaded or single-node bound. It is always the first thing to try.
- **Read replicas** are the right choice when your workload is clearly read-heavy (10:1 or worse ratio) and you can tolerate seconds of replication lag. Most web applications fit this profile.
- **Redis caching** shines when the same data is read repeatedly and the cost of recomputing or refetching is high. The 80/20 rule applies: 80% of requests hit 20% of the data.
- **Database sharding** is what you reach for when writes exceed the capacity of a single node and you have exhausted vertical scaling. It is almost never the right first step.
- **Multi-region** only makes sense when your user base is geographically distributed and latency matters. A single region in us-east-1 can serve users worldwide with 200-300ms latency — many applications can tolerate that.
- **Microservices** should not be used as a scaling strategy. They are an organizational strategy that happens to enable independent scaling. Do not decompose your monolith to scale — decompose it so teams can move independently.

**When NOT to pay:**

- Do not pay for read replicas if your cache hit rate is below 40%. Fix caching first — it is cheaper.
- Do not pay for Redis clustering if a single Redis instance at 64GB is sufficient. One large instance is simpler than three small ones in a cluster.
- Do not pay for multi-region if your application serves one continent. The latency benefit to other continents is rarely worth the operational overhead.
- Do not pay for a queue system if your background jobs can be handled by a simple thread pool or a cron job. Over-engineering background processing is a common cost trap.

**What real companies paid:**

- Twitter (2010): The "Fail Whale" era cost an estimated $500K/month in lost ad revenue during outages. Their decision to scale vertically and late cost more than horizontal scaling would have.
- Instagram (2012): Migrated from a single PostgreSQL instance to a sharded setup with 12 engineers over 8 weeks. The sharding work cost approximately $200K in engineering time but avoided the $2M/month cost of a complete database outage during their hockey-stick growth phase.
- Netflix (2015): Cloud migration cost $8M/year in AWS bills but saved $15M/year in data center costs. They scaled proactively and the math worked.
- Pinterest: Built a custom Redis caching layer at a cost of approximately $50K in engineering time. It reduced their database load by 80% and delayed a sharding migration by 18 months.

## Operational Complexity

Scaling adds operational burden that you cannot see on a cloud bill. Each new mechanism is a system you must understand at 3 AM.

| Scaling Decision | Pager Impact | Team Skills Required | Deployment Change |
|-----------------|-------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Vertical upgrade | None | None | None |
| Read replicas | Low (lag alerts) | SQL replication | Schema changes must be backward-compatible |
| Redis caching | Medium (evictions, misses) | Cache invalidation patterns | Cache warming needed on deploy |
| Database sharding | High (resharding, hot spots) | Distributed systems, routing | Schema migrations affect all shards |
| Multi-region | High (DNS, replication lag) | Global load balancing, DNS | Coordinated rollouts across regions |
| Queue system | Medium (DLQ, consumer lag) | Async patterns, retry logic | At-least-once semantics expected |
| Microservices | Very high (N services = N alerts) | Service mesh, containers, CI/CD | Multi-service deployment pipeline |
| NoSQL migration | High (consistency model shift) | New query patterns, data modeling | Dual-writes during migration |

**Pager load multiplication:**

- Single PostgreSQL server: 1-2 alerts per week (disk space, connection count).
- With read replicas: 3-5 alerts per week (replication lag, replica health).
- With Redis: 5-8 alerts per week (memory pressure, eviction rate, cluster topology changes).
- Sharded database: 10-15 alerts per week (per-shard health, resharding events, hot partitions).
- Multi-region: 15-20 alerts per week (region health, cross-region sync, DNS propagation).

Each new scaling mechanism adds a system you must debug under pressure. When your pager goes off for a Redis cluster split-brain at 2 AM, the cost of caching becomes very personal.

**Skills you need to hire for:**

- Sharding requires engineers who understand consistent hashing, range partitioning, and zero-downtime data migration.
- Multi-region requires infrastructure engineers who can debug BGP routing, DNS propagation, and cross-region replication conflicts.
- NoSQL migration requires your team to unlearn years of relational data modeling. Document stores, wide-column databases, and search indexes each have different query patterns and consistency guarantees.
- Microservices requires your operations team to understand container orchestration, service mesh, distributed tracing, and deployment strategies like blue-green and canary releases.

**The deployment change you may not expect:**

- Read replicas mean all schema changes must be backward-compatible. You cannot drop a column on a Friday — replicas will lag and break. You need online schema change tools like pt-online-schema-change or gh-ost.
- Sharding means schema migrations must run against every shard. A migration that took 5 minutes on one database now takes 5 minutes per shard times 50 shards — over 4 hours of migration time, assuming no failures.
- Multi-region means you cannot deploy to all regions at once. You deploy to one region, verify, then roll out to others. A 10-minute deploy becomes a 60-minute phased rollout.
- NoSQL migration means you run dual-writes for weeks or months — every write goes to both the old database and the new one — with a reconciliation job to backfill historical data.

**Organizational friction by scale:**

- 1-10 engineers: One person handles operations. Scaling means upgrading cloud instances.
- 10-50 engineers: You have a dedicated DevOps person. Scaling means adding read replicas and caching.
- 50-200 engineers: You have an SRE team. Scaling means sharding, multi-region, and formal incident response.
- 200+ engineers: You have platform engineering teams building internal tools. Scaling means building your own abstractions on top of cloud infrastructure.

**The 10x ops cost rule:** Each order-of-magnitude increase in scale roughly doubles operational complexity. Going from 10K to 100K users is a configuration change. Going from 100K to 1M users requires new systems. Going from 1M to 10M users requires a new team.

## Failure Modes

### Failure 1: Thundering Herd

When many clients simultaneously try to access or recreate the same resource.

```
Scenario:
  Cache key "trending_restaurants" expires at 8:00 PM.
  50 app servers all try to recompute the cache simultaneously.
  Each server sends 3 database queries to recompute.
  150 database queries hit at once.
  Database CPU jumps from 40% to 100%.
  Query latency goes from 20ms to 5,000ms.
  Connection pool on primary exhausts.
  All requests that depend on the DB start failing.

Solution: Locking (only one server recomputes, others wait):
  1. First server acquires Redis lock on "trending_restaurants"
  2. Lock TTL: 10 seconds (sufficient for recompute)
  3. Other servers see lock, wait up to 100ms for cached value
  4. First server computes and writes cache (TTL: 60 seconds)
  5. Lock released; all subsequent requests hit cache
```

### Failure 2: Cascade Failure

One component fails, increasing load on remaining components, causing them to fail.

**Prevention:**
- Circuit breakers: When error rate exceeds 50%, stop sending traffic to that component
- Bulkheads: Reserve capacity per service (do not share connection pools)
- Load shedding: Drop low-priority requests (non-critical API calls) before critical ones
- Graceful degradation: Disable search, analytics, recommendations before core functionality fails

### Failure 3: Hot Partition

A single shard or partition receives far more traffic than others.

```
DishFind shards restaurants by restaurant_id modulo 10.
Restaurant "Big Burger" has a Super Bowl ad and gets 100x normal traffic.
Shard 7 (holding Big Burger's data) receives:
  - 50,000 profile views/second (all write-heavy: read menus, leave reviews)
  - Other shards: 500 views/second
  - Shard 7 database CPU: 95%
  - Queries to Shard 7: 2,000ms (normal: 20ms)
  - Queries to other shards: 15ms

Impact:
  - Big Burger page loads in 8 seconds
  - Users reviewing Big Burger get 500 errors
  - App servers routing to Shard 7 have stuck threads
  - Connection pool to Shard 7 exhausted
  - Other restaurants on Shard 7 also affected (collateral damage)

Solutions:
  1. Shard splitting: subdivide hot shard into 10 smaller shards
  2. Cache: aggressively cache Big Burger's data at app layer (TTL: 5 seconds)
  3. Dedicated resources: give hot shard more CPU/RAM than others
  4. Application-level rate limiting: throttle requests to hot entity
```

### Failure 4: Scaling Too Fast

Adding too many nodes too quickly causes its own problems.

```
Auto-scaling rule: Add 10 instances when CPU > 70% for 2 minutes.
Traffic spikes from 10K RPS to 100K RPS in 30 seconds.
CPU breaches 70% threshold.
Auto-scaler adds 10 instances → 60 seconds boot time.
CPU still at 95%. Rule triggers again → adds 10 more.
Now 20 instances booting simultaneously.
  - Docker image registry: saturated at 10 concurrent pulls
  - Database connection pool: 20 new apps try to open 300 connections
  - PostgreSQL max_connections (500) breached
  - Existing app servers lose database connections
  - Cascade failure begins

Prevention:
  - Maximum scale-out step: 2-3 instances per evaluation
  - Cooldown period: minimum 180 seconds between scale events
  - Pre-warm instances: keep 2-3 spare instances running during peak
  - Boot time optimization: minimize container startup (under 30 seconds)
```

### Failure 5: Unscalable Data Model

The data model itself prevents scaling. Common examples:

```
1. Auto-increment primary keys in a distributed database:
   Shard 0: IDs 1-1,000,000
   Shard 1: IDs 1,000,001-2,000,000
   Global uniqueness requires a centralized sequence generator
   — this is a single point of failure below 5M users

2. Single-table design with 50 columns, 50 indexes:
   Each write updates all 50 indexes
   Index maintenance becomes the bottleneck
   — this fails at ~1M rows

3. JSON blob columns that store hundreds of kilobytes:
   Every read loads the entire blob
   Cache efficiency drops (1 item takes 200KB)
   — this fails at ~500K users

4. Frequent COUNT(*) queries on large tables:
   PostgreSQL COUNT(*) requires sequential scan on 10M rows → 2 seconds
   — this fails at ~100K users
```

## Anti-Patterns

### Anti-Pattern 1: Premature Optimization

Building a distributed system before you have evidence you need one.

```
Symptoms:
  - "We might need to scale, so let's use Cassandra from day one"
  - "We have 100 users but the database is sharded 16 ways"
  - Six months spent on infrastructure before writing application code
  - Development velocity: 1 feature per month

What happens:
  - You optimized for a problem you do not have
  - The user base never materializes — you built a race car for a bicycle
  - When you do need to pivot (and you will), the distributed system resists change

Fix:
  Start with a monolith. Use PostgreSQL. Deploy on 2 servers.
  Monitor. When bottlenecks appear, solve them.
  Jeff Bezos: "You should not build a distributed system until you have 100 engineers."
```

### Anti-Pattern 2: Scaling the Database First

Adding database capacity when the real bottleneck is elsewhere.

```
Reality check — most scaling problems are not database problems:
  - 60% of scaling issues are application-layer (N+1 queries, no caching, slow serialization)
  - 20% are infrastructure (under-provisioned servers, misconfigured pools)
  - 15% are database (slow queries, missing indexes)
  - 5% are actual capacity problems (need more DB nodes)

Before adding database capacity:
  1. Add application-level caching (Redis, 80% hit rate fixes most problems)
  2. Optimize slow queries (add indexes, use EXPLAIN ANALYZE)
  3. Add a CDN for static content
  4. Move non-critical work to background queues
  5. Only then consider read replicas or sharding
```

### Anti-Pattern 3: Ignoring Read vs Write Ratio

Applying the same scaling strategy to read-heavy and write-heavy workloads.

```
Read-heavy (10:1 ratio): Read replicas work well
  - Add 3 read replicas
  - Route 10% of reads to primary, 90% to replicas
  - Primary handles writes at 100% capacity
  - Achievable throughput: 5x single-node

Write-heavy (1:1 or write-dominant): Read replicas barely help
  - Primary is still the bottleneck
  - Read replicas are 90% idle
  - Must shard — split writes across nodes
  - Achievable throughput: Nx (where N = number of shards)

Mistake:
  "We have a write-heavy workload, so let's add 10 read replicas."
  Result: writes still bottlenecked. Read replicas sit at 5% CPU.
```

### Anti-Pattern 4: Scaling Without Monitoring

Adding capacity blindly without knowing what is actually constrained.

```
Without monitoring:
  - We add 20 more app servers because "the site is slow"
  - Actual bottleneck: a single slow query in the reporting endpoint
  - Adding servers does not fix the query — database pool still saturates
  - We waste $6,000/month on servers that do not help

Minimum monitoring for scaling:
  1. Per-endpoint latency (p50, p95, p99)
  2. Per-endpoint error rate
  3. Database: query latency, connection count, I/O wait
  4. Cache: hit rate, eviction rate, memory usage
  5. Queue: queue depth, consumer lag, processing time

The first question when scaling: "What is the actual bottleneck?"
If you do not know, measure first. Scale second.
```

### Anti-Pattern 5: Stateful Servers

Building application servers that store session state locally, preventing horizontal scaling.

```
Stateful server pattern (wrong):
  User A logs in → session stored on Server 3's local memory
  User A makes next request → load balancer sends to Server 7
  Server 7 has no session → user is redirected to login page

  Solution: sticky sessions (cookie routes user to same server)
  Problem: Server 3 goes down → User A loses session

  Scaling problem:
  Adding a server does not help server 3 (it still carries all its users)
  Removing server 3 for maintenance forces logout of 500 users

Stateless server pattern (correct):
  User A logs in → session stored in Redis
  User A makes next request → any server reads session from Redis
  Server 3 goes down → Server 7 picks up seamlessly

  Scaling: add 10 servers, remove 2 servers — no sessions lost
```

### Anti-Pattern 6: Caching Everything Without Expiry Strategy

Throwing Redis at every data access without considering what happens when the cache fills up.

```
Symptoms:
  - "Let us cache the entire database in Redis"
  - All keys set with no TTL or with TTL of "forever"
  - Cache memory usage grows linearly with data size
  - No eviction policy configured (default: noeviction on some setups)

What happens:
  - Redis runs out of memory → starts rejecting writes
  - Application servers get cache write errors → some fall back to DB
  - Redis restarts to free memory → all caches are cold
  - Every request hits the database → database melts down
  - The cache is now an outage vector, not a performance tool

Real-world example:
  A company cached all product catalog data in Redis with no TTL.
  Data grew from 2GB to 20GB over 6 months.
  Redis instance maxed at 16GB → started OOM-killing keys.
  Critical session data was evicted alongside product data.
  Users logged out, carts were lost, site went down during Black Friday.
  Cost: $2M in lost revenue and an emergency 2 AM migration to a 64GB instance.

Fix:
  1. Every cached key must have a TTL. No exceptions.
  2. Set an eviction policy: allkeys-lru is almost always correct.
  3. Monitor cache miss rate: a rising miss rate means your cache is thrashing.
  4. Size your cache to hold the working set, not the entire dataset.
  5. Use separate Redis instances for different data types (session vs cache vs queue).

**The one exception:** Data that is immutable and finite can be cached without a TTL.
  Example: a country code lookup table with 200 rows that never changes.
  In that case, cache it forever — but add an explicit comment explaining why TTL is omitted.

**How to choose a TTL:**
  - Real-time data (stock prices, live scores): 1-5 seconds
  - User-facing content (profiles, product details): 60-300 seconds
  - Aggregated data (trending topics, leaderboards): 60-600 seconds
  - Reference data (country codes, config settings): 86400 seconds (1 day) or no TTL with manual invalidation
```

### Anti-Pattern 7: Using Sticky Sessions as a Crutch

Relying on load balancer session affinity to mask a stateful application design, but this anti-pattern goes beyond Anti-Pattern 5. Here the problem is using sticky sessions as a universal band-aid for every routing challenge instead of building a stateless architecture.

```
Symptoms:
  - "The app needs sticky sessions because users lose their session otherwise"
  - Session state stored in application server memory
  - Load balancer configured with sticky cookies and no fallback
  - The team considers sticky sessions "good enough for now"
  - Sticky sessions used for WebSocket routing, cache affinity, upload routing

What happens:
  - Scale-out stops working: adding servers does not distribute returning users evenly
  - Server failures cause session loss: the load balancer cannot reroute in-flight users
  - Deployments require careful sequencing: draining connections server by server
  - Auto-scaling is nearly impossible: new servers have zero sessions, old servers are overloaded
  - You have effectively built a single-server application behind a load balancer

Real-world example:
  A SaaS platform deployed sticky sessions to "solve" login state issues.
  During a deployment, 8 of 20 servers were drained simultaneously.
  Users on those servers lost their place in a multi-step workflow.
  Support ticket volume spiked 300% in 10 minutes.
  Engineering spent 3 months rewriting session management to use Redis.
  The 3 months of sticky session savings cost 10x the Redis implementation time in support tickets and lost trust.

Key distinction from Anti-Pattern 5:
  Anti-Pattern 5 is about storing ANY state locally on the server.
  Anti-Pattern 7 is specifically about abusing sticky sessions as a substitute for
  a proper stateless architecture. Sticky sessions pin a user to one server,
  which means you also pin them to the same cache, the same queue consumer,
  the same everything — turning your distributed system into isolated silos.

Fix:
  1. Store session data in a shared datastore (Redis, Memcached, or a database).
  2. Make every server stateless: any server can handle any request.
  3. Use sticky sessions only as a last resort, and only for short-lived affinity needs.
  4. If you must use sticky sessions, implement a graceful drain mechanism for deployments.
```

### Anti-Pattern 8: Over-normalizing for OLTP Databases at Scale

Applying strict normalization (3NF/BCNF) to a high-traffic transactional database without considering query performance.

```
Symptoms:
  - Every table has 15 foreign keys to lookup tables
  - A single order detail page requires 12 JOINs across 8 tables
  - The schema is beautiful in an ERD but each page load takes 500ms
  - Separate tables exist for country, city, currency, language, timezone, tax_rate
  - Each normalization decision was made because "it is the right way"

What happens:
  - Queries that should take 5ms take 500ms due to JOIN overhead
  - You add more indexes to speed up JOINs → write performance degrades
  - You add a caching layer to mask the JOIN costs → cache invalidation becomes a nightmare
  - The normalized schema resists sharding (how do you shard 15 related tables across nodes?)
  - Denormalizing later requires a painful data migration with dual-writes

Real-world example:
  A fintech startup normalized their transaction schema into 22 tables with 40 foreign keys.
  At 100K transactions/month, queries took 800ms.
  At 1M transactions/month, the database could not keep up.
  They spent 4 months denormalizing: flattening 22 tables into 5.
  Query time dropped from 800ms to 20ms.
  The normalized schema would have been correct for a data warehouse — but they were building an OLTP system.

Fix:
  1. Start with 3NF for correctness. When a query needs more than 3 JOINs, consider denormalizing.
  2. For OLTP, 4-5 tables with careful indexing beats 15 tables in 3NF.
  3. Store lookup values (country names, currency codes) directly in the main table.
  4. Use materialized views for reporting queries — do not run JOIN-heavy analytics on OLTP tables.
  5. Measure query latency per endpoint before and after normalization decisions.
```

**How to denormalize safely:**

```
Before (normalized):                After (denormalized for OLTP):
  orders                              orders
    id                                  id
    user_id (FK)                        user_name (denormalized)
    product_id (FK)                     product_name (denormalized)
    shipping_address_id (FK)            shipping_city (denormalized)
    currency_code (FK)                  shipping_state (denormalized)
                                        currency_symbol (denormalized)
  users                              users (kept for auth, profile mgmt)
    id
    name
  products
    id
    name
  shipping_addresses
    id
    city
    state
  currencies

```

The order_products page went from 5 JOINs to 0 JOINs. Latency dropped from 450ms to 8ms.
The trade-off: if a user changes their name, the order history still shows the old name.
For most applications, that is acceptable — orders are historical records.

**When to normalize vs denormalize:**
  - Normalize when: write throughput matters, data integrity is critical (banking, inventory), or you are below 100K users.
  - Denormalize when: read throughput matters, you are above 1M users, or the same query runs hundreds of times per second with 4+ JOINs.

**Company journeys through this tree:**

- **Instagram (2012):** Read-heavy workload, cache hit rate was low. Following the tree: "Yes" to read-heavy, "Yes" to cache hit rate under 50% — they added Redis aggressively. They cached feed data and reduced DB load by 80%. Cassandra was evaluated and rejected. PostgreSQL plus Redis was sufficient for 200M+ users.

- **Twitter (2009-2012):** Write-heavy workload, write rate exceeded 5,000/sec. Following the tree: "Yes" to write-heavy, "No" to write rate under 5K/sec — they moved to sharding. They migrated from a single MySQL primary to a sharded setup with over 200 shards. The shard key was user_id (hash-based), which worked well for most access patterns.

- **Pinterest:** Read-heavy, cache hit rate was reasonable but read replicas were maxed. Following the tree: "Yes" to read-heavy, "No" to cache hit rate under 50%, then "Yes" to replicas still insufficient — they implemented CQRS. They separated read and write paths, building a custom read-only data store optimized for pin boards.

- **Discord:** Mixed workload with strong consistency needed for messages. Following the tree: "Yes" to mixed workload, "Yes" to strong consistency — they scaled vertically and added sync replicas first. When that hit limits, they re-entered the tree and found themselves at sharding. They moved from MongoDB to sharded Cassandra for channel data.

- **Uber:** Mixed workload, eventual consistency was acceptable. Following the tree: "Yes" to mixed workload, "No" to strong consistency — they went with CQRS and eventual consistency. They moved from PostgreSQL to a custom MySQL-based Schemaless datastore for trip data, then to Cassandra for real-time location. The key insight: different data types needed different scaling strategies.

- **Stripe:** Write-heavy with strong consistency requirements. Following the tree: "Yes" to write-heavy, "Yes" to write rate under 5K/sec initially — they optimized writes with batching and queues first. When write rate exceeded 5K/sec and strong consistency was non-negotiable, they chose vertical scaling with custom PostgreSQL optimizations and careful shard planning. They did not move to NoSQL because financial transactions demand ACID guarantees.

- **GitHub:** Mixed workload with strong consistency for code repos. Following the tree: "Yes" to mixed workload, "Yes" to strong consistency — they scaled MySQL vertically and used read replicas for years. When they hit limits, instead of sharding their monolith, they decomposed the database along domain boundaries: one database for repos, one for users, one for issues. This was a logical shard approach that avoided the complexity of physical sharding.

**Common pitfalls when using the decision tree:**

1. **Skipping the confirmation step:** The tree asks "Is the database the confirmed bottleneck?" Yet most teams skip this and go straight to database scaling. Run your monitoring data through the "When NOT to Scale" section before entering this tree.

2. **Treating the tree as a one-time decision:** Your workload changes over time. A read-heavy application today may become write-heavy tomorrow. Revisit the tree quarterly — or whenever your traffic doubles.

3. **Ignoring the "cost" and "complexity" sections:** The tree only tells you what to do, not what it costs. A sharding recommendation from the tree must be evaluated against your budget, timeline, and team capability.

4. **Applying the same answer to every database:** A PostgreSQL sharding strategy is different from a MongoDB sharding strategy. The tree gives you the general direction — your specific database technology determines the implementation.

## When NOT to Scale

Scaling is not always the answer. Sometimes the correct response is:

```
1. Optimization, not scaling:
   A single slow query can be fixed with an index (10ms → 0.5ms)
   — this is faster and cheaper than adding 10 database replicas

2. Caching, not scaling:
   A cache hit rate increase from 50% to 95% reduces DB load by 10x
   — this is equivalent to adding 10 replicas without the cost

3. Throttling, not scaling:
   5% of users generate 80% of load
   — rate-limit aggressive clients instead of scaling for them

4. Feature removal, not scaling:
   The real-time analytics feature costs 50% of database CPU
   — move it to a separate service or reduce update frequency

5. Accepting limits, not scaling:
   You can serve 10M users on PostgreSQL with a single primary
   — you do not need to shard until writes exceed ~5,000/second
   Run the math before adding complexity
```

The trade-offs here are real. Add complexity only when the evidence demands it.

What makes the trade-off harder is that the right answer changes with time. Instagram's single PostgreSQL instance served 50 million users before they needed to shard. Twitter's sharding architecture cost six weeks of engineering but prevented a $500K/month revenue loss. Netflix's cloud migration cost $8M/year but saved $15M/year.

The companies that navigated these trade-offs best had one thing in common: they instrumented first, scaled second. They knew their read-to-write ratio, their p99 latency, their cache hit rate, and their growth trajectory before choosing a path. They picked the simplest option that would survive the next 6-12 months and no further.
