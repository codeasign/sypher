## How Consensus Appears in System Design Interviews

Consensus appears in system design interviews in three ways. First, as a dedicated design problem: design a distributed configuration service (like etcd or ZooKeeper). Second, as a component within a larger design: how would you ensure consistency in a distributed database, a lock service, or a leader election mechanism. Third, as a consideration in broader discussions: what happens when the coordinator fails in a distributed file system or a message queue.

The interviewer is testing four things: whether the candidate knows when consensus is needed, whether they can explain the trade-offs between consensus and eventual consistency, whether they understand the failure modes of consensus systems, and whether they can reason about the operational implications of deploying a consensus system.

## What the Interviewer Is Testing

**Awareness of the problem**: The candidate should identify that consensus is needed when multiple nodes must agree on a single value despite failures. They should know the FLP impossibility result and its implications.

**Algorithm knowledge**: The candidate should be familiar with at least one consensus algorithm (Raft is preferred for interviews due to its understandability). They should be able to explain the phases of leader election and log replication.

**Trade-off reasoning**: The candidate should discuss the safety vs liveness trade-off, the overhead of consensus (multiple round trips and disk fsyncs), and the scalability limitations.

**Failure mode analysis**: The candidate should describe what happens when the leader fails, when a network partition occurs, and when a slow node causes tail latency.

**Operational knowledge**: The candidate should mention cluster sizing (odd number of nodes), monitoring (leader changes, commit latency), and the importance of disk performance.

## Level-by-Level Expectations

### Associate / Junior (0-2 years)

Define consensus as the problem of getting distributed nodes to agree on a value. Name at least one consensus algorithm (Raft). Explain that a quorum is a majority of nodes. Describe what happens when a leader fails in simple terms. Acknowledge that consensus adds latency compared to single-node systems.

### Mid-Level (2-5 years)

Explain the Raft leader election and log replication in detail. Describe the minimum cluster sizes for fault tolerance. Compare consensus to eventual consistency and explain when each is appropriate. Discuss the performance implications of different cluster sizes. Mention the FLP impossibility result at a high level.

### Senior (5-8 years)

Describe the internal architecture of a consensus node (log, state machine, messaging layer). Explain log matching and the commitment rules in detail. Discuss the implications of different network partition scenarios. Compare Raft, Paxos, and Zab, including their trade-offs. Describe how multi-Raft achieves horizontal scalability. Discuss operational concerns: monitoring, compaction, backup, and recovery.

### Staff+ (8+ years)

Design a consensus system from scratch, including the interaction between leader election and log replication. Discuss the subtle safety properties and how they are enforced. Describe the impact of fsync batching on throughput and durability. Analyze the performance characteristics of different cluster topologies (single region, multi-region). Discuss the evolution of consensus algorithms (EPaxos, Flexible Paxos, WPaxos). Relate consensus to broader distributed systems concepts like Byzantine fault tolerance and blockchain.

## Phrase Bank

Use these phrases to communicate clearly and demonstrate depth.

**Explaining the core idea**: "Consensus ensures that a majority of nodes agree on a value before that value is considered committed. This guarantees that even if the leader fails, the new leader will find the committed value on at least one node."

**Describing quorum**: "For an N-node cluster, the quorum is N/2 plus 1. With 5 nodes, we need 3 to agree. This guarantees that any two quorums overlap by at least one node."

**Describing leader election**: "When the leader fails, followers detect the failure through missed heartbeats. The first follower to time out starts an election by incrementing its term and requesting votes. It wins if it receives a majority."

**Describing log replication**: "The leader appends each client request to its log, then sends the entry to all followers. A follower acknowledges only if its log matches the leader's at the preceding entry. The leader commits when a majority of followers have acknowledged."

**Describing the safety guarantee**: "If a value is committed, it can never be overwritten. The new leader always has the committed value because the quorum for the election overlaps with the quorum for the commit."

**Addressing performance**: "Consensus writes require fsync on at least a majority of nodes. This adds 3-10ms to each write. Throughput is bounded by the leader's network bandwidth, typically 10,000 to 30,000 ops/sec."

**Addressing scalability**: "A single consensus group does not scale beyond 7-9 nodes. For larger systems, use Multi-Raft: partition the data into ranges and run a separate Raft group for each range."

**Addressing multi-region**: "Multi-region consensus adds cross-region latency to writes. A leader in US East writing to followers in US West (40ms) and EU West (80ms) has write latency bounded by the slowest link needed for quorum."

**Describing failure recovery**: "When a failed leader recovers, it becomes a follower, catches up on missed log entries, and continues. When a follower recovers, the leader detects it through heartbeats and replicates any missing entries."

**Describing monitoring**: "The key metrics are leader changes per hour, commit latency at p99, and follower commit index lag. Leader changes should be near zero in steady state."

**Explaining when NOT to use consensus**: "Consensus is overkill if the system can tolerate eventual consistency, if throughput requirements exceed 30K ops/sec, or if sub-millisecond write latency is required. Use CRDTs, last-writer-wins, or single-leader replication instead."

## Red Flags

Interviewers watch for these mistakes. Avoiding them demonstrates strong understanding.

**Red flag 1: Suggesting a 2-node cluster.** A 2-node cluster is an even number. If one node fails, the remaining one node cannot form a majority. 2-node clusters also risk split-brain. Always use an odd number.

**Red flag 2: Claiming consensus works at scale with hundreds of nodes.** A single consensus group with 100 nodes would require 51 nodes to respond for quorum. The leader's outbound bandwidth would be saturated, and tail latency would spike. Consensus groups are limited to 3-7 nodes.

**Red flag 3: Ignoring the fsync requirement.** Consensus writes are not durable until they are fsynced to disk on a majority of nodes. Proposing a consensus system without durable storage is a critical flaw. The data durability guarantee depends on disk persistence.

**Red flag 4: Proposing consensus for all consistency needs.** Using consensus for every write (including non-critical metadata, ephemeral state, or high-throughput logging) wastes resources. Consensus should be reserved for data that requires strong consistency.

**Red flag 5: Ignoring the network partition scenario.** The candidate should explain what happens during a partition: the majority side continues, the minority side cannot commit writes. They should not claim the system remains fully available during a partition.

**Red flag 6: Claiming zero-latency consensus.** All consensus algorithms require at least one network round trip plus disk writes. Proposing sub-millisecond consensus writes without acknowledging the hardware cost shows lack of understanding.

**Red flag 7: Not mentioning leader changes.** The candidate should describe the leader election process and what happens to in-flight requests during the election. Failing to address this suggests incomplete knowledge.

**Red flag 8: Suggesting read-after-write consistency without quorum reads.** If a client writes to the leader and then reads from a follower that has not received the write, the client sees stale data. The candidate should explain read quorums or leader reads for strong consistency.

## Practice Drills

### Drill 1: Design a Distributed Lock Service (15 minutes)

Requirements: A lock service that allows clients to acquire and release locks with a timeout. Must handle client crashes without releasing the lock permanently. Must be fault-tolerant across data centers.

Practice approach: Start with a 3-node Raft cluster. The lock state is stored as a key-value pair in the log. Lock acquisition is a write to the log (put lock_key = client_id with a lease duration). Lock release is another write. Heartbeats extend the lease. If a client fails to renew, the lock expires automatically after the lease period.

### Drill 2: Design a Configuration Service (20 minutes)

Requirements: A configuration service that stores application configuration key-value pairs. Must support watches (notify clients when a value changes). Must handle 10,000 reads per second and 100 writes per second. Must survive 2 node failures.

Practice approach: Use a 5-node Raft cluster. Configs are stored as keys in the Raft state machine. A watch is maintained as a list of connected clients and the keys they are watching. When a key is updated, the leader broadcasts the change to all watching clients. Reads from followers with optional quorum reads for strong consistency. Use a lease-based session mechanism for client health.

### Drill 3: Design a Leader Election Mechanism for a Database (10 minutes)

Requirements: A primary-backup database must elect a primary. The election must guarantee that only one node acts as primary at any time. The election must complete within 500ms of the primary failure.

Practice approach: Use an etcd cluster (3 nodes) for leader election. Each database node creates an ephemeral key in etcd (election/primary). The first node to create the key becomes primary. Other nodes watch the key. When the primary crashes, etcd deletes the ephemeral key (session expires), and the next node creates the key and becomes primary. Total failover time: etcd session timeout (1 second) plus key creation time (under 10ms).

### Drill 4: Analyze a Consensus Failure (10 minutes)

Scenario: A 5-node etcd cluster experiences 3 simultaneous node failures. The remaining 2 nodes are alive but cannot form a quorum. The application is unresponsive. What steps do you take?

Expected approach: Confirm that quorum is lost (need 3 of 5, have 2). Check if any of the 3 failed nodes can be restarted. If at least 1 restarts, quorum is restored with 3 of 5. If none can restart, restore from a recent snapshot on an offline cluster, then bring up the restored cluster with new member addresses. Document the loss of any writes that were not included in the snapshot.

### Drill 5: Compare Consensus Algorithms (10 minutes)

Practice comparing Raft, Paxos, and Zab across these dimensions: understandability, implementation complexity, performance in steady state, performance under leader changes, ecosystem support, and community adoption. The most important insight: all three achieve the same safety guarantees. The differences are in how they achieve liveness and how easy they are to implement correctly.

### Drill 6: Design a Fault-Tolerant Configuration Service (15 minutes)

A startup needs a configuration service that stores application configuration (key-value pairs). The service must survive 2 simultaneous node failures. Configuration is read frequently (10,000 reads per second) but written rarely (10 writes per second). The application needs to be notified when configuration changes (watching). The team has no budget for dedicated hardware.

Practice approach: Use a 5-node Raft cluster deployed across 3 availability zones in a single cloud region (2 nodes in AZ-A, 2 in AZ-B, 1 in AZ-C). Writes go through the Raft leader (10 ops/sec is well within Raft's capacity). Reads are served by any node using read leases for linearizable consistency. Watching is implemented as long-polling connections to each node, with the leader broadcasting change notifications.

## Sample Interview Scripts

The following scripts show how a strong candidate responds to interview questions about consensus.

### Script 1: Design a Distributed Lock Service

Interviewer: "Design a distributed lock service for a distributed system."

Candidate: "I will use a Raft-based consensus cluster for the lock service. The cluster needs 5 nodes for fault tolerance (tolerates 2 failures). Each lock is a key-value pair in the Raft state machine, with the key being the lock name, the value being the client identifier, and an associated lease expiration time.

For lock acquisition, the client sends an Acquire(lock_name, client_id, lease_duration) request. The request goes to any node; if it hits a follower, the follower forwards it to the leader. The leader appends the request to the Raft log. The entry is committed when a majority of nodes acknowledge it. The leader checks the state machine: if the lock is free or expired, it assigns the lock to the client and responds with success. If the lock is held by another client, it responds with failure or busy.

For lock release, the client sends a Release(lock_name, client_id) request. The leader validates that the client holds the lock, then appends a release entry. On commit, the lock is freed.

For client crashes, the lease mechanism handles it. Each lock has a TTL. The client must periodically extend the lease by sending a heartbeat. If the client stops heartbeating, the lock expires automatically. The lease duration must be long enough to tolerate GC pauses but short enough that locks are released promptly.

For consistency after leader change, the new leader inherits the same state machine. All locks and their lease times are preserved because they were committed to a majority before the leader change.

The system handles the split-brain case because only the side with a majority can elect a leader and commit writes. The minority side rejects all writes."

### Script 2: Consensus in a Distributed Database

Interviewer: "You are designing a distributed database. How do you ensure data consistency across replicas?"

Candidate: "I will use Raft consensus for log replication. Each write is appended to the Raft log, replicated to a majority of nodes, and committed. This provides linearizable consistency: once a write is committed, all subsequent reads see it.

The database maintains an in-memory state machine that is updated by applying committed log entries. Snapshotting periodically captures the state of the state machine to bound the log size. New replicas join by receiving a snapshot followed by incremental log entries.

Read requests can be served by the leader for linearizable reads, or by followers with read leases for slightly relaxed consistency. For maximum read throughput, I would configure follower reads with a 1-second lease, allowing followers to serve reads without contacting the leader for that duration.

The primary operational concern is disk latency for fsyncs. I would configure the system with dedicated SSDs for the Raft log, separate from the data storage. This prevents disk contention between the consensus protocol and the database engine."

### Script 3: Network Partition Analysis

Interviewer: "A 5-node Raft cluster experiences a network partition that isolates 2 nodes. Describe what happens."

Candidate: "The cluster splits into two groups: Group A with 3 nodes and Group B with 2 nodes. Group A has a majority of 3 out of 5 nodes. Group A continues to elect a leader (if needed) and commit writes. Group B has only 2 nodes, which is not a quorum for a 5-node cluster. Group B cannot elect a leader or commit any writes.

During the partition, all client requests that reach Group B are rejected or timeout. Clients should retry with exponential backoff. Eventually, clients that can retry will reach Group A and succeed.

When the partition heals, the Group B nodes reconnect. They discover that Group A's leader has a higher term (or at least valid term) and a more up-to-date log. The Group B nodes step down as followers and catch up by receiving all the log entries they missed during the partition. In-flight writes that were accepted by Group A are committed. Writes that were sent to Group B are lost and must be retried by the client.

This scenario demonstrates the CP (Consistency and Partition tolerance) behavior of consensus: during a partition, availability is sacrificed on the minority side to maintain consistency across the system."

## Additional Practice Scenarios

**Scenario 1**: Design a cluster membership service for a microservice architecture. The service must track which services are alive, their health check endpoints, and their configuration. Must handle 100,000 service instances. Must be available across 3 data centers.

**Scenario 2**: A 3-node Raft cluster has a leader that experiences a hardware fault causing every fsync to take 500ms. Client write latency jumps from 2ms to 500ms. Diagnose the problem and provide a solution.

**Scenario 3**: You are migrating a ZooKeeper ensemble from version 3.5 to 3.6. The ensemble has 5 nodes serving 500 Kafka brokers. Plan the migration with zero downtime for Kafka.
