# Replication — Interview Playbook

Replication appears in every stage of a system design interview. Whether the question is explicitly about databases or tangentially about availability, the interviewer wants to hear you reason about data consistency, failure modes, and operational trade-offs. This page maps the replication interview landscape and gives you the framework, vocabulary, and practice drills to handle any replication question.

## How This Topic Appears

Replication questions come in four categories. Each tests a different dimension of your knowledge.

### Direct Questions

These questions state the topic explicitly. They test your ability to explain replication end-to-end.

1. "Design a replication strategy for a global social network with 100 million users."
2. "How would you implement replication for a financial transaction system with strict consistency requirements?"
3. "Compare synchronous and asynchronous replication. When would you use each?"
4. "Design the replication layer for a multi-region e-commerce platform."

### Design Questions (Implicit)

Replication is a component of a larger system. The interviewer waits to see if you bring it up.

5. "Design Twitter's timeline service." (Replication matters for the tweet database and the fanout queue.)
6. "Design a real-time collaborative document editor like Google Docs." (CRDT-based replication is the core architecture.)
7. "Design YouTube's video metadata service." (Replication handles read-scale for video metadata lookups.)

### Debugging Questions

These present a broken system and ask you to diagnose the problem.

8. "Users report that their comments disappear and reappear on page refresh. What is happening?"
9. "Our database primary failed and the replica was promoted. Now some user data from the last 5 minutes is missing. Why?"
10. "Write latency suddenly increased by 100x. Replicas are healthy. Where do you look?"

### Trade-off Questions

These ask you to compare approaches and explain why you chose one.

11. "Why not just use synchronous replication everywhere?"
12. "Your system needs to span 5 regions. Single-leader or multi-leader? Why?"
13. "What is the maximum number of replicas you can add before replication becomes counterproductive?"

## What the Interviewer Is Actually Testing

Every replication question tests these five things, in order of importance:

1. **Ability to identify the consistency requirements of the system.** You cannot design replication without understanding the data. Financial transactions need strong consistency. News feeds tolerate eventual consistency. The candidate who asks "what consistency does this system need?" before drawing a diagram is the candidate who gets hired.

2. **Understanding of failure modes.** The interviewer wants to know you have seen a replication lag spike or a split-brain situation, or at least know what they look like. Mentioning specific incidents (Recovery: WAL disk full, split-brain fencing, cascading lag) signals production experience.

3. **Quantitative reasoning.** Saying "replication lag might be a problem" is weak. Saying "at 5,000 writes/second, each 1 KB, with 3 replicas on 10 Gbps networking, the primary's outbound WAL bandwidth is 15 MB/s and the bottleneck is the replica's single-threaded apply at 8,000 entries/second, so we expect 0.6 seconds of lag at peak" demonstrates mastery.

4. **Trade-off awareness.** Every replication decision is a trade-off. The candidate who only describes benefits without costs misses the point. For every feature you propose, state what you are giving up: "synchronous replication guarantees zero data loss but adds 2-5 ms per write."

5. **Operational maturity.** The candidate who says "we will deploy Patroni for automated failover, monitor replication lag in Grafana with a p99 alert at 10 seconds, and have a runbook for WAL disk full" shows they understand that replication is operated, not just designed.

## Level-by-Level Expectations

### L3 / L4 (Junior / Mid-Level)

**What they ask:** "What is replication?" "How does synchronous replication differ from asynchronous?"

**Competent answer:** "Replication copies data from a primary to replicas. Synchronous replication waits for the replica to acknowledge the write before telling the client it is done. Asynchronous replication acknowledges immediately and replicates in the background."

**Strong answer:** Adds trade-offs: "Synchronous replication guarantees zero data loss on failover but increases write latency by at least one network round-trip and reduces throughput. Asynchronous replication is fast but risks losing the most recent writes if the primary crashes before replication completes. Financial systems typically use synchronous replication for transactions and asynchronous for reporting."

**What sets you apart at this level:** Mentioning replication lag as a concern. Naming the components (WAL, LSN, replication slot). Giving a specific threshold: "I would set a 5-second timeout on sync replication to avoid blocking."

### L5 (Senior)

**What they ask:** "Design a replication strategy for a multi-region application." "A user's write is not visible on the next page load. Diagnose and fix."

**Competent answer:** "Use single-leader replication with read replicas in each region. Route writes to the primary. Route reads to the nearest replica. Monitor replication lag."

**Strong answer:** "I would use multi-leader replication with one leader per region. Each leader accepts local writes for low latency. Leaders replicate to each other asynchronously. Conflict resolution uses last-writer-wins based on server-assigned timestamps. For the read-after-write problem, the write response includes the leader's commit LSN, and the application verifies the replica's LSN exceeds it before reading. If the replica is behind, the application reads from the leader for the next 5 seconds."

**What sets you apart at this level:** Describing the conflict resolution mechanism in detail. Mentioning the speed-of-light limitation on cross-region sync. Providing a concrete capacity plan with numbers.

### L6 (Staff)

**What they ask:** "The database experiences a cascading lag failure during peak traffic. How do you redesign the system to prevent this?"

**Competent answer:** "Add more replicas. Monitor lag better. Use synchronous replication for critical data."

**Strong answer:** "The root cause is that write throughput on the primary exceeds replica apply throughput. I would: (1) implement replica-of-replica cascading to reduce WAL sender load on the primary, (2) add a dedicated sync replica with no read traffic for durability, (3) implement query prioritization on replicas so reporting queries do not block WAL replay, (4) add a write buffer between the application and the primary to absorb write bursts, and (5) implement automatic replica degradation — if a replica falls behind by 30 seconds, remove it from the read pool and trigger a diagnostic alert. At the architecture level, I would evaluate whether this workload needs sharding, because if the write rate consistently exceeds replica apply capacity, replication itself cannot solve the problem."

**What sets you apart at this level:** Root cause analysis. Systemic thinking — the fix is not a single configuration change but a multi-layered approach. Sharding evaluation shows you know when replication is the wrong tool.

### L7 / L8 (Principal / Director)

**What they ask:** "Design a global replication strategy for a system that must maintain strong consistency with p99 write latency under 10 ms across 5 continents."

**Competent answer:** "That is not physically possible with synchronous replication due to the speed of light. I would explore alternative approaches."

**Strong answer:** "Strong consistency and single-digit millisecond writes across continents are physically impossible — the speed of light imposes a minimum of 67 ms between hemispheres. The solution must relax one constraint. Options: (1) redefine 'strong consistency' to per-region linearizability with async cross-region replication, (2) use a strongly consistent shard-per-region model where user data lives in one region and cross-region queries are read-only, (3) use Calvin/Fauna-style deterministic transaction ordering that batches cross-region writes into epochs. Of these, option 2 (shard-per-region) is the most practical and is what Google, Meta, and Amazon actually do. The key insight is that user data is naturally local: 95% of a user's interactions involve their own data or data in their region. Design for that locality."

**What sets you apart at this level:** Naming the physical limit explicitly. Pivoting from impossible constraints to practical solutions. Referencing how actual large-scale systems solve the problem.

## The FAANG Framework for Answering

Use this framework for any replication design question. It ensures you cover the interviewer's signals in the right order.

**Step 1 — Clarify Requirements (2 minutes)**
- "Is this a read-heavy or write-heavy workload?"
- "What consistency guarantees does the application need?"
- "How many users? What is the data size? What regions?"
- "What is the acceptable write latency? Read latency?"

**Step 2 — State the Core Trade-off (1 minute)**
- "This system needs high read throughput and availability. The main trade-off is between replication lag (async gives us 3 ms writes but potential stale reads) and write latency (sync gives us consistency but 5-10 ms writes). Based on the requirements, I will start with..."

**Step 3 — Draw the Architecture (3 minutes)**
- Primary node + replica count + replication mode.
- Connection poolers, load balancers, failover coordinator.
- Read/write splitting logic.
- Monitoring and alerting.

**Step 4 — Walk Through the Write Path (2 minutes)**
- "A write arrives at the primary. Here is what happens..."
- Show the WAL append, replica stream, acknowledgement.
- Mention what breaks: "If the replica is slow, this happens..."

**Step 5 — Walk Through the Read Path (1 minute)**
- "A read arrives at the load balancer. It picks a replica. If the application needs read-after-write consistency..."
- Describe the stale read mitigation.

**Step 6 — Discuss Failure Modes and Mitigations (2 minutes)**
- "The primary crashes — here is the failover process."
- "A replica lags — here is how we detect and handle it."
- "A network partition — here is how we prevent split-brain."

## Phrase Bank

Using precise language signals experience. Here are expert-level phrasings mapped to context.

| Context | Novice Phrase | Expert Phrase |
|---------|---------------|---------------|
| Consistency | "Data might be a little old" | "The replica's readable LSN lags behind the primary's commit LSN by 14 bytes, which represents approximately 2 writes at current throughput" |
| Trade-off | "Sync is slow but safe" | "Synchronous replication trades write latency and throughput for a durability guarantee against single-node failures, but it does not protect against the scenario where both primary and sync replica fail simultaneously" |
| Failover | "Switch to the backup" | "Patroni's distributed consensus via etcd prevents split-brain by ensuring only one node holds the leader lease at any time, using a lease expiry of 15 seconds with a 5-second grace period" |
| Lag | "The replica is behind" | "The replica's apply rate of 8,000 entries/second is below the primary's commit rate of 11,000 entries/second, meaning the lag of 800 KB will grow without bound unless we throttle writes or add a faster replica" |
| Anti-pattern | "Do not do that" | "The synchronous standby count should never exceed the number of replicas you are willing to block writes for — setting it to 3 means a single replica failure stalls all writes" |
| Capacity | "Add more replicas" | "Each replica consumes approximately 100 Mbps of WAL bandwidth at our peak write rate, so 10 replicas would consume 1 Gbps of the primary's total 10 Gbps allocation" |
| Conflict | "The data might conflict" | "Last-writer-wins discards the earlier write unconditionally; for counter data we use a CRDT PN-Counter that merges increments, and for sets we use observed-remove sets that preserve add-before-remove semantics" |

## Red Flags

Interviewers are trained to spot these signals of shallow replication knowledge.

1. **"Replication is simple — just copy the data."** Shows you have never operated replication in production. The complexity is in consistency guarantees, failure recovery, and performance tuning.

2. **"Synchronous replication is always better."** Shows you do not understand the throughput and latency costs.

3. **"We use 5 replicas to be safe" with no justification.** Replica count should be calculated from throughput requirements, not intuition.

4. **No mention of replication lag.** The single most important operational metric for replication, and you ignored it.

5. **"Failover is automatic, we do not worry about it."** Shows you have never been paged for a failed failover.

6. **"We use strong consistency everywhere."** Either you do not know the cost of strong consistency, or the system does not actually use it and you are over-engineering.

7. **No mention of WAL or replication log.** The central data structure of replication. If you cannot name it, you do not understand the mechanism.

8. **"Split-brain cannot happen with our setup."** Split-brain can happen with any system that has automated failover. The question is not whether it can happen but how you prevent and detect it.

## Practice Drills

Each drill is a timed prompt. Set a 5-minute timer and answer verbally or in writing.

**Drill 1: Three-Tier Read Routing (5 min)**
"An e-commerce site uses one primary and three replicas. Some reads need strong consistency (order status), some need freshness within 10 seconds (product page), and some can be stale up to 5 minutes (recommendations). Design the read routing logic."

*Self-check:* Did you define three tiers? Did you mention LSN-based routing? Did you discuss the load balancer configuration?

**Drill 2: Replica Lag Diagnostic (5 min)**
"Your monitoring shows that one replica has 30 seconds of lag while the other two have under 1 second. The slow replica has the same hardware as the others. What do you check?"

*Self-check:* Did you check the blocking queries on the replica? The WAL apply rate? The network latency between primary and replica? The disk I/O on the replica?

**Drill 3: Cross-Region Write Latency (5 min)**
"You need to support writes from users in US-East, EU-West, and AP-Southeast with p99 under 20 ms. Data must be strongly consistent per-user but can be eventually consistent across users. Design the replication."

*Self-check:* Did you identify that per-region leaders solve the latency problem? Did you mention conflict resolution? Did you explain how strongly consistent reads work within a region?

**Drill 4: Failover Without Data Loss (5 min)**
"Design a failover process that guarantees zero data loss when the primary fails. What is the cost?"

*Self-check:* Did you say synchronous replication (at least one sync replica)? Did you explain that the primary waits for the sync replica ack? Did you mention that this increases p99 write latency by 2-5 ms? Did you discuss the edge case where both primary and sync replica fail?

**Drill 5: Capacity Estimation (5 min)**
"Estimate how many replicas you need for a system with 10 million DAU, each user making 20 reads and 2 writes per day. Peak traffic is 6x average. Each read returns 50 KB. Each write changes 2 KB. Assume replicas can serve 5,000 reads/second and apply 10,000 writes/second."

*Self-check:* Did you calculate peak reads/second? Peak writes/second? Did you account for the primary handling some reads? Did you add N+2 redundancy? Did you check whether network bandwidth is sufficient?

Replication interview questions test whether you can reason about distributed data under realistic constraints. Practice the drills until the trade-offs and vocabulary come naturally. The next page challenges you to apply everything you have learned in hands-on practice problems.
