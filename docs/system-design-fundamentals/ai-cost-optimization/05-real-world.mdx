## Company Case Studies

### OpenAI's Own Cost Optimization

In 2023, OpenAI faced a challenge: they were spending millions of dollars per month on inference for their own products (ChatGPT, API, and internal tools). As a company that sells AI inference, they had access to their own models at cost, but the volume was so large that even internal cost optimization mattered.

**The approach:** OpenAI implemented a tiered model routing system for their own products. ChatGPT uses multiple model sizes behind the scenes. Simple queries (factual, structured) are handled by smaller, faster models. Complex queries (reasoning, creative) use the full GPT-4 model. The user never sees the routing -- the response is presented as a single unified experience.

**The insight:** Even within a single product, the distribution of query complexity is highly skewed. A small fraction of queries (maybe 10%) require the most expensive model. The rest can be handled by smaller, cheaper models. The key is building a classifier that accurately identifies the 10% that need the full model.

**The results:** OpenAI reportedly reduced their internal inference costs by 60-70% through model routing alone. This is the single most impactful optimization any organization can make.

**Key lesson for you:** Before building any complex optimization system, analyze your own traffic distribution. If you are like most applications, 80% of your queries can be handled by a model that costs 10% of your most expensive model. Route accordingly.

### GitHub Copilot's Batching Strategy

GitHub Copilot serves millions of developers with code completions. Each completion is a short inference call (50-200 tokens). The volume is enormous -- billions of tokens per month. Even small optimizations translate to significant cost savings.

**The approach:** Copilot uses aggressive batching. Code completions are non-urgent -- a 200ms delay is imperceptible to the user. Copilot batches requests from multiple users together, sending them to the inference endpoint as a single batch. This reduces the per-request overhead and takes advantage of batch pricing discounts.

**The insight:** Code completion is a natural fit for batching. Each completion is independent. The latency budget is generous (200-500ms). The model is the same for all users. The batch packing is straightforward: pack as many requests as possible into each batch, up to the model's maximum batch size.

**The results:** Batching improved throughput by 3-5x and reduced per-completion costs by 50-60%. The latency impact was negligible because the batch processing time is dominated by the longest completion, and completions are all short.

**Key lesson for you:** Look for natural batching opportunities in your workload. If you have a high volume of independent, short requests, batching is the highest-ROI optimization you can make.

### Notion AI's Tiered Model Routing

Notion AI integrates LLM capabilities into the Notion workspace. Users can ask questions, generate content, summarize documents, and more. Notion AI serves a diverse user base from individual freelancers to enterprise teams, each with different expectations and willingness to pay.

**The approach:** Notion AI implemented a tiered model routing system based on user plan and request complexity. Free trial users get responses from a smaller, cheaper model with a shorter context window. Pro users get a mid-tier model. Enterprise users get the best model with the longest context window.

Additionally, within each tier, the system uses a complexity classifier to route simple queries (formatting, templating) to the cheapest model in that tier, and complex queries (analysis, generation) to the best model in that tier.

**The insight:** Users in different plans have different expectations. Free users are evaluating the product. They need to see the value proposition, not the best possible output. Enterprise users are paying premium prices and require the best quality. The model routing aligns cost with revenue.

**The results:** Notion AI reportedly reduced overall inference costs by 40-50% compared to a single-model approach. Free tier users still had a positive experience because the small model handled their typical use cases (simple questions, summaries) well.

**Key lesson for you:** Align your cost optimization strategy with your business model. Free users should get the cheapest acceptable experience. Paying users should get value proportional to what they pay. This is not just a cost optimization strategy -- it is a pricing and product strategy.

### Jasper's Caching Strategies

Jasper is an AI content platform that generates marketing copy, blog posts, and social media content. Many of their users generate similar types of content: product descriptions, email subject lines, ad copy. The repetition of content types creates a natural opportunity for caching.

**The approach:** Jasper implemented a three-tier caching strategy:

1. **Exact-match cache**: If a user regenerates the same prompt, return the cached response. This handles the case where a user is iterating on a design and regenerates the same prompt multiple times.

2. **Template cache**: For common content templates (product descriptions, email subject lines), cache the full response. The template cache is pre-populated with high-quality examples and is updated weekly.

3. **Semantic cache**: For similar prompts (different products but the same template structure), use embedding similarity to find cached responses. The threshold is set conservatively (0.95 similarity) to avoid returning irrelevant content.

**The insight:** Content generation is a caching-friendly workload. Users often generate similar content with minor variations. The semantic cache captures the 80% of each response that is formulaic, leaving only the 20% of unique content to be generated by the model.

**The results:** Jasper achieved a 45% overall cache hit rate. For template-based content (product descriptions, meta descriptions), the hit rate was 70%. The cache reduced average response time from 3 seconds to under 50ms for cached responses, improving user experience alongside cost savings.

**Key lesson for you:** Analyze your workload for repetition patterns. If users generate similar types of content, caching can be extremely effective. The key is the semantic cache -- exact-match caching alone would miss the value of similar but not identical prompts.

## Open Source Tools

### LiteLLM

LiteLLM is an open-source Python library that provides a unified interface for 100+ LLM providers. It handles the API differences between providers and includes built-in cost tracking and cost-based routing.

**Cost tracking:** LiteLLM automatically tracks tokens and costs for every API call. It outputs structured logs that can be consumed by any monitoring system. The cost tracking supports all major providers out of the box.

**Cost-based routing:** LiteLLM supports "cost-based routing" where you define a maximum cost per request and the system selects the cheapest model that can handle the request. This is a simple but effective optimization.

**Limitations:** LiteLLM is a client-side library, not a service. It does not provide shared state across multiple services or instances. It is best suited for single-service applications.

### OpenRouter

OpenRouter is a service that provides access to multiple LLM providers through a single API. It handles provider selection, failover, and cost optimization.

**Cost optimization:** OpenRouter provides cost-based routing: you set a maximum cost per request, and OpenRouter selects the cheapest provider that meets your requirements. It also provides automatic failover: if a provider is down, requests are routed to the next cheapest available provider.

**Pricing:** OpenRouter adds a small markup (typically 5-10%) on top of provider pricing. The cost savings from provider selection and failover more than offset this markup for most users.

### Portkey

Portkey is an open-source AI gateway that provides observability, cost tracking, and cost optimization. It runs as a reverse proxy between your application and LLM providers.

**Cost tracking:** Portkey tracks every API call with detailed cost information. It provides dashboards for cost per feature, per team, and per user.

**Cost optimization:** Portkey supports model routing, fallback models, and rate limiting. It can be configured with custom routing rules based on request characteristics.

**Self-hosting:** Portkey can be self-hosted, making it suitable for organizations with data privacy requirements.

## Cloud Provider Cost Controls

### AWS Bedrock Pricing

AWS Bedrock provides access to foundation models from Anthropic, Meta, Stability AI, and others. AWS offers several cost control features:

- **Provisioned throughput**: Reserve a specific amount of throughput for your workloads. This provides predictable pricing and guaranteed availability, with a 30-40% discount compared to on-demand pricing.

- **Model evaluation**: AWS Bedrock provides built-in model evaluation tools that help you compare model quality across different providers. This helps you find the cheapest model that meets your quality requirements.

- **Cost allocation tags**: Tag your Bedrock API calls with team, project, and feature tags. These tags are visible in the AWS billing console, enabling chargeback.

### GCP Vertex AI Cost Controls

Google Cloud's Vertex AI offers several cost management features:

- **Model Garden**: A catalog of foundation models with transparent pricing. You can compare costs across models in a single dashboard.

- **Prediction cost optimization**: Vertex AI automatically routes requests to the most cost-effective hardware configuration. It uses preemptible and Spot VMs for batch inference, reducing costs by 60-80%.

- **Autoscaling**: Vertex AI endpoints automatically scale to zero when not in use. For low-traffic periods, you pay only for storage, not for idle compute.

- **Committed use discounts**: Reserve capacity for 1 or 3 years and receive up to 40% discount on inference costs.

### Azure OpenAI Committed Throughput

Microsoft Azure offers OpenAI models through the Azure OpenAI Service. Their cost control features include:

- **Provisioned throughput units (PTUs)**: Reserve capacity in units of throughput. PTUs provide guaranteed performance and are priced at a 20-30% discount compared to pay-as-you-go.

- **Data residency**: Azure OpenAI offers regional deployment options that minimize data transfer costs. If your users are in Europe, deploy in Europe and avoid cross-region data transfer fees.

- **Enterprise agreements**: Azure customers with enterprise agreements can negotiate volume discounts on OpenAI model usage as part of their overall Azure commitment.

## Lessons Learned

These case studies reveal several universal lessons for AI cost optimization:

**Lesson 1: Measure first, optimize second.** Every company that succeeded at cost optimization started by understanding their cost structure. They tracked costs per feature, per user, and per model before making any changes. Do not optimize blindly.

**Lesson 2: Model routing is the highest-impact single optimization.** Every company in the case studies used some form of model routing. It consistently reduced costs by 40-70%. If you can only implement one optimization, make it model routing.

**Lesson 3: Caching is a force multiplier.** Caching does not just save money on individual requests. By reducing the load on the inference pipeline, it reduces latency, improves throughput, and makes all other optimizations more effective. A 40% cache hit rate means 40% fewer requests through the router, compressor, and API.

**Lesson 4: Align optimization with business model.** Notion AI's tiered routing is the best example. Free users get cheap models. Paying users get better models. This is not just cost optimization -- it is product strategy. The optimization should reinforce the business model, not undermine it.

**Lesson 5: The optimization system itself has a cost.** Jasper's cache infrastructure, Notion AI's classifier, and GitHub Copilot's batch scheduler all have engineering and operational costs. The net benefit of optimization is savings minus the cost of the optimization system. Measure this explicitly.

**Lesson 6: Optimization is a continuous process.** Provider prices change. New models are released. Usage patterns shift. All the companies in this chapter continuously monitor and adjust their optimization strategies. The system you build today will be suboptimal in six months.

## Emerging Trends in Cost Optimization

### Agentic Workflows and Cost

Agentic AI systems (multi-step reasoning, tool use, self-correction loops) are inherently more expensive than single-turn inference. Each agentic step consumes tokens. A 5-step agentic workflow can cost 5-10x more than a single-turn response.

Cost optimization for agentic systems requires different strategies: sharing context across steps (avoiding redundant token processing), caching intermediate results, and using cheaper models for planning steps while reserving expensive models for execution steps.

### Streaming and Partial Results

Streaming responses (SSE) allow the user to see the beginning of a response while the model continues generating. This creates a new cost optimization opportunity: if the user cancels the request early (after seeing the first few tokens), the system saves the remaining output tokens. Early cancellation rates of 10-30% are common in practice.

### Multi-Modal Cost Optimization

Multi-modal models (vision, audio, video) have different cost structures. Image tokens are typically 100-1,000x more expensive than text tokens. Optimizing multi-modal costs requires techniques like: image resolution reduction, frame sampling for video, and caching common image embeddings.

### Real-Time vs. Batch Cost Optimization

Different latency requirements demand different optimization strategies. Real-time systems (chat, search, voice) require sub-second response times. Batch systems (document processing, data enrichment, report generation) can tolerate minutes of latency.

## Practical Implementation Guide

Building on the case studies, here is a practical guide for implementing cost optimization in your organization:

### Phase 1: Visibility (Week 1-2)

Implement cost tracking before any optimization. Use LiteLLM or Portkey to capture cost per request, per feature, and per team. Set up a Grafana dashboard with the key metrics: cost per day, cost per feature, cost per model, and cost trends. Share this dashboard with all teams.

### Phase 2: Quick Wins (Week 3-4)

Implement the low-risk, high-impact optimizations first:
1. Exact-match caching with Redis (2 days, 15-25% savings)
2. Rate limiting with cost-based caps (1 day, prevents spikes)
3. Model routing for the highest-volume feature (1 week, 40-60% savings on that feature)

### Phase 3: Deep Optimization (Month 2-3)

Implement the medium-complexity optimizations:
1. Semantic caching with vector database (2 weeks, 20-40% additional savings)
2. Prompt compression for long-context features (1 week, 20-40% savings on input tokens)
3. Batch processing for async workloads (1 week, 30-50% savings on batchable requests)

### Phase 4: Advanced (Month 3-6)

Implement the high-complexity optimizations:
1. Self-hosting for high-volume use cases (1-2 months, 60-90% savings on those requests)
2. Dynamic model routing with quality monitoring (1 month, continuous improvement)
3. Cost governance framework with chargeback (2 weeks, cultural change)

## Common Mistakes in Real-World Implementations

### Mistake 1: Over-Engineering the Classifier

Teams spend months building a perfect classifier for model routing. They use large models, complex feature engineering, and extensive training pipelines. The classifier is 99% accurate. But the engineering cost to build and maintain it exceeds the savings from better routing. A simple heuristic-based classifier (based on query length, keyword matching, and user tier) achieves 90% accuracy with 10% of the engineering cost.

**The fix:** Start with a simple classifier. Measure the savings. Only invest in a better classifier if the simple one is leaving money on the table.

### Mistake 2: Caching Without Invalidation Strategy

Teams implement caching and see immediate savings. They extend cache TTLs to maximize the hit rate. Six months later, users are getting stale responses. The cached answers are outdated. User trust is eroded. The cache becomes a liability.

**The fix:** Design the invalidation strategy before implementing the cache. For every cache entry type, define: how long is the response valid, what events trigger invalidation, and how users can report stale responses.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring the Cold Start Problem

A self-hosted model cluster scales to zero when not in use. When traffic spikes, the cluster takes 5 minutes to warm up. During those 5 minutes, requests time out or get routed to expensive API models, wiping out the savings.

**The fix:** Use a hybrid approach. Keep a minimum number of instances warm. Use API fallback during cold starts. Monitor the cold start frequency and cost impact.

### Mistake 4: Optimizing in Isolation

The infrastructure team builds a sophisticated cost optimization system without involving the product teams. The product teams do not trust the routing decisions. They bypass the system and use expensive models directly. The optimization system sees 10% of traffic and saves less than expected.

**The fix:** Involve product teams in the optimization design process. Make the routing transparent. Let teams configure their own routing rules. Show them the cost impact of their decisions.

### Mistake 5: Measuring Gross Savings Instead of Net

The team reports 60% cost savings from their optimization system. But the savings are gross -- they do not subtract the cost of the optimization infrastructure, the engineering time to build and maintain it, and the opportunity cost of not building product features instead. The net savings are closer to 30%.

**The fix:** Track net savings explicitly. Every month, calculate: (baseline cost without optimization) - (actual cost with optimization) - (infrastructure cost) - (amortized engineering cost). Report the net number. Optimize the net, not the gross.
