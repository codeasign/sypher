## The Core Tension

Every metrics system is built on a single inescapable trade-off: **granularity versus cost**. The more detail you capture, the more you pay in storage, CPU, network bandwidth, and query latency. The less detail you capture, the less visibility you have when things go wrong.

This trade-off manifests at every level of the system:

- **Cardinality**: more labels give you more dimensions for slicing data, but each unique label combination creates a new time series. A single high-cardinality label can explode the series count from 1,000 to 1 million.
- **Resolution**: shorter scrape intervals give you finer-grained data, but they multiply storage and network costs. Moving from 15-second to 5-second scrapes triples the cost.
- **Retention**: keeping more history lets you spot year-over-year trends, but it requires more disk space and slows down queries over long time ranges.
- **Bucket granularity**: more histogram buckets give you more precise percentile estimates, but each bucket adds storage and computation overhead.

The art of metrics design is finding the right balance for each service. A high-traffic payment service needs fine granularity. A low-traffic admin dashboard can tolerate coarse metrics.

The granularity versus cost trade-off is not a one-time decision. It changes as your system grows. A metric that was fine at 100,000 requests per day might be too expensive at 10 million requests per day. The label cardinality that was manageable for a single service might explode when the same metric is used across 100 services. You must continuously reevaluate the granularity of your metrics as your system scales.

The key to managing this trade-off is to treat metric granularity as a configuration parameter, not a hard-coded constant. If a service's traffic triples, you should be able to reduce its metric granularity (increase scrape interval, drop low-value labels) without redeploying code. This requires a centralized configuration system for metric instrumentation, which many organizations lack.

Another dimension of the trade-off is the cost of not having enough granularity. If your histogram buckets are too coarse, you cannot tell whether p99 latency is 200ms or 900ms. If your scrape interval is 60 seconds, you miss short-lived latency spikes that last 5 seconds. If you drop a label that you later need for debugging, you have to deploy a new version of the service to add it back. The cost of missing granularity is slower debugging and longer incident response times.

The granularity-versus-cost trade-off also affects alerting. Coarse metrics (60-second scrape interval, 10 buckets per histogram) produce noisier alerts because the data has higher variance. Fine metrics (5-second scrape interval, 50 buckets per histogram) produce cleaner alerts but cost 10x more. The choice of metric granularity directly affects the quality of your alerting.

The practical approach is to use different granularity levels for different use cases:
- **Critical path metrics**: 5-second scrape, 20+ histogram buckets, 3-5 labels. Used for the most important services (payment, authentication, core API).
- **Standard metrics**: 15-second scrape, 10 histogram buckets, 5-8 labels. Used for most services.
- **Background metrics**: 60-second scrape, no histogram, 3 labels. Used for batch jobs, admin tools, background workers.

### The Data Quality Trade-Off

An often-overlooked dimension of the core tension is data quality versus data volume. Higher quality data (exact measurements, full resolution, all labels) costs more to collect, store, and query. Lower quality data (sampled, downsampled, aggregated) is cheaper but loses information.

Consider the trade-off for percentile accuracy. A histogram with 20 buckets gives you a p99 estimate that is accurate to within the width of the bucket that contains the 99th percentile. If the 99th percentile falls in the 500ms-1000ms bucket, the p99 estimate could be anywhere from 500ms to 1000ms. That is a 500ms uncertainty. A histogram with 50 buckets gives you narrower buckets and a more accurate p99, but costs 2.5x more to store and query.

The quality-versus-volume trade-off applies to sampling as well. If you sample 1 in 100 requests, you reduce the data volume by 100x, but you lose the ability to detect rare events. An error that happens once in 10,000 requests will be detected only once in 100 samples. If you need to detect that error quickly, you cannot sample.

The practical approach is to use different quality levels for different data uses:
- **SLA measurement**: 100 percent of requests, no sampling, full resolution. Used for contractual compliance.
- **Alerting**: 100 percent of requests, full resolution. Used to detect and alert on anomalies.
- **Dashboard visualization**: 10 percent sampling for high-volume metrics (under 100ms latency), 100 percent for low-volume metrics (error rates). The dashboard shows approximate values, which are sufficient for visual analysis.
- **Capacity planning**: 5-minute downsampled data. The trend is more important than the exact value.

### The Operational Complexity Trade-Off

The final dimension of the core tension is feature richness versus operational complexity. A metrics system with more features (multi-tenancy, long-term storage, global querying, cross-region replication) costs more to operate.

Every feature you add to the metrics system adds operational burden:
- **Multi-tenancy**: requires namespace management, access control, and resource isolation. Each tenant is another axis of failure.
- **Long-term storage**: requires tiered storage management, data migration, and lifecycle policies. The S3 bucket that stores cold data needs its own monitoring.
- **Global querying**: requires cross-region networking, latency management, and fan-out query handling. A slow region slows down the global query.
- **Cross-region replication**: requires network bandwidth, conflict resolution, and failover testing. The replication pipeline is itself a system that needs monitoring.

The trade-off is between the value of each feature and the cost of operating it. A startup with 10 services does not need multi-tenancy. A company with 100 teams across 5 regions cannot operate without it.

## Decision Matrix

When you design a metrics strategy, you face a set of recurring decisions. Each decision has consequences for cost, reliability, and debuggability.

| Decision | Option A | Option B | Option C | Recommendation |
|----------|----------|----------|----------|----------------|
| Collection model | Pull (Prometheus) | Push (StatsD) | Hybrid agent | Pull for long-lived services, push for ephemeral, hybrid for production |
| Label cardinality | Low (under 100 per metric) | Medium (100-10,000) | High (over 10,000) | Keep under 100 per metric unless you have a specific debugging need |
| Scrape interval | 5 seconds | 15 seconds | 60 seconds | 15 seconds default, 5 seconds for latency-critical, 60 seconds for batch |
| Histogram buckets | Fixed (10 buckets) | Dynamic (exponential) | Custom | Fixed for simplicity, exponential for wide dynamic range |
| Retention (raw) | 7 days | 30 days | 90 days | 30 days for production, 7 days for staging |
| Downsampling | 5-minute rollups | 1-hour rollups | None | 5-minute for warm, 1-hour for cold, both configured |
| Storage tier | SSD only | HDD only | Hybrid | SSD for hot, HDD for warm, object storage for cold |
| Alerting sensitivity | Aggressive (low thresholds) | Moderate | Conservative (high thresholds) | Moderate for most, conservative for noisy signals |
| Query language | PromQL | SQL | Custom DSL | PromQL for time-series, SQL for relational context |
| Deployment | Single server | Sharded cluster | Multi-region | Single for dev, sharded for prod, multi-region for global |

## CAP Theorem for Metrics

Metrics systems face a version of the CAP theorem. The three properties are:

- **Consistency**: every query returns the most recent data. No stale reads.
- **Availability**: every query returns a response (possibly with stale data).
- **Partition tolerance**: the system continues to operate during network partitions.

A metrics system is fundamentally a write-always, read-sometimes system. Writes are cheap and frequent. Reads are expensive and less frequent. The consistency model is relaxed compared to a transactional database.

### Consistency in Pull-Based Systems

In a pull-based system, consistency is naturally weak. The collector scrapes the target at a fixed interval. Between scrapes, the data in the database is stale. The query returns data that is up to 15 seconds old (with a 15-second scrape interval). This is acceptable for monitoring because:

- Trend detection does not need sub-second accuracy.
- Alerting rules evaluate over windows (5 minutes, 10 minutes) and are not sensitive to a few seconds of staleness.
- Dashboards are used for debugging, not for real-time control.

### Availability in Pull-Based Systems

If a target is unreachable, the collector records a scrape failure. The database continues to serve queries with the data it has. The missing data appears as a gap in the chart. This is a graceful degradation: the system is available but not fully consistent.

### Partition Tolerance in Pull-Based Systems

During a network partition, the collector cannot reach the target. The target continues to operate and emit metrics, but those metrics are not collected. When the partition heals, the next scrape captures the current state. The gap in the data is permanent.

A push-based system handles partitions differently: the target keeps sending metrics to the collector. If the collector is on the other side of the partition, the metrics are lost. If the target buffers data locally and retransmits when the partition heals, the system can tolerate partitions.

### Practical Trade-Off

For a metrics system, the practical choice is: **sacrifice consistency, maximize availability, and engineer partition tolerance**.

This choice is justified by the use case. Monitoring is an observability tool, not a transactional system. A dashboard showing data that is 15 seconds stale is acceptable. A dashboard that fails to load is not acceptable. An alert that is delayed by a scrape interval is acceptable. An alert that never fires because the infrastructure is partitioned is not acceptable.

The CAP choice has specific implications for each component:

**Query frontend**: must always return a response. If one shard is unresponsive, return partial results from the other shards. The dashboard shows "incomplete data" or "some data missing" rather than failing entirely. The partial results include the latest data from available shards and a note about which shards are missing.

**Alerting engine**: must evaluate rules even during partitions. If the alerting engine cannot reach the storage layer, it should use cached data from the last successful evaluation. The cached data might be up to 5 minutes old, which means alert evaluation is delayed but not lost. When the partition heals, the alerting engine catches up by evaluating all missed evaluations.

**Ingestion layer**: must accept data even if the storage layer is unavailable. The WAL provides this buffer: incoming samples are written to the WAL and acknowledged immediately. If the storage layer is down, the WAL grows until storage is restored. Once storage is back, the WAL is replayed and all buffered data is ingested. This can buffer for hours or even days, depending on the WAL size.

- Sacrifice consistency: accept that data is up to 15 seconds stale.
- Maximize availability: ensure the query path works even when some targets are unreachable.
- Engineer partition tolerance: use push-based collection for ephemeral targets, pull-based for persistent targets, and hybrid agents for the best of both.

## Failure Modes

A metrics system has five major failure modes. Each one can take down the monitoring infrastructure, leaving you blind in production.

### Failure Mode 1: Cardinality Explosion

A bug in instrumentation code or a misconfigured label value causes a sudden spike in the number of active time series. For example, a label set to the current timestamp produces a new time series every second. Within minutes, the series count goes from 10,000 to 10 million.

**Symptoms**: the metrics server runs out of memory, scrapes start timing out, query latency climbs to minutes, and the server is OOM-killed by the operating system.

**Root cause**: a label with unbounded values. Common culprits are user_id, email, timestamp, and request_id.

**Mitigation**: enforce cardinality limits at the collection layer. Reject metrics that exceed the limit and increment a counter. The counter is itself a metric that alerts the operator. Hard limit: 100,000 series per metric. Soft limit: alert at 80,000.

**Recovery**: stop the offending metric from being emitted. Restart the metrics server to clear the memory. The lost data is the price of the cardinality explosion.

### Failure Mode 2: Scrape Overload

The scrape target cannot respond to scrape requests in time. The /metrics endpoint is slow because the application is under load or the metric registry has grown too large.

**Symptoms**: scrape failures, gaps in metric data, and increased CPU usage on the target.

**Root cause**: the target is emitting too many metrics, or the /metrics endpoint is not optimized for fast serialization.

**Mitigation**: reduce the number of metrics per target (aim for under 10,000), use a faster serialization format, or increase the scrape interval.

**Recovery**: the gap in data is permanent. The next successful scrape resumes data collection.

### Failure Mode 3: Storage Saturation

The time-series database runs out of disk space. This happens when the ingestion rate exceeds the compaction rate, or when retention is set too high for the available disk.

**Symptoms**: write failures, query failures, and the database refusing new data.

**Root cause**: disk space fills up faster than expected. Common causes: cardinality explosion (see above), retention too long, or insufficient disk allocation.

**Mitigation**: monitor disk usage and alert at 80 percent. Use tiered storage to move cold data to cheaper storage. Configure retention policies at the database level.

**Recovery**: delete old data or add more disk. The database resumes accepting writes when space is available.

### Failure Mode 4: Alert Fatigue

Too many alerts fire, most of them false positives. The on-call engineer silences alerts because they are not actionable. When a real incident happens, it is missed because the engineer has learned to ignore alerts.

**Symptoms**: alerts are acknowledged without investigation, alert channels are muted, and the team stops trusting the monitoring system.

**Root cause**: alert thresholds are too sensitive, alerts are not properly tuned, or the system has too many low-signal alerts.

**Mitigation**: every alert must have a runbook. If there is no runbook, the alert is not actionable and should be deleted. Tune thresholds to reduce false positives. Use alert fatigue as a metric: the number of alerts per shift should be under 5.

**Recovery**: audit all alerts, delete non-actionable ones, and tune thresholds for the rest.

### Failure Mode 5: Time Skew

Metrics are meaningless if the timestamps are wrong. Time skew between targets and the collector causes data to be assigned to the wrong time bucket. Queries return incorrect results.

**Symptoms**: charts show data in the wrong time range, rates are miscalculated, and alerting rules fire at the wrong time.

**Root cause**: clock drift on the target or collector machine. Without NTP synchronization, clocks can drift by seconds per hour.

**Mitigation**: run NTP on every machine. Monitor clock skew as a metric. Alert if the skew exceeds 1 second.

**Recovery**: fix the clock synchronization. The mis-timestamped data is permanent and cannot be corrected.

## Anti-Patterns

These anti-patterns appear in almost every organization that adopts metrics. Recognizing them saves months of debugging.

### Anti-Pattern 1: Dashboard Blindness

You create a dashboard with 50 panels, each showing a different metric. The dashboard is a beautiful wall of charts. Nobody looks at it because the information density is too high. When something breaks, the team cannot find the relevant panel.

**Fix**: create layered dashboards. The top-level dashboard has 5 to 10 panels showing high-level health. Each panel links to a more detailed dashboard. The drill-down path should be obvious: start at the top, see the problem, click through to the relevant component.

### Anti-Pattern 2: Alerting on Every Metric

You set an alert on every metric because you want to know about every problem. The result is 200 alerts per shift. The on-call engineer silences everything. Real incidents are missed.

**Fix**: only alert on symptoms that require human action. If the alert does not have a runbook, it should not exist. If the alert fires and the engineer can safely ignore it, it should not exist.

### Anti-Pattern 3: Perfect Cardinality

You add every possible label to every metric because you want to be able to slice data in every dimension. The result is a cardinality explosion that crashes the metrics server.

**Fix**: start with 3 to 5 labels per metric. Add more labels only when you have a specific debugging need that cannot be met with the current labels. Remove labels that are not used in any dashboard or alerting rule.

### Anti-Pattern 4: The Gauge-Only Strategy

You use only gauges because they are simple. Every metric is a gauge: current request count, current latency, current error count. You lose the ability to compute rates, detect trends, and handle resets.

**Fix**: use counters for cumulative values (requests, errors, bytes) and gauges for instantaneous values (memory, CPU, connections). Use histograms for distributions (latency, request size).

### Anti-Pattern 5: The Single Pane of Glass

You try to put every metric from every system into a single dashboard. The dashboard has 100 panels and takes 30 seconds to load. Engineers stop using it because it is too slow.

**Fix**: use multiple dashboards organized by service, layer, and audience. The SRE team has a different dashboard than the product team. The on-call dashboard is different from the weekly review dashboard.

### Anti-Pattern 6: The Dashboard as a Data Dump

You export every metric your service emits into a dashboard panel because you might need it someday. The dashboard panel is a chart without labeling, without a meaningful title, and without a reference line for the expected value. When something breaks, nobody can figure out what the chart is showing.

**Fix**: every panel must have a title that explains what it measures, a unit label, and a reference line for the expected value or alert threshold. If the panel is not immediately interpretable by someone who does not know the service, it is a data dump, not a dashboard.

### Anti-Pattern 7: Ignoring Errors in Metrics

Your application has a counter for errors, but you never check it because the error rate is normally zero. When a bug introduces a 5 percent error rate, nobody notices for 3 days because the dashboard was showing the request rate (which was fine) but not the error rate.

**Fix**: always have an error rate panel next to every traffic panel. The error rate should be computed as a ratio, not an absolute count. A sudden change in traffic volume masks an absolute error count but not an error ratio. Every monitoring system must track: errors / total requests.

### Anti-Pattern 8: Metrics Hoarding

You keep every metric at full resolution forever because you might need it someday. The storage bill grows exponentially. At some point, the cost exceeds the value, but nobody makes the decision to delete old data because deleting data feels like destroying information.

**Fix**: set retention policies and enforce them. Data older than the retention period is automatically deleted. If someone needs data from outside the retention window, they must request it through a formal process that justifies the cost. Most requests will be satisfied by downsampled data or logs.

### Anti-Pattern 9: The Unlabeled Metric

You emit a metric with no labels because adding labels seems like extra work. The metric is just http_requests_total with a single value. When latency spikes, you cannot tell which endpoint is slow, which method is failing, or which status code is being returned. The metric is useless for debugging.

**Fix**: every metric must have at least the labels that identify the component responsible for the data. For an HTTP API, the minimum labels are endpoint, method, and status_code. Without these labels, a single metric is indistinguishable from noise.

### Anti-Pattern 10: Confusing Correlation with Causation

You see two metrics that look correlated: CPU usage and error rate both go up at the same time. You assume high CPU causes errors. You add more CPU. The errors continue. The real cause is a memory leak that triggers garbage collection, which uses CPU, and the garbage collection pauses cause request timeouts.

**Fix**: correlation is not causation. When two metrics correlate, investigate the causal path. Use traces to follow a single request through the system and identify the actual cause. Metrics tell you what is happening. They do not tell you why. Always use traces and logs to confirm metrics-based hypotheses.

## When NOT to Use Metrics

Metrics are powerful, but they are not the right tool for every problem. Recognizing when to use something else is a sign of design maturity.

### Do Not Use Metrics for Debugging

Metrics tell you that something is wrong. They do not tell you why. If you need to understand the exact sequence of events that led to a failure, use traces. If you need to see the exact error message, use logs.

### Do Not Use Metrics for Billing

Billing requires exact counts of resources consumed. Metrics are approximate by design (sampling, compression, downsampling). If you need to bill a customer, use a dedicated billing system with exact counters backed by a transactional database.

### Do Not Use Metrics for Real-Time Control

Metrics are not real-time. The scrape interval introduces 15 seconds of latency. If you need to control a system in real time (throttle requests, shut down a faulty node), use a control loop with direct feedback, not a metrics query.

### Do Not Use Metrics for High-Cardinality Data

If your data has millions of unique values per dimension (user IDs, session IDs, device IDs), do not put it in metrics. Use logs for individual events and a dedicated analytics database for aggregation.

### Do Not Use Metrics for SLA Compliance

SLAs require exact measurements over specific time windows. Metrics give you approximate values with error margins. For SLA compliance, use a dedicated measurement system that records every request and its outcome.

### Do Not Use Metrics for User-Level Analytics

If you need to answer questions like "how many users signed up yesterday" or "what is the retention rate for users who joined in January," use a data warehouse, not a metrics system. Metrics are designed for real-time operational visibility, not for historical business analytics. A data warehouse can handle the cardinality of user-level data, can join across multiple data sources, and can support complex SQL queries that metrics systems cannot.

### Do Not Use Metrics for A/B Testing Analysis

A/B testing requires statistical significance calculations, multiple hypothesis testing corrections, and segment-level analysis. Metrics systems are not designed for this. Use a dedicated experimentation platform (or a statistical analysis library) to analyze A/B test results. The metrics system can provide the raw data, but the analysis should be done in a separate system.

### Do Not Use Metrics as a Data Lake

Metrics systems are not general-purpose storage. They are optimized for a specific workload: many small writes, fewer large reads, and time-based queries. If you find yourself storing non-time-series data in your metrics system (like configuration values, feature flags, or application logs), you are using the wrong tool. Use a dedicated storage system for each data type.

### Do Not Use Metrics for Every Service

Not every service needs the same level of instrumentation. A batch job that runs once a day and takes 5 minutes does not need a full metrics pipeline. A cron job that sends a weekly report email does not need a latency histogram. Use the minimum viable instrumentation for each service, not a one-size-fits-all approach.

### The Cost of NOT Using Metrics

The flip side of knowing when not to use metrics is knowing when you absolutely must. The cost of not having metrics is higher than most engineers estimate. Without metrics:

- **Incident detection**: you discover problems through user complaints, not through monitoring. The average time to detect an incident is 4x longer without metrics.
- **Incident response**: you spend 60 percent of incident time on diagnosis (finding the root cause) instead of on remediation (fixing the problem). With metrics, the diagnosis time drops to 20 percent.
- **Capacity planning**: you guess whether you need more servers. You either over-provision (wasting money) or under-provision (causing outages).
- **Performance optimization**: you cannot tell whether a change improved or degraded performance. Every optimization is a blind guess.
- **Team confidence**: every deployment is stressful because you cannot tell if the new version is working correctly. The team develops a culture of fear around releases.

The cost of metrics infrastructure is measurable and bounded (2 to 5 percent of infrastructure spend). The cost of not having metrics is unbounded: lost revenue, damaged reputation, and burned-out engineers.

## The Metrics Maturity Model

Organizations progress through stages of metrics maturity. Knowing where your organization falls on this spectrum helps you prioritize improvements.

### Stage 1: Ad Hoc (No Metrics)
Services are deployed without any instrumentation. Incidents are detected by users reporting problems. Debugging requires SSH access to individual servers. Capacity planning is guesswork. Deployment confidence is low.

### Stage 2: Basic (Infrastructure Metrics Only)
Infrastructure-level metrics are collected: CPU, memory, disk, and network. Application-level metrics are not instrumented. The team can detect when a server is overloaded but cannot tell which service is causing the load. Alerts are set on infrastructure metrics only.

### Stage 3: Service (Application Metrics)
Each service emits the four golden signals: latency, traffic, errors, and saturation. Dashboards exist for each service. Alerts are set on service-level metrics. The team can detect when a specific service is degrading and drill into its dashboards.

### Stage 4: Correlated (Cross-Service Visibility)
Metrics are correlated across services. A dashboard shows the health of the entire system, not just individual services. Cross-service alerts detect cascading failures. The metrics system is integrated with the incident response system.

### Stage 5: Predictive (Proactive Detection)
The metrics system uses machine learning to detect anomalies before they become incidents. Capacity planning is automated based on metric trends. The team spends more time on improvements than on firefighting. Metrics are used to drive business decisions, not just operational decisions.

Most organizations are at Stage 2 or Stage 3. The goal should be Stage 4: correlated visibility across services. Stage 5 requires significant investment in machine learning infrastructure.

## The Decision Tree for Metrics Design

When you face a metrics design decision, use this decision tree to guide your choice. The tree encodes the trade-offs discussed in this chapter.

**Question 1: Is the service ephemeral (under 5 minutes lifetime)?**
- Yes: use push-based collection with client-side buffering. Metrics are flushed before the process terminates.
- No: go to Question 2.

**Question 2: Is the target within the same datacenter as the collector?**
- Yes: use pull-based collection. The collector controls the scrape schedule.
- No: go to Question 3.

**Question 3: Is this a production service with an SLO?**
- Yes: use hybrid agent model. Local agent scrapes, forwards to cloud backend.
- No: use push-based collection for simplicity.

**Question 4: Does the metric have more than 10 labels?**
- Yes: review the label design. Most metrics should have 3-7 labels. Remove unused labels.
- No: go to Question 5.

**Question 5: Could any label have more than 10,000 unique values?**
- Yes: move the high-cardinality label out of metrics. Use logs or traces for that dimension.
- No: go to Question 6.

**Question 6: Is query latency more important than write throughput?**
- Yes: use recording rules to precompute expensive queries. Increase memory for index cache.
- No: use downsampling to reduce storage costs. Accept slower queries for long time ranges.

**Question 7: Is this service critical to business revenue?**
- Yes: use 5-second scrape interval, 20+ histogram buckets, 30-day raw retention.
- No: use 15-second scrape interval, 10 histogram buckets, 7-day raw retention.

This decision tree covers 80 percent of the decisions you will make in metrics design. The remaining 20 percent are context-specific and require deeper analysis of the trade-offs.

## The Organizational Impact of Metrics Design

The design of your metrics system has organizational implications beyond the technical architecture. These are often overlooked but have a larger impact on the success of the system.

**Team autonomy versus global standards**: A metrics system that gives every team complete freedom to choose their own metric names, labels, and instrumentation patterns results in chaos. No dashboard can span multiple teams. No alert can correlate across services. The organization cannot answer the question "is the system healthy?" in a unified way.

The solution is global standards with team-level autonomy within those standards. Every team must use the same metric naming convention, the same label taxonomy, and the same instrumentation library. Within those constraints, teams are free to add service-specific metrics and dashboards.

**The cost of bad metrics**: A metric that is never queried costs money (storage, CPU, network) and provides zero value. A metric that is queried but has incorrect values is worse: it costs money and actively misleads. A metric with high cardinality costs money and risks crashing the entire metrics infrastructure.

The organizational response is to treat metrics as a shared resource with a cost. Teams should be aware of the cost of their metrics. The metrics infrastructure team should provide dashboards showing each team's metric usage: active series count, storage consumption, and query frequency. Teams that consume more resources should justify the cost.

**Metrics as a product**: The best metrics systems are treated as products, not utilities. The metrics infrastructure team has a roadmap, user research, and feature requests. They measure user satisfaction (dashboard load time, alert accuracy) and continuously improve the product. This product mindset ensures the metrics system meets the needs of its users: the engineers who rely on it to keep the service running.
