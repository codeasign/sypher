## The Problem Story

At 2:47 PM on a Tuesday, your pager wakes up. The on-call dashboard shows a sea of red. CPU is at 100 percent. Memory is at 95 percent. Response times are climbing past 10 seconds. You have a hundred alerts screaming at you, but none of them tell you what actually broke or why.

You SSH into a box, run top, and see a Java process eating all the CPU. You kill it. The alert goes quiet. Five minutes later it fires again, because the orchestrator restarted the process as part of its health-check loop. You kill it again. The whole team burns three hours before someone notices that a new deployment at 2:30 PM shipped a regex that hits catastrophic backtracking on certain inputs.

Without metrics, you are flying blind. You can see the damage but not the cause. You can treat symptoms but not the disease. Metrics are the instrument panel of your system: they tell you altitude, airspeed, fuel level, engine temperature, and whether that blinking light means a door is open or the wing is on fire.

This scenario is not hypothetical. Every major outage post-mortem at every large tech company includes the phrase we did not have metrics for that component or the existing metrics did not capture that signal. When you design a distributed system, metrics are not an afterthought to add before launch. They are a first-class architectural concern, as fundamental as your data model or your API contract.

Consider the difference between a plane with a functioning instrument panel and one without. The instrument panel does not fix the engine, but it tells you the engine is overheating before it seizes. It tells you which engine, at what rate the temperature is rising, and whether the backup systems are compensating. Metrics give you the same kind of situational awareness for your software systems. They turn a blind panic into a structured investigation.

The cost of not having metrics is not just the incident response time. It is the accumulated uncertainty. Every deployment feels risky because you cannot tell if the new version is actually behaving differently. Every capacity planning decision is a guess. Every performance question requires a cycle of hypothesizing, instrumenting, deploying, and observing that takes days instead of minutes. Metrics collapse this cycle from days to seconds.

Another way to think about the problem is the signal-to-noise ratio in your system. Without metrics, every machine is equally important and equally mysterious. With metrics, you can rank machines by error rate, by latency, by traffic volume. You can focus on the outliers that signal real problems. Metrics turn a uniformly opaque system into a transparent one with clear hotspots and cold zones.

The stakes scale with the size of the system. A startup with 10 servers can get away with SSHing into each box and running top. A company with 1,000 servers cannot. A company with 10,000 servers cannot even know which servers are alive without a metrics system. Metrics are not optional at scale — they are the difference between operating a system and being operated by it.

## The Insight

Every system produces data as a side effect of running. A request arrives, a database query executes, a cache fills, a worker finishes. These individual events are logs. When you aggregate them over time — counting, timing, summarizing — you get metrics. A metric is a numeric representation of your system state at a point in time or aggregated over a window.

The fundamental insight is that you cannot manage what you do not measure. Latency degrades gradually before it fails. Memory leaks consume capacity long before the OOM killer fires. Throughput drops when a dependency slows down. Metrics let you detect these trends before they become incidents, and they let you pinpoint the root cause when an incident does happen.

Metrics are one leg of the observability triad alongside logs and traces. Each leg serves a different purpose:

- **Logs** answer: what happened at this specific moment? They are discrete, unstructured events.
- **Traces** answer: what path did this specific request take through the system? They follow a single request across services.
- **Metrics** answer: what is the overall health and behavior of the system right now? They are numeric, aggregated, and time-series oriented.

An observability strategy that leans on only one of these fails in production. Logs alone do not tell you the p99 latency is degrading. Traces alone do not tell you that error rates are climbing across all request types. Metrics alone do not tell you the exact stack trace of a failing call. You need all three, but metrics are the first line of defense because they are the cheapest to collect, the fastest to query, and the most natural to alert on.

## Core Vocabulary

To think clearly about metrics, you need precise language for the concepts involved. These terms appear in every design conversation, every job interview, and every production post-mortem.

**1. Gauge**
A gauge is a metric that represents a single numeric value that can go up or down at any time. The value at any point in time is the actual measurement, not an average over a window. Examples: current CPU utilization, memory usage, number of active connections, queue depth. Gauges are snapshot measurements. If you sample a gauge once per minute, you see the value at each sample point, but you miss everything that happened between samples.

**2. Counter**
A counter is a metric that only increases (or resets to zero on restart). Counters accumulate over time. Examples: total requests served, total bytes transferred, total errors encountered. Counters are useful for measuring rates: if you have a counter of requests, you can compute requests per second by taking the difference between two counter readings divided by the time interval. This is how most rate-based monitoring works under the hood.

**3. Histogram**
A histogram samples observations and counts them in configurable buckets. It tracks how many observations fall into each value range. Histograms let you compute percentiles without storing every individual value. For example, a latency histogram might have buckets at 1ms, 5ms, 10ms, 25ms, 50ms, 100ms, 250ms, 500ms, and 1000ms. If a request takes 30ms, it increments the 50ms bucket (the smallest bucket larger than the value). From these bucket counts, you can estimate p50, p99, and other percentiles.

**4. Summary**
A summary is similar to a histogram but computes configurable quantiles on the client side and exposes them directly. While a histogram pushes bucket computation to the server, a summary computes quantiles locally and exposes the p50, p95, p99 values as separate time series. Summaries use less storage on the server side because raw observations are never sent, but they cannot be aggregated across multiple instances because quantiles are not mathematically composable.

**5. Cardinality**
Cardinality is the number of unique combinations of label values for a metric. If you have a metric http_requests_total with one label endpoint that takes 100 different values and another label status_code that takes 10 values, the cardinality is 1000. High cardinality is the most common cause of metrics infrastructure failure. When a system promises to handle 1 million active time series and you emit 900 unique combinations for a single label, you are close to half the budget with just one metric.

**6. Label (Tag)**
A label or tag is a key-value pair that adds dimensions to a metric. For example, the metric http_requests_total might have labels method=GET, endpoint=/api/users, status=200. Labels let you slice and aggregate metrics along any dimension. They are the mechanism that turns a flat metric into a multi-dimensional data space. Choose labels carefully: too few and you cannot debug, too many and you cause a cardinality explosion.

**7. Bucket**
A bucket is a range boundary used by histograms. Each bucket represents a value range up to a specified boundary. The histogram counts how many observations fall into each bucket. Bucket selection is an art: you want more buckets in the range where your typical values fall, and fewer in the tail. For latency, a typical bucket layout is powers of two: 1ms, 2ms, 4ms, 8ms, 16ms, 32ms, 64ms, 128ms, 256ms, 512ms, 1024ms, and an infinity bucket.

**8. Aggregation**
Aggregation is the process of combining multiple metric values into a single value. Common aggregations include sum, average, min, max, and count. Aggregation can happen over time (rolling up 1-second resolution to 1-minute resolution) or across dimensions (summing http_requests_total across all endpoints to get total requests). Aggregation always loses information: when you sum across endpoints, you lose per-endpoint visibility.

**9. Rate**
A rate is a per-unit-time measurement derived from a counter. If your counter of total requests goes from 1000 to 1200 over 60 seconds, the rate is 3.33 requests per second. Rates smooth out spikes and give you a normalized view of activity. Most alerting rules should be based on rates, not raw counters, because a flat counter at a high value looks alarming but the system is actually idle.

**10. Time Series**
A time series is a sequence of data points indexed by time. Each unique combination of metric name and label values produces one time series. The metric ``http_requests_total{method=GET, endpoint=/api/users, status=200}`` is one time series. ``http_requests_total{method=POST, endpoint=/api/users, status=200}`` is a different time series. The total number of active time series is the primary capacity metric for any metrics infrastructure.

**11. Sample**
A sample is a single data point in a time series. It consists of a timestamp, a value, and a reference to its time series. A sample is the atomic unit of metric data. When you hear ingestion rate of 1 million samples per second, this means 1 million (timestamp, value, series_id) triples are being written to the database every second.

**12. Retention**
Retention is the duration for which metric data is kept before being deleted or downsampled. Raw data (full resolution) is kept for days or weeks. Downsampled data (reduced resolution) is kept for months or years. Retention policies balance storage cost against debugging needs. A 30-day raw retention policy means you can see per-second detail for the last month, but data older than 30 days is only available at 5-minute resolution.

**13. Resolution**
Resolution is the time interval between consecutive data points in a time series. A 15-second resolution means a new data point appears every 15 seconds. Higher resolution (smaller interval) captures more detail but costs more in storage. The effective resolution of a metric depends on both the scrape interval and the scrape timing jitter. If the scraper is delayed by 2 seconds, the effective resolution is 15 to 17 seconds.

**14. Downsampling**
Downsampling is the process of reducing the resolution of metric data over time. Raw 15-second data is aggregated into 5-minute averages, which are then aggregated into 1-hour averages. Downsampling computes min, max, avg, and count for each window. The original data is discarded after the downsampled data is computed. This is an irreversible operation: once raw data is downsampled, the original detail is gone forever.

**15. Recording Rule**
A recording rule is a precomputed metric query that is evaluated on a schedule and stored as a new metric. For example, a recording rule might compute the error ratio (errors divided by total requests) every 30 seconds and store it as error_ratio. Recording rules trade storage for query speed: the precomputed value takes space but makes dashboards and alerts faster because they do not need to recompute the ratio on every query.

**16. Meta-Metric**
A meta-metric is a metric about the metrics system itself. Examples: scrape duration, ingestion rate, query latency, number of active time series, number of scrape failures. Meta-metrics are essential for monitoring the monitoring system. Without them, you cannot tell if the metrics system is healthy or if it has fallen over.

This pipeline has several critical properties:
- **Collection is decentralized**: every application instance emits metrics independently.
- **Aggregation is centralized**: a single aggregator reduces cardinality and computes derived metrics.
- **Storage is distributed**: the database shards by time range and metric name.
- **Query is multi-path**: dashboards, alerting, and APIs all read from the same store.

## Step by Step

Understanding metrics means tracing one measurement from the moment your code emits it to the moment your dashboard renders a chart. Here is the complete lifecycle in 8 steps, with 4 diagrams to anchor each phase.

### Step 1: Instrumentation

Your application code emits a metric when something notable happens. A request arrives, a database query completes, a cache miss occurs. The instrumentation is a single function call in your code.

For a web server, you might increment a counter on every request:

```
http_requests_total{method=GET, endpoint=/api/users, status=200}
```

The counter library maintains an integer in memory. Every request increments it by 1. The CPU cost is a few nanoseconds. This is the cheapest observability signal you can produce.

### Step 2: Local Aggregation

Before anything leaves the application process, the metrics library performs local aggregation. This is the first and most important optimization in the pipeline. By aggregating locally, each application instance reduces the volume of data that must be transmitted, stored, and queried.

The local aggregation has several dimensions:
- **Time aggregation**: observations are collected over the scrape interval (15 seconds by default) and sent as a single batch. Without this, every HTTP request would produce a separate network packet to the metrics server.
- **Cardinality aggregation**: label values that are unique to a single request (like a trace ID) are dropped at this stage. The metric library knows which labels are safe to keep and which would cause cardinality explosions.
- **Pre-computation**: counters are summed, gauges are sampled at their current value, and histograms collect observations into bucket counts. The result is a compact representation that is much smaller than the raw event stream. For counters, this is trivial: store the latest integer. For histograms, the library maintains an in-memory data structure of bucket counts. For summaries, it maintains a sliding window of quantiles.

Local aggregation is critical for performance. Without it, every request would trigger a network call to the metrics server, adding latency to your hot path and overwhelming the infrastructure. With local aggregation, the metrics library batches observations and exposes them for scraping on a separate endpoint, typically /metrics.

### Step 3: Scrape

A metrics collector (Prometheus, Telegraf, or a custom agent) connects to your application's /metrics endpoint and reads the current state. This is called scraping.

The scrape interval is a critical tuning parameter. Scrape too fast (every second) and you waste CPU on both sides. Scrape too slow (every 5 minutes) and you miss short-lived spikes. A 15-second scrape interval is a safe default for most services.

The scraper reads the raw metric text, parses it into a structured format, and forwards it to the aggregator. Each scrape produces a snapshot of all metrics at that instant.

### Step 4: Ingestion

The aggregator receives scrape data from thousands of application instances. It must handle:
- **Deduplication**: if two scrapers hit the same target, discard duplicates.
- **Validation**: reject metrics with invalid names, labels that exceed cardinality limits, or timestamps too far in the past or future.
- **Normalization**: convert units, apply prefix mappings, standardize label names.

Ingestion is the bottleneck in most metrics pipelines. A large deployment might emit 10 million samples per second across all targets. The aggregator must process, validate, and route every sample.

### Step 5: Storage

The time-series database stores every metric sample as a triple: (timestamp, value, label set). Storage engines use specialized compression to handle the volume.

Two compression techniques dominate:
- **Delta-of-delta encoding for timestamps**: consecutive timestamps in a scrape are close together. Store the difference of the difference from the previous timestamp. This compresses from 64 bits to under 2 bits per timestamp on average.
- **XOR compression for values**: consecutive float values change slowly. XOR with the previous value produces many leading and trailing zeros, which compress to single bits.

These techniques compress a 16-byte sample to roughly 2 bytes on disk: a 8x reduction.

### Step 6: Aggregation and Downsampling

Raw data at full resolution (15-second intervals) is expensive to keep forever. Most systems apply downsampling: they compute min, max, avg, and count over rolling windows and discard the raw points.

A typical retention policy:
- **Raw data**: 7 to 30 days at full resolution.
- **5-minute rollups**: 3 to 6 months.
- **1-hour rollups**: 1 to 3 years.

Downsampling is irreversible. You trade resolution for longevity. Choose retention windows based on your compliance requirements and debugging needs. Most outages are diagnosed within the raw retention window. Year-over-year trend analysis works fine with hourly rollups.

### Step 7: Alerting

Alerting rules evaluate metric queries on a schedule. Every minute, the alerting engine runs each rule against the latest data. If the query returns a threshold violation, the alert transitions through states: Pending (transient) to Firing (confirmed) to Resolved.

Alerting is where metrics directly protect your service. A well-designed alert tells you what is wrong, which component is affected, and how to investigate. A poorly designed alert fires every night at 3 AM because of a scheduled batch job and gets silenced forever.

### Step 8: Visualization

Dashboards render metric queries as charts. A dashboard is not a random collection of graphs. It is a curated view organized by layers of abstraction:
- **Top level**: service health (overall error rate, latency, throughput).
- **Middle level**: component health (database connections, cache hit ratio, queue depth).
- **Bottom level**: resource health (CPU, memory, disk, network).

Each layer tells you where to look next. You start at the top, see elevated error rates, drill into the relevant component, and find the resource bottleneck.

## Variants

Metrics systems fall into several architectural categories. Each makes different trade-offs in cardinality tolerance, query flexibility, and operational complexity.

Each of these variants suits a different operational context. Choosing the wrong model for your environment is a common source of metrics infrastructure pain. Understanding the nuances of each is essential for system design.

### Variant 1: Pull-based (Prometheus Model)

Prometheus popularized the pull model. Each application exposes a /metrics endpoint, and Prometheus servers scrape those endpoints on a schedule.

Advantages: the monitoring system controls collection timing, can detect dead targets immediately, and applications do not need to know about the monitoring infrastructure. Disadvantages: Prometheus must be able to reach every target, which is difficult in private networks or with dynamic endpoints.

```
                                    ┌─────────┐
                                    │  [Web]  │
                                    │  :/met  │
                                    └────┬────┘
                                         │
         ┌───────────────────────────────┤
         │                               │
         ▼                               ▼
    ┌─────────┐                    ┌─────────┐
    │[Worker] │                    │[Worker] │
    │ :/met   │                    │ :/met   │
    └────┬────┘                    └────┬────┘
         │                               │
         └───────────────┬───────────────┘
                         │
                         ▼
              ┌──────────────────┐
              │ Prometheus       │
              │ Server           │
              │ scrape every 15s │
              └──────────────────┘
```

The pull model works best when you control the network topology and can ensure reachability. Kubernetes makes this natural: every pod exposes /metrics, and Prometheus discovers them through service discovery.

### Variant 2: Push-based (StatsD / Graphite Model)

The push model sends metrics from applications to a central aggregator. Applications emit UDP packets to a known collector address.

Advantages: applications do not need to be reachable, collection continues even if the application cannot serve HTTP, and the model naturally handles short-lived batch jobs. Disadvantages: the collector is a single point of failure, you lose visibility into dead applications (no scrape failure signal), and UDP can lose packets under load.

```
    ┌─────────┐
    │  [App]  │
    └────┬────┘
         │ UDP packet: counter 1
         │
         ▼
    ┌────────────────────┐
    │  <StatsD Daemon>   │
    │  Aggregate in      │
    │  memory, flush     │
    │  every 10 seconds  │
    └─────────┬──────────┘
              │ TCP batch to backend
              ▼
    ┌────────────────────┐
    │  ((Time-Series DB)) │
    └────────────────────┘
```

Push-based systems excel at collecting from ephemeral environments (Lambda functions, batch jobs, short-lived containers) and environments where network boundaries prevent inbound connections.

The push model is the default for StatsD, a widely-used metrics collection daemon originally built by Etsy. StatsD listens for UDP packets on port 8125, aggregates them in memory, and flushes the aggregated data to a backend (typically Graphite) every 10 seconds. The aggregation at the StatsD level is a critical second stage of compression: if 1,000 requests arrive in the 10-second flush window with the same metric name and labels, StatsD sends one data point instead of 1,000.

The downside of UDP-based push is reliability. UDP does not guarantee delivery. Under heavy load, the kernel drops UDP packets before they reach the StatsD process. The dropped packets are invisible: neither the sender nor the receiver knows they were lost. This means the metrics under-report during the exact moments when you most need accurate data — during traffic spikes.

### Variant 3: Hybrid Agent Model

Modern observability platforms use a hybrid approach. A local agent runs on each node, collects metrics via multiple mechanisms (pull from local processes, push from sidecars), and forwards them to a cloud backend.

The agent handles buffering, retransmission, compression, and authentication. The backend receives already-aggregated data. This model is what Datadog, New Relic, and Grafana Cloud use.

```
    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │               Host / Node                    │
    │                                              │
    │  ┌─────────┐  ┌─────────┐  ┌─────────┐     │
    │  │ [App 1] │  │ [App 2] │  │ [App 3] │     │
    │  │ /met    │  │ /met    │  │ /met    │     │
    │  └────┬────┘  └────┬────┘  └────┬────┘     │
    │       │            │            │           │
    │       ▼            ▼            ▼           │
    │  ┌────────────────────────────────────────┐ │
    │  │           {Agent Daemon}              │ │
    │  │    Scrape local endpoints             │ │
    │  │    Buffer, compress, encrypt          │ │
    │  │    Forward via HTTPS to backend        │ │
    │  └──────────────────┬─────────────────────┘ │
    └─────────────────────┼───────────────────────┘
                          │ HTTPS batch (every 60s)
                          ▼
              ┌──────────────────────┐
              │  Cloud Metrics       │
              │  Backend (SaaS)      │
              │  Global aggregation  │
              │  Multi-region store  │
              └──────────────────────┘
```

The hybrid model is the most practical for production. It combines the reliability of pull (local collection detects dead processes) with the flexibility of push (agent forwards to cloud without inbound firewall rules).

The agent in the hybrid model typically runs as a DaemonSet in Kubernetes or as a systemd service on VMs. It discovers local processes by reading the /proc filesystem, scanning for /metrics endpoints, or listening on a local Unix socket. The agent is responsible for:
- **Service discovery**: finding all instrumented processes on the node.
- **Scraping**: collecting metrics from each process on a configurable interval.
- **Buffering**: storing metrics in memory or on disk when the backend is unreachable.
- **Compression**: compressing metric batches before sending them over the wire.
- **Authentication**: attaching credentials or tokens for the cloud backend.
- **Retry**: re-sending failed batches with exponential backoff.

The agent is the most operationally complex component because it must run on every node, be updated regularly, and handle every failure mode gracefully. Managed agent solutions (Datadog Agent, Grafana Agent, AWS CloudWatch Agent) handle this complexity by providing automatic updates, built-in dashboards, and support contracts.

## Mental Model Check

These questions are designed to surface gaps in your understanding. If you get a question wrong, go back and re-read the relevant section. The goal is not to memorize answers but to develop an intuition for how metrics systems behave under different conditions.

Test your understanding with these ten questions. Cover the answers before reading them.

**Question 1: You are designing a metrics system for a video streaming platform. You want to track buffering events per video per user per device type. The platform has 10 million daily active users, 100,000 videos, and 5 device types. What is the cardinality of this metric, and why is that a problem?**

The cardinality is 10 million multiplied by 100,000 multiplied by 5 equals 5 trillion. This is catastrophically high. No metrics infrastructure can handle 5 trillion active time series. The solution is to aggregate at the source: emit separate metrics for per-video buffering rate (buffering events divided by total plays per video, with cardinality 100,000) and per-device buffering rate (5 time series). Drop user-level granularity entirely — that belongs in logs, not metrics.

**Question 2: A histogram with buckets [1ms, 5ms, 10ms, 25ms, 50ms, 100ms, 250ms, 500ms, 1000ms] receives observations of 3ms, 8ms, 12ms, 200ms, and 800ms. Which buckets do these fall into, and what can you infer about the distribution?**

3ms falls into the 5ms bucket (smallest bucket larger than 3ms). 8ms falls into 10ms. 12ms falls into 25ms. 200ms falls into 250ms. 800ms falls into 1000ms. With five observations, you cannot reliably infer any percentile, but you can see the data skews fast: 60 percent of observations are under 25ms, and 20 percent are above 500ms. This bimodal shape (most requests fast, some slow) suggests a caching layer.

**Question 3: Your counter http_requests_total shows 5000 at time T, 5100 at T+30s, and 5300 at T+60s. What is the request rate? What happens to the rate if a scrape misses a data point?**

The rate from T to T+30s is 100 divided by 30 equals 3.33 requests per second. From T+30s to T+60s it is 200 divided by 30 equals 6.67 requests per second. If a scrape misses a data point, the rate over the longer interval (T to T+60s) would be 300 divided by 60 equals 5 requests per second, which is the accurate average. Rate calculations are robust to missing individual scrape points as long as the counter monotonicity holds.

**Question 4: You notice that p99 latency on your dashboard is consistently 800ms, but users are complaining that the app feels slow. What might explain the discrepancy?**

Several explanations exist. First, p99 measures the 99th percentile of latencies, but user-perceived latency includes network time, DNS resolution, TLS handshake, and rendering time on the client — none of which your server-side metric captures. Second, p99 by request count ignores request distribution: if 10 percent of users make 90 percent of requests, the happy users dominate the percentile calculation. Third, your bucket boundaries might be too coarse — if the largest bucket before the infinity bucket is 1000ms, you lose resolution in the 800ms to 1000ms range. Fourth, the metric might exclude errors: if 1 percent of requests time out at 30 seconds, p99 only covers the successful 99 percent, but the user who hit the timeout had a terrible experience.

**Question 5: Your team wants to add a label environment to every metric, with values prod, staging, and dev. You have 500 metrics, each with an average of 10 label value combinations. What is the new cardinality, and what operational risk does this introduce?**

Current cardinality is 500 multiplied by 10 equals 5000 active time series. Adding the environment label multiplies by 3: 500 multiplied by 10 multiplied by 3 equals 15,000 time series. That is manageable by itself. The risk is that if any metric already has high cardinality in other labels, the multiplication by environment could push it over the infrastructure limit. For example, if one of your 500 metrics has a user_id label with 10,000 values, adding environment pushes it to 30,000 time series for that single metric. The safer approach is to use separate instances of the metrics infrastructure per environment, or to tag the metric at the collection layer rather than in the application code.

**Question 6: Your team has 50 dashboards, each with 20 panels. Your Grafana server takes 30 seconds to load any dashboard. Where do you start debugging?**

The most likely cause is that every panel queries the entire time range independently. If a dashboard shows "last 7 days" and all 20 panels query 7 days of data, the server makes 20 queries for 7 days each. The solution is to use dashboard-level time range variables, query caching, or panel-level time range overrides so that panels share query results. Another cause is expensive PromQL queries: rate and histogram_quantile functions over large time ranges are computationally expensive. Adding a recording rule that precomputes these functions every 30 seconds can reduce query latency from seconds to milliseconds.

**Question 7: You need to choose between keeping raw data for 90 days or keeping 5-minute aggregates for 2 years. Which do you choose and why?**

For most production systems, the answer is both: raw data for 30 days and downsampled data for 2 years. But if forced to choose, keep raw data for a shorter period and downsampled data for longer. The reason is that 90-day-old raw data is rarely needed at full resolution. When you debug an incident, you usually look at the last few hours. When you do capacity planning, you look at weekly or monthly trends that are better served by downsampled data. The exception is compliance: if regulators require you to produce exact metrics at second-level granularity for two years, you must keep the raw data.

**Question 8: Your application emits a counter metric http_requests_total that resets to zero every time the process restarts. How does the monitoring system handle these resets?**

The rate function in PromQL and similar query languages handles counter resets automatically. When the rate function detects a decrease in the counter value (which should be monotonically increasing), it assumes a reset occurred and adjusts the calculation. Instead of computing rate = (new - old) / time (which would be negative), it computes rate = (reset_value + new - old_start) / time, where reset_value is the last value before the reset. This gives an accurate rate even across restarts. The alternative is to use the increase function, which also handles resets but reports absolute increase rather than rate.

**Question 9: Your buddy says that p99 latency of 100ms means 99 percent of requests are under 100ms. Is that accurate?**

Not exactly. p99 latency of 100ms means that 99 percent of requests have latency at or below 100ms, assuming the histogram buckets are representative. But there is a subtlety: if the histogram has a bucket at 100ms and a bucket at 200ms, a request with 100ms latency falls into the 100ms bucket, and a request with 101ms falls into the 200ms bucket. The p99 is estimated by interpolating within the bucket that contains the 99th percentile rank. If the 99th percentile falls in the 100ms bucket, the estimate depends on how many requests are in that bucket and where in the bucket the percentile falls. The p99 is an estimate, not an exact measurement.

**Question 10: You are building a metrics system for a fleet of IoT devices. Each device sends data every 5 minutes. The fleet has 1 million devices. Each device emits 10 metrics. What is the cardinality, and what architecture would you recommend?**

The cardinality is 1 million devices multiplied by 10 metrics equals 10 million time series. This is high but manageable if each device is a single time series. The key constraint is that the scrape or push interval is every 5 minutes, not every 15 seconds. This means the ingestion rate is 10 million / 300 seconds = 33,333 samples per second, which is manageable. The architecture should use push-based collection because the devices are behind NAT and cannot be scraped. A push gateway (like Prometheus Pushgateway) can receive metrics from all devices, buffer them, and expose them for scraping by the main Prometheus server. The devices should not include high-cardinality labels in their metrics. Device ID should be stored as a label with careful cardinality management.
