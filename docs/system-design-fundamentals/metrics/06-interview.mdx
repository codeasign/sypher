## How the Topic Appears

Metrics surfaces in system design interviews in dozens of ways. Interviewers rarely ask directly about metrics. Instead, they embed metrics requirements into broader design problems. Here are the common phrasings:

**1. "How would you monitor this system?"**
The most direct form. The interviewer wants you to identify the key metrics for the system you just designed. Do not just list metrics — explain what each metric tells you and what threshold should trigger an alert.

**2. "You notice the system is slow. How do you find the bottleneck?"**
This tests your debugging approach. The interviewer wants to see you use metrics to narrow down the problem: start with overall latency, drill into component latency, and identify the specific service or resource that is the bottleneck.

**3. "Design a monitoring system for a distributed service."**
A standalone monitoring design question. The interviewer wants the full pipeline: instrumentation, collection, storage, alerting, and visualization. Cover cardinality limits, downsampling, and failure modes.

**4. "Your p99 latency is 200ms but users report the app is slow. Why?"**
Tests your understanding of what metrics actually measure versus what users experience. Discuss network time, client-side rendering, request distribution, and the difference between server-side and user-perceived latency.

**5. "How would you detect a memory leak in a production service?"**
Tests metric-driven debugging. Walk through: memory gauge trending up over time, GC pause histogram shifting right, and heap dump analysis after the metric confirms the leak.

**6. "How do you set up alerting for a new service?"**
Tests your alerting philosophy. Cover the four golden signals (latency, traffic, errors, saturation), the difference between page-worthy and informational alerts, and the requirement that every alert has a runbook.

**7. "Your metrics dashboard is slow. How do you fix it?"**
Tests your understanding of query performance. Discuss downsampling, pre-aggregation, query caching, and sharding. The interviewer wants to see you diagnose the bottleneck and propose a targeted fix.

**8. "How would you instrument a new microservice?"**
Tests your practical knowledge of metrics libraries and patterns. Cover counter/gauge/histogram selection, standard label conventions, and the /metrics endpoint pattern.

**9. "You have 1,000 microservices. How do you collect metrics from all of them?"**
Tests your architecture knowledge at scale. Discuss sharding, hierarchical aggregation, multi-region deployment, and the choice between pull and push collection.

**10. "Design a real-time dashboard for a video streaming platform."**
A concrete application of metrics concepts. Cover: buffering rate per video, CDN cache hit ratio, bitrate distribution, and startup latency. The interviewer wants to see you select the right metrics and design the dashboard layout.

**11. "How would you calculate storage requirements for your metrics infrastructure?"**
Tests your capacity planning skills. You should be able to compute: active series = services × replicas × metrics × labels, samples per second = active series / scrape interval, storage = samples per second × retention seconds × bytes per sample.

**12. "Your dashboard shows a sudden drop in requests. Is that good or bad?"**
Tests your ability to interpret metrics in context. A drop in requests could mean fewer users (bad for business, good for performance), a routing failure (bad), or a batch job completing (good). The answer depends on the context, and the interview wants to hear you ask clarifying questions.

**13. "How would you ensure your metrics system survives the failure of a single datacenter?"**
Tests multi-region architecture knowledge. Discuss regional independence, cross-region replication, and the global query layer. The key insight is that each region must be self-sufficient: it can operate independently without the global layer.

**14. "Design a metrics system for a serverless platform where functions run for 100ms on average."**
Tests your ability to handle ephemeral workloads. Push-based collection is essential because you cannot scrape a function that is not running. The metrics collection must happen during the function invocation and be flushed before the function terminates. Discuss client-side buffering, UDP push, and the Pushgateway pattern.

## What the Interviewer Tests

Interviewers use metrics questions to evaluate five specific skills:

**Signal vs. noise**: can you distinguish between metrics that matter and metrics that are interesting but not actionable? A candidate who lists 50 metrics without prioritization shows they do not understand operational reality.

**Breadth vs. depth**: can you go deep on one metric (explain exactly how p99 is computed from histogram buckets) and then zoom out to the system architecture? The best candidates alternate between layers naturally.

**Failure awareness**: do you know how metrics systems fail? Mentioning cardinality explosions, storage saturation, and alert fatigue shows production experience. Ignoring failure modes suggests classroom-only knowledge.

**Trade-off articulation**: can you explain why you chose a push model over pull, or 15-second scrapes over 5-second scrapes? Every decision has trade-offs, and interviewers want to hear you acknowledge both sides.

**Operational maturity**: do you think about runbooks, on-call rotations, and escalation policies? A candidate who designs a technically perfect system but forgets to make it operable is a liability in production.

## Level-by-Level Expectations

### L3 / Entry-Level

At the entry level, interviewers want to see that you understand the basics. You should be able to:
- Define counter, gauge, histogram, and label.
- Explain the difference between p50, p95, and p99 latency.
- List the four golden signals of monitoring.
- Describe a simple metrics pipeline: app emits metric, collector scrapes, database stores, dashboard shows.

**Example answer**: "For a web service, I would track request latency with a histogram, request count with a counter, and error count with a counter. I would set alerts when p99 latency exceeds 500ms or error rate exceeds 1 percent for more than 5 minutes."

### L4 / Mid-Level

At the mid-level, interviewers expect you to design a complete metrics system for a service. You should be able to:
- Design the instrumentation: what metrics to emit and what labels to use.
- Choose between pull and push collection and justify the choice.
- Calculate storage requirements given request volume and cardinality.
- Set up alerting rules with proper thresholds and notification paths.

**Example answer**: "I would use a pull-based model with Prometheus. Each service exposes a /metrics endpoint with standard labels: service, endpoint, method, status. I would track request count, latency histogram, error count, and active connections. With 100,000 requests per second and 50 metrics per endpoint, I need roughly 1 TB of storage for 30 days of retention."

### L5 / Senior

At the senior level, interviewers expect you to design a metrics system for an organization. You should be able to:
- Design a multi-team, multi-service metrics architecture with isolation.
- Handle cardinality constraints across the organization.
- Set metadata standards and governance for metric naming and labeling.
- Plan capacity for the metrics infrastructure itself.
- Design the on-call and escalation process around alerts.

**Example answer**: "I would deploy Prometheus per service with Thanos for global aggregation. Each service namespace gets its own Prometheus instance with a 1 million series limit. Standard labels are enforced through a validation webhook that rejects metrics with non-standard labels. The Thanos querier provides a global view for cross-service debugging. We alert on service-level SLOs, not on infrastructure metrics. Each alert has a runbook linked from the notification."

### L6 / Staff

At the staff level, interviewers expect you to design a metrics platform that serves the entire company. You should be able to:
- Design a multi-tenant metrics system with resource isolation and fair scheduling.
- Handle 100 million-plus active time series across multiple regions.
- Build self-service tooling so teams can add metrics without central approval.
- Design cost allocation and chargeback mechanisms for metric usage.
- Mentor other engineers on metrics best practices through design docs and reviews.

**Example answer**: "I would build a multi-tenant metrics platform based on M3 or a managed service. Each team gets a namespace with configurable cardinality limits and retention policies. The platform enforces a global label taxonomy: service, environment, and region are required; all other labels are optional but limited to 5 per metric. We charge teams by the number of active time series they emit, which incentivizes efficient instrumentation. The platform exposes meta-metrics so teams can see their own usage and plan accordingly."

### L7 / Principal

At the principal level, interviewers expect you to set the company-wide observability strategy. You should be able to:
- Define the company's metrics philosophy: what to measure, how to measure it, and what not to measure.
- Design the metrics system as part of a broader observability platform that includes logs, traces, and incident management.
- Influence organizational culture around on-call, incident response, and post-mortems.
- Evaluate build-vs-buy decisions for the metrics infrastructure.
- Represent the company's approach to observability in external communications and open source contributions.

**Example answer**: "Observability is a first-class product at our company, not a support function. Our strategy is: metrics for health, traces for debugging, logs for forensics. We use standard libraries with automatic instrumentation so every service emits the four golden signals by default. Our platform team runs the metrics infrastructure as an internal product with SLAs, a roadmap, and self-service onboarding. We participate in the Prometheus and OpenTelemetry communities to ensure our requirements drive the open source roadmap."

## FAANG Framework for Metrics Questions

When you face a metrics question in a system design interview, use this framework to structure your answer. The framework ensures you cover all the relevant dimensions without getting lost in details.

### Step 1: Understand the Context

Before you design anything, clarify the scope. Ask the interviewer:
- "What is the service we are monitoring?"
- "What is the expected scale? Requests per second? Number of services?"
- "What is the most important thing to get right: latency, reliability, or cost?"
- "Do we need real-time data, or is 15-second delay acceptable?"

### Step 2: Identify the Signals

Define the key metrics using the four golden signals:
- **Latency**: time to serve a request. Use a histogram with appropriate buckets.
- **Traffic**: requests per second (or bytes per second for data pipelines). Use a counter labeled by endpoint.
- **Errors**: failed requests as a rate. Use a counter labeled by error type.
- **Saturation**: how full the service is. Use gauges for CPU, memory, disk, and connection pools.

### Step 3: Design the Instrumentation

Define exactly what metrics each service will emit. Use the USE method (Utilization, Saturation, Errors) for resources and the RED method (Rate, Errors, Duration) for services.

For each service, specify:
- **Rate**: requests per second, broken down by endpoint, method, and status code.
- **Errors**: error rate as a ratio of total requests, broken down by error type.
- **Duration**: latency histogram with buckets appropriate for the service (under 100ms for APIs, under 10ms for caches, under 1 second for databases).
- **Utilization**: resource usage as a percentage of capacity (CPU, memory, disk, connections).
- **Saturation**: queue depth, connection pool usage, thread pool usage.

Specify which labels to include on each metric and which labels to explicitly exclude. The excluded labels are as important as the included ones because they prevent cardinality explosions.

### Step 4: Design the Collection

Decide how metrics get from the application to storage.
- **Pull**: Prometheus scrapes the /metrics endpoint. Good for long-lived services with stable network.
- **Push**: StatsD or similar. Good for ephemeral or batch services.
- **Hybrid**: local agent scrapes, then forwards to backend. Good for most production systems.

### Step 4: Plan the Storage

Calculate storage requirements and choose a topology.
- **Single instance**: Prometheus standalone. Good for under 1 million series.
- **Sharded**: Prometheus per service with Thanos. Good for 1-10 million series.
- **Distributed**: M3 or VictoriaMetrics cluster. Good for 10 million-plus series.

### Step 5: Design the Queries

Define the dashboards and alerts that will use this data.
- **Top-level dashboard**: 5-10 panels showing service health.
- **Drill-down dashboard**: per-component metrics for debugging.
- **Key alerts**: p99 latency over 500ms for 5 minutes, error rate over 1 percent for 5 minutes, any metric with zero samples (dead service).

### Step 6: Calculate the Capacity

Do the math. Show the interviewer you can compute the resource requirements.

- Active series: services × replicas × metrics × labels.
- Ingestion rate: active series / scrape interval.
- Storage per day: ingestion rate × bytes per sample × seconds per day.
- Storage per retention period: storage per day × retention days.
- Number of servers: active series / max series per server.
- Memory: index memory + memtable memory + query cache.
- Cost: servers × cost per server + storage cost.

Run through the numbers out loud. The interviewer wants to hear you think through the math, not just give the final answer. If you make a mistake, they will correct you. The important thing is to show the thought process.

### Step 7: Design the Alerting

Define the alerting rules that will protect the service. Each alert must have:
- **Condition**: the PromQL expression that triggers the alert.
- **Duration**: how long the condition must hold before the alert fires (avoids flapping).
- **Severity**: P0 (pages immediately), P1 (pages during business hours), P2 (ticket).
- **Runbook**: link to the document that tells the on-call engineer what to do.

For each service, define these alerts:
1. **Service down alert**: no successful requests for 5 minutes. P0.
2. **High error rate alert**: error rate over 5 percent for 5 minutes. P0.
3. **High latency alert**: p99 latency over 1 second for 5 minutes. P0.
4. **Resource exhaustion alerts**: CPU over 90 percent, memory over 90 percent, disk over 80 percent. P1.
5. **Dependency degradation alerts**: downstream service latency over 2 seconds, or downstream error rate over 1 percent. P1.
6. **Capacity warnings**: throughput approaching 80 percent of estimated capacity. P2.
7. **Certificate expiry alerts**: TLS certificate expiring in 30 days. P2.

### Step 8: Design the Operational Runbook

Show the interviewer you think about operations. Describe the runbook for the most critical alert.

Example runbook for "high error rate":
1. Open the service dashboard. Confirm the error rate is elevated.
2. Check the error breakdown by endpoint and status code. Which endpoint has the most errors? Which status code?
3. Check the dependency dashboard. Are any downstream services showing errors?
4. If a downstream service is failing: contact the downstream team. If the downstream is internal, check their dashboard. If the downstream is external, check the status page.
5. If no downstream failure: check the recent deployment history. Was there a deployment in the last 30 minutes? Roll back the most recent deployment.
6. If rollback resolves the issue: create a bug ticket for the deployment.
7. If rollback does not resolve: escalate to the senior on-call engineer with the dashboard link and deployment history.

Every step in the runbook references a dashboard or a tool. There are no steps that require SSH access or manual investigation. The runbook is executable by any on-call engineer, not just the service experts.

### Step 9: Plan for the Future

End your answer by describing how the system evolves as the organization grows.

- At 2x current scale: add more shards, increase retention period by 30 days.
- At 10x current scale: move to multi-region deployment, implement hierarchical aggregation.
- At 100x current scale: evaluate custom metrics system (like Netflix Atlas or Uber M3).
- At a new team joining: provide self-service onboarding, automated dashboard generation, and standardized instrumentation libraries.

The interviewer wants to see that you are not just solving the current problem but building a system that can grow with the organization.

### Step 10: Address Failure Modes

Show the interviewer you have thought about what can go wrong.
- "If cardinality explodes, we reject new time series and alert the operator."
- "If the metrics server crashes, we replay the WAL on restart."
- "If disk fills up, we delete old data first, then alert."
- "If a target is unreachable, we record the scrape failure and show a gap in the chart."

## Phrase Bank

Use these phrases in your interview answers. They demonstrate depth and precision.

**1. "The four golden signals of monitoring are latency, traffic, errors, and saturation."**
Opens any monitoring answer with the standard framework.

**2. "I would use a histogram with exponential bucket boundaries from 1ms to 10s."**
Shows you know how histograms work and what bucket layout to use.

**3. "Cardinality is the number of unique label combinations. A common failure mode is a label with a large number of unique values, like user_id."**
Demonstrates awareness of the most common metrics failure.

**4. "I would enforce standard labels across all services: service, endpoint, method, and status_code are required."**
Shows you think about governance and consistency.

**5. "The scrape interval determines the staleness of the data. A 15-second scrape interval means data is at most 15 seconds old."**
Demonstrates understanding of the pull model's timing characteristics.

**6. "I would use a sliding window for rate calculation to handle counter resets on process restart."**
Shows you understand the mechanics of counter-based rate calculation.

**7. "Downsampling is irreversible. I keep raw data for 30 days and downsampled data for 2 years."**
Demonstrates understanding of the granularity-versus-retention trade-off.

**8. "Every alert must have a runbook. If an alert does not have a runbook, it is not actionable and should be deleted."**
Shows operational maturity and awareness of alert fatigue.

**9. "I would use meta-metrics to monitor the metrics system itself: scrape duration, ingestion rate, query latency, and error rates."**
Shows you understand the monitoring loop.

**10. "The p99 latency is the latency below which 99 percent of requests fall. It is computed from histogram bucket counts using interpolation."**
Shows precise understanding of a fundamental concept.

**11. "I would use consistent hashing to shard time series across metrics servers, with a replication factor of 2 for high availability."**
Shows distributed systems knowledge applied to metrics.

**12. "A gauge is a snapshot. A counter is cumulative. A histogram is a distribution of values over a time window."**
Clean summary of the three metric types.

**13. "I would calculate storage as: active series multiplied by bytes per sample multiplied by samples per retention period."**
Shows you can do capacity planning math.

**14. "The cost of metrics infrastructure is 2 to 5 percent of total infrastructure cost for most organizations."**
Shows you have industry cost context.

**15. "I prefer pull-based collection for long-lived services because the collector controls the scrape schedule and detects dead targets immediately."**
A clear, justified opinion on a common design debate.

**16. "The RED method stands for Rate, Errors, Duration. It gives you three panels per service."**
A concise summary of the standard microservice monitoring approach.

**17. "The USE method stands for Utilization, Saturation, Errors. It applies to resources, not services."**
The complementary framework for infrastructure monitoring.

**18. "I would use a burn-rate alert for SLO monitoring. The alert fires when the error budget is being consumed faster than expected."**
Shows advanced alerting knowledge beyond simple threshold-based alerts.

**19. "The scrape failure is itself a signal. If the metrics system cannot reach a target, that means the target is either dead or partitioned."**
Demonstrates understanding of meta-metrics and the monitoring loop.

**20. "I would use client-side buffering for metrics in ephemeral environments. The buffer is flushed when the process terminates."**
Shows understanding of push-based metrics for short-lived workloads.

**21. "The WAL provides durability. Without a WAL, a crash loses however much data was in the memtable."**
Shows understanding of storage internals and crash recovery.

**22. "I would use histogram_quantile to compute percentiles from bucket counts. The accuracy depends on bucket width."**
Shows precise understanding of percentile computation.

**23. "The cost of metrics infrastructure is dominated by index memory, not storage. Each active time series needs roughly 256 bytes in the index."**
Shows understanding of the actual cost drivers in a metrics system.

**24. "I would use a sidecar pattern for metrics collection in Kubernetes. The sidecar scrapes the local pod and forwards to the central collector."**
Shows Kubernetes-specific metrics architecture knowledge.

**25. "The difference between a histogram and a summary is that histograms are aggregated on the server side and summaries are aggregated on the client side."**
A precise technical distinction that demonstrates depth.

**26. "I would use exponential bucket boundaries for latency histograms. Powers of two give good coverage from 1ms to 10s."**
Shows practical histogram configuration knowledge.

**27. "The most important meta-metric is scrape duration. If scrape duration exceeds the scrape interval, the system is permanently overloaded."**
Shows understanding of the fundamental bottleneck in pull-based systems.

**28. "I would use a circuit breaker pattern for the metrics ingestion pipeline. If the backend is slow, the collector stops sending data and buffers locally."**
Shows awareness of backpressure and self-preservation patterns.

**29. "The four golden signals are latency, traffic, errors, and saturation. Every service should have dashboards and alerts for all four."**
The most important metrics framework. Use it to open any monitoring answer.

## Red Flags

These phrases and behaviors will hurt your metrics interview answer. Avoid them.

**1. "We can just use CloudWatch / Datadog / whatever."**
Mentioning a specific product without context suggests you cannot design systems, only configure them. If you mention a product, explain why it is the right choice and what trade-offs it makes.

**2. Listing 20 metrics without grouping or prioritizing.**
This suggests you do not understand which metrics matter. Always organize metrics by concern: latency, traffic, errors, saturation.

**3. "We will alert on everything."**
This is a red flag for operational maturity. You must distinguish between page-worthy alerts and informational signals.

**4. Not mentioning cardinality.**
Cardinality is the defining challenge of metrics at scale. Not mentioning it suggests you have never operated a metrics system in production.

**5. "We will keep all data forever."**
This is either naive (you will run out of disk) or expensive (you will pay a fortune). Always discuss retention policies and downsampling.

**6. Designing a system that cannot handle the stated scale.**
If the interviewer says 10 million requests per second and you design a single Prometheus server, you have failed the scale component. Always verify the expected scale before designing.

**7. Forgetting about the operational side.**
A technically perfect system that has no runbooks, no escalation policy, and no on-call rotation is not production-ready. Always include operations in your design.

**8. "Metrics alone are enough."**
No observability expert relies on metrics alone. Acknowledge the role of logs and traces alongside metrics. The best observability strategy combines all three.

**9. Not knowing the difference between p50, p95, and p99.**
These are fundamental concepts. If you cannot explain them clearly, you appear inexperienced.

**10. Suggesting metrics for everything.**
If you try to put user_id into a metric, the interviewer will question your understanding of cardinality. Know what belongs in metrics and what belongs in logs or traces.

**11. "We will use logs for everything."**
Logs are important, but they are not a replacement for metrics. Logs are expensive to store and slow to query at scale. Metrics are designed for exactly the use case logs struggle with: high-volume, low-latency aggregation.

**12. Not mentioning dashboards.**
A metrics system without dashboards is like a plane without a cockpit display. The data is there, but nobody can see it. Always mention the visualization layer and the dashboard hierarchy.

**13. "We will build our own time-series database."**
Unless you are Netflix or Uber, building your own time-series database is a mistake. The operational cost of maintaining a custom time-series database far exceeds the licensing cost of a commercial or managed service.

**14. Designing a system that is too complex for the stated scale.**
If the interviewer says 100 services, do not design a 50-server multi-region cluster. Design a single Prometheus with Thanos sidecar and explain that it can scale up as needed. Over-engineering is as bad as under-engineering.

**15. Forgetting to mention the cost.**
The interviewer wants to know that you understand the financial impact of your design. A system that costs $50,000 per month to run is very different from one that costs $500 per month. Always include a rough cost estimate.

## The Mental Model for Interview Success

The single most important mental model for the metrics interview is the dashboard hierarchy. Everything you design should be organized around this concept:

**Top level: service health (5-10 panels)**. This is what the on-call engineer sees first. It must load in under 2 seconds and tell the engineer if the service is healthy at a glance.

**Middle level: component health (10-20 panels per component)**. This is what the engineer drills into when the top level shows a problem. It breaks down the overall metrics by component.

**Bottom level: resource health (5-10 panels per resource)**. This is what the engineer uses to find the root cause. It shows the resource metrics (CPU, memory, disk, network) for the failing component.

Every metric question in an interview can be answered by referencing this hierarchy. When the interviewer asks "how would you monitor this service," you describe the three levels. When they ask "how would you find the bottleneck," you describe drilling down through the levels. When they ask "how would you set up alerting," you describe what alerts at each level.

## How to Handle Follow-Up Questions

Interviewers will challenge your design with follow-up questions. Here is how to handle the most common ones:

**"Your metrics server is running out of memory. What do you do?"**
First, check the meta-metrics: is it a cardinality explosion (new labels being added) or a data volume increase (more samples per second)? If it is cardinality, identify the offending metric and reject it. If it is data volume, add more servers to the cluster.

**"Your dashboard shows a flat line for a metric. What does that mean?"**
It could mean the service is healthy and the metric is constant, or it could mean the service is dead and the metric is stale. Check the scrape status: is the target being scraped? If not, the service is likely down. If yes, check the metric value: is it a gauge that happens to be constant, or a counter that is not being incremented?

**"Your alert for high latency keeps firing during normal traffic. What do you do?"**
The alert threshold is too sensitive. Review the latency distribution over the last 30 days. Set the threshold at the 99th percentile of normal latency, not the mean. Use a longer evaluation window. Most latency alerts benefit from a 5-minute window.

**"A new service is emitting 10,000 metrics with random labels. What happens?"**
The cardinality explodes. The metrics server runs out of memory. The solution is to enforce cardinality limits at the collection layer. Reject metrics with labels that are not in the predefined schema. Rate-limit new metric registrations.

## Practice Drills

### Drill 1: The Four Golden Signals Drill

Pick any service and list the four golden signals for it. Practice until you can do this reflexively.

- **Payment service**: latency (payment processing time), traffic (transactions per second), errors (failed payments by decline reason), saturation (connection pool usage).
- **Video encoding service**: latency (encoding time per video), traffic (videos per second), errors (encoding failures by error code), saturation (worker queue depth).
- **API gateway**: latency (request processing time), traffic (requests per second), errors (HTTP 5xx rate), saturation (connection count versus limit).

### Drill 2: The Failure Mode Drill

For each failure mode, describe what metric would detect it, what threshold triggers the alert, and what the runbook says.

- **Memory leak**: memory_bytes gauge trending up. Alert at 80 percent of heap limit. Runbook: trigger heap dump, analyze with memory profiler, roll back the last deployment.
- **Database slow query**: query_latency_seconds histogram shift. Alert when p99 exceeds 1 second. Runbook: identify the slow query from pg_stat_statements, add an index, or optimize the query.
- **Cache failure**: cache_hit_ratio dropping below 80 percent. Alert when below 70 percent for 5 minutes. Runbook: check cache server health, verify cache key format, check for cache invalidation bugs.
- **Consumer lag**: consumer_lag gauge growing. Alert when lag exceeds 10,000 messages. Runbook: scale up consumers, check for processing errors, check the DLQ.
- **Disk full**: disk_usage_bytes gauge at 95 percent. Alert at 80 percent for proactive response. Runbook: delete old logs, increase disk size, check for log rotation issues.

### Drill 3: The Capacity Planning Drill

Given a set of inputs, calculate the metrics infrastructure requirements.

- **Input**: 500 services, 10 replicas each, 1,000 metrics per service, 10 labels per metric, 15-second scrape interval, 30-day retention.
- **Active series**: 500 × 10 × 1,000 × 10 = 50 million.
- **Samples per second**: 50 million / 15 = 3.33 million.
- **Storage per day**: 3.33 million × 86,400 × 2 bytes = 576 GB.
- **Storage for 30 days**: 576 GB × 30 = 17.3 TB.
- **Servers**: 50 million / 500,000 per server = 100 servers.
- **Memory**: 50 million × 256 bytes = 12.8 GB for index, plus memtables.

### Drill 4: The Arrow of Time Drill

Explain what happens when you change each of these parameters, holding everything else constant.

- **Decrease scrape interval from 15s to 5s**: 3x more data, 3x more network traffic, 3x more storage. Better time resolution for spike detection.
- **Add one high-cardinality label (user_id) to a metric**: potentially millions of new time series. Storage and memory increase proportionally. Query latency increases because the index grows.
- **Double the retention period**: double the storage cost. Query latency for old data stays the same if the cold tier is separate. Query latency for all data increases slightly because more chunks must be scanned.
- **Switch from pull to push collection**: no more scrape failure detection, but ephemeral services can now emit metrics. UDP packet loss becomes a concern.

### Drill 5: The Label Design Drill

For each service, design the labels for the http_requests_total metric. The goal is to maximize debugging power while minimizing cardinality.

- **API Gateway**: method (GET, POST, PUT, DELETE), endpoint (/users, /orders, /payments), status_code (200, 404, 500). Cardinality: 4 × 10 × 10 = 400. Good.
- **Database Proxy**: query_type (SELECT, INSERT, UPDATE, DELETE), table_name (users, orders, payments), status (success, error). Cardinality: 4 × 10 × 2 = 80. Good.
- **Background Worker**: job_type (email, report, cleanup), queue (high, medium, low), status (success, failed, retry). Cardinality: 3 × 3 × 3 = 27. Good.
- **User Service**: method, endpoint, status_code (same as API gateway). But adding user_tier (free, pro, enterprise) as a label: 4 × 10 × 10 × 3 = 1,200. Still manageable. Adding user_id: 4 × 10 × 10 × 1,000,000 = 400 million. Catastrophic.

### Drill 6: The Metric Selection Drill

Given a service, select the 10 most important metrics. Practice until you can do this for any service in under 2 minutes.

- **Authentication Service**: login_requests_total (counter), login_latency_seconds (histogram), login_errors_total by error_type (counter), active_sessions (gauge), session_creation_latency_seconds (histogram), token_validation_latency_seconds (histogram), password_reset_requests_total (counter), brute_force_detections_total (counter), database_connection_pool_usage (gauge), upstream_auth_provider_latency_seconds (histogram).
- **Content Delivery Service**: file_requests_total (counter), file_latency_seconds (histogram), file_errors_total by error_type (counter), bytes_transferred_total (counter), cache_hit_ratio (rate), cache_evictions_total (counter), origin_requests_total (counter), origin_latency_seconds (histogram), active_connections (gauge), bandwidth_usage (gauge).

### Drill 7: The Cost Justification Drill

Your manager asks why the metrics system costs $5,000 per month. Explain the cost in terms the manager understands.

"Five thousand dollars per month covers 10 servers that process 2 million metrics per second from 500 services. Each server costs $500 per month from the cloud provider. The cost is 2 percent of our total infrastructure budget, which is within the industry standard of 2 to 5 percent. This system prevents incidents from going undetected. The average incident costs us $10,000 in engineering time and lost revenue. If the metrics system prevents just one incident per month, it pays for itself twice over."

### Drill 8: The Design Review Drill

Review this metrics system design and identify the problems:

"A single Prometheus server scrapes all 500 of our microservices every 15 seconds. We have 25 million active time series. Retention is 2 years at full resolution. We alert on 200 metrics."

Problems: single server cannot handle 25 million series (max is roughly 1 million). Retention of 2 years at full resolution would need petabytes of storage. 200 alerts cause alert fatigue. No mention of downsampling, sharding, or meta-metrics.

Corrected design: "We use 25 Prometheus servers, each scraping 20 microservices. Each server manages 1 million series. Raw data is kept for 30 days on SSD. Downsampled data is kept for 2 years on S3. We have 20 critical alerts. The global Thanos querier provides a unified view across all Prometheus servers."

### Drill 9: The Mock Interview Drill

Practice with a partner or a timer. Each drill is 15 minutes: 10 minutes to design, 5 minutes for feedback.

**Scenario 1**: Design the metrics system for a ride-sharing platform. 10 million users, 1 million rides per day, 50 microservices, 5 regions. Include: instrumentation, collection, storage, alerting, and dashboards.

**Scenario 2**: The company has 500 microservices and a single Prometheus server that is crashing. The on-call engineer silences alerts because they are all false. Design the migration to a scalable architecture. Include the migration steps, the target architecture, and the rollback plan.

**Scenario 3**: Your CTO wants to reduce the metrics bill by 30 percent. The current system has 50 million active series, 300,000 samples per second, and 90-day raw retention. Propose three cost reduction strategies with specific savings estimates and risks.

**Scenario 4**: A new team joins the company with 20 microservices. They need to be onboarded to the metrics system. Design the onboarding process. Include: instrumentation standards, dashboard templates, alerting rules, and the approval workflow. The onboarding should take under 1 week.

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even experienced engineers make these mistakes in metrics interview questions. Being aware of them helps you avoid them.

**Mistake 1: Designing for the wrong scale**. The interviewer says "design a metrics system for a startup." You design a 50-server multi-region cluster. The interviewer thinks you cannot match the solution to the problem. Always ask about the expected scale before designing.

**Mistake 2: Forgetting the operational cost**. You design a technically perfect system with 10 components, 50 servers, and 3 tiers of storage. The interviewer asks "who operates this?" You have no answer. Always include the operational model in your design.

**Mistake 3: Ignoring the human element**. You design a system with 200 alerts because every metric needs an alert. The on-call engineer silences everything. You have created alert fatigue. Always discuss the alerting philosophy and the expected number of alerts per shift.

**Mistake 4: Not mentioning the visualization layer**. You design the collection, storage, and query layers but forget to mention dashboards. The interviewer thinks you are collecting data for no purpose. Always include the dashboard hierarchy in your design.

**Mistake 5: Over-engineering the solution**. The interviewer asks for a simple monitoring system. You design a custom time-series database with machine learning anomaly detection. The interviewer thinks you cannot separate essential complexity from accidental complexity. The simplest solution that meets the requirements is the best solution.

### How to Study for the Metrics Interview

**Study the fundamentals**: Read the Prometheus documentation, the Thanos architecture doc, and the Google SRE book. These three resources cover the theory and practice of metrics at scale.

**Study the case studies**: Read the Netflix Atlas blog posts, the Uber M3 documentation, the Discord engineering blog, and the Cloudflare engineering blog. These case studies show how real companies solve metrics problems at scale.

**Practice the math**: The capacity planning math is the most testable part of metrics knowledge. Practice calculating active series, storage requirements, and server counts from different inputs. The math is simple arithmetic, but it must be automatic.

**Practice the frameworks**: The four golden signals, the RED method, and the USE method are the standard frameworks. Practice applying them to different services until the frameworks are automatic. A random service, apply the framework in under 30 seconds.

**Mock interviews**: Practice with a partner or a timer. The metrics portion of a system design interview is typically 10-15 minutes. Practice answering metrics questions within that time limit. The ability to articulate a complete answer under time pressure is a skill that must be practiced, not just studied. Remember: the interviewer is not testing your knowledge of PromQL syntax. They are testing your ability to think systematically about data collection, storage, and analysis at scale.
