## Core Tension

Every authorization system pulls between two opposing forces: **granularity** and **performance**. Fine-grained policies give you precise control over who can do what, down to individual resource attributes. Coarse-grained policies fly through evaluation but force you into broad, inflexible buckets.

The fundamental trade-off is simple:

As you design your system, every choice you make about how to evaluate permissions pushes you along this spectrum. There is no free lunch: your decision determines how your system behaves under load, how it evolves, and how it fails.

### Why This Trade-Off Exists

Authorization is a read operation. Every time a user makes a request, your system must check whether that request is permitted. The faster you make that check, the less information you can consider. A coarse check looks at the user's role and the resource type and returns a decision in microseconds. A fine-grained check evaluates the user's department, the resource's classification level, the time of day, the user's location, and whether the user has a signed NDA — each attribute adds a lookup, a comparison, and a potential branch in the evaluation tree.

The number of possible attribute combinations grows combinatorially. With 10 boolean attributes, there are 2^10 = 1,024 possible policy contexts. With 20 attributes, there are over a million. You cannot pre-compute decisions for all of them, but you cannot evaluate all of them on every request either. This is the computational heart of the trade-off.

### Real-World Impact

At Google's scale, a single microsecond of additional latency on the authorization path translates to measurable degradation across thousands of services. This is why Google's internal authorization system, Zanzibar, uses a relationship-based model (ReBAC) that is less expressive than full ABAC but far more cache-friendly. The trade-off was deliberate: they sacrificed expressiveness for the ability to serve billions of authorization checks per second from cache.

At a smaller scale, you face the same trade-off. If your authorization check adds 50 milliseconds to every API call, and your API handles 10,000 requests per second, you have just added 500 seconds of cumulative latency per second. The only way to absorb this is through caching, which brings its own set of trade-offs.

### The Latency Budget Approach

When you design a system, you typically allocate a latency budget for each layer. A common API target is 200ms P99 end-to-end latency. Within that budget, you need to account for network I/O, database queries, business logic, serialization, and authorization. If you allocate more than 10-20ms for authorization, you are likely to exceed your budget under load.

This forces a practical question: what authorization model can you evaluate within 10ms? For most systems, the answer is a simple role lookup. ABAC evaluation that requires attribute resolution across multiple services can easily take 50-100ms, consuming your entire budget.

The practical approach is to measure, not assume. Before you commit to an authorization model, prototype it and measure the P50, P95, and P99 latency under realistic load. If the numbers exceed your budget, you need to either simplify the model or invest in aggressive caching.

### The Read-to-Write Ratio

Authorization is overwhelmingly a read workload. For every policy change (a write), there are millions of authorization checks (reads). This ratio has a profound impact on your architecture:

- Because reads dominate, you can justify significant investment in read optimization: caching, pre-computation, and replication.
- Because writes are rare, you can afford a slower, more consistent write path that propagates changes to all caches.
- The ratio means you should optimize for the read path first and accept complexity in the write path.

A typical system might have one policy change per thousand requests, with each request triggering 2-5 authorization checks. This means the read-to-write ratio is between 2,000:1 and 5,000:1. Design your system accordingly: make reads fast and correct, and accept that writes may be slower and more complex.

### Policy Model: RBAC vs. ABAC

**RBAC** assigns roles to users and permissions to roles. You evaluate by checking whether the user's role contains the required permission. This is a single join — fast, cache-friendly, and easy to reason about. The evaluation logic in pseudocode:

```
user_roles = get_roles(user_id)
role_permissions = get_permissions(user_roles)
result = required_permission in role_permissions
```

This is a constant-time operation if you use a bitmap or hash set. The data model is straightforward: users have roles, roles have permissions, permissions are strings or enums.

**ABAC** evaluates against attributes of the user, resource, action, and environment. A policy might say "allow if user.department equals resource.department AND resource.visibility is 'internal' AND current_time is within business hours." This is powerful but requires evaluating multiple conditions, possibly fetching related objects, and applying a policy engine. The evaluation logic:

```
user_attrs = get_user_attributes(user_id)
resource_attrs = get_resource_attributes(resource_id)
env_attrs = get_environment_attributes()
policy = load_policy(resource_type, action)
result = evaluate(policy, user_attrs, resource_attrs, env_attrs)
```

Each attribute fetch is a potential network call, and the policy evaluation itself may require traversing a tree of conditions. A single ABAC evaluation can take 10-100 times longer than an RBAC evaluation.

The rule of thumb: start with RBAC and layer in ABAC only where your business rules demand it. Google's internal systems use RBAC for 80 percent of permissions and ABAC for the remaining 20 percent where relationship-based access is needed.

### Centralized vs. Distributed Evaluation

A **centralized Policy Decision Point (PDP)** is a single service that evaluates every authorization request. It is easy to audit, consistent, and straightforward to update. But it is a single point of failure and a throughput bottleneck. When the PDP goes down, no authorization decisions can be made — every service that depends on authorization becomes unavailable.

**Distributed evaluation** embeds the policy engine in each service or uses a sidecar proxy. Each node evaluates independently. This is horizontally scalable and resilient to PDP outages, but you now face consistency challenges — when you update a policy, how long until every node picks it up? You need a mechanism for distributing policy updates, and you need to tolerate a window where different nodes have different policy versions.

The latency profile differs significantly:

- **Centralized**: Each authorization check incurs a network round trip to the PDP. With a 1ms RTT and a 2ms evaluation time, P50 latency is about 3ms. But under load, queueing at the PDP can push P99 latency to 50ms or more.
- **Distributed**: Each authorization check is a local function call. P50 latency is 0.1-0.5ms. But each node must load and cache the full policy set, which can be memory-intensive.

Many large systems use a hybrid: a centralized PDP that publishes policy snapshots to local caches, with a fallback to the central service on cache miss. This gives you the low latency of distributed evaluation with the consistency of a centralized source of truth.

### Allow-Default vs. Deny-Default

**Deny-default** is the security industry standard. If no policy explicitly grants access, the request is denied. This is safe because a misconfiguration tends to break functionality (visible) rather than expose data (invisible). The downside: you get false positives during rollout, and your engineers will complain about mysterious 403 errors. Every new resource type requires an explicit policy before it can be accessed.

**Allow-default** is sometimes used in internal tooling or low-risk environments. Everything is permitted unless explicitly forbidden. This is convenient for development velocity but catastrophically dangerous — an unconfigured resource is an open door. A single omission in policy configuration can expose sensitive data to every user.

The mathematics of safety:

- Deny-default: an error in policy configuration causes a denial of service (deny), which is visible and immediately reported.
- Allow-default: an error in policy configuration causes a security breach (allow), which is invisible and may go undetected for months.

Always choose deny-default in production. If you need to use allow-default, isolate it to a non-critical path and enforce strict auditing.

### Policy as Code vs. Admin UI

**Policy as Code** means your authorization rules live in a version-controlled repository, go through code review, and deploy through CI/CD. This gives you audit trails, rollbacks, and testing. The trade-off is velocity — changing a permission requires a pull request and a deployment. For a critical access change that needs to happen in minutes, this is too slow.

**Admin UI** means operators can change permissions through a dashboard. This is fast and empowers non-engineers, but it introduces drift between declared policy and actual policy. You lose the audit trail, and a mistaken click can grant global admin access. There is no easy way to answer: "what was the policy last Tuesday at 3 PM?"

The pragmatic approach: use Policy as Code for production policies and an Admin UI with four-eyes approval for emergency changes. Log every UI change and reconcile it back into your policy repository. Run a periodic diff between the UI-deployed policies and the code-declared policies, and flag any divergence.

### Caching vs. Fresh Evaluation

**Aggressive caching** with long TTLs makes authorization fast — you serve decisions from memory without touching the policy engine. But a cached allow decision can persist after a policy change revokes access, creating a window of vulnerability.

**Fresh evaluation** guarantees correctness but adds latency. Every request hits the policy engine, which may need to fetch user attributes, resource metadata, and environmental context.

The standard pattern is a **two-tier cache**: a local in-memory cache (fast, millisecond access) backed by a distributed cache (Redis, for example) with a TTL of 30-60 seconds. This gives you sub-millisecond P99 latency for most requests while limiting the stale window to one minute.

A more sophisticated approach uses **cache warming**: when a policy changes, you proactively invalidate affected cache entries and pre-compute the new decisions. This requires a mechanism to map policy changes to affected users and resources, which is non-trivial for ABAC systems where policies reference attributes rather than specific identities.

### Coarse vs. Fine-Grained

**Coarse-grained** authorization operates at the resource level: "can user X read document Y?" There is no concept of fields within a document. This is simple to implement, fast to evaluate, and easy to cache. The authorization check is a single lookup: does the user have the "read" permission on this document?

**Fine-grained** authorization extends to the field level: "can user X read the salary field in document Y?" This requires attribute-based evaluation, more complex data structures, and more expensive queries. It is rarely needed outside of regulated industries. The authorization check requires evaluating the user's attributes against the field-level policy, which may differ for each field in the same document.

Fine-grained authorization is a leading indicator of over-engineering. If you are not in healthcare, finance, or a government-regulated domain, start coarse and add fine-grained controls only when an audit or compliance requirement forces you to. Even in regulated domains, consider whether row-level or resource-level access control is sufficient for 95 percent of your use cases.

### Policy Engine: Embedded vs. External

An **embedded policy engine** runs as a library inside your application process. The Open Policy Agent (OPA) Go library, for example, can be linked into your Go service and evaluate Rego policies directly. This eliminates the network hop and reduces latency to microseconds. The trade-off is that you are tied to the engine's language and version, and updating the engine requires a service deployment.

An **external policy engine** runs as a separate service or sidecar. This is language-agnostic, independently deployable, and can be shared across multiple services. The trade-off is the network round trip and the operational cost of running another service.

### Data Store: Relational vs. Purpose-Built

A **relational database** (PostgreSQL, MySQL) is a natural choice for RBAC. Roles, users, and permissions map naturally to tables with foreign keys, and joins are efficient. But for relationship-based models (ReBAC), graph traversals like "find all documents that user X can access through group membership" require recursive CTEs or multiple queries, which are slow at scale.

**Purpose-built stores** like Google's Zanzibar or open-source alternatives (SpiceDB, OPA's bundle server) are optimized for authorization workloads. They support efficient graph traversal, incremental updates, and snapshot-based consistency. The trade-off is operational complexity — you are running a specialized database that your team must learn and maintain.

## CAP and Policy Consistency

The CAP theorem applies to authorization in a subtle way. When your system is partitioned (a network failure between the policy engine and the policy database), you must choose between **consistency** (deny every request because you cannot verify the latest policy) and **availability** (serve cached or default decisions).

Your choice depends on your domain:

- **Financial systems**: choose consistency. A stale allow decision that authorizes a double withdrawal is worse than a false deny that a human must override. The cost of a false allow is measured in dollars; the cost of a false deny is measured in inconvenience.
- **Social media**: choose availability. A stale deny that prevents a user from seeing a post is worse than a stale allow that shows content they should not see. The cost of a false deny is user engagement; the cost of a false allow is a minor privacy leak.
- **Healthcare**: choose consistency with a graceful degradation path. Serve stale denies (safe) but never stale allows. A patient's medical record exposed to the wrong doctor is a HIPAA violation; a doctor temporarily unable to access a record is a delay in care.

The most sophisticated approach is **session-aware consistency**: within a single user session, maintain the same policy version. This prevents the confusing behavior where a policy change mid-session causes a user to see a page partially loaded with some resources accessible and others denied. Under this model, the policy version is pinned at the start of the session, and all subsequent authorization checks within that session use the same version. If the session outlives the policy version's validity, the user is forced to re-authenticate.

### How CAP Manifests in Distributed Architectures

In a microservice architecture, authorization consistency is even more complex. Consider a workflow where Service A checks authorization and then calls Service B. If Service A uses a newer policy version than Service B, the two services may disagree on whether the user is authorized for the overall operation. This is a form of **distributed authorization skew**.

To mitigate this, you can use a **global policy version number** that is propagated through request headers. Every service in the call chain uses the same policy version, ensuring consistent evaluation. This version number is also included in audit logs, making it possible to trace the exact policy state for any given request.

## Failure Modes

Authorization systems fail in specific, recurring ways. You need to know each one, how to detect it, and how to mitigate it.

### 1. Policy Evaluation Timeout

When your policy engine takes too long to evaluate a request, the caller times out. The default behavior is to deny the request — which is safe, but if the timeout is caused by a systemic issue (policy database overload, slow attribute resolution), every request fails and your service becomes effectively unavailable.

This failure mode is particularly dangerous because it is self-reinforcing. When the PDP is slow, clients retry, which increases load on the PDP, which makes it slower, which causes more retries. This is a classic thundering herd problem.

**Mitigation**: Set a hard timeout on every policy evaluation. Use a circuit breaker that trips after N consecutive timeouts and falls back to a cached policy snapshot. The circuit breaker should have a half-open state that periodically allows a request through to test whether the PDP has recovered. Log the timeout event with enough context (user, resource, action, policy version) to debug the policy that caused it.

### 2. Stale Cache Serving Old Allow Decisions

This is the most common authorization failure in production. A user's access is revoked, but the cached "allow" decision persists for the TTL duration. During that window, the user can access resources they should no longer have.

The exposure window is directly proportional to the cache TTL. A 60-second TTL gives a 60-second window where a revoked user can still access data. A 5-minute TTL gives a 5-minute window. The longer the TTL, the more performance you gain, but the more risk you accept.

**Mitigation**: Use short TTLs for allow decisions (5-10 seconds) and longer TTLs for deny decisions (minutes to hours). Subscribe to policy change events from your policy store and invalidate affected cache entries. For high-security environments, use a write-through cache that invalidates on policy change. This means the cache is updated synchronously when the policy changes, guaranteeing that no stale decisions are served.

Role explosion happens when you use roles to encode every dimension of access control. You start with Admin, Editor, and Viewer. Then you add regional variants (Admin-EU, Admin-US). Then resource-type variants (Admin-Documents, Admin-Settings, Admin-Users). Then status variants (Admin-Pending, Admin-Active). Before long you have hundreds of roles, and no one can tell you what each one actually does.

The problem is compounded by **role inheritance**. If Admin-EU inherits from Admin, and Admin-US inherits from Admin, and Admin inherits from Viewer, then a change to Viewer's permissions affects all derived roles. Understanding the effective permissions of a single role requires traversing the entire inheritance tree.

**Mitigation**: Use RBAC for the base structure and ABAC conditions for the dimensions. Instead of creating "Editor-EU-Documents" as a role, create a single "Editor" role with a condition that restricts access to documents where region equals the user's assigned region. This keeps your role count manageable and your policy logic explicit. If you use a system that supports role attributes, leverage those instead of creating new roles.

When your policy engine resolves attributes by calling other services, a single authorization check can fan out across your entire system. A policy like "allow if user's manager's direct reports include the resource owner" might require three service calls: one for the user's manager, one for the manager's direct reports, and one for the resource owner's identity.

Under normal load, this adds 200-300 milliseconds of latency. Under load, each service slows down, the cascading calls accumulate, and the authorization layer becomes the bottleneck that brings down the whole system. This is especially dangerous because the cascading calls are hidden inside the authorization check — your application code sees only a slow response, not the underlying fan-out.

**Mitigation**: Batch attribute resolution into a single request. If your policy needs three attributes, fetch them in one call rather than three. Pre-fetch attributes in parallel, not in series. Use a local attribute cache so that repeated evaluations for the same user or resource avoid network calls. For complex policies, pre-compute the decision and store it as a materialized permission that is updated asynchronously when the underlying attributes change.

### 5. Policy Drift Between Environments

Your authorization policies are tested in staging, reviewed in code review, and deployed to production. But production data is different, production roles are different, and production configurations are different. A policy that works perfectly in staging silently fails in production because a user attribute that the policy depends on does not exist or has a different format.

Concrete example: you write a policy that allows access to documents where `user.region == resource.region`. In staging, all users have a `region` attribute set to a string like "US-EAST-1". In production, one team uses "us-east-1" (lowercase) and another uses "us-east-1a" (with a zone suffix). The policy fails for users in those teams, but the failure is silent — they simply cannot access the documents, and they may not know why.

**Mitigation**: Replay production authorization decisions against your staging environment. This means capturing authorization logs from production and replaying them through your staging policy engine to verify that the decisions match. Run canary deployments of policy changes — route a small percentage of traffic to the new policy version and compare the decisions against the old version. Use synthetic monitoring that tests critical authorization paths from the perspective of real user personas, not just test accounts.

### 6. Zombie Permissions

When a user changes roles, leaves a team, or leaves the company, their old permissions can persist in cached decisions, inherited role memberships, or stale group memberships. These zombie permissions are a common source of data breaches.

The problem is insidious because the permissions are not actively granted — they are leftovers from a previous state. A user who moves from Engineering to Sales still has access to the Engineering team's repository because the group membership was never updated. A contractor whose contract ended still has a valid session token from a long TTL.

**Mitigation**: Implement a session-based permission model where permissions are re-evaluated at the start of each session. Use a mandatory re-authorization interval for sensitive operations — every 15 minutes, re-check the user's permissions against the current policy state. Run periodic audits that compare current permissions against current role assignments. Automate the de-provisioning process: when a user changes teams or leaves, trigger a revocation event that invalidates all cached permissions.

### 7. Policy Evaluation Under Load

When your system is under high load, authorization becomes slower for two reasons: the policy engine is competing for CPU with other services, and the attribute resolution services are also under load. This creates a feedback loop where high load causes slow authorization, which causes timeouts, which cause retries, which increase load.

This is difficult to reproduce in testing because it only manifests at production scale. A policy that evaluates in 2ms under normal load can take 500ms when the CPU is saturated.

**Mitigation**: Monitor authorization latency as a first-class metric, separate from application latency. Set up a dashboard that shows P50, P95, and P99 authorization latency, and alert when it exceeds thresholds. Use latency-sensitive load shedding: if the authorization path is taking too long, reject new requests rather than queueing them. Pre-compute authorization decisions for high-traffic resources during off-peak hours.

### 8. Policy Validation Gaps

A policy with a syntax error or a logical contradiction can pass validation and deploy to production. The policy engine may silently ignore the invalid rule, defaulting to deny for all requests that match it. This creates a hard-to-diagnose outage where some users cannot access resources and the error message is a generic 403.

**Mitigation**: Use a policy testing framework that runs against a comprehensive set of test cases. Each test case should specify a user, resource, action, and expected decision. Run these tests as part of your CI/CD pipeline. Include negative tests — cases that should be denied — to catch policies that are too permissive. Use property-based testing to discover edge cases you did not think of.

## Anti-Patterns

These are patterns that look like good ideas but inevitably cause pain. Recognize them, name them, and avoid them.

### 1. Hardcoding Permissions in Application Code

```python
# What you should NOT do
def delete_document(user, document_id):
    if user.role != "admin":
        raise PermissionDenied()
    # ... delete logic
```

This looks simple and works fine for a two-person team. But as your system grows, permissions become scattered across code paths, impossible to audit, and tied to deployment cycles. Changing a permission requires a code change, a pull request, and a deployment. There is no central place to answer "what permissions does a user with admin role have?" — you must grep through the entire codebase.

The deeper problem is that permissions become intertwined with business logic. The `delete_document` function now has two responsibilities: checking authorization and deleting the document. These responsibilities should be separate, so that each can be tested and changed independently.

**Instead**: Use a centralized policy engine. Your application code asks "can user perform action on resource?" and the policy engine answers. The policy is declared in a single location, versioned, and auditable.

```python
# What you SHOULD do
def delete_document(user, document_id):
    authorize(user, "delete", "document", document_id)
    # ... delete logic
```

### 2. No Versioning of Policies

When you update a policy, every service consuming it immediately gets the new behavior. If the new policy has a bug — a missing condition, an incorrect attribute reference — every service fails simultaneously. There is no rollback, no canary, no gradual rollout.

This is the authorization equivalent of deploying to production without a staging environment. You are gambling that your policy change is correct, and if it is not, you have no safety net.

**Fix**: Version your policies. Include the policy version in authorization logs. Use a routing mechanism that allows you to send a percentage of traffic to a new policy version. When you detect errors, route traffic back to the previous version. This is the same pattern you use for feature flags, applied to authorization.

### 3. Mixing Authentication and Authorization

```python
# What you should NOT do
def authenticate_and_authorize(token, resource):
    user = decode_token(token)
    if user.is_admin:
        return (user, True)
    if user has access to resource:
        return (user, True)
    return (None, False)
```

This function conflates two concerns. Authentication verifies identity. Authorization verifies permission. When you mix them, you cannot change one without risking the other. You cannot use different authentication providers without rewriting authorization logic. You cannot grant temporary authorization without affecting authentication state.

The function above also has a subtle bug: the admin check happens before the specific resource check. This means an admin user who no longer has access to a specific resource is still granted access through the admin shortcut. If the admin role is revoked but the user's session is still valid, they retain access.

**Fix**: Keep authentication and authorization as separate layers. Authentication produces a verified identity. Authorization consumes that identity and evaluates it against policies. The two layers communicate through a well-defined interface (the identity object) and nothing else.

```python
# What you SHOULD do
def authenticate(token):
    return decode_token(token)  # Returns identity

def authorize(identity, action, resource_type, resource_id):
    policy = load_policy(resource_type, action)
    return evaluate(policy, identity, resource_id)
```

### 4. Permissive Defaults for New Resources

When a new resource type is added to the system, the authorization policy for it is often missing. A common anti-pattern is to default to "allow" for resources without explicit policies. This is convenient during development but catastrophic in production.

The reasoning is usually: "we are adding this feature, and we want it to work for everyone initially. We will add fine-grained access controls later." But "later" never comes, and the permissive default becomes permanent. Years later, a security audit reveals that the new resource type has no access controls at all.

**Fix**: Enforce deny-default at the infrastructure level. Your policy engine should return deny for any policy query that has no matching rule. If a new resource type needs access, it must have an explicit policy — and that policy must go through the same review and deployment process as any other code change. This forces the team to think about authorization from day one, rather than treating it as an afterthought.

### 5. Using Groups as a Catch-All

Groups are a convenient abstraction for organizing users, but they are often misused as a substitute for proper policy design. You end up with groups like "can-delete-documents-and-view-reports" and "can-view-reports-and-edit-settings." These are just roles with extra steps, and they explode in number just like roles do.

The problem is that groups serve two purposes: organizational structure (who reports to whom) and permission assignment (who can access what). When you use groups for both, you create a tangled dependency where changing a team's organizational structure changes their permissions, and changing permissions requires restructuring the team.

**Fix**: Use groups for organizational structure (team membership, reporting lines) and roles for permissions. A user's permissions are the intersection of their group memberships and their role assignments, evaluated against explicit policies. This separation of concerns makes both dimensions independently modifiable.

### 6. Ignoring the Audit Trail

The most dangerous anti-pattern is not logging authorization decisions. When an incident happens — a data breach, an unauthorized access, a privilege escalation — you need to know what happened, when, and under which policy version. Without logs, you are blind.

The common objection is: "logging every authorization decision will generate too much data." This is true — at scale, you may generate millions of authorization logs per minute. But the solution is not to skip logging; it is to design a log pipeline that can handle the volume. Use structured logging, sample at a configurable rate, store logs in a scalable datastore, and set up retention policies.

**Fix**: Log every authorization decision: the user, the resource, the action, the policy version, the decision (allow or deny), and the evaluation time. Store these logs in an append-only store that cannot be tampered with. Run periodic audits that check for anomalies — unexpected allow decisions, patterns of denied access, policy evaluations that took longer than expected.

### 7. Over-Engineering Before You Need It

This is the meta-anti-pattern: designing a full ABAC system with attribute resolvers, policy engines, and distributed evaluation before you have a single customer. The system ends up being slow, complex, and hard to maintain, and it solves problems you do not have.

**Fix**: Start with the simplest possible authorization that meets your current needs. For most startups, this is a role check in middleware. When you have evidence that the simple approach is insufficient — when you find yourself writing workarounds or the role count is growing — then invest in a more sophisticated system. This is the YAGNI principle applied to authorization.

## When NOT to Use a Centralized Authorization System

Authorization systems are powerful, but they are not always the right tool. Here are situations where the overhead of a formal authorization system outweighs the benefits.

### Single-User or Personal Tools

If you are building a CLI tool, a personal script, or a single-user application, adding a policy engine is over-engineering. A simple `if user == owner:` check in the code is sufficient. The overhead of maintaining a policy store, a PDP, and a policy language is not justified. The authorization logic is a single line of code that will never change, and the consequences of a bug are limited to one user.

### Prototypes and MVPs

In the early stages of a product, you are iterating on functionality, not security architecture. Use a simple middleware that checks a hardcoded list of admin users. As the product matures and the team grows, you can introduce a formal authorization system. Trying to design the perfect authorization model before you understand the product is a recipe for wasted effort.

The key question: are you still figuring out what your product does? If yes, your authorization system should be the simplest thing that works. You can always replace it later — and you will have a much better understanding of the requirements when you do.

### Static Content

If your application serves static content (documentation, marketing pages, public API specifications), authorization is a non-concern. The content is public by design. Adding authorization adds latency and complexity with zero security benefit. Every millisecond you spend checking authorization for a public page is a millisecond your users wait for no reason.

### Trusted Internal Networks with No Multi-Tenancy

If your system runs on a fully trusted internal network, has no external users, and has no multi-tenancy, a simple VPN and network-level access control may be sufficient. Adding application-level authorization on top of network-level controls creates maintenance overhead without meaningful security improvement.

The caveat: "trusted internal network" is an increasingly rare environment. With the rise of zero-trust architectures, most organizations are moving toward explicit authorization at every layer. If your network is not truly trusted, you need authorization regardless of the network topology.

### Read-Only Datastores

If you are building a read-only data pipeline where all data is public or all consumers have the same access level, authorization adds unnecessary complexity. The data is either all accessible or all inaccessible. Complex policy evaluation is wasted.

### Homogeneous Access Patterns

If every user in your system has the same permissions — for example, a corporate wiki where all employees can read and write all pages — then authorization is a binary yes/no. You do not need a policy engine; you just need to verify that the user is authenticated. The cost of a policy engine is not justified for binary access.

### Short-Lived Services with No User Context

If you are building a batch processing job, a cron worker, or a queue consumer that does not operate on behalf of any user, authorization is often unnecessary. The service runs with its own service account and has a fixed set of permissions that are configured at deployment time. Adding a policy engine to this flow adds latency and complexity without meaningful benefit.

The exception is if the service processes data on behalf of multiple tenants. In that case, even though there is no interactive user, you still need to enforce tenant isolation through authorization.

## Testing Authorization

Authorization logic is notoriously difficult to test. The combinatorial nature of policies means that the number of possible test cases grows exponentially with the number of attributes. Here is a practical approach.

### Unit Testing Policies

Each policy should be tested in isolation. For a policy that says "allow access if user.role is admin OR (user.department equals resource.department AND resource.visibility is public)", you need at minimum these test cases:

1. Admin user, any resource → allow
2. Non-admin user, same department, public resource → allow
3. Non-admin user, same department, private resource → deny
4. Non-admin user, different department, public resource → deny
5. Non-admin user, different department, private resource → deny
6. Non-admin user, resource with missing visibility attribute → deny

Write these tests in a declarative format that is easy to read and maintain. Each test case should specify the inputs (user attributes, resource attributes, action) and the expected output (allow or deny).

### Integration Testing with Real Data

Unit tests verify that the policy logic is correct. Integration tests verify that the policy engine works correctly with real data sources. You need to test:

- That the attribute resolver correctly fetches all required attributes
- That the policy engine correctly parses the policy file
- That the cache is populated and invalidated correctly
- That the default deny behavior works when no policy matches

Run integration tests against a staging environment that mirrors production data schemas. This catches the most common class of authorization bugs: mismatched attribute names and types.

### Regression Testing with Production Logs

The most powerful testing technique is to replay production authorization logs against your policy engine. This works as follows:

1. Capture all authorization decisions in production along with the input attributes.
2. Build a test suite from the captured data: input → expected decision.
3. Run this suite against your policy engine after every change.
4. Flag any decision that differs from the recorded production decision.

This catches regressions that unit tests and integration tests miss. It also helps you discover the actual distribution of authorization patterns in production — which policies are evaluated most often, which attributes are most commonly queried, and which edge cases occur in practice.

### Property-Based Testing

Beyond concrete test cases, use property-based testing to discover edge cases. Define properties that your authorization system must satisfy:

- **Determinism**: the same inputs always produce the same output (unless policy changed)
- **Monotonicity of deny**: adding a deny rule never creates an allow decision
- **Monotonicity of allow**: adding an allow rule never creates a deny decision
- **Idempotency**: evaluating the same policy twice returns the same result

These properties can be tested with randomly generated inputs, which often discover edge cases that manual testing misses.

## Measuring Authorization in Production

Once your authorization system is deployed, you need to measure its behavior. Here are the metrics you should track.

### Latency Metrics

- **P50 authorization latency**: typical performance. Should be under 5ms for cached decisions.
- **P95 authorization latency**: the tail. Should be under 20ms.
- **P99 authorization latency**: the problem cases. Should be under 100ms.
- **Max authorization latency**: the outlier. Anything over 1 second should be investigated.

Track these per policy, per resource type, and per service. A policy that is fast for most resources may be slow for resources with many attributes or complex relationships.

### Cache Metrics

- **Cache hit ratio**: the percentage of authorization decisions served from cache. Should be above 95 percent for a well-designed system.
- **Cache miss rate**: the percentage of decisions that required a full policy evaluation. A rising miss rate indicates that the cache is not effective.
- **Stale decision rate**: the number of decisions served from cache that were later invalidated by a policy change. This should be zero in an ideal system.
- **Cache invalidation latency**: the time between a policy change and the corresponding cache invalidation. Should be under one second.

### Policy Metrics

- **Number of active policies**: should grow slowly over time. A sudden increase may indicate role explosion.
- **Policy evaluation complexity**: the number of conditions evaluated per request. A policy with more than 10 conditions is likely to be slow.
- **Attribute resolution count**: the number of external service calls per authorization check. This should be minimized.
- **Policy change frequency**: how often policies are updated. A high frequency may indicate instability or over-engineering.

### Error Metrics

- **Authorization timeout rate**: the percentage of authorization checks that time out. Should be under 0.01 percent.
- **Authorization error rate**: the percentage of checks that return an error (not timeout, not deny — actual errors). Should be under 0.001 percent.
- **Unexpected deny rate**: a sudden increase in denies may indicate a policy bug or a configuration error.
- **Unexpected allow rate**: a sudden increase in allows may indicate a security breach or a permissive policy change.
