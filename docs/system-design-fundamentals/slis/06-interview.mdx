SLI-related questions appear in system design interviews at all levels, from L3 to L7. This section covers how the topic surfaces, what interviewers look for at each level, a structured framework for answering, the vocabulary you should use, and the mistakes to avoid.

## How the Topic Appears

SLI questions rarely come as "design an SLI system." Instead, they emerge naturally within broader system design discussions. Here are the common phrasings that signal you should discuss SLIs.

1. "How would you measure whether this system is working correctly for users?"
2. "We are building a monitoring system for our microservice architecture. What metrics would you track?"
3. "The team is complaining about too many alerts. How would you improve the alerting system?"
4. "How do you decide when to deploy new features versus work on reliability?"
5. "Our customers are complaining about slow response times, but our dashboards show everything is green. What went wrong?"
6. "Design a reliability dashboard for a global video streaming service. What goes on it?"
7. "How would you set up a system to detect degradation before users notice it?"
8. "We have a 99.9 percent SLA with our customers. How do we ensure we are meeting it?"
9. "The on-call team is getting paged for every minor issue. How would you reduce alert fatigue while maintaining coverage?"
10. "Design the observability platform for a ride-sharing service with 50 million users."

Each of these questions creates an opening to discuss SLIs, SLOs, error budgets, and burn rate alerting. The interviewer wants to hear you mention these concepts unprompted.

## What the Interviewer Tests

When you bring up SLIs in an interview, the interviewer evaluates five dimensions of your understanding.

**Breadth**: Do you know the full SLI ecosystem — SLIs, SLOs, SLAs, error budgets, burn rates? Or do you only know one concept? The interviewer wants to see that you can connect the dots between measurement (SLI), target (SLO), contract (SLA), and operational decision (error budget). A candidate who only mentions SLIs without connecting them to error budgets is at a disadvantage.

**Practical experience**: Can you describe how you have used SLIs in a real system? Specific numbers matter. "We set the SLO to 99.9 percent over 30 days" is better than "we had SLIs." Even if you have not used SLIs in production, you should be able to describe a hypothetical design with concrete numbers. Interviewers can tell the difference between a candidate who has done this work and one who has only read about it.

**Tradeoff awareness**: Do you understand when SLIs are the right tool and when they are not? Can you discuss the precision vs. simplicity tradeoff? The strongest candidates proactively mention a tradeoff they encountered. "We originally used p999 for all latency SLIs, but the noise was overwhelming. We moved to p99 for general SLIs and kept p999 only for the most critical endpoints."

**Integration knowledge**: Do you know how SLI systems integrate with existing infrastructure — databases, caches, queues, CDNs? Or do you only know the theory? The interviewer wants to see that you understand the practical challenges of collecting SLI data from a real system. "We integrated with the existing Kafka pipeline by adding a new topic for metrics. Each service published to this topic every second, and the aggregator consumed from it."

**Operational maturity**: Do you understand how SLIs drive operational decisions — when to deploy, when to freeze, how to budget error budget? This is the most important dimension at senior levels and above. "When the error budget was 50 percent consumed with half the month remaining, we froze deployments and focused on reliability. This was a clear, data-driven decision that the whole team understood."

## Level-by-Level Expectations

The depth of SLI knowledge expected increases with the interview level.

### L3 / Entry Level

At the entry level, you are expected to know the basic definition of an SLI and the difference between an SLI, SLO, and SLA. You should be able to explain why SLIs matter.

**What to say**: "An SLI is a measurement of service performance, like request latency or error rate. An SLO is the target for that measurement. An SLA is the contractual commitment to customers. SLIs are important because they tell you whether the system is working from the user's perspective, not just from the server's perspective."

**What not to say**: Do not claim production experience with SLIs if you do not have it. State your understanding clearly and honestly.

### L4 / Mid Level

At the mid level, you are expected to explain how you would design SLIs for a specific service. You should discuss which SLI types to use, how to set SLOs, and how to measure them.

**What to say**: "For a web API service, I would define three SLIs. First, availability: the proportion of requests returning HTTP 2xx. Second, latency: the p99 response time. Third, throughput: requests per second. I would set the SLO based on what users need — if this is a user-facing API, 99.9 percent availability with p99 under 200 milliseconds over a 30-day window."

**What not to say**: Do not give generic answers. Apply the SLI choice to the specific service the interviewer described.

### L5 / Senior Level

At the senior level, you are expected to design the full SLI pipeline — instrumentation, collection, aggregation, and evaluation. You should discuss data structures (HdrHistogram, sliding windows), consistency considerations, and failure modes.

**What to say**: "The SLI pipeline has four layers. The instrumentation layer captures per-request metrics and batches them into 1-second aggregates. The collection layer validates and partitions the data by service. The aggregation layer maintains a sliding window of good/total counts and a histogram for latency percentiles. The evaluation layer compares the aggregated SLI against the SLO and computes burn rates. The key challenge is the sliding window — you need to update it efficiently without blocking collection."

**What not to say**: Do not gloss over the hard parts. Discuss the sliding window implementation, the HdrHistogram structure, and how you handle network partitions.

**Depth area: Discussing tradeoffs at the L5 level.** A senior-level answer should explicitly call out at least two tradeoffs. For example: "The precision of the latency histogram must be balanced against memory usage. A 4-significant-digit histogram gives 0.1 percent precision at the p99, but uses 1 MB per service. With 500 services, that is 500 MB of RAM just for histograms. I would use 3 significant digits (256 KB each) for most services and reserve 4 significant digits for the most latency-critical services."

Another tradeoff to discuss: "The SLO window duration affects both detection speed and stability. A 7-day window detects degradation faster but is noisy. A 90-day window is stable but slow to react. I would use a 30-day window as the default, with a 7-day warning window that triggers a non-critical alert if the SLI drops below a secondary threshold."

### L6 / Staff Level

At the staff level, you are expected to discuss the organizational impact of SLIs. How do you drive adoption across multiple teams? How do you balance velocity and reliability? How do you handle teams that resist SLOs?

**What to say**: "Adopting SLIs across the organization requires cultural change, not just technical change. I would start with a pilot team, help them define 3 SLIs, set up the pipeline, and demonstrate the value. Once the pilot shows results, I would provide tooling and templates so other teams can onboard themselves. The hardest part is getting teams to set SLOs that are realistic — not too aggressive, not too lenient. I would establish an SLO review board with representatives from each team."

**What not to say**: Do not propose a top-down mandate approach. Staff-level engineers understand that change requires buy-in, not command-and-control.

**Depth area: Organizational scaling at the L6 level.** A staff-level answer should include concrete details about how you would scale SLI adoption. For example: "I would create a centralized SLI platform team that owns the infrastructure — the collector, aggregator, and evaluator — and provides self-service SLI creation for product teams. The platform team handles the hard infrastructure problems (scaling, reliability, data retention) while product teams define their own SLIs and SLOs using a simple YAML configuration file. This separation of concerns allows each team to move at its own pace while benefiting from shared infrastructure.

"The key organizational challenge is preventing SLO fatigue. If every service has 10 SLIs, no one pays attention to any of them. I would enforce a limit of 5 SLIs per service and require teams to justify each SLI in a quarterly review. SLIs that are always green or always red are candidates for removal."

**Depth area: Handling SLO violations at scale.** "When a team misses its SLO, the response should be systematic, not punitive. The team conducts a blameless postmortem focused on the system, not the people. The postmortem identifies the root cause and produces an action plan to prevent recurrence. The SLO violation is tracked in a database that the SLO review board uses to identify systemic reliability issues across the organization. If 5 different teams missed their SLOs because of the same underlying database issue, the board can escalate the database issue as an organizational priority."

### L7 / Principal Level

At the principal level, you are expected to discuss the industry-wide evolution of SLI practices and how your contributions advanced the state of the art. You should have specific, quantitative results from your experience.

**What to say**: "At my previous company, I led the migration from threshold-based alerting to burn rate alerting across 500 services. We reduced false positive alerts by 80 percent while improving mean time to detection for real failures by 60 percent. The key insight was that burn rate thresholds needed to be calibrated per-service based on traffic volume — a one-size-fits-all threshold caused alert fatigue for high-traffic services and missed failures for low-traffic services."

**What not to do**: Do not give hypothetical answers. Principal-level engineers are expected to describe their actual contributions and results.

### Level Expectations Summary Table

| Level | Expected SLI Knowledge | Key Differentiator |
|-------|----------------------|-------------------|
| L3 | Basic SLI/SLO/SLA definitions, why SLIs matter | Awareness of the concept |
| L4 | Design SLIs for a specific service, set SLO targets | Application to a concrete system |
| L5 | Full pipeline design, data structures, failure modes | Implementation depth |
| L6 | Organizational adoption, cross-team standardization | Cultural and process understanding |
| L7 | Industry evolution, quantitative results, future directions | Track record of impact |

## FAANG Framework for SLI Questions

Use this framework when an interviewer asks about SLIs, monitoring, or reliability measurement. It ensures you cover all the essential dimensions.

### F — Formulate the User Journey

Start by identifying the critical user journey. Do not jump into metrics before establishing what matters to users.

"For a ride-sharing service, the critical user journey is: a rider requests a ride, a driver accepts, and the rider is picked up. The SLIs should measure this journey, not individual service health."

### A — Align SLIs to the Journey

Define 2 to 4 SLIs that measure the journey from the user's perspective.

"I would define three SLIs: ride request success rate, ride request latency, and driver acceptance time. Each of these maps to a step in the user journey."

### A — Aggregate with SLOs

Set SLO targets with appropriate windows. Explain why you chose the specific numbers.

"Ride request success rate should have an SLO of 99.95 percent over 30 days because this is the most critical user action. Latency should have an SLO of p99 under 2 seconds because users expect a quick response when requesting a ride in a hurry."

### N — Navigate Tradeoffs

Discuss the tradeoffs in your design. Show that you understand when SLIs are not the right choice.

"Hundred percent availability SLOs are not realistic. For the ride-matching engine, a 99.95 percent SLO gives us 21.6 minutes of error budget per month. That is tight enough to ensure reliability but realistic enough that the team can still deploy new features regularly."

### G — Govern with Error Budgets

Show how error budgets drive operational decisions. This demonstrates operational maturity.

"We would use the error budget to decide when to freeze deployments. If the error budget is more than 50 percent consumed halfway through the month, we freeze non-critical deployments and focus on reliability. If the budget is less than 25 percent consumed, teams can deploy freely."

### How to Use the Framework Under Time Pressure

In an interview, you have roughly 3 minutes to give a coherent SLI answer. The FAANG framework helps you stay organized, but you must move through it efficiently. Here is a time-budgeted approach:

- **0:00-0:30** — Formulate the user journey. Keep it to one sentence. "The critical journey is X."
- **0:30-1:30** — Align SLIs to the journey and aggregate with SLOs. Name 2 to 4 SLIs with specific numbers. "Availability SLI targeting 99.9 percent over 30 days."
- **1:30-2:00** — Navigate tradeoffs. Mention one or two key tradeoffs. "The precision tradeoff here is X."
- **2:00-2:30** — Govern with error budgets. Explain how you would use error budgets to make decisions.
- **2:30-3:00** — Handle the follow-up. The interviewer will ask a question about a specific aspect of your answer. Use the remaining time to dive deeper.

Practice with a timer. Most engineers speak too slowly in their first attempt and cover only half the framework. Aim for a pace that feels slightly rushed — it will sound natural to the interviewer.

## Phrase Bank

Use these phrases to demonstrate depth of SLI knowledge during interviews. Each phrase signals a specific area of expertise.

1. "The key distinction is that an SLI measures the user experience, not the server health."
2. "We use a 30-day rolling window because it smooths out daily traffic patterns while still providing meaningful signal."
3. "Burn rate alerts are superior to threshold alerts because they factor in the SLO and the time window."
4. "The error budget is the currency of reliability — it tells you how much failure you can tolerate before users are affected."
5. "A burn rate of 2 over 1 hour means we are consuming error budget twice as fast as sustainable."
6. "We set the SLO tighter than the SLA to create a buffer between internal operations and contractual obligations."
7. "For latency SLIs, we use HdrHistogram for precise percentile calculations without storing every request."
8. "The denominator matters as much as the numerator — excluding health checks from the total count prevents denominator pollution."
9. "Three to five SLIs per service is the sweet spot. Fewer misses failure modes, more creates alert fatigue."
10. "We run SLIs in shadow mode for 30 days before setting SLOs to understand the natural behavior of the metric."
11. "The precision vs. simplicity tradeoff means a p80 SLI is cheaper and more stable but less sensitive to tail latency."
12. "Composite SLIs should use the minimum of component SLIs, not the average, because the user experience is only as good as the worst component."
13. "SLI data without an SLO is just a metric — you need the target to make decisions."
14. "Alert on burn rate, not on SLI value. A falling SLI is not actionable unless you know how fast the error budget is being consumed."
15. "The cardinality of SLI dimensions matters — segmenting by region, device, and user tier creates exponential growth in data volume."
16. "Cross-region SLIs need separate baselines because geographic latency differences are inherent, not a failure."
17. "Instrument everything, SLI a subset — collect data from all endpoints but only set SLOs for critical user journeys."
18. "The SLO review process should require independent approval to prevent laddering."
19. "A quality SLI is harder to measure than availability or latency because it requires knowing the correct answer."
20. "In a multi-service architecture, each service should have its own SLIs with SLOs that cascade up to the user-facing composite SLI."
21. "The error budget is not just an engineering metric — it is a business decision. How much reliability do we need to trade for velocity?"
22. "Multi-window multi-burn-rate alerting catches both fast catastrophic failures and slow chronic degradation with a single configuration."
23. "For low-traffic services, use time-window SLIs instead of percentile-based SLIs to avoid noise from small sample sizes."
24. "The 30-day window is a convention, not a law. For rapidly changing systems, use a 7-day window. For stable systems, use a 90-day window."
25. "An SLO that is always met is too loose. An SLO that is never met is too tight. The right SLO is met approximately 95 percent of the time."

## Red Flags

These mistakes signal shallow understanding or inexperience with SLIs. Avoid them.

**Red Flag 1: Confusing SLIs with infrastructure metrics.** Saying "CPU utilization is one of our SLIs" shows you do not understand the difference between infrastructure monitoring and user-facing SLIs. CPU is an operational metric, not an SLI.

**Red Flag 2: Not mentioning error budgets.** If you discuss SLIs and SLOs but never mention error budgets, the interviewer knows you have not worked with SLIs in production. Error budgets are why SLIs matter operationally.

**Red Flag 3: Proposing 100 percent SLOs.** A 100 percent SLO reveals that you do not understand the practical limitations of distributed systems. Nothing is 100 percent reliable. Interviewers expect realistic targets.

**Red Flag 4: Ignoring the filtering problem.** If you do not discuss filtering out health checks, synthetic monitoring, and known scrapers from SLI computation, the interviewer knows you have not operated an SLI system. Denominator pollution is a known pitfall.

**Red Flag 5: Suggesting a single SLI for the whole system.** A single SLI cannot capture availability, latency, and quality. Interviewers expect you to decompose the problem into multiple SLIs.

**Red Flag 6: Not discussing the sliding window.** If you propose computing SLIs over a fixed period without explaining the rolling window mechanism, the interviewer knows you have not thought about the implementation details.

**Red Flag 7: Proposing threshold-based alerts instead of burn rate alerts.** Threshold alerts are the old way. Modern SLI systems use burn rate alerts. If you do not mention burn rates, the interviewer knows your knowledge is outdated.

**Red Flag 8: Setting the same SLO for all services.** Critical user-facing services should have tighter SLOs than internal batch processing services. A one-size-fits-all SLO indicates you are not thinking about the service's role and user expectations.

**Red Flag 9: Not mentioning the valid vs. invalid event distinction.** If you treat all traffic equally, including health checks and synthetic probes, your SLI is diluted. Interviewers expect you to know that filtering is essential.

**Red Flag 10: Proposing SLIs without explaining how you would measure them.** "We should track latency" is not enough. You need to say what percentile, what time window, and how you would compute it from the raw data. Interviewers want to see that you have thought about the implementation.

**Red Flag 11: Treating SLIs as static.** If you set SLIs once and never revisit them, they will drift away from the user experience. Interviewers expect you to mention quarterly SLI reviews and periodic recalibration.

**Red Flag 12: Ignoring the cost of measurement.** Every additional nine of precision in latency SLIs costs 10x more storage and compute. Interviewers want to see that you consider the cost-benefit tradeoff, not just the ideal metric.

**Red Flag 13: Proposing SLIs for every endpoint.** A service with 50 endpoints should not have 150 SLIs (availability, latency, quality for each). Interviewers expect you to identify the critical endpoints and focus on those, with the remaining endpoints having basic monitoring without SLOs.

**Red Flag 14: Not connecting SLIs to the SRE philosophy.** SLIs are not just metrics — they are part of a reliability management system that includes error budgets, deployment gates, and blameless postmortems. Interviewers want to see that you understand the cultural context, not just the technical implementation.

## Practice Drills

Use these drills to prepare for SLI-related interview questions. Each drill targets a specific aspect of SLI knowledge.

### Drill 1: SLI Definition

Pick a service you have built or used. Define 3 SLIs for it. For each SLI, specify:
- What it measures
- How it is computed (numerator and denominator)
- What the SLO target would be and why
- What the error budget would be over 30 days

Repeat this drill for 5 different services: a web API, a database, a batch processor, a CDN, and a real-time streaming service.

### Drill 2: SLI Pipeline Design

Design the SLI pipeline for a service handling 100,000 requests per second. Sketch:
- The instrumentation layer
- The collection layer (how many nodes, what queue)
- The aggregation layer (how many shards, data structures)
- The evaluation layer (evaluation frequency, alerting rules)
- The storage layer (time-series database, retention)

Compare your design to the production architecture described in this section. Identify the differences and understand why each choice was made.

### Drill 3: Failure Mode Analysis

For each of the 5 failure modes covered in the Tradeoffs section, describe a real scenario where you have seen it happen or could see it happening. For each scenario, describe:
- What metrics would first signal the failure
- How the failure would appear on the dashboard
- What the investigation process would look like
- How you would fix it and prevent recurrence

### Drill 4: Error Budget Calculation

Given a service with the following characteristics:
- 5 million requests per day
- SLO: 99.95 percent availability over 30 days
- Traffic is evenly distributed across the day

Calculate:
- Total requests over 30 days
- Maximum allowed failures over 30 days
- Daily error budget (expected failures per day)
- Time budget in minutes per month

Then, given that the service experienced a 15-minute outage on day 15, calculate:
- Remaining error budget
- Burn rate during the outage
- How much budget is consumed by this incident as a percentage of the total

### Drill 5: Interview Roleplay

Have a partner ask you one of the 10 SLI-related interview questions from the beginning of this section. Practice answering using the FAANG framework. Time yourself to keep the answer under 3 minutes. Afterward, have your partner identify:
- Did you mention SLIs, SLOs, error budgets, and burn rates?
- Did you use concrete numbers?
- Did you discuss tradeoffs?
- Did you avoid the red flags?

Record yourself and review the recording. Identify areas where you could be more specific or more concise.

### Drill 6: FAANG Framework Practice

For each of the 10 interview phrasings listed at the start of this section, write a one-paragraph answer using the FAANG framework. Do not use any notes. The goal is to internalize the framework so you can apply it automatically in an interview.

After writing each answer, check:
- Did you start with the user journey (F)?
- Did you align SLIs to the journey (A)?
- Did you aggregate with SLOs and concrete numbers (A)?
- Did you navigate tradeoffs (N)?
- Did you govern with error budgets (G)?

If you missed any of the five steps for any of the 10 questions, practice that question again.

### Drill 7: SLO Target Justification

Pick a service you know well and define its SLO targets. For each target, write a one-sentence justification that explains why that specific number was chosen.

Examples:
- "99.95 percent because our biggest competitor promises 99.9 percent and we need a margin."
- "p99 under 200 milliseconds because user research shows that 200ms is the threshold where users perceive the system as instant."
- "99.9 percent because our SLA requires 99.9 percent and we set the internal SLO at 99.95 percent for a safety buffer."

Practice justifying targets for three different services. The interviewers will ask "why that number?" — you need a crisp answer.

### Drill 8: Crisis Scenario Simulation

Simulate the following crisis scenario and practice your response:

It is Friday at 5:30 PM. Your service has a 99.9 percent availability SLO over a 30-day window. You check the dashboard and find that the service has consumed 85 percent of its monthly error budget after only 15 days. You have 15 days remaining. Your team was planning to deploy three new features next week.

What do you do? Walk through your reasoning step by step:
1. What is the current burn rate?
2. How many more days of normal operation can you tolerate?
3. What actions do you take with the deployment schedule?
4. How do you communicate this to your team and stakeholders?
5. What investigation do you launch into why the budget is nearly exhausted?

Practice this scenario until you can walk through it in under 2 minutes without notes.
