# Circuit Breaker Concepts

## The Payment Service Meltdown

Imagine a payment service that normally processes 10,000 requests per minute with a 200-millisecond average latency. One afternoon, the database backing the payment service hits a slow checkpoint operation. Response times climb from 200 milliseconds to 30 seconds.

Every caller -- the checkout service, the subscription service, the invoice service -- sends a request and waits. Each service has a thread pool of 200 threads. Within 3 seconds, 200 threads in the checkout service are all blocked on payment calls. New requests arrive at 50 requests per second. They enter an unbounded queue. The queue grows by 50 entries per second. After 10 seconds, the queue holds 500 entries.

Memory consumption climbs. Each queued request holds a 4 KB buffer for the request body. That is 2 MB of queued data, then 4 MB, then 8 MB. Meanwhile, the blocked threads each hold a 256 KB thread stack. 200 threads x 256 KB equals 51 MB just in thread stacks. The heap fills with response objects waiting to be processed.

After 20 seconds, the service hits its 512 MB memory limit. The garbage collector runs constantly, spending 80 percent of CPU time on GC. Throughput drops to near zero. The OOM killer terminates the process.

The checkout service is now down. The order service depends on checkout. The order service's threads block on checkout calls. 300 threads in the order service fill up within 5 seconds. The order service crashes. The notification service depends on the order service. The cascade continues.

After 90 seconds, four services are down. The entire site is unavailable for 45 minutes while engineers diagnose the root cause, restart services, and verify the cascade chain. Revenue loss: approximately $200,000 for an e-commerce platform doing $10M daily volume.

This is cascading failure. The circuit breaker pattern exists to prevent it.

## The Home Electrical Analogy

In your house, every circuit is rated for a maximum current. A 15-amp circuit can supply roughly 1,800 watts. If you plug a space heater drawing 1,500 watts, a hair dryer drawing 1,200 watts, and a microwave drawing 1,000 watts into the same circuit, the total draw exceeds 15 amps. The wire heats up. Insulation can melt at 60 degrees Celsius above ambient. A fire can start.

The circuit breaker in your electrical panel monitors current. When current exceeds 15 amps for more than a brief surge, the breaker trips. A bimetallic strip inside the breaker heats up and bends, releasing a spring-loaded switch. The circuit opens within milliseconds. Power to that circuit stops. No fire. No damaged wiring. You unplug some devices, reset the breaker, and everything works again.

The software circuit breaker follows the same logic. It monitors failures instead of current. It uses configurable thresholds instead of a physical bimetallic strip. When failures exceed the threshold, the breaker trips -- it opens the circuit. Calls stop immediately. No cascading failure. You wait for the system to recover, the breaker half-opens to test the waters, then closes when health returns.

The analogy extends to the reset mechanism. In your house, you do not flip the breaker back on immediately -- you first identify and fix the problem (unplug the space heater). In software, the breaker automatically probes after a timeout. If the problem is fixed, the breaker resets itself. If not, it trips again. This self-healing property is what makes the software circuit breaker more powerful than its physical counterpart.

## Core Vocabulary

**Circuit Breaker.** A stateful component that wraps calls to an external dependency and monitors for failures. It transitions between three states: CLOSED, OPEN, and HALF_OPEN. The circuit breaker is the central pattern in a family of resilience patterns that also includes bulkheads, timeouts, retries, and fallbacks. It was first described in Michael Nygard's book "Release It!" in 2007 and popularized by Netflix's Hystrix library in 2012.

**Closed State.** The normal operating state. All calls pass through to the dependency. The circuit breaker counts failures. When the failure count or rate exceeds the configured threshold, it transitions to OPEN. In CLOSED state, the overhead is minimal: a single atomic read of the state variable plus a counter increment. This takes approximately 50 nanoseconds.

**Open State.** The dependency is presumed unhealthy. All calls return immediately with an error or fallback response without touching the dependency. This gives the dependency time to recover. The breaker stays OPEN for the duration of the reset timeout. No threads are blocked. No network connections are consumed. The cascade is stopped at this point. The open state is the fail-fast mechanism: a request that would take 2 seconds to timeout now fails in under 1 microsecond.

**Half-Open State.** After the reset timeout expires, the breaker allows a limited number of probe requests through. If these succeed, the breaker transitions to CLOSED. If any fail, it transitions back to OPEN. The half-open state is the self-healing mechanism: it lets the system recover automatically without human intervention.

**Failure Threshold.** The number or percentage of failures that must occur within a time window before the breaker opens. A typical configuration is 5 failures in a 10-second sliding window. The threshold must be high enough to avoid false positives from transient errors (a single network hiccup) but low enough to open before cascading damage occurs (before thread pool exhaustion).

**Reset Timeout.** The duration the breaker stays OPEN before transitioning to HALF_OPEN. This gives the dependency time to recover. Typical values range from 5 seconds to 5 minutes depending on the dependency's recovery profile. A database connection pool recovers in 5-10 seconds. A database restart takes 60-120 seconds. A full service deployment can take 5-10 minutes.

**Health Check.** An endpoint or probe the circuit breaker calls in the HALF_OPEN state to determine if the dependency has recovered. This can be the same request path or a dedicated health endpoint. The health endpoint should be lightweight but exercise the actual dependency path.

**Fallback.** A degraded response returned when the breaker is OPEN. Examples: a cached response, a default value, a null object, or a polite error message. Fallbacks prevent the caller from failing entirely. A well-designed fallback maintains the core workflow at reduced fidelity.

**Bulkhead.** An isolation pattern that limits the number of concurrent calls to a dependency. Bulkheads protect callers from exhausting shared resources. The name comes from ship design: a ship's hull is divided into watertight compartments (bulkheads). If one compartment floods, the others keep the ship afloat.

**Cascading Failure.** A failure in one component that propagates to other components, causing a systemic outage. The propagation mechanism is usually shared resources: thread pools, connection pools, memory, or I/O bandwidth. Circuit breakers break the propagation chain by stopping calls at the first sign of trouble.

**Graceful Degradation.** The system continues to operate at reduced capacity during a partial failure. Fallbacks, caches, and feature flags enable graceful degradation. The user sees a slightly degraded experience rather than an error page.

**Sliding Window.** A time-based or count-based window that tracks recent failures. Old observations expire, so the breaker responds to current conditions, not historical anomalies. The sliding window has a configurable duration of 10-60 seconds.

**Error Budget.** The acceptable amount of failure over a time period. If your SLO is 99.9 percent uptime, your error budget is 0.1 percent of requests (864 failures per day at 1000 req/s). Circuit breaker thresholds can be set relative to the error budget.

**Retry Storm.** A cascade of retry attempts that overwhelms a struggling dependency. Without a circuit breaker, every caller retries 3 times on failure. A single failure generates 3 requests. If 100 callers all retry simultaneously, the dependency receives 300 requests instead of 100.

**Request Collapsing.** Combining multiple concurrent requests for the same resource into a single batch request. This reduces load on the dependency. It is commonly used alongside circuit breakers in bulkhead-isolated thread pools.

## Step by Step: Circuit Breaker in Action

### Step 1: Normal Operation (CLOSED)

The payment dependency responds in 200 milliseconds at p50 and 450 milliseconds at p99. The circuit breaker is in CLOSED state. Every request passes through. The breaker records each response. Successes and failures are tracked in the sliding window. At this point, the window shows 950 successes and 2 failures in the last 10 seconds. The failure rate is 0.2 percent. The system handles 10,000 requests per minute. CPU utilization is 40 percent. Thread pool utilization is 30 percent.

### Step 2: Failures Begin

The payment database starts a checkpoint operation that causes lock contention on the transactions table. Response times climb from 200 milliseconds to 5 seconds, then to 15 seconds. The checkout service has a 2-second timeout on payment calls. Every call times out.

The first 3 failures arrive within 2 seconds. The circuit breaker records each one in its sliding window. You have configured a threshold of 5 failures in a 10-second window. The breaker stays CLOSED for now, but the failure count is rising. The sliding window now shows 950 successes and 5 failures -- 3 new failures plus 2 from before.

### Step 3: Threshold Exceeded

Two more requests arrive and time out. The failure count hits 7 within the 10-second window. The threshold of 5 has been exceeded. The circuit breaker transitions from CLOSED to OPEN. This transition happens instantly -- a single CAS instruction in under 20 nanoseconds.

The thread pool in the checkout service has lost 5 threads to the 2-second timeouts. Those 5 threads were blocked for 10 total thread-seconds. Without the breaker, the next 195 calls would each block for 2 seconds, consuming 390 additional thread-seconds. With the breaker, zero additional threads are blocked.

### Step 4: Open State -- Requests Blocked

The breaker is now OPEN. Every subsequent call to the payment service returns immediately without making a network request. The caller receives a fallback response: a cached balance from 60 seconds ago with a warning that the balance might be stale.

Threads in the caller are not blocked. The thread pool remains available for other dependencies: inventory calls, shipping calls, user profile calls. The payment service thread pool has time to drain and recover. The reset timeout starts counting: 30 seconds by default.

### Step 5: Drain and Recovery Period

For the next 30 seconds, all calls to the payment service are fast-failed. The payment service's thread pool begins to drain. The database checkpoint completes. Connection pool health checks restore idle connections. Request queue drops to zero. Memory pressure normalizes.

During this period, the fallback cache is serving approximately 50 requests per second with stale data. The checkout success rate drops from 99.9 percent to 98.5 percent -- the payment fallback returns a cached response, and the checkout completes successfully with the cached data.

### Step 6: Half-Open -- Probe Requests

After 30 seconds, the reset timeout expires. The breaker transitions to HALF_OPEN. It allows one probe request through (halfOpenMax=3 is configured). The first probe request reaches the payment service. The payment service responds in 150 milliseconds -- healthy again.

The breaker records this success and allows a second probe. The second probe also succeeds in 140 milliseconds. The third probe succeeds in 155 milliseconds. All three probes are successful.

### Step 7: Success Threshold Exceeded -- Back to CLOSED

Three consecutive probe requests succeeded. The halfOpenSuccessThreshold of 3 is met. The breaker transitions from HALF_OPEN to CLOSED. The sliding window is reset. Full traffic resumes.

### Step 8: Recovery Complete

The breaker is CLOSED again. All traffic flows to the payment service. The first 100 requests after recovery show an average latency of 210 milliseconds. The system has returned to normal operation without human intervention. The total outage window was roughly 35 seconds.

## Variants

### Count-Based Circuit Breaker

The count-based variant tracks failures as an absolute count within a sliding window. When the failure count exceeds the threshold, the breaker opens.

**Configuration:** failureCount=5, windowSize=10 seconds, resetTimeout=30 seconds.

**Behavior:** The breaker opens after exactly 5 failures regardless of the total request volume. This works well for services with steady request rates of 50-500 requests per second. It works poorly for burst traffic where 5 failures arrive in 200 milliseconds.

**Strengths:** Simple to understand. Easy to debug. No math involved. The relationship between configuration and behavior is linear and predictable.

**Weaknesses:** Does not account for request volume. A service receiving 5 requests per day takes a full day to open the breaker. A service receiving 5,000 requests per second opens the breaker on a 0.1 percent failure rate.

### Time-Based (Rate-Based) Circuit Breaker

The rate-based variant tracks the failure rate as a percentage. When the failure rate exceeds the threshold, the breaker opens.

**Configuration:** failureRate=50%, minimumRequests=10, windowSize=60 seconds, resetTimeout=30 seconds.

**Behavior:** The breaker opens when 50 percent or more of at least 10 requests fail. This adapts automatically to traffic volume. During low traffic of 1 request per minute, 10 failures might never accumulate within the 60-second window. During high traffic of 1,000 requests per second, a 0.5-second spike of 50 percent failures triggers the breaker immediately.

**Strengths:** Scales with traffic volume. The same configuration works for services with 1 request per minute and 10,000 requests per second. Provides proportional protection.

**Weaknesses:** Requires a minimum request count to avoid opening on isolated failures. The rate calculation introduces complexity. At low traffic volumes, the breaker might not open quickly enough.

### Adaptive Circuit Breaker

The adaptive variant adjusts its parameters dynamically based on observed latency and error patterns using techniques like exponentially weighted moving averages (EWMA).

**Behavior:** The adaptive breaker computes a moving average of response times and error rates. When the average latency exceeds 2 standard deviations above the historical baseline, the breaker opens. When latency returns to within 1 standard deviation of the baseline, it closes.

**Algorithm:** The adaptive breaker maintains three EWMA values: latency mean, latency variance, and error rate. Each new observation updates the EWMA with a decay factor alpha of 0.1 to 0.3.

**Challenges:** Adaptive breakers require a 5-10 minute warm-up period. They can confuse a legitimate traffic surge with a failure. They require more computational overhead of approximately 200 nanoseconds per request for floating-point operations.

### Exponential Backoff Circuit Breaker

The exponential backoff variant increases the reset timeout on each successive open cycle.

**Configuration:** initialTimeout=5s, multiplier=2, maxTimeout=300s.

**Behavior:** A flapping dependency cycles: OPEN for 5 seconds, HALF_OPEN, fails, OPEN for 10 seconds, HALF_OPEN, fails, OPEN for 20 seconds. The increasing timeouts give the dependency increasingly long recovery windows.

**Advantages:** Prevents rapid cycling. Gives the dependency increasingly long recovery periods. Reduces load on the monitoring system from repeated transitions.

**Disadvantages:** Adds complexity. Recovery time becomes unpredictable. A dependency that recovers in 3 seconds might wait 20 seconds.

### Circuit Breaker with Fallback

The fallback variant returns a degraded response when the breaker is OPEN, rather than propagating an error.

**Fallback Types:**
- Cache fallback: Return the last known good response with a 60-second TTL.
- Default value fallback: Return a sensible default like "5-7 business days" for shipping.
- Stub fallback: Return a static response like "market closed" for stock prices.
- Null object fallback: Return a non-null object with no-op methods.

## Mental Model Check

**Question 1.** Your circuit breaker is configured with failureCount=5 and windowSize=10 seconds. The dependency fails 3 times, then succeeds 100 times over 60 seconds, then fails 3 more times. Does the breaker open?

**Answer.** No. The sliding window requires 5 failures within any 10-second window. The failures are not clustered within 10 seconds: 3 failures occur, then 100 successes take 60 seconds, then 3 more failures occur. At no point do 5 failures exist in the same 10-second window.

**Question 2.** Your circuit breaker is in OPEN state with a 30-second reset timeout. After 30 seconds, it transitions to HALF_OPEN and sends a probe request. The probe request times out after 5 seconds. What happens next?

**Answer.** The breaker transitions back to OPEN state immediately upon the probe failure. A new 30-second reset timeout begins. The breaker will keep cycling between OPEN and HALF_OPEN until a probe request succeeds.

**Question 3.** You have 10 services that all call the same payment dependency. Each has its own independent circuit breaker. What happens when 8 of them detect failures and open their circuits?

**Answer.** The 8 services with open circuits stop calling the payment dependency. Traffic drops by roughly 80 percent. The 9th and 10th services continue calling. The reduced load may allow the dependency to recover. If the dependency cannot handle even 20 percent traffic, the remaining services will also open their breakers.

**Question 4.** You configure a circuit breaker with halfOpenMax=50. Is this a good idea?

**Answer.** This is a poor configuration. The purpose of HALF_OPEN state is to probe a potentially overwhelmed dependency with minimal traffic. Sending 50 probe requests might overwhelm a barely-recovered dependency. A typical halfOpenMax is 1 to 5.

**Question 5.** Your dependency has a recovery time of 2 seconds. Your reset timeout is 60 seconds. You are burning 58 seconds of potential uptime every cycle. How do you fix this?

**Answer.** Reduce the reset timeout to 5-10 seconds. Better yet, use exponential backoff starting at 5 seconds with a multiplier of 2. Even better: implement a continuous health check that probes the dependency at fixed intervals regardless of the circuit breaker state.

**Question 6.** You have a rate-based breaker with failureRate=50%, minRequests=10, and windowSize=60 seconds. In the first 59 seconds, 9 requests are sent and all succeed. In the 60th second, 10 requests are sent and 6 fail. Does the breaker open?

**Answer.** At the moment the 10th request's failure is recorded, you have 9 successes and 6 failures = 15 total requests. 6/15 = 40 percent. Still below 50 percent. You need 10 failures to exceed 50 percent with 19 total requests (10/19 = 52.6 percent). So the breaker does not open yet.

**Question 7.** You deploy a new version of your service. The circuit breaker state is in-memory and resets on deployment. The dependency is unhealthy. How many requests will fail before the breaker opens?

**Answer.** Exactly the failureCount (in count-based mode). On deployment, the sliding window is empty. The first 5 requests are all sent to the unhealthy dependency and all fail. For a 50-instance deployment, that is 250 failed requests hitting an unhealthy dependency.

## Key Takeaways

- The circuit breaker pattern prevents cascading failures by detecting unhealthy dependencies and stopping calls immediately.
- Three states define the lifecycle: CLOSED (normal), OPEN (rejecting), and HALF_OPEN (probing).
- Count-based breakers track absolute failure counts. Rate-based breakers track failure percentages. Adaptive breakers adjust thresholds dynamically.
- Fallbacks provide degraded responses, enabling graceful degradation.
- The reset timeout must match the dependency's recovery profile. Exponential backoff provides a balanced approach.
- Circuit breakers and bulkheads complement each other.
- The sliding window mechanisms provide the data foundation for the threshold evaluator.
