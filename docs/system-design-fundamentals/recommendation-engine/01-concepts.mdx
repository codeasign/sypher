## Problem Story

You open Netflix on a Friday night. There are over 15,000 titles available in your region. You scroll for 12 minutes, read 4 synopses, give up, and watch The Office for the 37th time.

This is the paradox of choice at scale. When a platform offers millions of items, users do not welcome abundance -- they freeze. Research from Columbia University showed that when presented with 30 jam varieties, customers were 10 times less likely to purchase than when offered only 6. Now multiply that by 15,000.

Every second a user spends browsing without finding something relevant is a second closer to churn. Netflix estimates that its recommendation engine saves the company over $1 billion per year in reduced churn. Amazon attributes 35% of its total revenue -- roughly $200 billion annually -- to its recommendation engine. YouTube reports that 70% of total watch time comes from recommendations, not search. TikTok's "For You" page is entirely recommendation-driven, and users spend an average of 95 minutes per day on the app.

The core problem is not about showing everything you have. It is about showing the right thing at the right time, with enough confidence that the user acts. A system that can predict what a user wants before the user knows they want it is the holy grail of platform businesses. This is why every major tech company invests heavily in recommendation systems -- not as a feature, but as the primary driver of engagement and revenue.

## The Insight

A recommendation engine is a search engine where the query is implicit. Instead of typing keywords, you signal intent through behavior: what you watched, what you liked, what you skipped, how long you hovered, what you bought after seeing, what you added to your cart and later abandoned. Every micro-interaction is a data point.

The insight is simple: **past behavior is the best predictor of future behavior**. If User A and User B both watched the same 5 movies and User A then watched a sixth movie, User B is statistically likely to enjoy that sixth movie too. This is the foundation of collaborative filtering, and it works because human preferences follow predictable patterns.

This insight extends beyond entertainment. Amazon uses purchase history to predict what you will buy next. Spotify uses listening history to predict what you will enjoy. LinkedIn uses connection patterns to suggest who you should network with. Google uses search history to predict what you are looking for before you finish typing. The same mathematical principles apply across radically different domains.

### The Cafeteria Analogy

Imagine you walk into a cafeteria with 500 menu items. You have never been there before. A lunch lady hands you a tray and says, "Pick what you want."

You freeze.

Now imagine instead that the lunch lady looks at 50 other people who share your tastes. She notices that people who chose the chicken sandwich also chose the sweet potato fries. People who chose the salad also chose the iced tea. She looks at your tray -- you chose the chicken sandwich. She says, "You might also like the sweet potato fries."

This is collaborative filtering. The lunch lady is the model. The other diners are the user community. The correlation between chicken sandwich and sweet potato fries is the item-item similarity matrix.

Now imagine a different scenario. The lunch lady does not look at other people at all. Instead, she analyzes the chicken sandwich itself: it is warm, savory, contains protein, has a crispy texture. She then looks at other items with those attributes: grilled chicken wrap, turkey club, chicken quesadilla. She recommends those instead.

This is content-based filtering. The lunch lady is analyzing item features rather than user behavior. She needs no data from other diners because she understands what makes items similar at the feature level.

Now imagine a third scenario. The lunch lady asks you to fill out a card: "Rate these 10 items from 1 to 5." You fill it out. She then uses these explicit ratings to match you with the items that best fit your stated preferences.

This is the explicit feedback approach. It is high-signal but high-effort. Most users will not fill out the card. But the ones who do provide extremely valuable data.

### The Mathematical Insight

At the mathematical level, recommendation is about predicting missing values. You have a matrix where rows are users, columns are items, and cells are interactions (ratings, clicks, purchases). Most cells are empty -- a user has interacted with only a tiny fraction of all available items. The recommendation problem is: fill in the empty cells with predicted values, then sort by those predictions.

## Core Vocabulary

Before you dive deeper, you need to understand the language of recommendation systems. Here are 16 essential terms.

**Collaborative Filtering (CF):** A method that makes predictions based on the preferences of many users. It assumes that if User A agrees with User B on past items, User A will likely agree with User B on future items. CF is the most widely used technique in production systems and works without any knowledge of what the items actually are -- it only needs interaction patterns.

**Content-Based Filtering:** A method that recommends items similar to those a user has liked in the past, based on item features. It does not require data from other users. Cold start for new items is minimal because you only need the item's features. This approach is common in news recommendation systems where collaborative filtering struggles due to the ephemeral nature of articles.

**Matrix Factorization:** A technique that decomposes the user-item interaction matrix into two lower-dimensional matrices -- one representing latent user factors, one representing latent item factors. This is the math behind most modern CF systems. The dot product of a user vector and an item vector gives a predicted rating. Matrix factorization was the winning technique in the Netflix Prize competition.

**Embedding:** A dense vector representation of a user or item in a latent space. Embeddings capture semantic relationships. In a well-trained embedding space, the vector for "The Dark Knight" is closer to "Batman Begins" than to "The Notebook." Embeddings are the cornerstone of modern deep learning-based recommenders. Typical embedding dimensions range from 64 to 256.

**Cold Start:** The problem of making recommendations when you have no data. There are three variants: cold user (new user, no history), cold item (new item, no interactions), and cold system (new platform, no users, no items). Each requires a different strategy. Cold start is one of the hardest problems in recommendation systems and often requires product-level solutions like onboarding questionnaires.

**Hybrid Recommender:** A system that combines multiple recommendation techniques -- typically CF plus content-based -- to overcome the weaknesses of each. Most production systems are hybrid. There are several hybridization strategies including weighted, switching, cascade, and feature augmentation.

**Candidate Generation:** The first stage of a two-stage recommender. It retrieves a small subset of items -- typically hundreds from a catalog of millions -- using a cheap, fast method. This stage prioritizes recall over precision. Common techniques include ANN search, item-based CF, and trending lists.

**Ranking:** The second stage of a two-stage recommender. It takes the candidates from the first stage and scores them using a more expensive, more accurate model. This stage prioritizes precision over recall. Ranking models are typically deep neural networks running on GPU.

**Relevance Score:** A numerical value assigned to each candidate item representing how likely the user is to interact with it. The system uses this score to sort and filter the final recommendation list. Relevance scores are typically between 0 and 1.

**Diversity:** A measure of how different the recommended items are from each other. Recommending five different genres is diverse. Recommending five Marvel movies is not. Diversity prevents the "filter bubble" problem. Most production systems enforce diversity constraints during the post-processing stage.

**Serendipity:** The quality of being pleasantly surprising. A serendipitous recommendation is both relevant and unexpected. Pure relevance optimization leads to obvious, boring recommendations. Serendipity requires deliberate exploration and diversity.

**Exploration vs. Exploitation:** The fundamental tension in recommendation systems. Exploitation shows the user what they are likely to engage with based on past data. Exploration shows the user something new to gather more data. Too much exploitation leads to filter bubbles. Too much exploration leads to irrelevant recommendations. The classic solution is the epsilon-greedy algorithm or Upper Confidence Bound (UCB).

**A/B Testing:** The gold standard for evaluating recommendation changes. You split traffic into a control group (current model) and a treatment group (new model) and compare metrics like click-through rate, conversion rate, and session duration. A/B tests for recommendation systems typically run for 1-2 weeks to account for the novelty effect.

**Implicit Feedback:** Signals that are not explicit ratings but still indicate preference. Examples: clicks, dwell time, purchase history, scroll depth, add-to-cart. Implicit feedback is noisy but abundant. A click does not mean the user liked the item, but 100 clicks over 10 sessions is a strong signal. Implicit feedback is 100x to 1,000x more abundant than explicit feedback.

**Explicit Feedback:** Direct ratings from users. Examples: star ratings, thumbs up/down, likes. Explicit feedback is high-signal but very sparse -- most users never rate most items. On Amazon, fewer than 5% of purchases result in a rating. On Netflix, fewer than 10% of members rate titles.

**Position Bias:** The tendency of users to interact with items at the top of a recommendation list regardless of relevance. Position-1 items get 10x more clicks than position-5 items, even after correcting for relevance. This bias must be modeled and corrected during training to avoid a self-reinforcing feedback loop.

The diagram above shows the standard architecture of a modern recommendation engine. The request flows from the user device through an API gateway to the recommendation service, which runs two stages: candidate generation and ranking. The candidate generation stage retrieves a small set of relevant items from a catalog of millions using multiple parallel strategies. The ranking stage scores those items using a more sophisticated model with feature engineering. Post-processing applies business rules, filters, and diversity boosting. The data platform feeds the pipeline with user profiles, item catalog, and real-time events. A training pipeline runs offline to update models and publish the latest embeddings.

This architecture is used by Netflix, Amazon, YouTube, Spotify, TikTok, and virtually every major online platform. The specific algorithms and models differ, but the pipeline structure is universal.

## How It Works

A modern recommendation engine processes a request through 10 distinct stages. Each stage has a specific purpose, computational profile, and latency budget.

### Stage 1: Request Intake and Authentication

When you open Netflix, your device sends a request containing your user ID, device type, time of day, and context parameters. The API gateway validates your authentication token, extracts the user ID from the session, and forwards the request to the recommendation service.

The service looks up your user profile from the profile cache. This profile contains your account tier (premium vs. standard), language preference, geographic region, parental control settings, and a summary of your recent activity. The profile is cached in Redis with a TTL of 5 minutes to reduce load on the primary database.

If the profile is not in cache (a cache miss), the service reads it from the persistent store (DynamoDB). This adds 20-50 milliseconds to the request but should happen for fewer than 5% of requests.

### Stage 2: Context Assembly and Feature Retrieval

The system assembles a context vector that captures the current situation. This context is critical because a user's preferences change dramatically with context.

The context vector includes four categories of features:

**Temporal features:** day of week (Monday vs. Saturday), hour of day (8 AM commute vs. 10 PM wind-down), season (holiday season, summer blockbuster season), whether it is a special date (release date of a highly anticipated sequel)

**Device features:** mobile vs. TV vs. desktop vs. tablet, screen resolution, network bandwidth estimate, operating system

**Session features:** how long the user has been browsing (first page vs. 10th page), how many items the user has already seen in this session, whether the user just finished watching something, what the user searched for in this session

**Social features:** whether the user is browsing alone or with others (Netflix profiles support multiple viewers), whether there are shared watchlists or group recommendations

All of these features are assembled into a vector and sent to the feature store. The feature store looks up precomputed user features (7-day engagement count, genre distribution, average watch time) and item features (popularity score, freshness score, quality score). The assembled feature vector is typically 200-500 dimensions.

You might want thrillers at 10 PM alone but family comedies at 3 PM on a Saturday with your kids. A user who searches for "cooking" at 5 PM wants different results than someone who searches at 10 AM. Context is what makes recommendations feel intelligent rather than robotic.

### Stage 3: User Embedding Retrieval

The system retrieves the user embedding from the embedding store. The user embedding is a dense vector (typically 128 dimensions) that captures the user's latent preferences. It is computed by the training pipeline and updated every 6 hours.

The user embedding is the most compact representation of a user's taste. Two users with similar embeddings have similar preferences. The embedding is computed by the user tower of the two-tower model, which takes the user's interaction history, profile attributes, and context as input.

For a new user with no embedding (cold start), the system uses a default embedding based on the user's region, language, and device type. This is a rough approximation but is better than random. The embedding is updated after the user's first few interactions.

### Stage 4: Candidate Generation (Retrieval)

The candidate generation stage takes the user embedding and retrieves the top-N most similar items from the catalog. This stage must be fast -- it operates on a catalog of millions and returns results in under 50 milliseconds.

There are five common retrieval strategies used in production systems:

**Strategy 1: Approximate Nearest Neighbor (ANN) Search.** You store all item embeddings in a vector database like FAISS or Milvus. ANN search finds the closest items to the user embedding in the latent space using techniques like IVF (Inverted File Index) or HNSW (Hierarchical Navigable Small World graphs). This is the most common approach for deep learning recommenders because it directly leverages the learned embedding space.

**Strategy 2: Two-Tower Model Retrieval.** A neural network with two towers -- one for the user, one for the item. The user tower encodes user features and context into a user embedding. The item tower encodes item features into an item embedding. The dot product between the two gives a relevance score. You precompute item embeddings offline and look them up at serving time. At retrieval time, you compute the dot product of the user embedding with all item embeddings (or use ANN on the precomputed embeddings).

**Strategy 3: Locality-Sensitive Hashing (LSH).** You hash items into buckets such that similar items fall into the same bucket with high probability. At serving time, you hash the user embedding and retrieve all items in the same bucket. LSH is the fastest approach but has lower recall. It is useful when latency constraints are extremely tight (under 5 milliseconds).

**Strategy 4: Item-to-Item Similarity (Item-Based CF).** Given the last 3 items the user interacted with, you look up the item similarity matrix to find similar items. This is Amazon's approach and works well for e-commerce where users have clear recent intents. The item similarity matrix is precomputed offline.

**Strategy 5: Rule-Based Retrieval.** For items that do not fit the embedding approach, you use rules: trending items in the user's region, newest items in the user's preferred categories, editorial picks, and sponsored content. These retrievers add diversity and business value that pure embedding-based retrieval might miss.

### Stage 5: Candidate Expansion and Deduplication

A single retrieval strategy might miss relevant items. Most production systems run multiple retrieval strategies in parallel. Here is how Netflix does it:

- **ANN from user embedding:** retrieves 200 items using the FAISS index
- **Item-to-item similarity:** given the last 3 items the user watched, find 100 similar items for each watch. Total: 300 items
- **Trending now:** retrieve the top 20 trending items in the user's region from a Redis cache updated every 5 minutes
- **Fresh content:** retrieve the 20 newest items added to the catalog in the user's preferred genres
- **Editorial picks:** retrieve 10 items from human-curated lists, personalized to the user's language and region

These candidates are merged into a single pool. Deduplication removes items that appear in multiple lists. Items the user has already interacted with (watched, purchased, disliked) are filtered out using a rule-based filter that checks against the user's recent interaction history.

The resulting candidate pool should contain 400 to 600 unique items. If the pool is below 300 items (which can happen for niche users), the system runs a fallback retriever that returns the top 200 most popular items globally. This ensures the ranking stage always has enough candidates to produce high-quality results.

### Stage 6: Feature Assembly for Ranking

For each candidate item, the system assembles a feature vector for the ranking model. The feature vector includes features from three sources:

**User features:** user embedding (128 floats), engagement history summaries (7-day watch count, 30-day genre distribution), demographic features (age group, region), account features (subscription tier, account age)

**Item features:** item embedding (128 floats), content features (genre, runtime, release year, language), performance features (global popularity, average rating, completion rate), freshness features (days since release, days since added to catalog)

**Cross features:** user-genre affinity (how often the user watches this genre), user-director affinity, user-actor affinity, average rating the user gives to items with similar characteristics, time since the user last interacted with an item from this category, whether the user has watched other items in this series or collection

**Context features:** hour-of-day embedding, day-of-week embedding, device-type embedding, session-depth feature (how many items the user has seen in this session), streaming quality indicator

Each feature vector is approximately 350 dimensions. For 500 candidates, this is 500 x 350 = 175,000 floating point values, or approximately 700 KB of data. Feature assembly takes under 10 milliseconds.

### Stage 7: Deep Neural Network Scoring

The ranking stage takes the 500 candidate items with their assembled feature vectors and scores each one using a deep neural network. This model is more expensive than the retrieval models -- it can take up to 10 milliseconds per item when run sequentially -- but it is also much more accurate.

The ranking model architecture is a 3-layer feedforward neural network:
- Input layer: 350 features
- Hidden layer 1: 256 units, ReLU activation, 20% dropout
- Hidden layer 2: 128 units, ReLU activation, 20% dropout
- Output layer: 1 unit, sigmoid activation

The model is trained using binary cross-entropy loss. Positive examples are items the user interacted with (clicked, watched, purchased). Negative examples are items the user was shown but did not interact with, plus randomly sampled items the user was not shown.

In production, the ranking model is served on GPU using TensorFlow Serving or PyTorch Serve. With a batch size of 500, the entire batch can be scored in 15-30 milliseconds. Without a GPU (using CPU only), the same scoring takes 100-200 milliseconds -- a 5-10x slowdown.

The output is a single relevance score per item, between 0 and 1. A score of 0.95 means the model is highly confident the user will interact with the item. A score of 0.15 means the model predicts the user is unlikely to engage.

### Stage 8: Business Logic and Filtering

Before the ranked list reaches the user, it passes through a business logic layer. This layer is not ML-based -- it consists of deterministic rules that encode business requirements. This layer handles:

**Filtering:** remove items the user has already watched, purchased, or explicitly disliked. This filter uses a Redis set of watched/disliked item IDs for the user and runs in under 1 millisecond.

**Licensing:** remove items not available in the user's region or that have expired licenses. This is critical for streaming services where content rights vary by country.

**Age restrictions:** remove items not appropriate for the user's profile settings. A child's profile should not see R-rated content regardless of relevance.

**Sponsorship insertion:** insert sponsored items at specific positions. Typically sponsored slots are at positions 2, 5, and 8 in the recommendation list. The sponsorship may come with a minimum relevance threshold (sponsored item must score in the top 20% of all candidates).

**Diversity enforcement:** ensure that no more than 3 items from the same genre appear in the top 10, and no more than 2 items from the same franchise or series. This prevents the list from being dominated by one type of content.

**Freshness boost:** apply a multiplicative boost to items published within the last 7 days. The boost decays linearly from 1.5x at day 1 to 1.0x at day 7.

### Stage 9: Diversity and Serendipity Reranking

Pure relevance ranking produces a list that is too homogeneous. If you watched one Marvel movie, the system will recommend 10 more Marvel movies. If you bought a camera, the system will show you 10 more cameras. This is the filter bubble -- the system traps you in a narrow corridor of your existing preferences.

To break the bubble, production systems add a diversity reranking step. The most common algorithm is **Maximum Marginal Relevance (MMR)** . The algorithm works as follows:

```
selected = []
remaining = all candidates sorted by relevance score
for i in range(num_results):
    best_item = argmax over remaining of:
        lambda * relevance(item)
        - (1 - lambda) * max_similarity(item, selected)
    selected.append(best_item)
    remaining.remove(best_item)
```

The algorithm selects items one at a time. At each step, it picks the item that maximizes a weighted combination of relevance and diversity. The diversity term penalizes items that are too similar to items already selected. The lambda parameter controls the trade-off: lambda = 1.0 gives pure relevance ranking, lambda = 0.0 gives pure diversity ranking.

Netflix uses a variant called "sliding window MMR" that applies the diversity constraint within a sliding window of 5 items. This ensures diversity locally (consecutive items are different) while preserving the global relevance ranking.

The typical value for lambda is 0.7 (70% relevance, 30% diversity). This was empirically determined by A/B testing. Lowering lambda to 0.5 increases diversity by 40% but reduces click-through rate by 8%. The optimal value depends on the specific platform and content type.

Beyond MMR, some systems add an exploration mechanism. TikTok reserves 10% of the feed for items outside the user's preference cluster. These items are randomly sampled from a "cold start" pool of content that needs exposure. If the user engages with an exploration item, it is immediately pulled into the regular recommendation pipeline.

### Stage 10: A/B Variation and Response Assembly

The final stage applies A/B testing logic. The system checks which experiment variant the user is assigned to. If the user is in a treatment group, the system may apply different models, different diversity parameters, or different business rules.

The experiment assignment is deterministic and stable -- the same user always sees the same variant for the duration of the experiment (typically 1-2 weeks). The assignment is computed by hashing the user ID with the experiment ID and taking the result modulo the number of variants.

The system also applies personalization rules that are not model-based:

- **New user:** if the user has fewer than 10 interactions, use a popularity-based fallback with onboarding preference signals
- **High-engagement user:** if the user has more than 500 interactions, increase exploration by 20% to prevent boredom
- **Inactive user:** if the user has not visited in 30 days, show a "Welcome Back" mix of popular items and items from previously engaged categories
- **High-value user:** if the user has a premium subscription, show higher-quality recommendations (filter out low-rated items)

Finally, the top 50 items are serialized as JSON and returned to the client. The response includes the item IDs, titles, thumbnail URLs, and a "recommendation reason" for each item ("Because you watched Stranger Things," "Trending in your region"). This reason is displayed in the UI and increases user trust in the recommendations.

## Variants of the Recommendation Engine Pattern

There is no single recommendation engine. Different problems require different approaches. Here are the major variants with their strengths, weaknesses, and use cases.

### Variant 1: Collaborative Filtering (User-Based and Item-Based)

Collaborative filtering is the oldest and most proven approach. It comes in two flavors.

**User-based CF:** Find users similar to the target user, then recommend items that those similar users liked. If you and I have both watched and liked the same 5 movies, and you watched a 6th movie that I have not seen, the system recommends it to me. User similarity is typically computed using Pearson correlation or cosine similarity on the user's rating vectors.

**Item-based CF:** Find items similar to items the user has liked. If you liked "The Matrix," the system looks at all users who liked "The Matrix" and finds what else they liked. This is the approach Amazon popularized with "Customers who bought this also bought."

Item-based CF scales better than user-based CF. Here is why: item similarity is more stable -- items do not change over time, while user preferences do. A user who liked action movies last year may prefer documentaries this year, but "The Matrix" will always be an action movie. This stability means the item similarity matrix can be precomputed offline and updated infrequently.

Item-based CF also handles the "cold item" problem better. When a new item is added, it initially has no interactions. However, as soon as one user interacts with it, the item can be linked to other items through that user's interaction history.

**When to use user-based CF:** Platforms where user communities are strong and preferences are stable. Examples: Goodreads (book recommendations where communities form around reading tastes), Letterboxd (film social network).

**When to use item-based CF:** Platforms where the item catalog is large and relatively stable, and user preferences shift. Examples: Amazon (product recommendations), Netflix (movie recommendations).

### Variant 2: Content-Based Filtering

Content-based filtering ignores what other users do. It focuses entirely on the item itself and the user's own history. If you liked "The Matrix," the system extracts features from that movie -- genre (sci-fi), director (Wachowskis), actors (Keanu Reeves), themes (simulation, reality) -- and finds other items with similar features.

The advantage of content-based filtering is that it has no cold start problem for new items. As soon as a new item is added to the catalog with its features, it can be recommended. There is also no cold start problem for new users -- as soon as the user rates one item, the system can recommend similar items.

The disadvantage is that it tends to produce boring, homogeneous recommendations. If you watch sci-fi, you get more sci-fi. It never surfaces unexpected items from different genres, which limits serendipity. The system also cannot discover user preferences that are not reflected in the item features. If you like movies with complex narrative structures, but that feature is not encoded in the item metadata, the system cannot learn it.

Content-based filtering is commonly used in news recommendation, where collaborative filtering struggles because news articles are ephemeral and user-item interactions are sparse. A news article published 24 hours ago has completely different relevance than one published 10 minutes ago. By the time collaborative filtering has enough interaction data on an article, the article is already stale.

**Feature engineering for content-based filtering:**
- Text features: TF-IDF vectors on item descriptions, titles, and tags
- Categorical features: genre, category, language, content rating
- Numerical features: price, runtime, release year, popularity score
- Image features: embeddings from a pre-trained CNN for visual similarity
- Audio features: for music and podcast recommendations, spectrogram-based features

### Variant 3: Matrix Factorization with Alternating Least Squares (ALS)

Matrix factorization is the mathematical engine behind most modern collaborative filtering systems. It decomposes the sparse user-item interaction matrix into two dense matrices -- user factors and item factors.

The ALS algorithm alternates between fixing the user factors and solving for the item factors, and vice versa. Each step is a convex optimization problem that can be solved efficiently. ALS is particularly well-suited for implicit feedback datasets because it can handle weighted observations (a click is not as strong as a purchase, but it is stronger than nothing).

The algorithm was popularized by the Netflix Prize competition, where the winning team used a combination of matrix factorization models. Today, ALS is implemented in Spark MLlib and can scale to billions of interactions on a cluster.

**Key hyperparameters:**
- Number of latent factors: 64-256 (higher = more expressive, but more prone to overfitting)
- Regularization parameter: 0.01-0.1 (higher = more regularization)
- Number of iterations: 10-20 (more iterations = better convergence but slower training)
- Alpha (confidence parameter for implicit feedback): 20-50 (higher = more weight on positive observations)

### Variant 4: Deep Neural Network Based Recommenders

Deep learning recommenders extend matrix factorization by incorporating additional features beyond just the user-item interaction. The two-tower model is the most common architecture.

The user tower takes user features (demographics, interaction history, context) and produces a user embedding. The item tower takes item features (metadata, content features, popularity) and produces an item embedding. The dot product of the two embeddings predicts the interaction probability.

The two-tower architecture can be extended with:
- **Cross layers:** explicitly model feature interactions (e.g., user genre preference crossed with item genre)
- **Attention mechanisms:** weigh different parts of the user's history differently (e.g., a movie from 3 years ago should matter less than a movie from yesterday)
- **Sequence models:** treat the user's interaction history as a sequence and use RNNs or Transformers to capture temporal patterns
- **Multi-task learning:** predict multiple outcomes simultaneously (click, watch time, share, purchase) with shared representations

### Variant 5: Session-Based Recommenders

Session-based recommenders do not use long-term user history. They only use the current session's interactions to make predictions. This is essential for scenarios where users are anonymous or where preferences are highly context-dependent.

The classic session-based approach is the Session-based Recommender with GRUs (GRU4Rec), which treats the sequence of items in a session as a time series. More modern approaches use Transformers (SASRec, BERT4Rec) to capture complex patterns in session sequences.

**Use cases:**
- E-commerce: a user browsing for baby products is probably shopping for a gift, not building a long-term interest in baby products
- Retail: a user buying a grill in June is hosting a barbecue, not starting a grill collection
- News: a user reading articles about an election is in a news-consuming mood, not necessarily switching their interests permanently

Session-based recommenders typically use a sliding window of 10-20 interactions and have no concept of a persistent user profile.

### Variant 6: Hybrid Recommender Systems

Hybrid systems combine collaborative filtering and content-based filtering to overcome the weaknesses of each. There are several hybridization strategies:

**Weighted hybrid:** Combine the scores from CF and content-based models using a weighted average. The weights can be tuned per user or per context. For example, a new user with few interactions gets a higher weight on content-based scores, while a power user gets a higher weight on collaborative signals.

**Switching hybrid:** Use CF when the user has enough interaction data; switch to content-based when the user is new (cold start). The switch threshold is typically 10-20 interactions. This is the simplest hybrid approach.

**Cascade hybrid:** Use CF to generate a candidate set (stage 1), then use content-based to rerank within that set (stage 2). This gives you the broad discovery of CF plus the fine-grained relevance of content-based.

**Feature augmentation hybrid:** Use content-based features to enrich the user-item matrix. For cold-start items, fill in the missing interaction data using content-based predictions. Then run CF on the augmented matrix.

**Meta-level hybrid:** Use one model to generate features that feed into another model. For example, use a CF model to generate user embeddings, then use those embeddings as features in a content-based model.

Netflix uses a hybrid approach combining all of these strategies to varying degrees. Their system combines collaborative filtering signals (what similar users watched) with content-based signals (genre, actors, director) and contextual signals (time of day, device). The specific blend is optimized per user and per context through A/B testing.

### Variant 7: Bandit-Based Recommenders (Explore-Exploit)

Bandit-based recommenders frame the recommendation problem as a sequential decision-making task. At each step, the system must choose between exploiting known preferences and exploring unknown items to gather more information.

The epsilon-greedy algorithm is the simplest: with probability (1 - epsilon), show the best known recommendation (exploit). With probability epsilon, show a random item (explore). Epsilon is typically 0.1 to 0.2.

More sophisticated bandit algorithms include:
- **Upper Confidence Bound (UCB):** choose the item with the highest upper confidence bound on the predicted score. Items with high uncertainty get explored more.
- **Thompson Sampling:** maintain a probability distribution over the model parameters. Sample from the distribution and choose the best item under the sampled parameters. This naturally balances exploration and exploitation.
- **Contextual Bandits:** extend bandits with context features. The decision depends on both the user's current context and the exploration-exploitation trade-off.

Bandit algorithms are particularly useful for news recommendation, where the optimal item changes daily, and the system must continuously explore new articles.

## Mental Model Check

Use these questions to verify your understanding of the recommendation engine pattern.

**Q1: Your startup has a new social media app with zero users. You need to show recommendations on the feed. What approach do you use?**

A1: You have no user data, so collaborative filtering is impossible. You must use a content-based approach. Since there are no users, you can seed the system with popularity-based recommendations -- show the most popular content globally. Alternatively, you can use a rule-based system: show recent posts from followed accounts, trending topics in the user's region, and editorial picks. As users generate interactions, you gradually introduce collaborative filtering. The key is to collect data aggressively during the early phase by showing diverse content and tracking engagement.

**Q2: What is the difference between candidate generation and ranking? Why not just use one model?**

A2: Candidate generation is a cheap, fast retrieval step that reduces the catalog from millions to hundreds. It prioritizes recall (do not miss relevant items) and must complete in under 50 milliseconds. Ranking is an expensive, accurate scoring step that prioritizes precision (sort the best items to the top) and can take up to 10 milliseconds per item. Running a ranking model on 10 million items would take over 24 hours per request. The two-stage architecture is a practical necessity for latency and cost. Think of it as the difference between finding all possible restaurants in your city (candidate generation) versus reading reviews and menus to pick the best one (ranking).

**Q3: Your recommendation engine shows users the same few categories repeatedly. Users are complaining that recommendations are boring. What is the problem and how do you fix it?**

A3: The problem is insufficient diversity. The system is optimizing purely for relevance, which creates a filter bubble. You fix it by adding a diversity reranking step using Maximum Marginal Relevance (MMR) with lambda set to 0.7. You also increase the exploration rate -- show users items from outside their typical preference cluster at least 10% of the time. You can also add a "surprise me" button that explicitly requests serendipitous recommendations, or introduce separate recommendation rows like "Discover something new."

**Q4: A new blockbuster movie is released on your platform. Collaborative filtering cannot recommend it because no one has watched it yet. What do you do?**

A4: This is the cold item problem. You solve it with a hybrid approach. First, use content-based filtering to recommend the movie based on its features (genre, director, actors, studio). Second, use a "fresh content" booster that temporarily boosts new items by 1.5x regardless of the model score for their first 7 days. Third, use exploration -- show the new movie to a small percentage of users (5%) to gather initial interaction data. Once you have 1,000+ interactions, collaborative filtering can take over and the content-based boost decays.

**Q5: Your recommendation system needs to serve 10 million users with a p99 latency of under 200 milliseconds. Your current single-model approach takes 500 milliseconds per request. What changes do you make?**

A5: You need a two-stage architecture. First, implement candidate generation using ANN search with FAISS or a similar vector database. This reduces the candidate set from millions to 500 items in under 50 milliseconds. Second, use a lightweight ranking model for the scoring stage -- a shallow 3-layer neural network with 256 units per layer, not a deep model. Third, cache user embeddings, item embeddings, and popular recommendation results in Redis. Fourth, precompute batch recommendations for the top 20% of users (most active) and serve them from cache (sub-5ms latency). Fifth, move the ranking model to GPU inference for batch processing. These changes should bring p99 latency from 500ms to under 200ms.

**Q6: How do you evaluate whether a new recommendation model is better than the current one?**

A6: You use a combination of offline and online evaluation. Offline metrics include AUC-ROC, precision@k, recall@k, NDCG@k, and mean average precision. These are computed on a held-out test set of user interactions. However, offline metrics are not sufficient because they do not capture user behavior changes. You must run an A/B test where you split traffic 50/50 between the current model (control) and the new model (treatment). The experiment runs for at least 7 days to account for weekly cycles. Key online metrics are click-through rate, session duration, 7-day user retention, and revenue per user. The new model is deployed only if it shows a statistically significant improvement (p-value under 0.05) on at least one primary metric without degrading any other metric.

**Q7: How do you handle the position bias problem in your training data?**

A7: Position bias occurs because items at position 1 get clicked more often regardless of relevance. To correct this, you need to model the position effect. One approach is inverse propensity scoring (IPS): train a position bias model that predicts the probability of interaction based on position alone, then divide each interaction by this probability to upweight items shown in lower positions. Another approach is to include position as a feature during training but set it to a constant value (position 1) during inference. A third approach uses randomization: during a small fraction of requests (1%), randomly shuffle the order of items and record the resulting interaction patterns. This gives you unbiased interaction data that can be used to correct the bias in the main training data.
