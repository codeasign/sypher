# Autoscaling in Production

## Company Case Studies

### Netflix: Chaos Engineering and Autoscaling

Netflix was one of the earliest adopters of cloud autoscaling at massive scale. The streaming platform serves over 260 million subscribers across 190 countries, with traffic patterns that vary dramatically by time of day, day of week, and content release schedule.

**The Challenge**: Netflix's traffic is dominated by evening viewing in each time zone. As the sun sets across the US, traffic rises from 10,000 requests per second to over 100,000 requests per second in a 2-hour window -- a 10x increase. The evening peak shifts by time zone: East Coast at 8 PM ET, Central at 8 PM CT, Mountain at 8 PM MT, West Coast at 8 PM PT. A naive autoscaling setup would need to scale independently for each region.

**The Solution**: Netflix uses a combination of predictive and reactive autoscaling across multiple AWS regions. The system is called **Scryer** -- a predictive autoscaling engine that uses time-series forecasting to predict capacity needs 2-4 hours ahead.

Scryer processes three years of historical traffic data and generates hourly forecasts using a combination of ARIMA (AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average) and neural network models. The forecast accounts for:

- **Seasonal patterns**: Higher traffic on weekends, lower on weekdays.
- **Content release impact**: A new season of a popular show drives a 3-5x traffic increase for the first 48 hours.
- **Holiday effects**: Christmas and New Year's Eve are the highest traffic days of the year, 2x normal peak.
- **Regional events**: The Super Bowl causes a 50% traffic drop during the game and a 200% spike at halftime.

The predicted capacity is pre-provisioned 30 minutes before the forecasted demand, giving the 3-minute warm-up time a 27-minute buffer. The reactive component handles deviations from the forecast, adjusting capacity by up to 20% in either direction.

**The Results**: Netflix reduced over-provisioning by 40% while maintaining p99 latency under 200ms during peak hours. The system handles the evening ramp without any human intervention. During the Stranger Things season 4 release, Scryer predicted the 4.2x traffic increase with 95% accuracy and pre-provisioned capacity 45 minutes before the release.

**Key Lesson**: Predictive autoscaling is essential for managing large, predictable traffic patterns. Reactive scaling alone cannot handle a 10x evening ramp-up without significant over-provisioning.

### Uber: Cell-Based Autoscaling

Uber's ride-hailing platform operates in over 10,000 cities worldwide. Each city has its own traffic pattern, and events (concerts, sports games, holidays) create localized spikes. Uber's original autoscaling approach used a single global autoscaling group, which caused problems during city-specific events.

**The Challenge**: A concert in São Paulo should not trigger autoscaling in Tokyo. But with a global autoscaling group, all instances see the same metric aggregation. Uber needed a way to isolate scaling decisions by city or region.

**The Solution**: Uber implemented **cell-based autoscaling** using their own infrastructure platform. Each city (or group of small cities) is a "cell" with its own autoscaling group. The cell has a minimum of 3 instances and a maximum of 50 instances. The scaling metric is request count per cell, with a target of 500 requests per second per instance.

The cell architecture isolates failures. When a concert in Rio de Janeiro causes a 10x traffic spike, only the Rio cell scales out. The São Paulo cell, the Buenos Aires cell, and the Bogota cell are unaffected.

Uber's autoscaling also handles the "surge" pattern. During a rainstorm, demand for rides increases by 3-5x in a 15-minute window. The autoscaler launches additional instances for the affected cells. The warm-up time is 45 seconds because Uber uses pre-baked container images with all dependencies included.

**The Results**: Cell-based autoscaling reduced the blast radius of scaling events by 95%. A city-specific event no longer causes global scaling activity. The system scales from 3 to 50 instances in a single cell within 90 seconds of a traffic surge.

**Key Lesson**: Cell-based isolation prevents noisy neighbors from affecting global scaling decisions. Each workload segment should have its own autoscaling group with its own metrics and policies.

### Discord: Burst Capacity for Voice and Gaming

Discord handles over 150 million monthly active users, with traffic patterns driven by gaming events, software releases, and community gatherings. The platform's real-time voice and messaging service requires low latency and high availability.

**The Challenge**: Discord's traffic spikes are driven by gaming events. When a popular game releases a new patch, millions of players return simultaneously. The Discord voice channels for that game see a 5-10x increase in concurrent users within minutes. The messaging service sees a corresponding spike in text traffic.

**The Challenge Amplifier**: Discord's voice service is stateful -- each voice channel is tied to a specific server instance. Autoscaling a stateful service is harder than autoscaling a stateless web API. New instances must be configured with the correct voice region, codec settings, and peer routing tables.

**The Solution**: Discord uses a two-tier autoscaling approach:

1. **Stateless HTTP API layer**: The messaging API uses standard CPU-based autoscaling with a target of 60% CPU. The autoscaler uses a 60-second metric interval and 180-second cooldown. This layer scales from 200 to 1,200 instances during peak gaming events.

2. **Stateful voice layer**: The voice service uses a different approach. Each voice server has a maximum capacity of 5,000 concurrent voice connections. The autoscaler monitors the number of available voice slots across all servers. When available slots drop below 20%, the autoscaler launches new voice servers. New servers are pre-configured with the latest codec and routing tables via a launch template.

Discord uses a **load shedding** mechanism in addition to autoscaling. When the voice service is under extreme load, the system gracefully degrades: it reduces voice quality from 128 kbps to 64 kbps, which doubles the capacity of each voice server. This provides a second line of defense before autoscaling completes.

**The Results**: During the 2023 League of Legends World Championship, Discord's voice service scaled from 500 to 3,500 voice servers in 12 minutes without any service degradation. The load shedding mechanism activated for 45 seconds during the peak, then deactivated as new capacity came online.

**Key Lesson**: Stateful services require different autoscaling strategies than stateless services. Combining autoscaling with load shedding provides a robust defense against extreme traffic spikes.

### Cloudflare: Global Autoscaling at the Edge

Cloudflare operates a global edge network with servers in 330 cities. Each server handles traffic for millions of websites. The traffic pattern is a superposition of all customer traffic -- a DDoS attack on one customer should not affect the autoscaling of other customers.

**The Challenge**: Traditional autoscaling does not work at Cloudflare's scale. You cannot launch a new server in a data center in 5 minutes when a DDoS attack hits in 5 seconds. The warm-up time is too long. Cloudflare needed a different approach.

**The Solution**: Cloudflare uses **always-on autoscaling** -- they run enough capacity at all times to handle peak traffic for every customer, plus a 30% buffer for attacks. This is not autoscaling in the traditional sense; it is capacity planning with a generous safety margin.

The key insight is that Cloudflare's traffic is highly correlated across customers. When one customer gets a traffic spike, many others do too (it is a global event like a news story or a holiday). The 30% buffer absorbs the correlated spikes.

For individual customer spikes, Cloudflare uses **rate limiting** and **load shedding** rather than expanding capacity. If one customer's traffic doubles, the edge server sheds excess traffic with a 429 response rather than triggering a global capacity expansion.

**The Evolution**: Cloudflare has since introduced **Smart Autoscaling** for their Workers serverless platform. Workers scale automatically based on request volume, with a 50ms cold start time. The autoscaler uses a combination of request rate and CPU time, with a target of 70% CPU utilization. Workers are pre-warmed in each of the 330 cities, so the 50ms cold start applies only when a Worker is invoked for the first time in a city.

**Key Lesson**: For some workloads, buffer capacity is more practical than reactive autoscaling. When warm-up time exceeds the acceptable response time, you must pre-provision. The cost of 30% buffer capacity is the insurance premium against scale-out failures.

## Open Source Implementations

### Kubernetes Horizontal Pod Autoscaler (HPA)

Kubernetes HPA is the most widely used open-source autoscaler. It manages the replica count of a deployment, statefulset, or replica set based on observed metrics.

**Architecture**: HPA runs as a controller in the Kubernetes control plane. It queries the metrics server (or custom metrics API) at a configurable interval (default 15 seconds). It calculates the desired replica count using the same proportional control formula as AWS target tracking:

```
desiredReplicas = ceil(currentReplicas * (currentMetricValue / desiredMetricValue))
```

**Key Features**: Supports CPU, memory, custom metrics, and external metrics. Supports multiple metrics in a single HPA (the largest result wins). Supports scaling behavior configuration (stabilization window, policy, select policy).

**Limitations**: Does not support predictive scaling. Does not support scheduled scaling natively (use Vertical Pod Autoscaler or Cluster Autoscaler for that). The metrics pipeline has latency -- the metrics server scrapes every 15 seconds, and the HPA evaluates every 15 seconds, but the metrics are averaged over a 60-second window.

### Kubernetes Cluster Autoscaler (CA)

Cluster Autoscaler manages the size of the Kubernetes node pool. It adds nodes when pods cannot be scheduled (due to resource constraints) and removes nodes when they are underutilized.

**Architecture**: CA watches for pods in Pending state (unschedulable). If pods cannot be scheduled because of insufficient CPU or memory, CA launches a new node. It also watches for nodes with low utilization (below 50% CPU and memory for 10 minutes) and drains them.

**Key Features**: Works with any cloud provider (AWS, GCP, Azure). Supports multiple node pools with different instance types. Supports scale-down with pod disruption budgets.

**Limitations**: Node warm-up time is 2-5 minutes. CA is conservative about scale-down -- it will not terminate a node if it would disrupt a PDB-protected pod. CA does not know about pod-level metrics (HPA handles that).

### KEDA (Kubernetes Event-Driven Autoscaling)

KEDA extends HPA with event-driven scaling. It supports 50+ event sources (SQS, Kafka, RabbitMQ, Prometheus, etc.) and can scale to zero when there is no work.

**Architecture**: KEDA introduces a ScaledObject custom resource. The ScaledObject defines the event source, the target metric value, and the deployment to scale. KEDA creates an HPA behind the scenes and updates it as the metric changes.

**Key Features**: Scale to zero (no pods running when there is no work). Supports 50+ event sources. Supports multiple triggers per ScaledObject. Supports authentication for event sources.

**Limitations**: Scale-to-zero introduces cold start latency (the first request after idle triggers a pod launch). Not ideal for latency-sensitive workloads. Requires additional components (operator, metrics adapter, event source scalers).

## Cloud Provider Implementations

### AWS Auto Scaling

AWS provides the most mature autoscaling ecosystem. The key components are:

- **Auto Scaling Groups**: Manage instance lifecycle. Support mixed instances, spot instances, capacity rebalance, and lifecycle hooks.
- **Target Tracking Policies**: Predefined metrics (CPU, memory, request count per target, SQS queue depth). Custom metrics via CloudWatch.
- **Step Scaling Policies**: Multi-tier thresholds with configurable actions.
- **Scheduled Scaling**: Cron expressions or specific timestamps.
- **Predictive Scaling**: ML-based forecasting using 24 hours to 14 days of historical data.
- **Scaling Plans**: Combine multiple scaling strategies into a single plan.

AWS autoscaling integrates with CloudWatch for metrics, ELB for health checks, SNS for notifications, and CloudTrail for auditing.

### GCP Autoscaling

GCP Managed Instance Groups (MIGs) provide autoscaling with the following features:

- **CPU utilization**: Target utilization based autoscaling.
- **Load balancing utilization**: Based on the load balancer's serving capacity.
- **Custom metrics**: Any metric from Cloud Monitoring.
- **Schedule-based autoscaling**: Cron-based capacity changes.
- **Predictive autoscaling**: Available in beta, uses ML for forecasting.
- **Scale-in controls**: Limit the number of instances that can be removed in a time window.

GCP's autoscaling is simpler than AWS but has fewer features. The key differentiator is GCP's **managed instance group with stateful configuration**, which preserves instance names and disks during autoscaling.

### Azure Autoscaling

Azure Virtual Machine Scale Sets (VMSS) provide autoscaling with:

- **CPU, memory, and disk metrics**: Standard autoscaling based on host metrics.
- **Schedule-based scaling**: Scale on a predefined schedule.
- **Custom autoscale**: Using Application Insights or Azure Monitor metrics.
- **Scale-in policy**: Default, NewestVM, or OldestVM.
- **Autoscale predictive**: Available in preview, uses ML.

Azure's autoscaling integrates with Azure Load Balancer and Application Gateway for health checks and traffic distribution.

## Lessons Learned

### Lesson 1: Start Simple, Add Complexity Gradually

The most common autoscaling failure is over-engineering from the start. Teams implement predictive scaling, multiple policies, custom metrics, and lifecycle hooks before the basic target tracking policy is working correctly.

**Start with**: A single target tracking policy on CPU at 50% target. Set cooldown to 300 seconds. Set minimum to 3, maximum to 3x the baseline. Run for 2 weeks.

**After 2 weeks**: Add request count per target as a secondary metric. Adjust the cooldown based on observed thrashing. Enable predictive scaling if traffic patterns are predictable.

**After 1 month**: Add lifecycle hooks for custom actions. Implement mixed instances for cost optimization. Add scheduled scaling for known events.

### Lesson 2: Test Your Autoscaling Before You Need It

Most teams discover autoscaling misconfigurations during a production incident. The first real test of the autoscaling system should not be a Black Friday traffic spike.

**Test patterns**: Use load testing tools (k6, Locust, Artillery) to generate traffic spikes. Start with a 2x spike, then 5x, then 10x. Verify that the autoscaler launches the correct number of instances and that the metric returns to the target. Test scale-in by reducing the load and measuring how quickly the system terminates excess instances.

**Chaos testing**: Randomly terminate instances in the autoscaling group to verify that the replacement mechanism works. Test with unhealthy instances to verify that the health check → replacement cycle functions correctly.

### Lesson 3: Monitor the Autoscaler Itself

The autoscaler is a critical system component. It must be monitored and alarmed just like the application it manages.

**Key metrics to monitor**: DesiredCapacity vs ActualCapacity (gap indicates warm-up delay). ScalingActivityCount (spikes indicate thrashing). FailedScalingActions (indicates configuration errors). Min/Max capacity reached (indicates the bounds may need adjustment).

**Alarms**: If the autoscaler fails to launch an instance, send a critical alert. If the autoscaler takes more than 5 minutes to respond to a metric breach, send a warning. If the desired capacity hits the maximum for more than 10 minutes, send a critical alert.

### Lesson 4: Budget for Autoscaling

Autoscaling does not save money by default. It optimizes the cost-to-performance ratio. The total cost of autoscaling includes:

- **Instance cost**: The actual compute resources consumed. Autoscaling reduces this compared to fixed provisioning for peak, but may increase it compared to a fixed minimum if the autoscaler is too aggressive.
- **Churn cost**: Launching and terminating instances has overhead. Each launch costs API call time, AMI storage, and the warm-up period during which the instance is running but not serving traffic.
- **Monitoring cost**: Detailed monitoring (1-minute metrics) costs more than basic monitoring (5-minute metrics). The cost of CloudWatch detailed monitoring for 200 instances is approximately $140/month.
- **Engineering cost**: Configuring, testing, and maintaining autoscaling policies requires engineering time. A complex autoscaling setup with multiple policies, lifecycle hooks, and custom metrics can take 2-4 weeks to implement and tune.

### Spotify: Autoscaling for Audio Streaming

Spotify serves over 500 million active users with a microservices architecture running on Google Cloud Platform and Kubernetes. Each microservice has its own autoscaling configuration, tuned for its specific workload profile.

**The Challenge**: Spotify's traffic is driven by daily listening patterns. Morning commute (7-9 AM) sees a 3x spike as users download playlists for offline listening. Lunch break (12-2 PM) sees a 2x spike in browsing and discovery. Evening (6-10 PM) is the peak for streaming, with 5x normal traffic. The transitions between these patterns are sharp -- traffic changes by 50% in 15 minutes.

Additionally, Spotify's services have different scaling characteristics:

- **Playlist service**: Stateless, CPU-bound. Scales easily with standard HPA.
- **Audio streaming service**: Stateful (maintains streaming sessions), network-bound. Requires careful connection draining.
- **Search service**: Memory-bound with large indexes. Each instance must load the search index into memory, which takes 3-5 minutes.
- **Recommendation service**: Batch processing on user data. Can tolerate 30-minute processing delays.

**The Solution**: Spotify uses a tiered autoscaling approach with service-specific configurations:

1. **Playlist service**: Standard HPA with CPU target of 60%. Scales from 50 to 500 pods during peak hours.
2. **Audio streaming service**: HPA using request count per pod with a target of 200 concurrent streams per pod. Scale-in protection is enabled on all streaming pods. Connection draining takes 30 seconds on scale-in.
3. **Search service**: Predictive scaling using daily traffic patterns. Search indexes are pre-loaded on new pods before they are added to the service mesh. A warm pool of 20 pre-loaded search pods is maintained at all times.
4. **Recommendation service**: Scheduled scaling. 50 pods run during non-peak hours for batch processing. 200 pods run during peak hours.

Spotify uses a **global load balancer** that distributes traffic across three GCP regions. Each region has its own autoscaling configuration. During a region failure, traffic is redistributed to the remaining regions, and the autoscalers in those regions scale up to handle the additional load.

**The Results**: Spotify maintains p99 latency under 100ms for audio streaming and under 500ms for search during peak hours. The platform handles the morning commute surge, lunch browsing spike, and evening peak without any human intervention. The tiered autoscaling approach reduced compute costs by 35% compared to the previous fixed-capacity approach.

**Key Lesson**: Different services within the same platform need different autoscaling strategies. A one-size-fits-all approach leads to either over-provisioning (for services that need less) or under-provisioning (for services that need more).

### Dropbox: Autoscaling for Storage Infrastructure

Dropbox migrated from its own data centers to AWS and implemented autoscaling for its storage and processing infrastructure. The migration was driven by the need to handle variable traffic patterns without maintaining expensive dedicated hardware.

**The Challenge**: Dropbox's traffic is driven by file sync events. When a user modifies a file, the change must be propagated to all their devices. Sync events are bursty -- a user may make 100 changes in 5 minutes during a presentation edit, then zero changes for 2 hours. The overall traffic follows a daily pattern with peaks during business hours in each time zone.

Additionally, Dropbox handles file processing (thumbnail generation, virus scanning, OCR, full-text indexing). These are CPU-intensive tasks that are queued and processed asynchronously. The processing load is proportional to the number of files uploaded, which varies throughout the day.

**The Solution**: Dropbox uses a two-tier autoscaling architecture:

1. **Serving tier**: The API servers that handle file sync and user requests use target tracking on request count per instance. Each instance handles 500 req/s. The autoscaling group spans 3 AZs with minimum 20 and maximum 200 instances.

2. **Processing tier**: The file processing workers use queue-based autoscaling. Each worker picks up jobs from an SQS queue. The autoscaler monitors the queue depth and scales the worker pool accordingly. The workers use spot instances for 80% of the pool and on-demand for 20%.

Dropbox also implemented a **gradual scale-in** strategy for the processing tier. When the queue is empty, workers are not terminated immediately. Instead, they are kept alive for 5 minutes in case new files arrive. This prevents the "cold worker" problem where every new file triggers a worker launch.

**The Results**: Dropbox reduced its compute costs by 40% after migrating to AWS and implementing autoscaling. The processing tier handles 3x traffic spikes during business hours without any processing delay. The spot instance strategy saved an additional 60% on processing costs compared to on-demand pricing.

**Key Lesson**: Queue-based autoscaling is an effective pattern for asynchronous processing workloads. The queue acts as a buffer that decouples the arrival rate from the processing rate, allowing the autoscaler to maintain the optimal number of workers.

## Open Source Implementations (Continued)

### Prometheus + Alertmanager for Custom Autoscaling

Prometheus can be used as the metrics source for custom autoscaling solutions. While Prometheus does not directly perform autoscaling, its alerting rules can trigger scaling actions through the Alertmanager webhook receiver.

**Architecture**:
1. Prometheus scrapes metrics from instances at configurable intervals (15 seconds).
2. An alerting rule checks if the average CPU exceeds 60% for 5 minutes.
3. Alertmanager receives the alert and sends a webhook to a custom scaling service.
4. The scaling service calls the cloud provider's API to launch instances.
5. The scaling service records the scaling action and manages cooldown.

This approach gives full control over the scaling logic but requires maintaining the custom scaling service.

### Knative Autoscaling for Serverless Containers

Knative provides automatic scaling for container-based workloads on Kubernetes. It scales pods from zero to N based on request volume. Knative is designed for event-driven and serverless workloads.

**Key features**:
- **Scale to zero**: No pods running when there is no traffic. Cold start on the first request.
- **Concurrency-based scaling**: Target based on concurrent requests per pod.
- **Activator**: Routes requests to pods during scale-from-zero transitions.
- **Queue proxy**: Measures request concurrency per pod and reports to the autoscaler.

Knative is ideal for workloads with intermittent traffic (cron jobs, webhooks, background processors) where running zero pods during idle periods saves significant cost.

## Cloud Provider Implementations (Continued)

### AWS Predictive Scaling in Detail

AWS Predictive Scaling uses a machine learning model that analyzes up to 14 days of historical metric data. The model identifies daily and weekly patterns and generates a 24-hour forecast.

**Model details**:
- **Algorithm**: Holt-Winters exponential smoothing with daily and weekly seasonality.
- **Training data**: 1-14 days of historical CloudWatch metrics.
- **Forecast horizon**: 24 hours, updated every 30 minutes.
- **Confidence interval**: The forecast includes a prediction interval. The autoscaler uses the upper bound for pre-provisioning.

**Integration**: Predictive scaling creates scheduled scaling actions for the forecasted capacity. These actions execute 30 minutes before the forecasted demand. The reactive scaling policy runs alongside and adjusts for any forecast error.

**Limitations**:
- Requires 14 days of historical data for the model to converge.
- Cannot predict novel patterns (first-time events, marketing campaigns).
- The forecast is generated once per region, not per AZ.
- The forecast does not account for external events (product launches, holidays).

### GCP Managed Instance Group Autoscaling

GCP's autoscaling is simpler than AWS but has some unique features:

**Autoscaling policies**:
- **CPU utilization**: Target CPU utilization across instances. Default target is 60%.
- **Load balancing utilization**: Target utilization of the load balancer's serving capacity.
- **Custom metrics**: Any metric from Cloud Monitoring.
- **Multiple metrics**: You can specify multiple metrics. The autoscaler uses the largest result.

**Scale-in control**: GCP allows you to limit the number of instances that can be removed per time window. This prevents scale-in storms.

```yaml
autoscaling:
  coolDownPeriodSec: 300
  cpuUtilization:
    target: 0.5
  scaleInControl:
    maxScaledInReplicas:
      percent: 10
      timeWindowSec: 1800  # Max 10% removed per 30 minutes
```

**Predictive autoscaling**: GCP's predictive autoscaling (GA since 2024) uses ML to forecast CPU utilization and pre-provisions capacity.

### Azure VMSS Autoscaling

Azure's autoscaling integrates with Azure Monitor and Application Insights.

**Autoscaling conditions**:
- **Scale out**: CPU > 75% for 10 minutes, add 2 instances.
- **Scale in**: CPU < 25% for 30 minutes, remove 1 instance.
- **Schedule**: Scale to 20 instances at 9:00 AM on weekdays, scale to 5 at 7:00 PM.

**Unique features**:
- **Scale-in policy**: Choose between Default (balance across VMs), NewestVM (terminate newest first), or OldestVM (terminate oldest first).
- **Autoscale predictive**: Uses ML to forecast load and pre-provision capacity.
- **Notification**: Email notification for autoscale events.
- **Autoscale with Azure Functions**: Functions can trigger autoscaling actions through the Azure Management API.

## Cross-Cloud Autoscaling Comparison

Choosing between cloud providers for autoscaling depends on your workload requirements and existing infrastructure.

### Decision Matrix

| Feature | AWS | GCP | Azure |
|---------|-----|-----|-------|
| Target tracking | Yes (predefined and custom) | Yes (CPU, LB, custom) | Yes (CPU, memory, disk) |
| Step scaling | Yes (multi-tier) | No (use custom metrics) | Yes (multi-condition) |
| Scheduled scaling | Yes (cron) | Yes (cron) | Yes (schedule) |
| Predictive scaling | Yes (ML-based) | Yes (GA 2024) | Yes (preview) |

| Notifications | SNS, CloudWatch | Cloud Monitoring | Azure Monitor |

### Choosing a Provider

**Choose AWS when**: You need the most mature autoscaling ecosystem with the most features. AWS has the longest history of autoscaling and the widest set of integrations.

**Choose GCP when**: You are running Kubernetes-native workloads and want simpler autoscaling. GCP's managed instance groups integrate seamlessly with GKE.

**Choose Azure when**: You are already invested in the Microsoft ecosystem (Active Directory, .NET, SQL Server). Azure's autoscaling integrates natively with Azure Monitor and Application Insights.

## Organizational Lessons

### The Autoscaling Ownership Model

A common organizational failure is unclear ownership of autoscaling configuration. The application team owns the code, the platform team owns the infrastructure, and the SRE team owns the SLAs. Who owns the autoscaling configuration?

**Best practice**: The application team owns the scaling policy (which metric, what target, what are the min/max bounds). The platform team provides the autoscaling infrastructure and tooling. The SRE team sets the SLAs and monitors the scaling behavior. The three teams collaborate on the initial configuration and review the scaling activity quarterly.

### The Autoscaling Review Process

Successful organizations have a structured review process for autoscaling changes:

1. **Proposal**: The application team proposes a change to the scaling policy, including the expected impact on cost and performance.
2. **Review**: The platform team reviews the proposal for infrastructure compatibility. The SRE team reviews the proposal for SLA impact.
3. **Staging test**: The change is deployed to the staging environment and load-tested for 24 hours.
4. **Canary**: The change is deployed to 5% of production traffic for 24 hours.
5. **Full rollout**: The change is rolled out to 100% of production.
6. **Monitoring review**: After 1 week, the three teams review the scaling activity and cost impact.
7. **Optimization**: Based on the review, the policy is adjusted.

### Kubernetes Configuration: Production Web Application

```yaml
apiVersion: autoscaling/v2

  name: web-app

    apiVersion: apps/v1
    kind: Deployment
    name: web-app
  minReplicas: 10
  maxReplicas: 100
  metrics:
  - type: Resource
    resource:
      name: cpu
      target:
        type: Utilization
        averageUtilization: 50
  - type: Pods
    pods:
      metric:
        name: requests_per_second
      target:
        type: AverageValue
        averageValue: 500
  behavior:
    scaleDown:
      stabilizationWindowSeconds: 300
      policies:
      - type: Percent
        value: 10
        periodSeconds: 60
    scaleUp:
      stabilizationWindowSeconds: 0
      policies:
      - type: Percent
        value: 100
        periodSeconds: 15
      selectPolicy: Max
```

These configuration examples show how the same autoscaling design translates across different cloud providers and platforms. The core concepts are the same -- minimum/maximum bounds, target metrics, cooldown periods, and scheduled actions -- but the syntax and specific features differ.
