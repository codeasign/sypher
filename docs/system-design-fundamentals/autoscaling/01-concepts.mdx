# Understanding Autoscaling

## The Black Friday Meltdown

Your e-commerce platform has been running on 20 servers for six months. Peak traffic is 5,000 requests per second. The servers run at 40% CPU during normal hours and 70% during the evening rush. Everything is stable. Your CFO is happy with the predictable cloud bill.

Black Friday arrives. Traffic starts climbing at 6:00 AM. By 7:30 AM, traffic hits 8,000 requests per second. The servers hit 85% CPU. By 8:15 AM, traffic hits 15,000 requests per second. The load balancer health checks start failing. Servers are rejecting connections. The database connection pool is saturated. The checkout page renders in 14 seconds. At 8:23 AM, your CDO sends a Slack message to the engineering team: "The site is down. Revenue is bleeding. Fix it now."

The operations team scrambles. They SSH into the AWS console and manually launch 40 new instances. The instances take 4 minutes to initialize. By the time the new servers are ready, traffic has already dropped -- the morning rush is over and frustrated customers have abandoned their carts. You provisioned 40 servers for 20 minutes of peak traffic. The cloud bill for December shows a $12,000 overage from manual provisioning. The postmortem reveals that the manual scaling took 12 minutes from first alert to full capacity. The company lost an estimated $340,000 in revenue during those 12 minutes.

The root cause was not a lack of servers. The cloud provider had infinite capacity. The root cause was the gap between demand and action -- a human had to notice the alert, decide to scale, and manually execute the scaling action. By the time the human acted, the opportunity was gone.

## The Insight

**Autoscaling** is the practice of automatically adjusting the number of compute resources (servers, containers, or function instances) based on real-time demand, without human intervention.

Think of the ventilation fan in a commercial kitchen. When no one is cooking, the fan runs at a low speed. When a chef fires up six burners and two griddles, the heat and smoke rise. A sensor detects the change and automatically increases the fan speed. When the cooking stops, the fan slows back down. The chef never touches a switch. The fan adjusts to exactly the right speed for the current cooking load. Autoscaling works the same way for your infrastructure. It measures a signal (CPU, request rate, queue depth), compares it against a target, and adds or removes capacity to keep the signal at the desired level. The operations team never touches a console.

## Core Vocabulary

### Scale Out (Scale In)

**Scale Out** is the act of adding more instances to a resource pool to handle increased load. **Scale In** is the reverse -- removing instances when demand drops. These terms distinguish horizontal scaling actions from vertical scaling (scale up/scale down, which changes instance size). Autoscaling systems primarily manage horizontal scaling because cloud instances can be added and removed in seconds, while resizing an instance requires a reboot.

### Autoscaling Group

An **Autoscaling Group** (ASG) is a logical collection of compute instances managed as a single unit. The group defines a minimum size, maximum size, and desired capacity. The autoscaler adjusts the desired capacity based on current conditions. AWS Auto Scaling Groups, GCP Managed Instance Groups, and Azure Virtual Machine Scale Sets are the cloud provider implementations. The ASG also manages instance health -- if an instance fails a health check, the ASG terminates it and launches a replacement.

### Launch Template

A **Launch Template** (or Launch Configuration) is the blueprint for new instances. It specifies the machine image (AMI), instance type, security groups, IAM roles, user data scripts, and storage volumes. When the autoscaler decides to launch a new instance, it uses the launch template to create it. A well-configured launch template ensures every new instance is identical and ready to join the load balancer pool.

### Target Tracking

**Target Tracking** is a scaling policy that maintains a specific metric value. You set a target (for example, "keep CPU utilization at 50%") and the autoscaler adjusts capacity to hit that target. If actual CPU rises above 50%, the autoscaler adds instances. If CPU drops below 50%, the autoscaler removes instances. This is the simplest and most common scaling policy because it maps directly to a business goal. AWS provides predefined target tracking policies for CPU, memory, request count per target, and average network throughput.

### Step Scaling

**Step Scaling** is a policy that adds or removes a specific number of instances based on how far the metric is from the threshold. A step scaling policy has multiple tiers: when CPU exceeds 60%, add 2 instances; when CPU exceeds 80%, add 4 instances; when CPU drops below 40%, remove 1 instance; when CPU drops below 20%, remove 3 instances. Step scaling gives fine-grained control over scaling behavior but requires more tuning than target tracking.

### Scheduled Scaling

**Scheduled Scaling** adjusts capacity on a predetermined schedule. You define a cron expression or a timestamp and a desired capacity. Scheduled scaling is useful for predictable traffic patterns: "scale to 50 instances at 8:00 AM on weekdays, scale to 10 instances at 8:00 PM." It is the most predictable form of scaling but cannot react to unexpected traffic surges.

### Predictive Scaling

**Predictive Scaling** uses machine learning to analyze historical traffic patterns and forecast future demand. The autoscaler provisions capacity ahead of the predicted traffic spike, eliminating the warm-up delay. AWS Predictive Scaling uses 24 hours to 14 days of historical data to generate forecasts. Predictive scaling works best for traffic patterns that follow a daily or weekly cycle, like a SaaS platform with higher usage during business hours.

### Cooldown Period

A **Cooldown Period** is a waiting time after a scaling action during which the autoscaler will not perform another scaling action. Cooldowns prevent thrashing -- the rapid oscillation of adding and removing instances when metrics fluctuate near a threshold. A typical cooldown is 60 to 300 seconds. During cooldown, the metric may remain elevated or drop further, but the autoscaler waits for the system to stabilize before re-evaluating. Without cooldowns, a brief CPU spike could trigger a scale-out, which immediately lowers CPU, triggering a scale-in, which raises CPU again, triggering another scale-out.

### Warm-Up Time

**Warm-Up Time** (or initialization time) is the period between when an instance is launched and when it can handle traffic. During warm-up, the instance boots, runs user data scripts, downloads application code, connects to the database, and registers with the load balancer. Warm-up time varies from 30 seconds (container) to 5 minutes (VM with full application deploy). Autoscaling systems must account for warm-up when deciding how many instances to add. A system with 3-minute warm-up that adds 10 instances during a spike will not see the benefit for 3 full minutes.

### Scale-In Protection

**Scale-In Protection** prevents an instance from being terminated during a scale-in event. This is critical for instances that are processing long-running tasks, handling WebSocket connections, or holding in-memory state. Without scale-in protection, the autoscaler could terminate an instance that is mid-request, causing a user-facing error. Scale-in protection is typically set at the instance level and removed when the instance is safe to terminate.

### Desired Capacity

**Desired Capacity** is the number of instances the autoscaler is currently targeting. The autoscaler modifies desired capacity to match demand. If desired capacity is 10 and the minimum is 5, the autoscaler will launch 5 instances. If desired capacity drops to 3 and the minimum is 5, the autoscaler will not go below 5. The desired capacity is the central control variable -- every scaling policy ultimately adjusts this number.

## How It Actually Works -- Step by Step

### Step 1: Metrics Collection

Every instance in the pool reports metrics at regular intervals. The metrics agent on each instance collects CPU utilization, memory usage, network I/O, and application-level metrics (request count, queue depth, latency percentiles). These metrics are sent to a centralized monitoring system -- CloudWatch, Prometheus, or Datadog. The collection interval is typically 60 seconds but can be as fast as 1 second for critical metrics.

### Step 2: Metric Aggregation

The monitoring system aggregates metrics across all instances in the autoscaling group. The aggregation function is typically average, sum, or percentile. For CPU-based scaling, the average CPU across all instances is the most common metric. For request-based scaling, the sum of requests per second across all instances is used. The aggregated metric is evaluated every evaluation period (typically 60 to 300 seconds).

### Step 3: Policy Evaluation

The autoscaler controller evaluates the aggregated metric against the scaling policy. For a target tracking policy at 50% CPU, the controller compares the current average CPU (58%) against the target (50%). Because the current value exceeds the target, the controller determines that more capacity is needed.

### Step 4: Capacity Calculation

The controller calculates the desired capacity needed to bring the metric back to the target. For target tracking, the calculation is:

```
desired_capacity = ceil(current_capacity * (current_metric / target_metric))
```

With current capacity of 4 instances, current CPU of 58%, and target CPU of 50%:

```
desired_capacity = ceil(4 * (58 / 50)) = ceil(4.64) = 5
```

The controller requests 5 instances. Since the minimum is 3 and the maximum is 50, the new desired capacity of 5 is within bounds.

### Step 5: Launch Instances

The autoscaler controller calls the cloud provider's compute API to launch new instances. The launch template specifies the AMI, instance type, security groups, and any user data scripts. The API returns immediately, but the instances are not yet ready. They enter a Pending state.

### Step 6: Instance Initialization and Warm-Up

The new instance boots the operating system, runs the user data script, downloads the application artifacts, starts the application server, and connects to required services (database, cache, message queue). The application server performs a health check. The instance registers with the load balancer target group. During this entire warm-up period, the instance cannot serve traffic.

### Step 7: Registration with Load Balancer

Once the instance passes its health check, the load balancer registers it as a healthy target. Traffic begins flowing to the new instance. The load balancer uses a slow-start (or warm-up) mode that gradually increases the traffic share to the new instance over a configurable period, preventing a cold instance from being overwhelmed by a sudden flood of requests.

### Step 8: Metric Stabilization

After the new instance is in service, the aggregate metrics begin to reflect the added capacity. Average CPU drops as the new instance absorbs traffic. The autoscaler observes the new metrics at the next evaluation period. The cooldown timer prevents additional scaling actions during this stabilization period.

### Step 9: Scale-In Decision

When demand drops, the metric falls below the target. The autoscaler evaluates the policy again. With 5 instances and average CPU at 32%, the target tracking policy calculates that 3 instances are sufficient. The controller sets desired capacity to 3. The controller must decide which instances to terminate.

### Step 10: Connection Draining and Termination

Before terminating an instance, the autoscaler puts it in a Draining state. The load balancer stops sending new connections to the instance. Existing in-flight requests have a configurable timeout (30 to 300 seconds) to complete. After the draining period, the instance is terminated. The autoscaler respects scale-in protection -- instances with the protection flag set are not terminated.

## Variants and Flavors

### Variant 1: Reactive Autoscaling (Metric-Based)

Reactive autoscaling responds to current metrics in real-time. This is the most common variant. The autoscaler observes CPU, memory, request count, or custom metrics and adjusts capacity when thresholds are breached. The response time depends on the metric collection interval, evaluation period, and warm-up time. For a system with 60-second metrics and 3-minute warm-up, the total response time is approximately 5 minutes from traffic spike to new capacity online.

**Strengths**: Simple to configure, works for unpredictable traffic patterns, no historical data required.

**Weaknesses**: Always lags behind demand -- capacity arrives after the spike has already begun. Cannot handle flash crowds that grow faster than the warm-up time.

### Variant 2: Scheduled Autoscaling

Scheduled autoscaling adjusts capacity on a predetermined timetable. You define scaling actions with cron expressions or specific timestamps. This variant is ideal for predictable patterns: higher traffic during business hours, lower traffic at night, reduced capacity on weekends, or capacity adjustments for known events like product launches.

**Strengths**: Zero reaction time -- capacity is ready before traffic arrives. No warm-up delay because instances are launched ahead of time. Predictable cost.

**Weaknesses**: Cannot respond to unexpected traffic surges. Requires accurate traffic forecasting. If the schedule is wrong, you either over-provision (waste money) or under-provision (fail to serve traffic).

### Variant 3: Predictive Autoscaling

Predictive autoscaling uses machine learning models to forecast traffic and pre-provision capacity. The model is trained on historical metrics (24 hours to 14 days) and identifies daily, weekly, and seasonal patterns. The forecast generates capacity recommendations that are applied ahead of the predicted demand. Predictive scaling can be combined with reactive scaling -- the predictive component handles the baseline forecast, and the reactive component handles deviations from the forecast.

**Strengths**: Capacity is ready before demand arrives. Reduces the warm-up gap to near zero. Learns and adapts to changing patterns.

**Weaknesses**: Requires 14 days of historical data. Cannot predict novel patterns. ML model adds complexity and cost. May over-provision if the forecast is too conservative.

### Variant 4: Event-Driven Autoscaling

Event-driven autoscaling reacts to external events from a message queue or event stream. When a queue depth exceeds a threshold, the autoscaler launches workers to process the backlog. When the queue drains, the workers are terminated. This variant is common for batch processing, image processing pipelines, and job queues. The metric is usually queue depth (SQS queue size, Kafka consumer lag) rather than CPU or memory.

**Strengths**: Directly tied to work volume. Handles massive bursts if the queue is durable. Workers are ephemeral -- no idle capacity.

**Weaknesses**: Latency between enqueue and processing (workers must boot). Queue depth can grow unbounded if workers cannot keep up. Requires careful configuration of batch size and worker concurrency.

## Deep Dive: The Autoscaling Control Loop

The autoscaling system is a closed-loop control system. It continuously measures the output (the metric), compares it against the target, and adjusts the input (desired capacity) to minimize the error. This is the same feedback loop that controls a thermostat, a cruise control system, or a chemical processing plant.

### The Four Phases of the Control Loop

Every autoscaling evaluation cycle goes through four phases:

**1. Measure**: The system collects metrics from all instances in the group. The metrics are sampled at a configurable interval (typically 60 seconds for basic monitoring, 1 second for detailed monitoring). The samples are aggregated into a single value per metric (average, sum, or percentile depending on the policy).

**2. Compare**: The system compares the aggregated metric against the target. For a target tracking policy at 50% CPU, the system checks: is the current average CPU above or below 50%? The comparison result is the error signal -- how far the system is from the desired state.

**3. Decide**: The system calculates the required adjustment. For target tracking, this is the proportional control formula. For step scaling, this is the threshold-based decision tree. The decision accounts for the cooldown state -- if the system is in cooldown, no decision is made.

**4. Act**: The system executes the scaling action. It launches new instances (scale-out) or terminates existing instances (scale-in). The action is logged and the cooldown timer is started. The system then returns to the Measure phase for the next cycle.

### Control Loop Characteristics

The control loop has several important characteristics that affect autoscaling behavior:

**Dead time**: The delay between a change in the input (traffic spike) and the first measurable change in the output (metric rising). Dead time is caused by the metric collection interval -- if the spike happens 1 second after the metrics are collected, the system waits 59 seconds for the next data point.

**Time constant**: The speed at which the system responds to the error. A shorter metric collection interval and evaluation period give a shorter time constant, making the system more responsive. A longer time constant makes the system more stable but less responsive.

**Overshoot**: When the system adds capacity, it takes time (warm-up) before the metric responds. During this time, the system may continue to see elevated metrics and add even more capacity. When the first batch of instances comes online, the metric drops sharply, potentially below the target. This overshoot is a natural consequence of the warm-up delay.

**Steady-state error**: The residual difference between the metric and the target after the system has stabilized. Pure proportional control (target tracking) always has some steady-state error because the adjustment is proportional to the error. A 10% error produces a 10% adjustment, which reduces the error to approximately 1%, which produces a 1% adjustment, and so on. The system approaches the target asymptotically.

### The Scaling Equation

The complete autoscaling equation can be expressed as:

```
ΔC(t) = K_p * (M(t - d) - T) + S(t)
```

Where:
- ΔC(t) = change in desired capacity at time t
- K_p = proportional gain (typically 1.0 for target tracking)
- M(t-d) = metric value at time t-d (d is the measurement delay)
- T = target metric value
- S(t) = scheduled scaling contribution at time t

This equation shows that autoscaling is a proportional controller with a delay. The delay (d) is the sum of the metric collection interval, the evaluation period, and the alarm duration. The proportional gain (K_p) is fixed at 1.0 for target tracking but can be adjusted for step scaling by choosing different step sizes.

### Understanding the Delay

The total delay in the control loop is the sum of several components:

```
total_delay = metric_interval + evaluation_periods * metric_interval + alarm_duration + warm_up_time
```

For a typical configuration:
- metric_interval = 60 seconds
- evaluation_periods = 2
- alarm_duration = 0 (alarm triggers immediately after breach)
- warm_up_time = 180 seconds
- total_delay = 60 + 2*60 + 0 + 180 = 360 seconds (6 minutes)

This means the system takes 6 minutes from the start of a traffic spike to the moment new capacity is serving traffic. This is a hard physical limit of the control loop. No amount of tuning can reduce it below the sum of the component delays. The only way to bypass this limit is to use predictive scaling, which pre-provisions capacity before the spike occurs.

## Variants and Flavors (Continued)

### Variant 5: Target Tracking vs. Step Scaling -- When to Use Each

The choice between target tracking and step scaling is not arbitrary. Each has a specific use case.

**Target tracking** is the right choice when:
- You have a single metric that correlates well with overall load
- You want the autoscaler to automatically determine the right number of instances
- You do not have specific knowledge of how many instances to add per degree of metric change
- The relationship between load and capacity is roughly linear

**Step scaling** is the right choice when:
- You have expert knowledge of the exact capacity requirements at each load level
- The relationship between load and capacity is non-linear (adding 10 instances at 60% CPU is wrong, but adding 10 instances at 90% CPU is correct)
- You need to enforce specific scaling behavior that target tracking cannot achieve
- You want different scaling behaviors for different times of day or different traffic patterns

### Variant 6: Simple Scaling

Simple scaling is the predecessor to step scaling. It has a single threshold and a single action. When the metric exceeds the threshold, the system performs the configured action. When the metric drops below the threshold, the system performs the scale-in action.

Simple scaling is rarely used in production because it lacks the multi-tier granularity of step scaling. It is still available in AWS for backward compatibility but is not recommended for new deployments.

### Variant 7: Manual Scaling

Manual scaling is the absence of autoscaling -- a human operator monitors the system and adjusts capacity through the console or CLI. This is the baseline against which all autoscaling approaches are measured.

Manual scaling is acceptable for:
- Systems with very low traffic volume (under 100 req/s)
- Systems where capacity is fixed by contract (dedicated hardware)
- Development and staging environments
- Systems that are being retired

Manual scaling is not acceptable for:
- Production systems with variable traffic
- Systems with SLAs that require fast recovery from failures
- Systems that run outside business hours

## How Instance Termination Works

When the autoscaler decides to scale in, it must choose which instances to terminate. The termination policy determines the order of termination.

### Termination Policies

**Default (Oldest Launch Template)**: Terminates instances that were launched using the oldest launch template or configuration. This ensures that instances running older code are terminated first, which is useful during rolling deployments.

**Oldest Instance**: Terminates the instance that has been running the longest. This is useful for ensuring that no instance runs indefinitely.

**Newest Instance**: Terminates the instance that was launched most recently. This is useful if you want to minimize the cost of terminated instances (newer instances have run for less time).

**Closest to Next Instance Hour**: Terminates instances that are closest to the next hourly billing boundary. This minimizes the cost of partial instance hours. For example, an instance that has been running for 55 minutes is terminated instead of an instance that has been running for 10 minutes, because the 55-minute instance will incur another hour of billing at the 60-minute mark.

**Allocation Strategy**: For autoscaling groups with multiple instance types, the allocation strategy determines which specific instance to terminate based on the instance type distribution.

You can also configure a custom termination policy by specifying a Lambda function that receives the list of candidates and returns the instance to terminate.

## Mental Model Check

### Question 1

You run a video transcoding service on AWS. Transcoding jobs arrive via SQS queue. Each job takes 3-8 minutes to process. CPU utilization is the primary bottleneck. What scaling metric and policy would you use?

**Answer**: Use SQS queue depth as the scaling metric, not CPU. CPU utilization on a transcoding instance is always near 100% while it is working, so CPU-based scaling would never trigger (the metric is always high) or would trigger incorrectly. A better approach is to use a target tracking policy on the SQS queue depth (ApproximateNumberOfMessagesVisible) with a target of, for example, 100 messages. When the queue grows beyond 100, new instances launch. When the queue drains below 100, instances terminate. This directly ties capacity to the backlog of work.

### Question 2

Your web application experiences a sudden traffic spike from 1,000 to 10,000 requests per second in 30 seconds. Your autoscaling group has a 60-second metric interval, 300-second evaluation period, and 180-second warm-up time. How long after the spike starts before new capacity serves traffic?

**Answer**: 540 seconds (9 minutes). The timeline is: metric collection at 60 seconds (first data point showing the spike), evaluation period completes at 60 + 300 = 360 seconds (policy triggers), warm-up takes 180 seconds, so total is 360 + 180 = 540 seconds from the spike start. During those 9 minutes, the existing instances are handling 10x their normal load. This is why reactive scaling alone is insufficient for flash crowds -- you need predictive scaling or buffer capacity.

### Question 3

What is the difference between cooldown period and warm-up time? Can they overlap?

**Answer**: Warm-up time is the period from instance launch to the instance being ready to serve traffic. Cooldown period is the time after a scaling action during which the autoscaler will not perform another action. They can overlap -- the cooldown timer starts when the scaling action is initiated (or completed, depending on implementation), while the instance is still warming up. In AWS Auto Scaling, the cooldown period starts at the end of the scaling action. The purpose of the cooldown is to prevent the autoscaler from reacting to the metric changes caused by the scaling action itself (for example, CPU dropping as new instances come online, which could trigger a premature scale-in).

### Question 4

You have an autoscaling group with minimum 2, maximum 20, and desired capacity 4. A target tracking policy targets 50% CPU. Current average CPU is 65%. How many instances will the autoscaler target?

**Answer**: Using the formula `ceil(current_capacity * (current_metric / target_metric))` = `ceil(4 * (65/50))` = `ceil(5.2)` = 6 instances. The autoscaler will set desired capacity to 6, which is within the minimum (2) and maximum (20) bounds. The autoscaler will launch 2 new instances (from 4 to 6).

### Question 5

Your compliance team requires that no instance processing a user request is terminated during scale-in. What mechanism do you use?

**Answer**: Use scale-in protection combined with a lifecycle hook. Set the instance-level scale-in protection flag to true on every instance. Attach a lifecycle hook to the autoscaling group for the "instance-terminating" transition. When the autoscaler decides to scale in, it moves the instance to the "terminating:wait" state and triggers the lifecycle hook. A custom process (Lambda function) checks if the instance has active connections. If it does, the function extends the wait timeout. When connections are drained, the function signals CONTINUE to allow termination. Alternatively, the application running on the instance can remove scale-in protection when it is safe to terminate. This ensures no in-flight requests are interrupted.

### Question 6

Your autoscaling group has been running with 10 instances at 45% CPU for a week. On Monday at 9:00 AM, traffic spikes and CPU reaches 80%. The autoscaler launches 6 new instances (total 16). By 9:15 AM, CPU drops to 35%. The autoscaler terminates 2 instances (total 14). By 9:30 AM, CPU is back at 45%. Was the autoscaling behavior correct?

**Answer**: Yes, the behavior was correct. The autoscaler detected the 80% CPU breach, calculated that 16 instances were needed to bring CPU to 50%, and launched 6 instances. After warm-up, the new instances brought CPU below the target (35%) because the target tracking calculation assumes a linear relationship, and the actual demand may have dropped slightly during the warm-up period. The autoscaler then scaled in to bring CPU back toward the target. The system stabilized at 14 instances and 45% CPU. This is a normal adjustment cycle, not thrashing -- there was no oscillation between the same two capacities.

### Question 7

You are designing an autoscaling system for a critical financial trading application. The application cannot tolerate any latency degradation during scaling events. What approach do you take?

**Answer**: For a zero-tolerance latency scenario, reactive autoscaling is insufficient because of the warm-up gap. You need a combination of: (1) significant buffer capacity -- run at 25% CPU target so there is headroom for spikes, (2) predictive scaling to pre-warm capacity before expected market events (market open, economic data releases, options expiration), (3) scheduled scaling for known events, (4) a pre-warmed buffer pool of instances that are ready to join the target group within seconds. The cost of maintaining buffer capacity is the insurance premium against latency degradation. For a financial trading firm, this cost is justified.

### Question 8

You have two autoscaling groups: Group A serves user-facing API traffic. Group B processes background batch jobs. Both use the same instance type and the same launch template. How would you configure their scaling policies differently?

**Answer**: Group A (user-facing) should have: (1) target tracking on request count per target at 500 req/s, (2) aggressive scale-out with 60-second evaluation period, (3) cooldown of 120 seconds (short), (4) minimum of 3 instances across 3 AZs, (5) ELB health checks. Group B (batch processing) should have: (1) target tracking on SQS queue depth, (2) conservative scale-out with 300-second evaluation period (batch jobs can wait), (3) cooldown of 600 seconds (long), (4) minimum of 0 instances (can scale to zero), (5) EC2 health checks (no load balancer needed), (6) spot instances for cost savings. The user-facing group prioritizes responsiveness. The batch group prioritizes cost efficiency.

### Question 9

Your application has a cold start problem -- new instances take 8 minutes to initialize because they must download a 5 GB model file from S3. How do you handle autoscaling with this long warm-up time?

**Answer**: An 8-minute warm-up time makes reactive autoscaling nearly useless for any traffic pattern that changes faster than 8 minutes. Several approaches can mitigate this: (1) Use a warm pool with instances that have already downloaded the model file and are in a stopped state. When scaling is needed, start the stopped instances (takes 30-60 seconds instead of 8 minutes). (2) Pre-bake the model file into the AMI so that every new instance already has the model. This increases AMI size but eliminates the download time. (3) Use predictive scaling to pre-warm capacity 10 minutes before the forecasted demand. (4) If the model file can be loaded lazily (on first request instead of at startup), reduce the health check path to a simple ping and load the model on-demand. The first few requests to the new instance will be slow, but the instance becomes ready much faster.

### Question 10

You notice that your autoscaling group consistently runs at 30% CPU, which is below the 50% target. What is wrong?

**Answer**: Several possibilities: (1) The minimum size is too high -- the autoscaler cannot scale in below the minimum, so excess capacity runs permanently. Reduce the minimum to match the baseline traffic. (2) The scale-in cooldown is too long -- the system cannot remove instances fast enough to match the demand drop. Reduce the cooldown for scale-in. (3) The scale-in policy is too conservative -- step scaling policies often have asymmetric thresholds (aggressive scale-out, conservative scale-in). Adjust the scale-in threshold. (4) Predictive scaling is over-provisioning -- if predictive scaling is enabled, the forecast may be too conservative. Check the forecast accuracy and adjust the confidence interval. (5) The instance size is too large -- you may be using instances that are too powerful for the workload. Switch to a smaller instance type or use a mixed instances policy with smaller instances.

## Summary

Autoscaling is the mechanism that automatically adjusts compute resources based on real-time demand. It is a critical component of any production system that experiences variable traffic. The core concepts to remember are:

- **Metrics drive decisions**: Choose the right metric for your workload (CPU for compute-bound, request count for network-bound, queue depth for batch processing).
- **Policies define behavior**: Target tracking is simpler and preferred. Step scaling gives fine-grained control. Scheduled scaling handles predictable patterns. Predictive scaling eliminates the warm-up gap.
- **Lifecycle management**: Instances go through Pending, InService, Draining, and Terminating states. Each state has specific behaviors and configuration options.
- **Failure modes**: Thrashing, runaway scaling, slow scale-out, and scale-in storms are the most common failure modes. Each has specific causes and mitigations.
- **Cost-performance trade-off**: Autoscaling does not save money by default. It optimizes the trade-off between cost and performance. The optimal balance depends on your business priorities.
