# Error Budgets — Ace the Interview

## How This Topic Appears in Interviews

Error budgets appear in system design interviews in these common phrasings:

1. "Design an error budget system for a large-scale microservice architecture."
2. "How would you implement error budgets in a multi-tenant SaaS platform?"
3. "Your team keeps burning through the error budget every month. How do you fix it?"
4. "Design a burn rate alerting system for a service with a 99.9% SLO."
5. "How do error budgets relate to SLOs and SLIs? Walk me through the full pipeline."
6. "A service has 30 minutes of downtime in a 30-day window. Is it meeting its 99.9% SLO?"
7. "How would you handle error budget policy when deploying to production?"
8. "Design a system that prevents deployment when the error budget is exhausted."
9. "Your payment service has a 99.99% SLO. The database had 45 seconds of unplanned downtime this month. Walk me through the decision process."
10. "How do you calculate error budget burn rate, and what thresholds do you alert on?"

## What the Interviewer Tests

**At the core, interviewers want to know if you understand the operational feedback loop that error budgets create.** They are not testing whether you can memorize the formula. They are testing whether you can apply the formula to make practical engineering decisions.

**Signal 1: Precision.** Candidates who say "error budget is the amount of downtime you can have" without showing the math score lower. Candidates who say "for a 99.9% SLO over a 30-day window, the budget is 1 - 0.999 times 2,592,000 seconds, giving 2,592 seconds of allowed downtime" score higher. Precision signals that you have worked with these numbers in practice.

**Signal 2: Policy thinking.** The interviewer wants to see that you understand how error budgets create behavioral change. A candidate who says "the error budget is 2,592 seconds" and stops is missing the point. A candidate who says "when the budget is exhausted, we stop all deployments and enter a maintenance-only mode" shows they understand the feedback loop.

**Signal 3: Multi-window awareness.** The best candidates discuss burn rate across multiple time windows. They understand that a 1-hour window catches fast burn, a 6-hour window catches medium burn, and a 24-hour window catches slow burn. They can calculate the time to exhaustion at each burn rate.

**Signal 4: Multi-service complexity.** The interviewer wants to see how you handle the real world. A system with 50 microservices, each with its own SLO, cannot be managed with a single budget. The best candidates discuss budget aggregation, per-service budgets, and cascading failure attribution.

**Signal 5: Trade-off articulation.** Error budgets are fundamentally about trading reliability for velocity. The interviewer wants to see you discuss this trade-off explicitly. When do you let a deployment proceed even though the budget is tight? When do you freeze? These judgment calls separate senior candidates.

## Level-by-Level Expectations

### L3 / Early Career

At this level, the interviewer expects you to understand the basic concepts and perform simple calculations.

**What you must demonstrate:**
- You can calculate error budget from an SLO and time window
- You understand the relationship between SLI, SLO, and error budget
- You can explain why error budgets exist (they balance reliability and velocity)
- You can describe a simple deployment gating policy

**Example question:** "A service has a 99.9% SLO over 30 days. It has experienced 2 hours of downtime. Is it within budget?"

**Your response:** "The total window is 30 days, which is 2,592,000 seconds. The allowed downtime at 99.9% is 0.1% of that, or 2,592 seconds, which is 43.2 minutes. Two hours of downtime is 7,200 seconds, which is more than 2,592 seconds. The service has exhausted its budget. It is not meeting its SLO."

**Typical weakness at this level:** Stopping at the calculation. The interviewer wants to hear what you would do about the exhausted budget, not just the math.

### L4 / Mid-Level

At this level, the interviewer expects you to design a simple error budget system and discuss policy.

**What you must demonstrate:**
- You can design SLIs for a given service
- You can define an error budget policy (what happens when budget is exhausted)
- You understand burn rate and can calculate time to exhaustion
- You can discuss the trade-off between reliability and deployment velocity

**Example question:** "Design an error budget policy for a team of 10 engineers working on a payment service."

**Your response:** "I would start by defining the SLO: 99.99% availability over a 30-day window, which gives 25.92 seconds of allowed downtime per month. The SLI would be the ratio of successful payment transactions to total payment transactions, with a 5-second timeout window. For policy: at 75% budget remaining, business as usual. At 50% remaining, deployments require a second reviewer. At 25% remaining, only critical bug fixes are deployed. At 0%, all deployments stop and a formal incident review is triggered. I would also track burn rate across three windows: 1-hour, 6-hour, and 24-hour, each with different alerting thresholds."

**Typical weakness at this level:** Not discussing the burn rate multi-window strategy. Many candidates know about policies but not about early detection of budget exhaustion.

### L5 / Senior

At this level, the interviewer expects you to design a multi-service error budget system and handle complex scenarios.

**What you must demonstrate:**
- You can design error budgets across a dependency graph of services
- You understand cascading failures and budget attribution
- You can design a federated budget system for multiple teams
- You can discuss the organizational impact of error budgets

**Example question:** "Design an error budget system for a platform with 20 microservices organized into three tiers: API gateway, business logic, and data storage."

**Your response:** "I would start with a top-down approach. The user-facing SLO is 99.9%, which becomes the composite target. Each tier gets a budget allocation: API gateway gets 99.99% (tightest because it is the entry point), business logic gets 99.95%, and data storage gets 99.9%. The composability means 0.9999 * 0.9995 * 0.999 = 0.9984, which is 99.84% — still above the 99.8% target. I would track budgets per service and also a 'shared budget pool' for cascading failures. When the API gateway fails due to a data storage issue, the budget burn is attributed to the data storage service, not the API gateway. This prevents unfair budget exhaustion on downstream services. I would also implement a 'budget transfer' mechanism: services that consistently under-burn can donate budget to services that are struggling."

**Typical weakness at this level:** Not discussing the organizational implications. Error budgets are as much about team behavior as about technology.

### L6 / Staff

At this level, the interviewer expects you to design organization-wide error budget systems and discuss strategic trade-offs.

**What you must demonstrate:**
- You can design error budgets for a multi-product, multi-team organization
- You understand the incentive effects of error budget policies
- You can design a system that handles exception requests and policy overrides
- You can discuss the relationship between error budgets and incident response maturity

**Example question:** "Design an error budget system for a company with 500 engineers across 40 teams, each with different risk tolerance and business criticality."

**Your response:** "I would not use a single error budget policy for the entire organization. Instead, I would implement a tiered system. Tier 1 services (payment, authentication, core database) have a strict budget with automatic deployment gating. Tier 2 services (search, recommendations) have a moderate policy with manual review gates. Tier 3 services (internal tools, dashboards) have a looser policy with informational alerts only. Each tier has a different SLO target and a different policy for budget exhaustion. I would also create a 'budget council' — a rotating group of senior engineers who review exception requests. When a team wants to deploy despite having exhausted their budget, they present their case to the council. This creates accountability without being rigid. Additionally, I would track error budget trends per team to identify teams that consistently over-burn, and pair them with SRE mentors to improve their reliability practices."

**Typical weakness at this level:** Not discussing the exception handling process. The best error budget systems have well-defined override mechanisms for the cases where the policy is too rigid.

### L7 / Principal

At this level, the interviewer expects a strategic discussion about error budgets as an organizational lever.

**What you must demonstrate:**
- You can discuss error budgets as a tool for organizational alignment
- You understand the cultural prerequisites for successful error budget adoption
- You can design a system that evolves with the organization's maturity
- You can discuss the limitations of error budgets and when not to use them

**Example question:** "Our company wants to adopt error budgets across all teams, but engineering culture is currently 'move fast and break things.' How do you approach this transformation?"

**Your response:** "Error budgets are a cultural change, not a technical one. You cannot impose error budgets on a culture that celebrates speed over reliability. I would start with a pilot program with 2-3 teams that already care about reliability. Document their success stories: lower pager fatigue, fewer production incidents, better deployment confidence. Use those stories to build a case for wider adoption. I would also design the initial policy to be generous — maybe 50% more budget than the SLO requires — to avoid the perception that error budgets are a punishment. As the organization matures, tighten the policy. The goal is to create a culture where teams voluntarily use their error budget to make informed decisions, not to feel policed by a central SRE team."

## FAANG Interview Framework

### The C-AR-D Approach

When answering an error budget design question, structure your response around four sections:

**1. Clarify (2 minutes)**
- Confirm the scope: "How many services? What are the SLO targets? Is this a single service or a multi-service system?"
- Ask about the organization: "How many teams? What is the deployment frequency? Is there an existing SRE team?"
- Understand the constraints: "Is there a regulatory requirement? What is the cost of downtime?"
- This section shows you do not jump to solutions without understanding the problem.

**2. Calculate (3 minutes)**
- Show the math: "For a 99.9% SLO over 30 days, the budget is 2,592 seconds."
- Calculate burn rates: "At 2x burn rate, the budget lasts 15 days. At 10x burn rate, it lasts 2 hours."
- Show multi-window awareness: "I need to track 1-hour, 6-hour, and 24-hour windows."
- This section signals that you are technically precise.

**3. Design (5 minutes)**
- Draw the architecture: data sources, aggregation, alerting, policy engine
- Define the components: SLI collection, budget calculation, burn rate monitoring, deployment gating
- Show the policy: what happens at each budget consumption level
- This section is the bulk of your answer.

**4. Discuss (5 minutes)**
- Trade-offs: "Stricter budgets reduce deployment velocity but improve reliability."
- Failure modes: "What happens when the monitoring system itself goes down?"
- Extensions: "How would this design scale to 500 services?"
- This section shows depth and seniority.

### Practice Drills

**Drill 1: The 3-Minute Calculation**

Set a timer for 3 minutes. Calculate the error budget for a service with the following SLOs, each with a 30-day window:
- 99%
- 99.9%
- 99.99%
- 99.999%
- 99.9999%

Then calculate the budget burn rate per second for each level. Answer: 99% = 2,592,000 seconds budget, 0.83 seconds per second of burn rate. 99.9% = 2,592 seconds, 0.83 milliseconds per second. 99.99% = 259.2 seconds, 0.083 milliseconds per second. 99.999% = 25.92 seconds, 0.0083 milliseconds per second. 99.9999% = 2.592 seconds, 0.00083 milliseconds per second.

**Drill 2: The Policy Design Blitz**

Pick a random service (e.g., video encoding, payment processing, email delivery). Spend 5 minutes designing:
- The SLO target and justification
- Three SLIs with measurement details
- A four-stage error budget policy (green, yellow, orange, red)
- A burn rate alerting strategy with three windows

**Drill 3: The Scenario Walkthrough**

Practice walking through these scenarios without notes:
1. "The database has been slow for the last 2 hours, consuming 15% of the monthly error budget. What do you do?"
2. "A new deployment went out and immediately caused a 10% error rate. The budget is at 40% remaining with 20 days left. Do you rollback?"
3. "The error budget monitoring system itself goes down for 4 hours. How do you handle the missing data?"
4. "A business stakeholder wants to deploy a critical feature, but the budget is exhausted. How do you handle the conflict?"

**Drill 4: The Multi-Service Budget Calculation**

Your system has three services with these SLOs:
- Gateway: 99.99%
- Orders: 99.9%
- Notifications: 99.5%

Calculate the composite SLO. Then, if the Gateway has 10 seconds of downtime, the Orders service has 200 seconds, and the Notifications service has 1,500 seconds, determine which services are within budget (30-day window).

**Answer:** Composite SLO = 0.9999 * 0.999 * 0.995 = 0.9939 = 99.39%. Gateway budget = 259.2 seconds (10 used, within budget). Orders budget = 2,592 seconds (200 used, within budget). Notifications budget = 12,960 seconds (1,500 used, within budget). All services are within budget individually, but the composite SLO of 99.39% may be below the user-facing target.

**Drill 5: The Burn Rate Analysis**

A service has a 99.9% SLO over a 30-day window. In the last 6 hours, it consumed 14% of the total budget. Calculate the burn rate and determine if an alert should fire.

**Answer:** Budget consumed in 6 hours = 14% of 2,592 = 362.88 seconds. Expected consumption in 6 hours = (2,592 / 720) * 6 = 21.6 seconds. Burn rate = 362.88 / 21.6 = 16.8x. This exceeds the 10x fast burn threshold, so a critical alert should fire. At this burn rate, the budget will be exhausted in 720 / 16.8 = 42.8 hours, even though the service has only been burning for 6 hours.

**Drill 6: The Deployment Gate Decision**

Your deployment gate checks three conditions:
1. Budget remaining: 12%
2. Burn rate (1-hour): 3.2x
3. Budget remaining without current incident: 18%

The deployment is a critical security patch. The deployment gate policy says: "Block all deployments when budget is under 10% or burn rate exceeds 5x." The budget is at 12% (above 10%) and the burn rate is 3.2x (under 5x). However, the budget is trending down rapidly. Do you approve the deployment?

**Answer:** The policy technically allows the deployment (12% above 10% threshold, 3.2x below 5x threshold). However, the trend is concerning. The correct approach is to approve the security patch (critical) but require a post-deployment monitoring plan. Also, investigate why the burn rate is 3.2x and whether the security patch might exacerbate the issue. The exception process should be used here: approve the deployment, but flag it for review.

**Drill 7: The Communication Challenge**

You need to explain to your VP of Product why deployments are blocked for the rest of the month. The error budget is exhausted. The VP wants to deploy a feature that was promised to a major customer. Write a 3-sentence response.

**Model response:** "Our error budget is our reliability bank account, and it is currently empty. If we deploy this feature and it causes any instability, we cannot absorb the failure without violating our commitment to all customers. We can deploy this feature on the first day of next month when the budget resets, or we can discuss a one-time exception with a detailed rollback plan."

**Drill 8: The System Design Whiteboard**

In 10 minutes, sketch a complete error budget system for a company with 10 services, 3 regions, and 500 developers. Include:
- Data collection architecture
- Budget calculation service
- Alerting strategy
- Deployment gate integration
- Dashboard design

Practice this drill until you can draw the architecture from memory in under 10 minutes. The interviewer wants to see that you can design a complete system, not just explain the theory.

## Phrase Bank

Use these phrases to sound confident and precise in your interview responses:

1. **"The error budget is the amount of failure your system can tolerate while still meeting its SLO."** — Opening definition.

2. **"For a 99.9% SLO over a 30-day window, that gives us 2,592 seconds of allowed downtime."** — Shows calculation precision.

3. **"The burn rate tells us how fast we are consuming the budget. At 2x burn rate, we would exhaust the budget in half the window."** — Shows burn rate understanding.

4. **"I would use a multi-window approach: 1-hour for fast burns, 6-hour for medium burns, 24-hour for slow burns."** — Shows mature alerting strategy.

5. **"When the budget is exhausted, we enter a maintenance-only mode. No risky changes, no new deployments."** — Shows policy thinking.

6. **"Error budgets create a shared vocabulary between engineering and product teams."** — Shows organizational awareness.

7. **"The trade-off is reliability versus velocity. Tighter budgets mean fewer deployments but more stability."** — Shows trade-off articulation.

8. **"I would track budgets per service and also maintain a composite budget for the user-facing experience."** — Shows multi-service thinking.

9. **"Cascading failures should be attributed to the root cause service, not the downstream service that was affected."** — Shows failure attribution awareness.

10. **"The monitoring system itself needs an error budget. Otherwise, you cannot detect when your observability stack is broken."** — Shows meta-level thinking.

11. **"I would implement a budget council to review exception requests when teams need to deploy despite exhausted budgets."** — Shows governance design.

12. **"Error budgets are not about punishing teams. They are about creating a data-driven framework for risk decisions."** — Shows cultural understanding.

13. **"The granularity of the budget window matters. Shorter windows catch fast failures but create more noise. Longer windows are more stable but react slowly."** — Shows design trade-off thinking.

14. **"I would exclude planned maintenance from the budget if the business accepts it, but include it if the team needs the discipline to minimize downtime."** — Shows nuanced policy design.

15. **"The goal is not zero failures. The goal is to make sure failures stay within the budget."** — Core philosophy statement.

## Red Flags

Avoid these mistakes in your interview:

1. **Answering without doing the math.** Even for a simple question, show the calculation. Interviewers want to see precision.

2. **Confusing SLI, SLO, and error budget.** These are distinct concepts. SLI is the measurement. SLO is the target. Error budget is the allowed deviation. Mixing them up signals confusion.

3. **Failing to discuss policy.** The calculation is necessary but not sufficient. The policy around what happens when the budget is exhausted is the most important part.

4. **Ignoring multi-service complexity.** Real systems have many services. A single-budget answer for a multi-service question shows lack of experience.

5. **Not mentioning burn rate.** Error budgets are dynamic. Burn rate tells you how fast you are consuming budget. An answer that only discusses total budget is incomplete.

6. **Being too rigid about enforcement.** A policy that never allows exceptions is unrealistic. Mention that you need a review process for exceptions.

7. **Forgetting about the monitoring system itself.** The monitoring system that tracks error budgets can also fail. Discuss how you handle monitoring downtime.

8. **Not discussing the organizational impact.** Error budgets change team behavior. Failing to mention this signals that you do not understand the cultural dimension.

9. **Claiming error budgets solve all problems.** Error budgets are a tool, not a silver bullet. Discuss their limitations honestly.

10. **Using vague language.** "Some downtime" is not acceptable. "43.2 minutes of allowed downtime" is precise. Use concrete numbers.

## Follow-Up Questions to Expect

Interviewers often follow up with deeper questions after you give your initial answer. Be prepared for these:

**Follow-up 1: "What if the budget is exhausted because of a DDoS attack?"**
Your response should acknowledge that the DDoS is not a normal operational failure. Consider excluding DDoS-triggered errors from the budget, or providing a separate "security incident" budget. The key is that the team should not be penalized for events they cannot control.

**Follow-up 2: "How do you handle the budget for a newly launched service?"**
A new service has no historical data. Your response: start with a generous SLO (99%) and a short window (7 days). As the service matures and collects data, tighten the SLO and extend the window. This gives the new service room to breathe while still establishing the reliability habit.

**Follow-up 3: "How do you convince a skeptical engineering team to adopt error budgets?"**
This is a culture question. Your response: start with a pilot on one service. Show the team how error budgets helped them make better deployment decisions. Share data: "We had 50% fewer rollbacks after adopting error budgets." Let the results speak for themselves rather than mandating adoption.

**Follow-up 4: "What if the monitoring system that tracks the error budget goes down?"**
Your response: the monitoring system itself must have an error budget. If the monitoring system goes down, you lose visibility into every other service's budget. The monitoring system should be treated as a Tier 1 service with a 99.99% SLO. Also, implement a "last known good" cache that stores the most recent budget state for at least 48 hours.

**Follow-up 5: "How do you set SLOs for a brand new service with no historical data?"**
Your response: use industry benchmarks for similar services. Set the initial SLO conservatively (looser than you expect). After 30 days of data, tighten it. After 90 days, lock it in. The initial SLO is a starting point, not a permanent commitment.

## Mock Interview Questions with Model Answers

### Question 1: Design an Error Budget for a Payment Service

**Prompt:** "Our payment service processes 1 million transactions per day. The business requires 99.99% availability. Design the error budget system."

**Sample Answer:** "I would start by defining the SLO: 99.99% availability over a 30-day rolling window. The budget is 0.01% of the window, which is 259.2 seconds per month. I would also define a latency SLO: 99.9% of requests complete in under 500ms.

For the SLI, I would measure the ratio of successful payment transactions to total payment transactions. A successful transaction is one that returns HTTP 2xx within 500ms. Transactions that return 5xx, timeout, or exceed 500ms are failures.

For the policy: at 50% budget remaining, normal operations. At 25%, require a second reviewer for deployments. At 10%, block all non-critical deployments. At 0%, block all deployments and trigger an incident review.

For burn rate alerting: 1-hour window at 10x burn rate (pages on-call), 6-hour window at 2x burn rate (creates a ticket), 24-hour window at 1.5x burn rate (dashboard trend)."

### Question 2: Handling Error Budget Exhaustion

**Prompt:** "The payment service has exhausted its error budget for the month. The VP of Product wants to deploy a critical feature. What do you do?"

**Sample Answer:** "I would start by acknowledging the tension. The business needs the feature, and the reliability policy says we should not deploy. I would not simply block the deployment or approve it. Instead, I would assess the risk.

First, I would check the burn rate. If the budget was exhausted gradually (low burn rate), the service may be inherently unreliable, and deploying would make things worse. If the budget was exhausted by a single incident that has been fixed, the service may be stable now.

Second, I would check the deployment risk. Is this a database schema change (high risk) or a frontend tweak (low risk)? Is there a rollback plan? Is there a canary deployment strategy?

Third, I would propose a compromise: deploy the feature to 10% of traffic. Monitor the burn rate for 24 hours. If the burn rate stays under 1x, roll out to 100%. If the burn rate spikes, rollback immediately.

This approach respects the spirit of the error budget (protecting reliability) while giving the business an option to move forward with appropriate safeguards."

### Question 3: Multi-Service Error Budget Architecture

**Prompt:** "Design an error budget system for a platform with 50 microservices. How do you track budgets across services?"

**Sample Answer:** "I would use a three-tier approach. First, each service has its own individual budget based on its SLO. Second, critical user journeys have composite budgets that aggregate the services involved. Third, the overall platform has a top-level budget.

For the individual budgets, each service defines its SLO based on its criticality: payment services at 99.99%, content services at 99.9%, internal tools at 99%. Each service has its own budget, burn rate alerts, and deployment gate.

For the composite budgets, I would define the critical user journeys: checkout, search, and login. The checkout composite budget combines the cart, payment, and notification services. If any of these services fails, the checkout fails, and the composite budget is consumed.

For the top-level budget, I would calculate the weighted average of all service SLOs, weighted by traffic volume. This gives a single number that represents the overall platform health. The top-level budget is used for executive reporting, not for deployment gating.

The key challenge is attribution. When a checkout fails, is it because the payment service failed or the cart service failed? I would use distributed tracing to identify the root cause and attribute the budget consumption to the correct service."

### Question 4: Error Budgets and SLA Compliance

**Prompt:** "Our customer SLA is 99.9% availability. How do you set the internal SLO and error budget?"

**Sample Answer:** "The internal SLO should be tighter than the customer SLA. If the SLA is 99.9%, I would set the SLO to 99.95%. This gives us a buffer of 0.05% for measurement error, edge cases, and incidents that are not visible to customers.

The error budget would be based on the SLO, not the SLA. For a 30-day window: budget = (1 - 0.9995) * 2,592,000 = 1,296 seconds. This is half of what the SLA allows (2,592 seconds). The extra buffer ensures that we are always performing better than our SLA commitment.

I would also set a warning threshold at 60% of the SLO budget. If we consume 60% of the SLO budget, we are at 99.97% actual availability, which is still above the 99.9% SLA but approaching the 99.95% SLO. This gives us early warning before we get close to the SLA limit."

### Question 5: Error Budgets for a New Service

**Prompt:** "We are launching a new service with no historical data. How do we set the initial SLO and error budget?"

**Sample Answer:** "For a new service, I would start with a conservative SLO of 99% over a 7-day window. This is generous enough that the team can deploy frequently without worrying about budget exhaustion, but tight enough to start building the reliability habit.

After 30 days of data, I would review the actual availability. If the service is consistently achieving 99.5% or higher, I would tighten the SLO to 99.5%. After 90 days, I would tighten to 99.9% if the service is stable.

The window should also evolve. Start with 7 days, extend to 14 days after 30 days, and then to 30 days after 90 days. This gradual approach allows the team to build reliability practices without being overwhelmed by a tight budget from day one.

The key principle: start loose, tighten over time. A new service should not be burdened with the same reliability requirements as a mature service."

### Question 6: Error Budgets for a Third-Party Dependency

**Prompt:** "Our service depends on a third-party API with a 99.9% SLA. Our own SLO is 99.99%. How do we handle the error budget?"

**Sample Answer:** "This is a classic dependency problem. Our internal SLO (99.99%) is tighter than our dependency's SLA (99.9%). If the dependency is down for 30 minutes, it consumes our entire budget even though the failure is not our fault.

I would approach this in three ways. First, I would measure the dependency's actual availability and include it in our composite SLO calculation. If the dependency is consistently below 99.99%, we need to renegotiate the SLA or find an alternative provider.

Second, I would create a separate 'dependency failure' budget that is 30% of our total budget. This prevents dependency failures from consuming our entire budget and blocking our deployments.

Third, I would build a circuit breaker and fallback mechanism. If the dependency is slow or failing, we fail fast instead of waiting for timeouts. This reduces the impact on our error budget because we can return a cached response or a degraded experience instead of a complete failure."

### Question 7: Error Budgets for a Batch Processing System

**Prompt:** "Our batch processing system runs daily jobs that take 2-4 hours. The system does not have HTTP requests. How do we define SLIs and error budgets?"

**Sample Answer:** "Batch processing systems require a different approach because the SLI is not based on HTTP request/response cycles. I would define the SLI in terms of job completion: the fraction of daily batch jobs that complete successfully within the scheduled window.

The SLO would be: 99.9% of daily batch jobs complete within 4 hours of the scheduled start time. The error budget would be the number of jobs that can fail or exceed the time window.

For the SLI measurement, I would track:
- Job start time (scheduled vs. actual)
- Job completion time (elapsed duration)
- Job status (success, failure, timeout)
- Data quality checks (record count, schema validation)

The error budget calculation would be: (total jobs - failed jobs) / total jobs. If 3 out of 1,000 daily jobs fail in a month, the availability is 99.7%.

The key difference from request-based systems is that batch jobs have a longer feedback cycle. The burn rate is measured in days, not hours. Multi-window alerting would use 1-day, 3-day, and 7-day windows instead of 1-hour, 6-hour, and 24-hour."

### Question 8: Error Budgets for a Real-Time System

**Prompt:** "Our real-time messaging system has a latency SLO of 99.9% of messages delivered in under 100ms. How do we design the error budget?"

**Sample Answer:** "For a latency-based SLO, the error budget is measured in terms of messages that exceed the latency threshold, not in terms of seconds of downtime. The budget is: (1 - 0.999) * total messages = 0.1% of messages can exceed 100ms.

The SLI is the fraction of messages delivered in under 100ms. This requires measuring the latency of every message, which means instrumentation at the delivery point, not the send point.

The error budget policy would be similar to an availability-based budget, but the burn rate alerting would use message-level metrics instead of time-based metrics. A 'fast burn' would be: 10x the expected number of slow messages in a 1-hour window.

The challenge is that latency-based budgets are more sensitive to noise. A single noisy tenant or a network blip can cause a spike in slow messages. I would use a rolling percentile and exclude outliers above the 99.99th percentile to smooth out the noise."

## Telephone Game: Explaining Error Budgets

Interviewers often ask you to explain error budgets to different audiences. Practice these translations:

**To a Product Manager:**
"Think of error budgets as a reliability bank account. Every month, you get 43 minutes of downtime to spend. When you deploy a new feature, there is a small chance it will cause issues. If the issues are within the budget, great. If you exhaust the budget, we pause deployments until the account refills. This helps us balance shipping features with keeping the system stable."

**To a CEO:**
"We have a reliability target of 99.9% uptime, which means we can be down for about 43 minutes per month. Every outage, slow deployment, or database migration consumes some of those 43 minutes. When we run out of minutes, we stop deploying new features until the next month. This prevents us from making the system less reliable while still moving fast."

**To a Junior Engineer:**
"The error budget is the amount of failure we are allowed to have. Your deployment might introduce a small regression that causes 2 minutes of downtime. That is fine — we have budget for that. But if the team has already had 40 minutes of downtime this month, we should not deploy your change until next month. The budget tells us when it is safe to take risks."

**To a Customer:**
"We track our reliability very carefully. Our target is 99.9% uptime, and we have a dashboard that shows how we are doing in real time. If we are at risk of missing our target, we temporarily slow down our feature releases to focus on stability. This is how we ensure that our service stays reliable for you."

## Final Interview Tips

1. **Always show your math.** Even if the question is high-level, calculate the error budget. Interviewers want to see that you can do the arithmetic.

2. **Discuss the trade-off.** Every error budget decision is a trade-off between reliability and velocity. Explicitly mention the trade-off in your answer.

3. **Mention the burn rate.** The most common mistake is to only discuss the absolute budget. The burn rate is more important because it gives early warning.

4. **Talk about policy.** The calculation is step one. The policy around what happens when the budget is exhausted is step two. The exception process is step three.

5. **Be specific about numbers.** Do not say "some downtime." Say "43.2 minutes per month for a 99.9% SLO." Specificity signals experience.

6. **Acknowledge the organizational dimension.** Error budgets are as much about team behavior as about technology. Mention the cultural aspects.

7. **Have a structured answer format.** Use the C-AR-D framework: Clarify, Calculate, Design, Discuss. This shows you can think systematically.

8. **Practice the whiteboard.** Draw the architecture. Interviewers want to see you sketch the system, not just talk about it.

9. **Prepare for follow-ups.** The interviewer will likely ask "what if" questions. Have your answers ready for common follow-ups.

10. **Stay calm under pressure.** If you make a calculation error, correct it and move on. The interviewer cares more about your process than your arithmetic.

## Common Interview Mistakes — Deep Dive

Beyond the red flags listed earlier, here are specific mistakes candidates make in error budget interviews and how to avoid them.

### Mistake: Treating All Services the Same

Candidates often propose a single error budget policy for all services. This shows a lack of real-world experience. In practice, payment services, content services, and internal analytics services all have different reliability requirements.

**Better approach:** "I would segment services into tiers. Tier 1 services (payment, authentication) get a strict budget and aggressive policy. Tier 2 services (search, recommendations) get a moderate policy. Tier 3 services (internal tools) get a lenient policy."

### Mistake: Ignoring the Human Element

Candidates focus entirely on the technical implementation and forget that error budgets are about changing team behavior. The best technical system fails if the team does not buy into the policy.

**Better approach:** "I would start with a pilot on one team to build buy-in. I would make the budget visible on a shared dashboard. I would frame the budget as a resource to be spent wisely, not a punishment to avoid."

### Mistake: Over-Engineering the Solution

Candidates propose a Google-scale solution for a simple problem. A system with 5 services does not need multi-region budget aggregation and per-tenant budget tracking.

**Better approach:** "I would start with a simple per-service budget using Prometheus recording rules and a basic deployment gate. As the system grows, I would add burn rate alerts, then composite budgets, then per-tenant tracking."

### Mistake: Not Discussing the Exception Process

Candidates design a rigid policy with no room for exceptions. This shows a lack of understanding of how real organizations work. Every policy needs an exception process.

**Better approach:** "I would design a four-stage policy with a clear exception process. Exceptions require VP-level approval and are logged to an audit trail. The exception process is documented and reviewed quarterly."

### Mistake: Failing to Mention Monitoring the Monitor

Candidates design an error budget system but do not consider what happens when the monitoring system itself fails. This is a common blind spot.

**Better approach:** "The monitoring system itself has an error budget. It is treated as a Tier 1 service. If the monitoring system is down for more than 5 minutes, the on-call team is paged. A 'last known good' cache provides budget state during monitoring outages."
