# Error Budgets — How Companies Use It

## Company Case Studies

### Netflix: The Chaos Engineering Pioneer

Netflix was one of the first companies to popularize error budgets as part of their SRE practice. Their approach is distinctive because it is tightly integrated with their Chaos Engineering culture.

Netflix runs thousands of microservices, each with its own SLO and error budget. The critical user-facing service is the streaming playback API, which has a 99.99% SLO. The error budget for this service is extremely tight — approximately 52 seconds of allowed downtime per month.

What makes Netflix's approach unique is their use of Chaos Monkey. When a service has budget remaining, Chaos Monkey actively introduces failures to test resilience. This means error budgets at Netflix serve a dual purpose: they determine when you can deploy, and they determine how aggressively you test your system.

A key lesson from Netflix is that error budgets should not just gate risky behavior; they should also gate confidence-building behavior. When your service has plenty of budget, you should be investing in testing its resilience. When budget is tight, you should be conservative.

### Google: The Origin of Error Budgets

Google invented the concept of error budgets as part of their Site Reliability Engineering practice, documented in the Google SRE books. Their implementation is the most mature and well-documented.

Google's error budget system works at the service level. Each service has an SLO defined as a target over a rolling 28-day window. The error budget is the difference between the SLO target and 100%. Services that exhaust their error budget enter a "freeze" state — no new releases or configuration changes are allowed until the budget recovers.

The most influential aspect of Google's approach is the burn rate alerting system. Google was the first to document the multi-window approach: you cannot detect budget exhaustion with a single window. Short windows catch fast burns but miss slow leaks. Long windows catch slow leaks but react too slowly for incidents.

Google's Multi-Window, Multi-Burn-Rate Alerting:
- Fast burn (10x): 1-hour window, pages on-call
- Medium burn (2x): 6-hour window, creates a ticket
- Slow burn (1x): 3-day window, tracked for trend analysis

Google also popularized the concept of "error budget policy" as an organizational tool. When a service exhausts its budget, the team cannot deploy new features. This creates a powerful incentive: teams that want to ship quickly must also invest in reliability. The budget aligns the incentives of product teams and SRE teams.

### Uber: Error Budgets at Hyperscale

Uber operates at a scale where even small error rates produce significant user impact. Their system processes millions of trips daily, and each trip touches dozens of microservices.

Uber's error budget implementation is notable for its focus on multi-service dependency tracking. A single trip request can touch 40+ services: the rider app, the dispatch service, the driver matching service, the pricing engine, the maps service, the payment service, and the notification service, among others. If any of these services fails, the trip fails.

Uber's approach to this challenge is to define SLIs and error budgets at three levels:
- **Infrastructure level:** Network, compute, and storage availability
- **Platform level:** Shared services like maps and payments
- **Product level:** End-to-end user journeys like "request a trip"

This layered approach means a payment service outage draws from the payment service's budget first. If the outage cascades into failed trips, it also draws from the "request a trip" product-level budget. This gives Uber visibility into both root cause impact (infrastructure budget) and user impact (product budget).

One of Uber's key lessons is that the infrastructure layer should have the tightest budget because its failures affect everything above it. A compute outage affects all platform services, which in turn affects all product-level user journeys. By giving the infrastructure layer a 99.995% SLO, Uber ensures that infrastructure failures are rare enough to stay within the budgets of all dependent services.

### Cloudflare: Error Budgets for Edge Infrastructure

Cloudflare operates a global edge network spanning 330+ cities. Their error budget implementation is unique because their infrastructure is distributed across thousands of servers worldwide, and their customers depend on them for availability.

Cloudflare's approach uses error budgets to manage their control plane operations. The control plane is the set of services that manage customer configuration — DNS settings, firewall rules, load balancing policies. If the control plane goes down, customers cannot update their configuration, but existing configurations continue to work.

This distinction between control plane and data plane is critical for error budget design. The data plane (serving traffic) has a 99.999% SLO. The control plane (configuration updates) has a 99.9% SLO. The control plane has a looser budget because its failure does not affect existing traffic.

Cloudflare publishes their error budget status publicly in their system status dashboard. This transparency is unusual in the industry — most companies keep error budget data internal. Cloudflare's reasoning is that transparency builds trust with customers and holds the engineering team accountable.

**Lesson from Cloudflare:** Error budgets work best when they are transparent. Publishing budget status reduces the tension between SRE teams and product teams because everyone can see the objective data.

### Discord: Error Budgets for Real-Time Communication

Discord, the real-time messaging platform, uses error budgets to manage their voice and video infrastructure. Their SLO is measured in terms of "message delivery latency" rather than pure uptime, because a system that is technically up but delivering messages with 10-second delays is unacceptable for a real-time communication platform.

Discord's approach defines the SLI as: "the percentage of messages delivered within 500ms to all recipients in a channel." The SLO is 99.99% over a 24-hour window. The error budget is the number of messages that can exceed 500ms delivery time.

This latency-based budget is important because it catches degradation that a binary up/down check would miss. A server that is technically up but overloaded does not count as downtime for a traditional availability budget, but it does consume Discord's latency-based budget.

## Open Source Implementations

### Prometheus and Alertmanager

The most common open-source stack for error budget monitoring is Prometheus combined with Alertmanager. Prometheus stores the SLI metrics as time series, and Alertmanager handles the alerting logic.

A typical Prometheus recording rule for error budget calculation:

```prometheus
# Calculate error budget ratio over a 30-day window
rule:error_budget_ratio_30d = (
  1 - (
    sum(rate(http_requests_total{status=~"5.."}[30d]))
    /
    sum(rate(http_requests_total[30d]))
  )
)

# Calculate remaining budget
rule:error_budget_remaining_30d = (
  rule:error_budget_ratio_30d
  /
  0.999  # SLO target
)
```

The advantage of Prometheus is that it already has the time-series data you need for SLI calculation. The challenge is that Prometheus is not designed for complex window functions — calculating a 28-day rolling window requires significant memory and storage.

### Sloth: SLO and Error Budget Framework

Sloth is an open-source framework specifically designed for SLO and error budget calculation on top of Prometheus. It handles the multi-window burn rate logic that Google popularized.

Sloth generates Prometheus recording rules and alerting rules from a declarative SLO configuration. A typical Sloth configuration:

```yaml
version: "prometheus/v1"
service: "payment-api"
slos:
  - name: "requests-availability"
    objective: 99.9
    sli:
      events:
        total: "http_requests_total"
        errors: "http_requests_total{status=~\"5..\"}"
    alerting:
      page:
        - window: 1h
          burnRate: 10
      ticket:
        - window: 6h
          burnRate: 2
```

Sloth automatically generates the multi-window burn rate alerts for fast burn (1-hour, 10x) and medium burn (6-hour, 2x). It also generates the recording rules for budget remaining percentage and time to exhaustion.

### Pyrra: SLO Management Platform

Pyrra is another open-source SLO framework that extends Prometheus. It provides a web UI for visualizing error budget status, which is useful for teams that want a dedicated dashboard.

Pyrra's key feature is its ability to generate SLO reports: "In the last 30 days, the payment service had 99.94% availability, consuming 40% of its error budget." These reports can be sent to Slack or email, making error budget status visible to the entire team.

## Cloud Provider Implementations

### Google Cloud Monitoring SLO

Google Cloud Monitoring has native SLO support built into its monitoring platform. You can define an SLO with a target value and a compliance period (e.g., 99.9% over 30 days), and Cloud Monitoring automatically calculates the error budget and burn rate.

Google's implementation includes burn rate alerts out of the box. When you define an SLO, you can configure alert policies that fire when the burn rate exceeds a threshold over a given window. Google also provides a pre-built dashboard showing budget remaining, burn rate, and time to exhaustion.

### AWS CloudWatch SLO

AWS CloudWatch does not have native SLO support, but you can implement it using CloudWatch Metrics Math. You define a metric for total requests and a metric for error requests, then use Metrics Math to calculate the SLO compliance and error budget.

```json
{
  "metrics": [
    [ { "expression": "1 - (m1 / m2)", "label": "SLO_Compliance" } ],
    [ "Namespace": "AWS/ApiGateway", "MetricName": "5XXError", "id": "m1" ],
    [ "Namespace": "AWS/ApiGateway", "MetricName": "Count", "id": "m2" ]
  ],
  "period": 300,
  "stat": "Sum"
}
```

The challenge with CloudWatch is that long-window SLO calculations (28-30 days) require significant metric storage costs. AWS recommends using a separate monitoring platform (like Datadog or New Relic) for SLO tracking if you need long compliance windows.

### Datadog SLO Management

Datadog has a comprehensive SLO management feature that includes error budget tracking, burn rate alerts, and a dashboard. Datadog's key advantage is its ability to trace errors across services — you can see exactly which upstream service failure caused the budget consumption.

Datadog's SLO feature also supports composite SLOs, where you combine multiple SLOs into a single target. For example, you can define a composite SLO for the "checkout flow" that combines the checkout API SLO, the payment API SLO, and the notification API SLO.

## Lessons Learned

### Lesson 1: Start Simple, Iterate

The most common mistake teams make is trying to build a perfect error budget system from day one. Start with a single service, a single SLO, and a simple policy. Monitor for a month, adjust, then expand. Companies that try to implement Google-level complexity on day one usually abandon the system entirely.

### Lesson 2: The SLI Is Everything

Your error budget is only as good as your SLI. If you measure the wrong thing, your budget gives false confidence. Spend more time defining your SLIs than you spend on the budget calculation itself. Validate your SLI against actual user experience before you commit to a budget.

### Lesson 3: Error Budgets Are a Team Sport

Error budgets only work when the entire team understands and agrees to the policy. If engineers feel the budget is imposed by an SRE team, they will find ways to game the system. Involve every service owner in the SLO definition process.

### Lesson 4: Plan for Exceptions

Every error budget policy will encounter situations where the policy is too rigid. A critical security fix might need to be deployed even when the budget is exhausted. Design your policy with an exception process: a formal review board that can approve emergency deployments.

### Lesson 5: Budgets Should Be Visible

The teams that get the most value from error budgets make them visible to everyone. Display the budget status on a team dashboard. Include it in the daily standup. Create a weekly email that shows each team's budget status. Visibility creates accountability without requiring enforcement.

### Lesson 6: Burn Rate Is More Important Than Absolute Budget

Teams that only look at "budget remaining" on the last day of the month miss the point. The burn rate tells you if you are on track long before the budget is exhausted. Invest in burn rate alerts, not just budget status dashboards.

### Lesson 7: Automate the Policy

The most successful error budget implementations automate the policy enforcement. If the budget is exhausted, the deployment pipeline should automatically block the deployment — no human judgment required. Manual overrides should be possible but require specific approval.

### Lesson 8: Budgets Should Be Reviewed, Not Just Monitored

A quarterly review of the error budget system is essential. Are the SLOs still appropriate? Has the system changed in ways that require new SLIs? Are teams gaming the system? Budget reviews are an opportunity to improve the system, not just to check a box.

### Lesson 9: Prepare for the "Black Swan" Event

A single catastrophic event (data center failure, cloud provider outage) can exhaust the entire error budget in minutes. Your policy must account for this. Consider having a separate "catastrophic event" budget that is separate from the regular budget. This prevents a once-in-a-year event from blocking all deployments for the rest of the month.

### Lesson 10: Error Budgets Are Not a Substitute for Incident Response

Error budgets tell you when you are in trouble, but they do not fix the trouble. Teams must still invest in incident response practices: runbooks, on-call rotations, postmortems, and blameless culture. An error budget system without good incident response is like a smoke alarm without a fire extinguisher.

### Choosing a Platform

The choice depends on your team size and existing infrastructure:

**Small teams (under 10 engineers):** Use Datadog SLO or Google Cloud Monitoring. The setup is quick, and the built-in dashboards reduce the need for custom development. The cost is higher per metric, but the team size is small enough that the operational savings outweigh the cost.

**Medium teams (10-50 engineers):** Use Prometheus + Sloth + Grafana. The initial setup cost is higher, but the per-metric cost is lower. The team has enough engineers to maintain the infrastructure. The flexibility of Prometheus rules allows customizations that commercial tools may not support.

**Large teams (50+ engineers):** Use a combination of Prometheus (for collection) and a commercial platform (for SLO management). The commercial platform provides the higher-level SLO management features (composite SLOs, burn rate alerts, tenant tracking), while Prometheus handles the raw metric collection. This hybrid approach provides the best of both worlds.

## Industry Benchmarks

### Error Budget Consumption by Service Type

| Service Type | Typical SLO | Average Monthly Consumption | Common Exhaustion Rate |
|-------------|-------------|---------------------------|----------------------|
| Payment processing | 99.99% | 60-80% | 2-5% of months |
| User-facing API | 99.9% | 40-60% | 1-2% of months |
| Internal API | 99.5% | 30-50% | Less than 1% |
| Batch processing | 99% | 20-40% | Less than 1% |
| Storage service | 99.999% | 10-20% | Less than 0.1% |

Note: These benchmarks are from large-scale production systems. Your mileage may vary. The key takeaway is that payment services consistently consume more budget because the cost of an error (lost transaction) is immediately visible, while internal services have more tolerance.

### Recovery Time After Budget Exhaustion

| Window Size | Average Recovery Time | Recovery Strategy |
|-------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| 7 days | 3-5 days | Wait for good days to replace bad days |
| 30 days | 12-18 days | Wait for incident to age out of window |
| 90 days | 45-60 days | Needs active intervention, not passive waiting |

The recovery time for a 30-day window (12-18 days) is a significant business constraint. If you exhaust the budget on day 20, you cannot deploy for 10-12 days. This is why burn rate alerts are critical — they give you early warning so you can take corrective action before the budget is exhausted.

## The Future of Error Budgets

### AI-Assisted Budget Management

Several companies are experimenting with machine learning to predict error budget consumption. The model analyzes historical burn rate patterns, deployment schedules, and traffic patterns to predict when the budget will be exhausted. This allows teams to preemptively slow deployments before the budget is critically low.

### Error Budgets as a Service

Cloud providers are beginning to offer error budget as a managed service. Google Cloud Monitoring already has SLO tracking built in. AWS and Azure are likely to follow. This reduces the operational burden of implementing error budgets from scratch.

### FinOps Integration

The next frontier is integrating error budgets with cost tracking. If a deployment consumes 5% of the error budget, how much does that cost in terms of lost revenue? Companies are starting to assign a dollar value to error budget consumption, making the trade-off between reliability and velocity even more concrete.

### Budget Transparency

The trend toward transparency means more companies will publish their error budget status publicly. Cloudflare already does this. As users become more sophisticated, they will expect to see the reliability metrics of the services they depend on.

## Additional Case Studies

### Etsy: Error Budgets for Continuous Deployment

Etsy is known for its continuous deployment culture — they deploy 50+ times per day. Error budgets were critical for maintaining this velocity without sacrificing reliability.

Etsy's approach is notable for its focus on developer experience. They built a "deploy health" dashboard that shows the error budget impact of each deployment. After a deployment, the dashboard shows whether the deployment improved or degraded the error budget. This creates a direct feedback loop: developers see the reliability impact of their changes immediately.

The Etsy team found that the act of measuring and displaying the error budget was more important than the policy itself. Engineers naturally deployed more carefully when they could see the budget impact of their changes. The policy was rarely needed because the visibility alone changed behavior.

### Stripe: Error Budgets for API Reliability

Stripe's API is the backbone of online payments for millions of businesses. Their error budget system is designed around API response codes and latency percentiles.

Stripe's key insight is that error budgets should measure what the customer experiences, not what the server does. Their SLI measures the fraction of API requests that return a successful response within 500ms, as measured from the customer's perspective. This means they use client-side measurement, not server-side logs.

Client-side measurement is harder to implement (you need instrumentation in the client SDK), but it provides a more accurate picture of the user experience. Stripe publishes their API availability on their status page, measured from the client perspective.

### Twilio: Error Budgets for Communication APIs

Twilio, like Stripe, provides APIs that other businesses depend on. Their error budget system is noteworthy because they support multiple communication channels (SMS, voice, video, email) with different reliability characteristics.

Twilio's approach is to define separate SLOs for each channel. SMS delivery has a different reliability profile than voice calls, so they should not share the same budget. Each channel has its own SLI, SLO, and error budget. The composite SLO is calculated as a weighted average based on the traffic volume of each channel.

Twilio also publishes their error budget status to customers through a public API. This enables customers to build their own reliability monitoring around Twilio's service — a key differentiator in the competitive API market.

### Shopify: Error Budgets for E-Commerce at Scale

Shopify processes millions of transactions during Black Friday and Cyber Monday. Their error budget system is designed to handle extreme traffic spikes.

Shopify uses a "seasonal SLO" approach: the SLO is tighter during high-traffic periods (Black Friday: 99.99%) and looser during normal periods (99.9%). The error budget is calculated separately for each period. This prevents a bad day during normal traffic from exhausting the budget that is needed for the holiday season.

The seasonal SLO approach requires careful planning. The SLO transition must be automated and tested in advance. During the transition, the budget window is paused to prevent a double-consumption of budget.

## Tools and Implementation Patterns

### Prometheus Recording Rules for Budget Calculation

Here are the essential Prometheus recording rules you need for a basic error budget implementation:

```prometheus
# Total request rate over the budget window
- record: service:requests_total:rate_30d
  expr: sum(rate(http_requests_total[30d])) by (service)

# Error request rate over the budget window
- record: service:errors_total:rate_30d
  expr: sum(rate(http_requests_total{status=~"5.."}[30d])) by (service)

# Actual availability
- record: service:availability:ratio_30d
  expr: 1 - (service:errors_total:rate_30d / service:requests_total:rate_30d)

# Budget remaining (SLO target: 99.9%)
- record: service:error_budget:remaining_30d
  expr: service:availability:ratio_30d / 0.999

# Burn rate over 1 hour
- record: service:burn_rate:1h
  expr: (
    sum(rate(http_requests_total{status=~"5.."}[1h])) by (service)
    /
    sum(rate(http_requests_total[1h])) by (service)
  ) / (1 - 0.999)
```

These recording rules are evaluated every time Prometheus scrapes metrics. The results are stored as new time series that can be queried by dashboards and alerting rules.

### Implementation Checklist

Use this checklist when implementing error budgets for a new service:

1. Define the SLI: what are you measuring and how?
2. Set the SLO target and window duration
3. Calculate the error budget
4. Instrument the service to expose SLI metrics
5. Configure Prometheus to scrape the metrics
6. Create Prometheus recording rules for budget and burn rate
7. Configure Alertmanager rules for multi-window burn rate alerts
8. Build a Grafana dashboard showing budget remaining and burn rate
9. Implement the deployment gate in the CI/CD pipeline
10. Document the error budget policy and communicate it to the team
11. Set up a quarterly review cycle for SLO and budget adjustment

After completing the checklist, monitor the error budget for 30 days before making any policy adjustments. The first month of data gives you a baseline for tuning.

### Common Tooling Mistakes

1. **Using the wrong Prometheus function.** Use `rate()` for counters, not `increase()`. The `rate()` function handles counter resets correctly. The `increase()` function can produce spikes when instances restart.

2. **Not accounting for multiple instances.** If you have 10 instances of a service, each instance reports its own metrics. Your PromQL query must aggregate across all instances: `sum(rate(...)) by (service)`.

3. **Incorrect label matching.** Budget calculations require exact label matching. If one metric has a `service="payment"` label and another has `service_name="payment"`, the join will fail. Standardize your label naming conventions.

4. **Window mismatch.** If the budget window is 30 days but your recording rule uses a 28-day window, the budget calculation will be wrong. Configure the window once and use it consistently across all rules.

5. **Not testing the alerts.** Configure burn rate alerts and test them with synthetic data. A common mistake is to configure alerts but never verify that they fire correctly. Schedule quarterly tests of the alert pipeline.

6. **Ignoring data retention.** Prometheus default retention is 15 days. A 30-day budget window requires at least 35 days of retention. Extend the retention period and monitor disk usage.

7. **Over-alerting on slow burn.** A slow burn (1.5x) over 24 hours may not require an immediate response. Configure the alert severity based on the burn rate, not just the budget remaining. Slow burns create tickets; fast burns create pages.

8. **Manual alert configuration.** Every alert that requires manual configuration is a potential source of errors. Use a tool like Sloth or Pyrra that generates alerting rules from a declarative SLO configuration. This reduces the chance of misconfigured alerts.

9. **Not documenting the SLO rationale.** Every SLO should have a documented rationale: "99.9% was chosen because the business impact of downtime is $1,000/minute." Without documentation, future teams will not understand why the SLO was set at a particular level.

10. **Skipping the quarterly review.** Error budgets should be reviewed every quarter. Are the SLOs still appropriate? Has the system changed? Are the burn rate thresholds still correct? Without regular review, error budgets become stale and lose their relevance.
