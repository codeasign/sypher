## The Problem Story

A video streaming platform serves 50 million active users. The engineering team monitors latency, error rates, and throughput on dashboards covering every service. Every week, the on-call engineer gets paged forty times for latency spikes, error bursts, and degraded throughput. The team investigates each alert. Most turn out to be harmless. A batch job consumes CPU for thirty seconds. A cache warming event causes a two-second latency blip. A deployment causes a brief error spike that self-recovers. The team spends 60 person-hours per week investigating alerts that mean nothing. The real problem — a slow memory leak in the recommendation engine that raises p99 latency from 200ms to 350ms over six weeks — goes unnoticed until users start tweeting about buffering issues. The team was measuring everything and knowing nothing.

A payment processing company guarantees 99.99% uptime to its merchants. The engineering team tracks uptime by measuring server ping checks. Every server shows 99.99% availability. The merchants are happy. Then the company realizes that the ping checks measure whether the server process is running, not whether transactions are actually completing. A database deadlock causes transactions to fail silently for 23 minutes during peak hours. The ping checks show green. The server is running. The transactions are not processing. The merchants lose 340,000 dollars in revenue before anyone notices. The team was measuring the wrong thing perfectly.

A social media company sets a reliability target of 99.9% availability. The engineering team ships features rapidly, confident that 99.9% leaves only 8.7 hours of allowed downtime per year. At the end of the quarter, the team calculates actual availability. It is 99.2%. The team is shocked. They had no idea they were exceeding their error budget by 600%. The problem was that they never defined how to measure availability. Some teams measured server uptime. Some measured API response rates. Some measured page load success. Each measurement gave a different number. The 99.9% target was an aspiration, not a measurement. It was a goal without a definition, which is not a goal at all.

A cloud infrastructure provider promises 99.95% uptime for its compute service. Customers build their businesses on this promise. One month, a networking issue in a single availability zone causes a 30-minute outage. The provider's SLO dashboard shows 99.97% compliance for the month — the outage was contained to one zone out of three. But 30% of the provider's customers run exclusively in that one zone. Those customers experienced a 30-minute complete outage. Their effective availability was 99.93%. The provider met its SLO. The customers experienced a violation. The SLO was measuring the provider's perspective, not the customer's perspective.

A ride-sharing company sets a dispatch latency SLO of p99 under 5 seconds. The dispatch team optimizes the matching algorithm, runs load tests, and deploys improvements. The SLO dashboard shows green — p99 dispatch latency is 3.8 seconds. But the team also tracks the 90th percentile of the worst 1% of dispatches. Among the slowest 1% of dispatches, the median latency is 47 seconds. The p99 SLO hides the fact that 1% of riders are waiting almost a minute for a ride. The average of the tail tells a different story than the p99 boundary. The team was looking at the right metric at the wrong resolution.

An ad-serving platform defines its SLO as "99.9% of ad requests return a response within 100ms." The engineering team maintains this SLO across billions of requests per day. But the team notices that the SLO compliance rate is always exactly 99.9% — never higher, never lower. Investigation reveals that the ad server has a hard timeout set to 100ms. Any request that takes longer than 100ms is terminated and counted as a failure. The SLO is not measuring the system's performance. The system is being gated to exactly match the SLO target. The team is tuning the timeout to hit the number instead of improving the system.

A social media company sets a reliability target of 99.9% availability. The engineering team ships features rapidly, confident that 99.9% leaves only 8.7 hours of allowed downtime per year. At the end of the quarter, the team calculates actual availability. It is 99.2%. The team is shocked. They had no idea they were exceeding their error budget by 600%. The problem was that they never defined how to measure availability. Some teams measured server uptime. Some measured API response rates. Some measured page load success. Each measurement gave a different number. The 99.9% target was an aspiration, not a measurement. It was a goal without a definition, which is not a goal at all.

These stories share a single root cause: the teams had monitoring systems, metrics, and dashboards, but they lacked the connective tissue between raw measurements and business-relevant reliability targets. They had SLIs without SLOs. They had data without decisions. Service Level Objectives are the bridge that turns measurements into management.

## The Insight

A Service Level Objective (SLO) is a target value or range for a Service Level Indicator (SLI) over a defined time period. If the SLI measures the actual performance, the SLO is the line in the sand that separates good performance from bad performance. An SLO of "99.9% of requests complete in under 200 milliseconds over a 30-day rolling window" means that, at any given moment, you can look at the last 30 days of data and know whether the service is meeting expectations. This is the fundamental building block of data-driven reliability management.

The power of SLOs is not the target itself. The power is the error budget. If your SLO is 99.9%, your error budget is 0.1%. That 0.1% — 8.7 hours per year, 43 minutes per month, 86 seconds per day — is the amount of time the service is allowed to be in a bad state. The error budget transforms reliability from an abstract goal into a concrete resource. Every deployment, every configuration change, every experiment consumes error budget. When the budget is full, you can ship with confidence. When the budget is nearly empty, you stop shipping and focus on reliability. This is the critical feedback loop that makes SLOs operational rather than aspirational.

Think of SLOs as a fuel gauge for reliability. The SLI is the float that measures the fuel level. The SLO is the full tank mark. The error budget is the remaining fuel. When the fuel is low, you do not go on a road trip. You pull over and fix the engine. SLOs tell you exactly when to pull over. Without this gauge, you drive with the fuel light permanently on and hope you do not run out on the highway.

Before SLOs, teams operated on gut feel. Is the system reliable enough? Nobody knows. The latency dashboard shows a spike. Is that a problem? Nobody knows. The error rate increased by 0.5%. Is that an incident? Nobody knows. SLOs replace uncertainty with a binary signal: the service is meeting its objective, or it is not. That signal drives pager rotations, deployment decisions, capacity planning, and architectural investments. The signal is not judgmental — it is factual. The service is at 99.92% compliance with a 99.9% target. That is not good or bad. It is a state. It tells you exactly where you stand.

The most important insight about SLOs is that they are negotiated, not discovered. You do not find the right SLO by analyzing data. You choose the SLO based on what your users need and what your system can deliver. A 99.99% SLO is not better than a 99.9% SLO. It is more expensive. The question is whether your users need that extra 0.09% of reliability enough to justify the cost. SLOs force that conversation between engineering, product, and business stakeholders.

SLOs also create a shared language between teams. When the infrastructure team says the database availability is 99.99% and the application team says the user-facing availability is 99.9%, the conversation shifts from arguing about numbers to understanding why there is a gap. The gap reveals insight: the extra 0.09% of downtime comes from connection pool exhaustion, database failover delays, or network timeouts between the application and the database. The SLOs expose the blind spots between teams.

Another critical insight is that SLOs are not static. A service that starts with a 99% SLO should evolve to 99.9% as the system matures and as user expectations grow. The SLO should be reviewed quarterly and adjusted based on changes in user behavior, infrastructure capability, and business requirements. A static SLO is a stale SLO. An SLO that never changes means the team is not learning.

The relationship between SLIs, SLOs, and error budgets is the foundation of modern reliability engineering. Each SLO has exactly one SLI that it targets. Each SLI can have multiple SLOs at different thresholds — a service might have a 99.9% SLO for internal operations and a 99.95% SLO for SLA commitments. Each SLO has exactly one error budget. The error budget is the operational signal that drives daily decisions. This mapping from measurement to target to budget is the core architectural pattern of SLO-driven operations.

The error budget is also a tool for managing risk appetite. A team with a full error budget has a high risk appetite — they can deploy experimental changes, test in production, and iterate quickly. A team with a depleted error budget has a low risk appetite — they must be conservative, roll back aggressively, and prioritize stability over velocity. The error budget explicitly quantifies how much risk the team can tolerate at any given moment. This replaces the implicit, subjective, and inconsistent risk assessment that teams do without SLOs.

Finally, SLOs create accountability. When every service has a clear, measurable target, every team knows what they are responsible for. There is no ambiguity about whether the service is reliable enough. The SLO provides a single, objective, universally understood yardstick. Teams are judged by their SLO compliance, not by the volume of alerts they respond to or the number of incidents they handle. This shifts the focus from activity to outcomes.

## Core Vocabulary

**Service Level Indicator (SLI):** A quantitative measurement of some aspect of the service. Common SLIs include request latency, error rate, throughput, availability, and durability. A good SLI is directly observable, measurable from the client perspective, and tightly correlated with user experience. SLIs are the raw data. SLOs are what you do with it.

**Service Level Objective (SLO):** A target value or range for an SLI over a specified time period. An SLO has three parts: the SLI it targets, the threshold value, and the measurement window. "99.9% of requests succeed" is not an SLO. "99.9% of requests return HTTP 200-599 over a 30-day rolling window" is an SLO.

**Service Level Agreement (SLA):** A contractual commitment to meet SLOs, usually with financial penalties for failure. SLAs are legal documents. SLOs are engineering targets. Every SLA contains one or more SLOs, but not every SLO is part of an SLA. Most internal services operate on SLOs without SLAs. External customer-facing services have both.

**Error Budget:** The maximum amount of time the service is allowed to be in a non-compliant state over the measurement window. Calculated as 100% minus the SLO target. A 99.9% SLO gives an error budget of 0.1% — 43 minutes and 12 seconds per 30-day window. The error budget is the most important number in the SLO system because it is actionable.

**Burn Rate:** The rate at which the error budget is being consumed, expressed as a multiple of the nominal rate. A burn rate of 1.0 means the error budget will last exactly the measurement window. A burn rate of 2.0 means the budget will be exhausted in half the window. A burn rate of 10.0 means the budget will be gone in one-tenth of the window. Burn rate is the primary signal for alerting.

**Burn Window:** The time it would take to exhaust the entire error budget at the current burn rate. If the burn rate is 2.0 and the SLO is 99.9% over 30 days, the burn window is 15 days. Burn windows shorter than a few hours trigger immediate alerts. Burn windows of several days trigger scheduled maintenance.

**Measurement Window:** The time period over which the SLO is evaluated. Common windows are 7 days, 30 days, and 90 days. Longer windows smooth out noise but delay detection of sustained problems. Shorter windows detect problems faster but create more alert noise. The choice of window is a trade-off between sensitivity and stability.

**Compliance Period:** The calendar period over which SLO compliance is reported. For SLAs, this is typically a calendar month or quarter. Compliance periods are often distinct from measurement windows. You might use a 30-day rolling measurement window but report compliance on a calendar-month basis.

**Alerting Threshold:** A burn rate threshold that triggers an alert. The simplest threshold is 1.0 — if burn rate exceeds 1.0, the error budget is being consumed faster than expected. Production systems typically use multi-tier thresholds: low burn rate over long windows for scheduled responses, high burn rate over short windows for immediate page.

**Budget Consumption Percentage:** The fraction of the error budget that has been consumed so far in the current window. 0% means the budget is full. 100% means the budget is exhausted. Values above 100% indicate the service is in SLO violation. This is the single number every engineer should know about their service.

**SLO Violation:** A state where the service has consumed more than 100% of its error budget within the measurement window. Violations trigger incident response, post-mortems, and potentially SLA penalties. The time to recover from a violation is the time it takes for the good data to push the bad data out of the measurement window.

**Multi-Window Multi-Burn-Rate Alerting:** A sophisticated alerting strategy that uses multiple alerting thresholds with different burn rates and windows. A typical setup has four tiers: a high burn rate over a short window for immediate page, a moderate burn rate over a medium window for scheduled response, a low burn rate over a long window for planned maintenance, and a very low burn rate over a very long window for trend detection. This approach balances sensitivity with false positive rate.

**Good Window:** A 1-minute time window where the service meets the SLO threshold. If the SLO is 99.9% and the service has 99.95% good requests in that minute, the minute is counted as a good window. Good windows contribute to the numerator of the SLO compliance calculation.

**Bad Window:** A 1-minute time window where the service fails to meet the SLO threshold. If the error rate exceeds the SLO allowance in that minute, the minute is counted as a bad window. Bad windows consume error budget. The number of bad windows determines the remaining budget.

**Budget Exhaustion Time:** The estimated time until the error budget is completely consumed at the current burn rate. If the burn rate is 2.0 and the window is 30 days, the budget exhaustion time is 15 days. This metric is useful for planning — it tells the team how long they have before the SLO is violated.

**Compliance Percentage:** The ratio of good windows to total windows over the measurement period. For a 30-day window with 43,200 total minutes, if 43,157 minutes are "good" and 43 minutes are "bad," the compliance is 99.9%. This is the standard SLO compliance metric.

**Error Rate:** The fraction of requests that fail the SLO criteria. A 1% error rate means 1 out of every 100 requests is a failure. Error rate is the raw input to burn rate computation. It is typically measured over short windows (1-5 minutes) to provide a current view of service health.

**SLO Dashboard:** A visualization that shows the current SLO compliance, error budget remaining, burn rate, and alert status for one or more SLOs. The dashboard is the primary operational interface for SLO management. It should be the first thing an engineer checks when joining the on-call rotation.

**SLO Review:** A periodic meeting where the team reviews SLO compliance, discusses violations, and adjusts SLO targets. The review is typically weekly or bi-weekly. It is the forum for deciding whether SLO targets need to change based on operational experience.

**Budget Burn-Down Chart:** A visualization showing error budget consumption over time. The chart shows the remaining budget as a line that decreases when incidents occur and stays flat during healthy periods. The burn-down chart reveals whether the team is on track to meet the SLO target for the current window.

**SLO Attainability:** The feasibility of achieving an SLO target given the current system performance and infrastructure. An attainable SLO is one where the target is within reach with reasonable effort. An unattainable SLO causes frustration and is ignored. The gap between current performance and the SLO target should be measurable and closable.

### Terminology in Practice

These terms connect in a specific workflow. Here is how they interact in a real incident:

"The **SLI** for the checkout service is the fraction of requests that complete within 2 seconds. The **SLO** is 99.95% over a 30-day rolling window. The **error budget** is 0.05% — approximately 21 minutes and 36 seconds per 30 days. At 2:00 PM, a deployment causes a 5% error rate. The **burn rate** is 100 times the nominal rate. The **burn window** is 4 hours and 19 minutes — the budget would be exhausted in that time at the current burn rate. The **multi-window multi-burn-rate** alerting system detects a burn rate of 14.4 over a 5-minute window and pages the on-call engineer. The engineer rolls back the deployment. The error rate drops to 0.01%. The **budget consumption** for this event is 0.3% of the total error budget. The service is not in **SLO violation**, but the event is documented in the post-mortem."

## Step by Step

The SLO system follows a well-defined pipeline from raw measurement to operational decision. Each step builds on the previous one, creating a complete feedback loop between service behavior and engineering action.

### Step 0: Understand the Service Architecture

Before defining any SLO, you must understand the service's architecture. What components are involved? What dependencies exist? Where are the potential bottlenecks? What is the request path from the client to the service and back?

A service that depends on a database, a cache, and a third-party API has three potential points of failure. The SLO should measure the service's overall behavior, but the error budget should be traceable to specific dependencies. If the error budget is consumed by a third-party API latency spike, the team needs to know that the cause is external.

The architecture understanding also informs the choice of SLI. A read-heavy service should measure read latency. A write-heavy service should measure write success rate. A batch processing service should measure job completion time. The SLI should match the service's primary function.

### Step 1: Choose the Right SLI

The first step in building an SLO system is selecting what to measure. A good SLI is specific, client-facing, and measurable. Instead of "request latency," define "the proportion of HTTP GET requests to the search endpoint that return a complete response within 500 milliseconds, measured at the load balancer." This level of specificity removes ambiguity about what is being measured and how.

The SLI must be observable from the client perspective. Measuring server-side processing time is not enough. Network latency, TLS handshake time, and load balancer queueing all affect the user experience. The best SLI is measured at the edge of the network, as close to the client as possible. An SLI measured at the server that shows 50ms latency is misleading if the client experiences 350ms due to network latency and TLS overhead.

The SLI must also be directly correlated with user experience. A metric like CPU usage is correlated with user experience — high CPU usage often means slow responses — but it is not directly observable by the user. A user does not experience CPU usage. They experience latency. The SLI should measure the thing the user experiences, not the thing the infrastructure does.

A good SLI is also specific enough to be actionable. "Request latency" is too broad. It combines fast endpoints with slow endpoints, read requests with write requests, and peak traffic with off-peak traffic. A better SLI is "p99 latency of the search endpoint during peak hours." This is specific enough that a degradation in this SLI points directly to the search endpoint as the likely cause.

The SLI should be defined at a granularity that matches the service's architecture. For a microservice, the SLI is at the service level. For a monolithic application, the SLI may be at the endpoint level. For a database, the SLI may be at the query type level. The principle is the same: the SLI should be specific enough that a failure in the SLI corresponds to a failure in a specific component.

### Step 2: Set the SLO Target

The SLO target is a business decision, not a technical one. Ask three questions: What does the user need? What can the system deliver? What does the competition provide?

A payment API needs 99.99% availability because financial transactions cannot fail. A photo-sharing app might target 99.9% availability because users tolerate occasional slowness. A prototype service might target 99% because speed of iteration matters more than reliability. The same company can have different SLO targets for different services based on their business criticality.

The SLO target must be achievable. Setting a 99.999% target when your infrastructure supports 99.9% guarantees perpetual violation. The error budget will be permanently negative, which means the SLO is meaningless. Set targets that force improvement without being impossible. The ideal target is one that the team achieves 90% of the time but requires effort to maintain.

The SLO target should also be informed by the competition. If your competitors offer 99.99% availability for the same service, a 99.9% target puts you at a disadvantage. If the market standard is 99.9%, a 99.99% target is a differentiator. Competitive benchmarking is a useful input to the SLO target decision.

The SLO target should be reviewed and adjusted over time. A service that started at 99.9% and has consistently achieved 99.99% for six months should consider raising the target. A service that has never achieved its 99.99% target should lower it to something realistic. The SLO target is a living parameter that evolves with the system.

### Step 3: Define the Measurement Window

The measurement window determines how far back the SLO looks. A 7-day window detects problems quickly but is noisy. A 90-day window is stable but slow to detect degradation. The standard choice is 30 days — a balance between stability and responsiveness.

The window is almost always rolling, not calendar-based. A rolling 30-day window means that at any moment, the SLO reflects the last 30 days of performance. A calendar-month window means the SLO resets on the first of each month. Rolling windows are better for operational decisions because they are always current. Calendar windows are better for SLA reporting because they align with billing cycles.

The window size affects the error budget proportionally. A 7-day window with a 99.9% SLO has an error budget of 10 minutes and 4 seconds. A 30-day window has 43 minutes and 12 seconds. A 90-day window has 129 minutes and 36 seconds. A longer window gives more budget, which means the team can tolerate more failure over the window. This is a deliberate design choice — a longer window should correspond to a higher tolerance for temporary degradation.

The window size also affects the recovery time from an SLO violation. If the service violates its SLO at day 15 of a 30-day window, the violation will be resolved at day 45 — 30 days after the violation occurred. For a 7-day window, the violation would be resolved at day 22 — 7 days after the violation. Shorter windows mean faster recovery from violations.

### Step 4: Calculate the Error Budget

The error budget is the arithmetic difference between perfect performance and the SLO target. For a 30-day window with 99.9% SLO:

```
Total requests in 30 days: 100,000,000
SLO target: 99.9%
Allowed failures: 100,000,000 * (1 - 0.999) = 100,000
Error budget: 100,000 failed requests per 30 days
```

In time terms, 99.9% over 30 days means 43 minutes and 12 seconds of allowed downtime. The error budget is a finite resource. Every incident, every degraded deployment, every latency spike spends from this budget.

### Step 5: Track Budget Consumption

Every minute, the system evaluates whether the service met the SLO threshold during that minute. If the error rate exceeded the threshold, that minute is "bad" and consumes error budget. The system maintains a running count of bad minutes over the measurement window.

The budget consumption percentage is the ratio of bad time to total error budget. At 50% consumption, half the budget is spent. At 100% consumption, the service is in violation. The consumption percentage is the single most important operational metric for a service. Every engineer on the team should know their service's budget consumption without checking a dashboard.

The budget consumption is tracked in two forms: time-based and request-based. Time-based consumption counts the number of bad minutes. Request-based consumption counts the number of bad requests. Both are valid approaches, and the choice depends on the SLI type. For availability SLOs, time-based consumption is more common. For latency SLOs, request-based consumption is more common.

The budget consumption should be tracked in real time. When a deployment causes a spike in error rate, the budget consumption should update within 1 minute. The team should see the impact of their actions on the budget immediately. This creates a tight feedback loop between operational decisions and SLO compliance.

### Step 6: Compute Burn Rate

Burn rate is the ratio of actual error rate to the maximum allowed error rate. If the SLO is 99.9% (0.1% allowed errors) and the current error rate is 1%, the burn rate is 10. A burn rate of 10 means the error budget is being consumed 10 times faster than planned.

Burn rate is more useful than raw error rate because it normalizes for the SLO target. A 1% error rate is a burn rate of 10 against a 99.9% SLO but a burn rate of 1,000 against a 99.999% SLO. The same error rate means different things for different SLOs. Burn rate provides a common scale for alerting across services with different SLO targets.

The burn rate is computed over multiple window sizes simultaneously. The 5-minute burn rate tells you about immediate problems. The 30-minute burn rate tells you about sustained problems. The 6-hour burn rate tells you about slow degradation. The 3-day burn rate tells you about long-term trends. Each window size provides a different perspective on the same data.

The burn rate must be computed from the start of the alerting window, not from the start of the measurement window. When computing the burn rate over the last 5 minutes, the system looks at the error rate over exactly those 5 minutes. It does not look at the error rate over the last 30 days. This is important because the burn rate should reflect the current state, not the historical average.

### Step 6a: Compute the Burn Window

The burn window is the time until the error budget is exhausted at the current burn rate. It is calculated as:

```
burn_window = measurement_window / burn_rate
```

For a 30-day measurement window with a burn rate of 10, the burn window is 3 days. This means the error budget will be exhausted in 3 days if the current burn rate continues. The burn window is a more intuitive metric than burn rate for many engineers. "We have 3 days of budget remaining" is easier to understand than "the burn rate is 10."

The burn window should be tracked in the SLO dashboard alongside the burn rate. It provides a time-based perspective on budget consumption. When the burn window drops below 24 hours, the team should be alerted. When the burn window drops below 1 hour, the team should be paged.

### Step 6b: Calculate Budget Exhaustion Probability

The budget exhaustion probability estimates the likelihood that the error budget will be exhausted before the end of the measurement window. This is a statistical forecast based on the current burn rate and the historical pattern of burn rates.

If the current burn rate is 2.0 and the historical pattern shows that burn rates above 2.0 persist for an average of 30 minutes, the budget exhaustion probability is the probability that the burn rate will stay above 2.0 long enough to exhaust the budget. This calculation is complex and requires historical data, but it provides a forward-looking view of SLO compliance.

The budget exhaustion probability is an advanced metric. Most teams start with burn rate and burn window, then add exhaustion probability as they gain experience with SLOs.

### Step 7: Choose the SLO Granularity

SLO granularity determines how many SLOs a service has and how specific they are. A service can have a single SLO that covers all endpoints, or it can have per-endpoint SLOs, or it can have per-user-segment SLOs.

Per-service SLO is the simplest approach. One SLO covers all requests to the service. This is easy to implement and understand. The disadvantage is that a degradation in a low-traffic endpoint is hidden by the high-traffic endpoints. If the search endpoint has 1% of traffic and is completely failing, the per-service SLO still shows 99.9% compliance because the other 99% of traffic is healthy.

Per-endpoint SLO provides more granularity. Each significant endpoint has its own SLO. The search endpoint has a 99.9% SLO. The home page endpoint has a 99.99% SLO. The login endpoint has a 99.95% SLO. This granularity means more SLOs to manage — typically 5-10 per service — but provides actionable data for each endpoint.

Per-user-segment SLO provides the most granularity. Premium users get a higher SLO than free users. Mobile users get a different SLO than desktop users. Regional SLOs account for different infrastructure in different geographies. This granularity is appropriate for services with clearly differentiated user segments that have different reliability expectations.

The choice of granularity depends on the service's complexity and the team's operational maturity. Start with per-service SLOs and add granularity as needed. The principle is: define an SLO for every dimension that matters for decision-making. If you would make a different decision based on the search endpoint's performance versus the home page endpoint's performance, you need per-endpoint SLOs.

### Step 8: Configure Alerting Thresholds

Alerting thresholds define when the system pages an engineer. The standard approach is multi-window multi-burn-rate alerting (MWMBR). Instead of a single threshold, MWMBR uses multiple burn rate windows, each with a different sensitivity and response time.

The multi-window approach is essential because no single window size works for all scenarios. A short window (5 minutes) detects catastrophic failures quickly but produces false positives from brief blips. A long window (6 hours) filters out noise but takes too long to detect critical failures. By using multiple windows in parallel, the system can detect both fast catastrophic failures and slow creeping degradations with the same alerting framework.

The standard MWMBR configuration has four tiers:

- **Tier 1 - Page (critical):** Burn rate of 14.4 over 5 minutes. This catches catastrophic failures immediately. The 14.4 threshold is derived from the idea that the error budget should last at least 2 days at this rate. A 5-minute window prevents false positives from brief blips that last a few seconds. The response time is immediate — the engineer is paged within 5 minutes of the failure starting.

- **Tier 2 - Ticket (medium):** Burn rate of 6.0 over 30 minutes. This catches sustained degradation that a short window might miss. A 30-minute window with 6.0 burn rate means the budget would be exhausted in 5 days. The response time is within 30 minutes — the engineer creates a ticket and investigates within the hour.

- **Tier 3 - Notification (low):** Burn rate of 1.0 over 6 hours. This catches slow degradation that accumulates over time. A 6-hour window with 1.0 burn rate means the budget is being consumed at exactly the nominal rate. The response time is within the same day — the engineer schedules an investigation during regular hours.

- **Tier 4 - Dashboard (info):** Burn rate of 0.5 over 3 days. This catches long-term trends like memory leaks or gradual performance regression. A 3-day window with 0.5 burn rate means the budget is being consumed at half the nominal rate — a slow but persistent degradation. The response is a dashboard indicator with no notification. The team reviews these during weekly SLO reviews.

The MWMBR thresholds are not universal. They should be tuned based on the service's criticality and traffic pattern. A service with bursty traffic may need longer windows to avoid false positives. A service with steady traffic can use shorter windows. The tuning process involves monitoring the alert rate and adjusting thresholds until the false positive rate is acceptable.

### Step 9: Respond to Alerts

When an alert fires, the engineer follows a structured response that prioritizes speed of mitigation over depth of analysis. The goal is to stop the budget burn first and understand the root cause second.

1. **Acknowledge** the alert within 5 minutes. The acknowledgment confirms that a human has seen the alert and is responding. Most pager systems require acknowledgment within a specified time window, after which the alert escalates to the next tier of on-call engineers.

2. **Assess** the burn rate and burn window. A burn rate of 14.4 over 5 minutes requires immediate action — the budget is burning 14.4 times faster than normal. A burn rate of 1.0 over 6 hours allows time for analysis. The assessment should answer: how fast is the budget burning, how long until it is exhausted, and what is the likely cause.

3. **Mitigate** by rolling back the most recent deployment, scaling up capacity, or redirecting traffic. The mitigation should be the fastest path to stopping the budget burn, not the most elegant fix. Rollback is almost always the fastest mitigation. The team can investigate the root cause after the service is stable.

4. **Verify** that the burn rate drops below the alerting threshold. The verification should happen within 1 minute of the mitigation. If the burn rate is still above the threshold, the mitigation failed and the engineer should try a different approach.

5. **Document** the incident, including the root cause, the error budget consumed, the mitigation steps, and the follow-up actions. The documentation is completed within 24 hours of the incident. The post-mortem is scheduled within 5 business days.

The error budget consumed during the incident is deducted from the remaining budget. If the SLO was violated, the incident triggers a formal post-mortem with a review of the error budget consumption. The post-mortem includes a calculation of the budget consumed, the root cause, the actions taken, and the recommendations for preventing recurrence.

The incident response should also include a communication component. If the incident affects users, the team should post a status update within 15 minutes of acknowledgment. The status update should include the affected SLO, the current burn rate, the estimated time to resolution, and the next update time.

### Step 10: Review and Iterate

The SLO system is not static. The team should review the SLO targets, the alerting thresholds, and the response process on a regular schedule. The weekly SLO review covers: current compliance for all SLOs, any SLO violations from the past week, the effectiveness of alerting thresholds, and any adjustments to SLO targets.

The quarterly SLO review is more comprehensive. It covers: the overall SLO compliance trend, the error budget consumption pattern, the correlation between SLO violations and user impact, and the SLO targets for the next quarter. The quarterly review is the forum for adjusting SLO targets based on changes in user expectations, infrastructure capability, and business requirements.

The annual SLO review is strategic. It covers: the SLO program's effectiveness, the SLO coverage across the organization, the cost of SLO infrastructure, and the SLO strategy for the coming year. The annual review is the forum for making significant changes to the SLO program.

### Step 11: Close the Loop with Error Budget Policy

The error budget policy connects SLO compliance to engineering decisions. The policy defines what happens when the error budget is consumed at different levels. The policy is the mechanism that makes SLOs actionable.

The standard error budget policy has three zones:

- **Green zone (budget remaining over 50%):** Normal operations. Deployments proceed normally. Any change can be deployed. The team monitors the budget but is not constrained by it.

- **Yellow zone (budget remaining 20-50%):** Restricted operations. Only reliability improvements, bug fixes, and performance optimizations can be deployed. New features are blocked. The deployment pipeline adds a manual approval step. The team investigates the budget consumption trend.

- **Red zone (budget remaining under 20%):** Frozen operations. No deployments are allowed. The team enters incident response mode. The root cause of the budget consumption is identified and fixed. Deployments resume only when the budget recovers above 50%.

The error budget policy is enforced by the deployment pipeline. The pipeline checks the current budget consumption before allowing a deployment. If the budget is in the yellow zone, the pipeline blocks the deployment unless it is a reliability improvement. If the budget is in the red zone, all deployments are blocked.

## Variants

### Variant 1: Request-Based SLO

The most common SLO type measures the proportion of requests that succeed. This is straightforward to calculate and maps directly to user experience. The SLI is "proportion of requests that meet the success criteria."

Request-based SLOs are sensitive to traffic volume. A 10-minute outage during peak hours (100,000 requests per minute) consumes more error budget than the same outage during off-peak hours (1,000 requests per minute). This is correct behavior — the outage affected more users — but it means the SLO is not a pure measure of service health. It is a measure of user impact.

### Variant 2: Time-Based SLO

Time-based SLOs measure the proportion of time the service is available, regardless of request volume. The SLI is "the proportion of 1-minute windows where the service meets the success criteria." Each minute is treated as a single data point, regardless of how many requests were processed during that minute.

Time-based SLOs are useful for services with variable traffic. A database service that handles 10,000 requests per second during peak and 10 requests per second at 3:00 AM should not be penalized equally for failures during both periods. Time-based SLOs treat each minute equally, which means off-peak failures have the same weight as peak failures.

The disadvantage is that time-based SLOs can hide user-impacting failures during low-traffic periods. A service that fails completely for 5 minutes at 3:00 AM loses 5 minutes of error budget, even though only 50 users were affected. The same failure at 3:00 PM affects 50,000 users but consumes the same 5 minutes of budget.

Time-based SLOs are commonly used for infrastructure services that have variable request volumes. A DNS service, for example, processes millions of queries during peak hours and thousands during off-peak hours. A time-based SLO ensures that an off-peak outage does not consume disproportionate budget. The trade-off is that a peak-hour outage has less impact on the SLO than it should given the number of affected users.

The choice between request-based and time-based SLOs depends on the service's traffic pattern and the SLO's purpose. For user-facing services, request-based SLOs are more representative. For infrastructure services, time-based SLOs are more appropriate. For SLA compliance, the choice should match the SLA definition — if the SLA is defined in terms of uptime percentage, the SLO should be time-based.

### Variant 3: Aggregate SLO

Aggregate SLOs combine multiple SLIs into a single SLO. For example, a search service might have an aggregate SLO that requires: (1) 99.9% of queries return results in under 100ms, and (2) 99.99% of queries do not return errors, and (3) 99.9% of queries return the freshest results within 5 minutes of indexing.

Aggregate SLOs are more representative of the overall user experience but harder to debug. When the aggregate SLO is violated, you need to determine which component SLI caused the violation. This requires nested monitoring — each SLI tracked individually, with the aggregate computed from the conjunction.

### Variant 4: Percentile-Based SLO

Percentile-based SLOs target a specific percentile of the distribution. "p99 latency under 200ms" means the 99th percentile latency must be under 200ms — 99% of requests are faster than 200ms, and 1% can be slower. This is different from a request-based SLO that says "99% of requests complete in under 200ms." The percentile formulation is a statement about the distribution tail. The request-based formulation is a statement about the overall success rate.

Percentile-based SLOs are more informative than average-based SLOs. An average latency of 100ms can hide a p99.9 latency of 2 seconds. The percentile formulation ensures that the slowest requests are also acceptable. Most production systems use percentile-based SLOs for latency and request-based SLOs for error rate.

The choice of percentile matters. p50 (median) latency is the typical user experience. p95 latency is the experience of the slowest 5% of users. p99 latency is the experience of the slowest 1%. p99.9 latency is the experience of the slowest 0.1%. Each percentile provides a different perspective on the tail of the distribution. Most latency SLOs use p99 as the target because it captures the tail without being too sensitive to extreme outliers.

Percentile-based SLOs require histogram data for accurate computation. A simple counter of good and bad requests is not sufficient. The system must track the distribution of latencies using a histogram data structure. The histogram stores the count of requests in each latency bucket. The percentile is computed by finding the bucket that contains the Nth percentile of requests.

### Variant 5: Window-Based SLO

Window-based SLOs measure the proportion of time windows where the service is healthy, rather than the proportion of individual requests that succeed. This is a hybrid between request-based and time-based SLOs.

In a window-based SLO, each 1-minute window is classified as "good" if the error rate within that window is below a threshold. If the threshold is 1% errors per minute and the window has 0.5% errors, the window is good. If the window has 2% errors, the window is bad. The SLO is the proportion of good windows over the measurement period.

Window-based SLOs are useful for services where the individual request outcome is less important than the aggregate behavior. A notification service that sends push notifications may have a target of "at least 99% of 1-minute windows have fewer than 1% delivery failures." This captures the service's overall health without being sensitive to individual notification failures.

The threshold for classifying a window as good or bad is a configuration parameter. A lower threshold makes the SLO stricter — more windows are classified as bad. A higher threshold makes the SLO looser. The threshold should be set based on the service's acceptable error rate within a short time window.

### Variant 6: Composite SLO

Composite SLOs combine multiple SLOs into a single metric. A composite SLO might require that all three of a service's SLOs — availability, latency, and error rate — are simultaneously met. The composite is in violation if any component SLO is violated.

Composite SLOs are useful for executive reporting. A single number that captures the overall health of a service is more digestible than three separate SLOs. The composite SLO should be displayed on the executive dashboard alongside the individual SLOs for drill-down.

The disadvantage of composite SLOs is that they hide which component caused the violation. A composite SLO that drops from 99.9% to 99.5% requires investigation to determine whether the latency SLO, the error rate SLO, or the availability SLO caused the drop. The composite should always be accompanied by the individual SLOs for debugging.

Composite SLOs are also useful for multi-service systems. A user-facing flow that involves three services can have a composite SLO that requires all three services to meet their individual SLOs. The composite SLO represents the end-to-end user experience.

## Common SLO Pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: Setting SLOs Based on Current Performance

The most common pitfall is setting the SLO based on the current measured performance. If the service currently achieves 99.95% availability, the team sets the SLO to 99.95%. This is backwards. The SLO should be based on what users need, not on what the system currently delivers. Setting the SLO to current performance means the error budget is always exactly consumed. There is no room for improvement and no signal about degradation.

The correct approach is to set the SLO based on user expectations, subtract a margin for improvement, and use the gap between current performance and the SLO as a target for improvement. If users need 99.9% and the system delivers 99.95%, set the SLO at 99.9%. The 0.05% gap is the improvement target.

### Pitfall 2: Too Many SLOs

A team defines 20 SLOs for a single service. Every endpoint, every operation, every dependency gets its own SLO. The result is 20 dashboards, 20 alert rules, and 20 possible violations. The team spends all their time managing SLOs and no time improving the service.

The fix is to start with 3 SLOs per service: one for availability, one for latency, and one for error rate. Add more SLOs only when a specific reliability concern justifies a new SLO. Each SLO should have a clear decision associated with it. If you cannot articulate what decision the SLO drives, you do not need it.

### Pitfall 3: Ignoring the Error Budget

A team defines SLOs, sets up dashboards, configures alerts, and then ignores the error budget. Deployments proceed regardless of budget consumption. The SLO is a dashboard widget, not a decision tool. The team is spending effort on SLO management without getting the operational benefit.

The fix is to integrate the error budget into the deployment pipeline. Block deployments when the budget is low. Make the budget visible in every engineer's terminal. Review the budget in every team meeting. The SLO is only useful if it drives decisions.

### Pitfall 4: Treating All SLOs Equally

A team has 10 SLOs and treats them all with the same severity. A 5-minute violation of the home page SLO triggers the same response as a 5-minute violation of an internal admin endpoint SLO. The team is paged for everything, which means they stop responding to anything.

The fix is to tier SLOs by criticality. The home page has a 99.99% SLO with a 5-minute alert window. The admin endpoint has a 99% SLO with a 1-hour alert window. The pager is reserved for the home page SLO. The admin endpoint SLO creates a ticket. Different SLOs have different operational responses.

### Pitfall 5: No SLO for the SLO System

The team builds an elaborate SLO system but does not monitor the system itself. The SLO evaluator crashes. The instrumentation layer stops emitting metrics. The database fills up. The team discovers the failure when the CEO asks for the monthly SLO report and the dashboard shows no data.

The fix is to define an SLO for the SLO system itself. The SLI is "the fraction of minutes where the SLO system successfully evaluated all configured SLOs." The target is 99.9% over a 30-day window. If the SLO system itself is not reliable, the reliability data is not trustworthy.

### Pitfall 6: Annual SLO Windows

A team uses a calendar-year SLO window. The error budget resets on January 1. A December outage causes the team to miss the annual SLO target. The team accepts the failure and starts fresh in January. The SLO has no operational impact because the team knows a reset is coming.

Annual SLOs are too long to drive operational behavior. The team cannot connect today's deploy decision to the annual SLO target. The SLO becomes a compliance checkbox rather than an operational tool. Use 30-day rolling windows for operational SLOs and annual windows only for compliance reporting.

### Pitfall 7: The Perfection Trap

A team refuses to define SLOs until they can measure everything perfectly. They spend months building the perfect instrumentation system, the perfect aggregation pipeline, and the perfect alerting rules. By the time the SLO system is ready, the service has had three major outages that would have been caught by a simple 99.9% availability SLO.

The fix is to start with an imperfect SLO and iterate. Measure latency from the load balancer logs instead of from the client. Use a 1% sample instead of 100% instrumentation. The first version of the SLO should be operational within a week. Perfection comes from iteration, not from upfront design.

### Pitfall 8: SLO as a Blame Tool

Management uses SLO compliance as a performance metric. Teams that miss their SLO are penalized. Engineers stop shipping changes because they fear causing an SLO violation. The error budget is never used because nobody wants to risk the penalty. The SLO system becomes a barrier to innovation instead of an enabler of safe innovation.

The fix is to use SLOs as a learning tool, not a blame tool. When a team misses its SLO, the response is investigation and learning, not punishment. The error budget is explicitly allocated for innovation — the team is expected to use it. The SLO review is a discussion about what the team learned and how to improve, not about who is responsible for the failure.

## SLO Maturity Model

### Level 1: Ad Hoc

The team has no formal SLOs. Reliability is measured by intuition, anecdotal evidence, and whatever dashboards happen to exist. The team knows the service is "slow" or "down" but cannot quantify it. The CEO asks "is the system reliable?" and the answer is "we think so." Incidents are discovered by users, not by monitoring.

At this level, the team should implement a single availability SLO. Measure the fraction of requests that succeed. Display the number on a dashboard. This is the minimum viable SLO.

### Level 2: Measurement

The team has one or more SLOs defined. The SLOs are displayed on dashboards. The team can answer the question "is the service meeting its SLO?" at any moment. But the SLOs do not drive decisions. The team looks at the SLOs but does not change their behavior based on them.

At this level, the team should implement the error budget and start tracking consumption. The error budget should be displayed alongside the SLO compliance. The team should discuss the error budget in their weekly meeting.

### Level 3: Action

The team uses SLOs to drive operational decisions. The error budget governs the deployment pipeline. When the budget is low, the team stops shipping features and focuses on reliability. The on-call engineer responds to SLO alerts. The SLO dashboard is the primary operational interface.

At this level, the team has 3-5 SLOs per service, multi-window multi-burn-rate alerting, and a functioning error budget policy. The SLO system is integrated into the development workflow.

### Level 4: Optimization

The team actively optimizes their SLOs. SLO targets are reviewed and adjusted quarterly. The team has a systematic process for improving SLOs. The SLO system is instrumented and monitored. The team knows the cost of each SLO and optimizes the cost-benefit trade-off.

At this level, the team has automated SLO tuning. The SLO targets are adjusted based on historical performance and user behavior. The alerting thresholds are tuned to minimize false positives while maintaining sensitivity.

### Level 5: Autonomous

The SLO system drives automated remediation. When the burn rate exceeds a threshold, the system automatically rolls back the deployment, scales up capacity, or redirects traffic. The human engineer is notified but does not need to take immediate action. The SLO system is self-healing.

At this level, the team has automated runbooks for common SLO violation scenarios. The system can detect the root cause of a violation and apply the appropriate mitigation. The human engineer reviews the automated response and confirms the resolution.

## SLO Implementation Strategies

### Strategy 1: Start with Availability

The simplest SLO to implement is availability. The SLI is "the fraction of requests that return a successful response." The measurement is straightforward — every request either succeeds or fails. The counter is easy to instrument. The SLO target is easy to understand.

Start with an availability SLO for every service. This gives the team the foundational SLO experience without the complexity of latency histograms, percentile calculations, or multi-dimensional SLIs. Once the availability SLO is working, add latency SLOs for the most critical endpoints.

### Strategy 2: Use the 3-5-3 Rule

The 3-5-3 rule is a heuristic for SLO target selection. Start with 3 nines (99.9%) for most services. If the service is critical to revenue, move to 5 nines (99.999%). If the service is internal or experimental, use 3 nines or less. The rule is:

- 3 nines (99.9%): Most services. This is the industry standard for acceptable reliability.
- 5 nines (99.999%): Revenue-critical, customer-facing services. This is expensive and requires significant infrastructure investment.
- 3 nines or less (99.9% or lower): Internal services, prototypes, experimental services. Speed of iteration matters more than reliability.

The 3-5-3 rule is a starting point, not a final answer. The actual SLO target should be negotiated based on user needs, system capability, and business requirements.

### Strategy 3: Implement Error Budget Policies Gradually

Do not implement the full error budget policy on day one. Start with monitoring and visibility. Show the error budget on the dashboard. Let the team get used to seeing the number. After a month, introduce the error budget review in the weekly team meeting. After two months, implement the deployment gate — block deployments when the budget is below 50%.

The gradual implementation gives the team time to adjust to the SLO mindset. Sudden imposition of error budget policies causes resistance. Gradual adoption builds understanding and buy-in.

### Strategy 4: Align SLOs with On-Call Rotations

The on-call rotation should be aligned with SLOs. The on-call engineer is responsible for monitoring the SLO dashboards and responding to SLO alerts. The on-call handoff includes a review of the current SLO compliance and any active SLO violations.

The SLO dashboard should be the first thing the on-call engineer checks when they start their shift. The dashboard shows the current state of all SLOs, any active violations, and the budget consumption trend. The on-call engineer knows which services need attention and which are healthy.

### Strategy 5: Celebrate SLO Wins

When a team meets its SLO target for a full quarter, celebrate. When a team recovers from an SLO violation, celebrate the recovery. When a team improves its SLO target from 99.9% to 99.99%, celebrate. The celebration reinforces the importance of SLOs and makes the team feel good about reliability work.

The celebration can be simple: a mention in the team meeting, a message in the company chat, or a team lunch. The key is to make reliability visible and valued. SLOs should be associated with positive outcomes, not just with pager alerts and incident reviews.

## SLO Definition Template

Every SLO should be defined using a consistent template. The template captures all the information needed to implement, monitor, and respond to the SLO. Here is the standard template:

```yaml
slo_id: api-availability
service: user-api

  return a successful response (2xx or 3xx status code) within
  500 milliseconds.
sli_type: request_based
measurement: |

  - Status code is 2xx or 3xx
  - Response time is under 500ms
  All other requests are classified as bad.
measurement_window: 30_days_rolling
slo_target: 0.999
error_budget: 0.001
alerting:
  - tier: page
    burn_rate: 14.4
    window: 5_minutes
    channel: pagerduty
    cooldown: 30_minutes
  - tier: ticket
    burn_rate: 6.0
    window: 30_minutes
    channel: jira
    cooldown: 60_minutes
  - tier: notify
    burn_rate: 1.0
    window: 6_hours
    channel: email
    cooldown: 240_minutes
  - tier: trend
    burn_rate: 0.5
    window: 3_days
    channel: dashboard
    cooldown: none
dependencies:
  - service: user-database
    impact: if the database is slow, API latency increases
  - service: user-cache
    impact: if the cache is down, API latency increases
owner: team-user-api
review_schedule: quarterly
```

The template ensures consistency across SLOs and makes it easy to automate SLO configuration. Each SLO has a unique ID, a clear description, a defined SLI type, measurement criteria, alerting configuration, and ownership information. The template is stored in the SLO configuration repository and version-controlled alongside the service code.

## SLO Calculation Examples

### Example 1: Availability SLO Calculation

A web service has a 99.9% availability SLO over a 30-day rolling window. The service processes an average of 100 requests per second. Over 30 days, the total request volume is:

```
30 days * 24 hours * 60 minutes * 60 seconds * 100 req/sec = 259,200,000 requests
```

The error budget is:
```
259,200,000 * (1 - 0.999) = 259,200 allowed failures
```

Now the service experiences a 10-minute outage. During the outage, no requests succeed. The total failed requests are:
```
10 minutes * 60 seconds * 100 req/sec = 60,000 failed requests
```

The budget consumption is:
```
60,000 / 259,200 = 23.1% of the error budget
```

The remaining budget is:
```
259,200 - 60,000 = 199,200 failures remaining
```

### Example 2: Latency SLO Calculation

An API service has a p99 latency SLO of 500ms. The service processes 1 million requests in a 1-hour window. The p99 latency is measured from the latency histogram.

The latency histogram has these buckets: 100ms, 200ms, 300ms, 400ms, 500ms, 750ms, 1000ms, 2000ms, 5000ms.

After processing 1 million requests, the histogram counts are:

```python
buckets = [100, 200, 300, 400, 500, 750, 1000, 2000, 5000]
counts  = [500000, 200000, 100000,  50000,  50000, 30000, 20000, 15000, 35000]
```

To find p99:
- Total requests: 1,000,000
- p99 target index: 1,000,000 * 0.99 = 990,000
- Cumulative counts: 500K + 200K + 100K + 50K + 50K + 30K + 20K + 15K = 965,000
- The 990,000th request falls in the 2000ms bucket (cumulative at 750ms is 950K, at 1000ms is 970K, at 2000ms is 985K)
- Wait, 965K + 15K = 980K, the 990,000th request is in the 5000ms bucket
- p99 latency is approximately 5000ms

The service is violating the 500ms p99 SLO because p99 is approximately 5000ms. Only 950K out of 1M requests (95%) are under 750ms. The p99 is closer to 5000ms. The service needs optimization to reduce the tail latency.

### Example 3: Burn Rate Calculation

A service has a 99.9% SLO. Over the last 5 minutes, the service processed 30,000 requests. 500 of those requests failed. What is the burn rate?

```
Error rate over 5 minutes: 500 / 30,000 = 1.67%
Allowed error rate (from SLO): 1 - 0.999 = 0.1%
Burn rate: 1.67% / 0.1% = 16.7
```

A burn rate of 16.7 over 5 minutes exceeds the page threshold of 14.4. The on-call engineer is paged.

### Example 4: Budget Exhaustion Time Calculation

Using the same scenario, how long until the error budget is exhausted?

```
Measurement window: 30 days = 43,200 minutes
Burn rate: 16.7
Budget exhaustion time: 43,200 / 16.7 = 2,587 minutes = 43 hours = 1.8 days
```

The budget will be exhausted in 1.8 days if the current burn rate continues. This is a critical situation requiring immediate response.

### Example 5: Multi-Service Aggregate SLO

A user-facing flow depends on three services: Service A (99.9% SLO), Service B (99.95% SLO), and Service C (99.99% SLO). What is the end-to-end SLO for the flow?

```
End-to-end SLO = min(99.9%, 99.95%, 99.99%) = 99.9%
```

Wait, this is not quite right. The end-to-end SLO is the probability that all three services are healthy simultaneously:

```
End-to-end SLO = 0.999 * 0.9995 * 0.9999 = 0.9984 = 99.84%
```

The end-to-end SLO (99.84%) is lower than any individual SLO because the probability of all three services being healthy simultaneously is the product of their individual probabilities. This means the user experience is less reliable than any single service's target. To achieve an end-to-end SLO of 99.9%, each service must have a higher individual SLO, or the dependencies must be reduced.

## Mental Model Check

### Question 1

Your service has a 99.9% SLO over a 30-day rolling window. At the current error rate, the burn rate is 0.5. How long will it take to exhaust the error budget?

**Answer:** A burn rate of 0.5 means the error budget is being consumed at half the nominal rate. The nominal rate would exhaust the budget in 30 days. At 0.5 burn rate, the budget will be exhausted in 60 days. Since the measurement window is 30 days, the budget will never be exhausted at this burn rate — the window is shorter than the exhaustion time. This means the system is performing above the SLO target. The actual availability is 99.95%, which is better than the 99.9% target.

### Question 2

Your team is debating between a 99.9% SLO and a 99.99% SLO for a new internal API. The 99.99% SLO would require a 200% increase in infrastructure cost. What factors should guide the decision?

**Answer:** The decision depends on the cost of failure versus the cost of reliability. If the API is used by critical internal systems where a 5-minute outage costs 50,000 dollars in lost revenue, the 99.99% SLO is justified. If the API is used by non-critical systems where a 5-minute outage costs 500 dollars, the 99.9% SLO is sufficient. The key question is: what is the business impact of exceeding the error budget? The SLO should be set so that the cost of achieving the SLO is less than the cost of failing to meet it.

### Question 3

A service has a 99.9% SLO over a 30-day window. It experiences a 10-minute complete outage during peak traffic (50,000 requests per minute). How much error budget is consumed, and is the service in violation?

**Answer:** The error budget is 0.1% of (30 days * 24 hours * 60 minutes) = 43.2 minutes. The outage lasted 10 minutes. The outage consumed 10 / 43.2 = 23.1% of the error budget. The service is not in violation — 23.1% consumption is below 100%. However, if the outage had lasted 44 minutes, the service would be in violation. The outage also consumed 500,000 bad requests out of the total error budget of 43.2 minutes worth of requests. The remaining budget is 33.2 minutes or approximately 33,200,000 requests.

### Question 4

Your team uses a 99.9% SLO with a 7-day window. You notice that the service consistently shows 99.95% availability on weekdays but drops to 99.5% on Sundays due to a weekly batch job. Should you change the SLO?

**Answer:** The SLO itself is fine. The problem is the batch job. The batch job consumes error budget on Sundays that could be used for unexpected failures during the week. The solution is to fix the batch job to not degrade the service, or to run it at a lower priority that does not affect the SLI. Changing the SLO to hide the batch job would mask a real reliability problem. The correct approach is to treat the Sunday degradation as an incident and fix the root cause.

### Question 5

Your team is implementing SLOs for the first time. You have dashboards for latency, error rate, throughput, CPU usage, memory usage, disk I/O, network bandwidth, database query time, cache hit rate, and garbage collection time. Which of these should become SLIs?

**Answer:** The SLIs should be the ones that directly measure user-facing behavior: latency, error rate, and throughput. CPU usage, memory usage, disk I/O, and garbage collection are infrastructure metrics — they are useful for debugging but they are not SLIs. They are correlated with user experience but they do not measure it directly. The rule is: an SLI should be something that, if it were the only metric you had, you could still determine whether the service is working. Infrastructure metrics fail this test. A service can have high CPU usage but still serve requests correctly. A service can have low CPU usage but be returning errors on every request.

### Question 6

A service has a 99.9% SLO over a 30-day window. The service processes 1 million requests per day. On a typical day, 500 requests fail. The team considers this normal. After a deployment, the failure rate increases to 2,000 requests per day. How many days until the SLO is violated?

**Answer:** The SLO allows 0.1% failures over 30 days — 0.1% of 30 million requests = 30,000 allowed failures. At 500 failures per day, the service consumes 15,000 failures in 30 days, which is 50% of the budget. The remaining budget is 15,000 failures. At 2,000 failures per day, the remaining budget will be consumed in 15,000 / 2,000 = 7.5 days. The SLO will be violated in 7.5 days if the failure rate does not decrease. This means the team has 7.5 days to fix the deployment before the SLO is violated.

### Question 7

A team defines an SLO for "API latency" as the average latency across all endpoints. The average latency is 120ms. The SLO target is 200ms. The team is meeting the SLO. Users are complaining that the API is slow. What is the most likely explanation?

**Answer:** The average latency hides the tail. The average latency of 120ms could mean that 90% of requests complete in 50ms and 10% complete in 750ms. The average is 120ms, but the p99 latency is 750ms. Users who experience the slow requests — the 10% of requests that take 750ms — are the ones complaining. The average-based SLO was meeting the target while the user experience was poor. The fix is to switch to a percentile-based SLO, targeting p99 latency under 500ms instead of average latency under 200ms.

### Question 8

Your team uses a 99.9% SLO with a 30-day rolling window. The service experiences a 30-minute outage that consumes 60% of the error budget. The team fixes the root cause and the service returns to normal. 15 days later, the same outage occurs again. After the second outage, is the service in SLO violation?

**Answer:** The first outage consumed 60% of the budget. The remaining budget was 40%. Over the next 15 days, the service performed normally, so the budget was not further consumed. The second outage consumed another 60% of the original budget. The total consumption is 120% of the original budget. The service is in SLO violation. The violation started during the second outage when the cumulative consumption exceeded 100%. The service will remain in violation until the first outage's data ages out of the 30-day window — 15 days after the second outage.

### Question 9

A team implements SLOs and notices that the error budget is always at 95% remaining. The service has never come close to violating its SLO. The team starts ignoring the SLO dashboard because it never changes. What is the likely problem, and what should the team do?

**Answer:** The SLO target is too loose. The service is performing far better than the target, which means the error budget is never threatened. The team has no incentive to improve reliability because the SLO is always green. The fix is to tighten the SLO target to match the actual performance. If the service consistently achieves 99.99% availability, the SLO should be 99.99% or even 99.995%. The error budget should be small enough that the team feels the pressure of budget consumption. The goal is to make the SLO useful, not comfortable.

### Question 10

A service has a 99.9% SLO over a 30-day window. The team notices that the compliance percentage fluctuates between 99.88% and 99.92% on a daily basis. The fluctuations are driven by normal traffic patterns — higher latency during peak hours and lower latency during off-peak. The team is making deployment decisions based on whether the SLO is green or red at the moment of deployment. Is this the right approach?

**Answer:** The SLO should not be used for minute-by-minute deployment decisions. The 30-day rolling window is designed to smooth out short-term fluctuations. A deployment decision should be based on the trend, not the current value. If the compliance is trending downward over weeks, that is a signal to slow down deployments. If the compliance is fluctuating within a normal range, deployments should proceed normally. The team should look at the 7-day trend of the compliance percentage, not the current value. The SLO is a strategic tool, not a tactical gate.

### Question 11

A team has a 99.9% SLO for a service that processes 10 million requests per day. The team defines a "good request" as one that completes in under 500ms. The p99 latency is consistently 450ms. The p99.9 latency is 950ms. Is the service meeting the SLO?

**Answer:** The service is meeting the SLO. The SLO says 99.9% of requests must complete in under 500ms. The p99 latency is 450ms, which means 99% of requests are under 450ms. The p99.9 latency is 950ms, which means 99.9% of requests are under 950ms, not 500ms. The p99.9 is over 500ms, which means 0.1% of requests take longer than 500ms. Since the SLO allows 0.1% of requests to fail (100% - 99.9% = 0.1%), the service is exactly at the boundary. If the p99.9 latency were 1,000ms, the service would be in violation. The team should watch the p99.9 trend closely.

### Question 12

Your team has 10 microservices. Each service has its own SLO. The composite end-to-end SLO for the user-facing flow that touches all 10 services is 99.0%. The product manager wants the end-to-end SLO to be 99.9%. What is the simplest approach to achieve this?

**Answer:** The simplest approach is to reduce the number of services in the critical path. If the flow touches 10 services, each with 99.9% SLO, the end-to-end probability is 0.999^10 = 99.0%. To achieve 99.9% end-to-end, either each service must have a much higher individual SLO (0.999^10 = 0.990, so 0.9999^10 = 0.999, requiring 99.99% per service), or the number of services in the path must be reduced. The most practical approach is to reduce the number of dependencies. If the flow touches 3 services instead of 10, each with 99.97% SLO, the end-to-end SLO is 99.9%.

### Question 13

A service has a 99.9% SLO with a 30-day window. The service experiences a 15-minute outage that consumes 35% of the error budget. The team fixes the root cause. The budget recovers over 30 days as the outage data ages out. After 15 days, the same outage occurs again. What is the budget consumption after the second outage?

**Answer:** After the first outage, 35% of the budget was consumed. After 15 days, half of the outage data has aged out of the 30-day window. The remaining consumption from the first outage is 35% * 15/30 = 17.5%. The second outage consumes another 35%. The total consumption is 17.5% + 35% = 52.5%. The service is not in violation (52.5% is under 100%), but the budget is more than half consumed. The team has 47.5% of the budget remaining for the next 15 days. If a third outage occurs, the service will be in violation.

### Question 14

A team defines a 99.99% SLO for a service that runs on spot instances. Spot instances can be terminated at any time with 2 minutes notice. The service handles spot instance termination by draining connections and restarting on new instances. The restart takes 30 seconds. How does the spot instance termination affect the SLO?

**Answer:** The 30-second restart during a spot instance termination counts against the SLO. If the service has 100 instances and one instance is terminated each day, the total downtime per day is 30 seconds. Over 30 days, the total downtime is 900 seconds = 15 minutes. The error budget for a 99.99% SLO over 30 days is 4.32 minutes. The spot instance terminations alone consume 15 minutes of budget, which is 347% of the error budget. The service will be in perpetual SLO violation. The fix is to either use on-demand instances instead of spot instances, or to set a lower SLO that accounts for the spot instance behavior.

### Question 15

A team has a 99.9% SLO. The service has a daily batch job that runs at 2:00 AM and takes 10 minutes. During the batch job, the service's latency increases by 200% because the batch job consumes database resources. Should the team exclude the batch job window from the SLO measurement?

**Answer:** Excluding the batch job window from the SLO measurement would hide a real reliability problem. The batch job degrades the service, and users who are active at 2:00 AM experience the degradation. The correct approach is to fix the batch job to not degrade the service. This could involve running the batch job on a separate database replica, throttling the batch job to limit its resource consumption, or running the batch job during off-peak hours when traffic is lowest. If none of these approaches are feasible, the team should set a separate SLO for the batch job window that reflects the expected performance during that period.
