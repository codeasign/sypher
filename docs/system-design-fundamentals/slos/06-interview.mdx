## How the Topic Appears

SLOs appear in system design interviews in several forms. The interviewer may ask directly about reliability measurement, or the topic may emerge naturally during a design discussion. Here are common phrasings:

1. "How would you define a reliability target for this system?"
2. "How do you know when this system is working correctly?"
3. "How would you measure whether this service meets its performance goals?"
4. "Design a system for monitoring and alerting on service health."
5. "How would you decide when to stop shipping features and focus on reliability?"
6. "You have dashboards showing latency and error rates. How do you turn this data into decisions?"
7. "The CEO asks: is the system reliable? How do you answer?"
8. "Design an alerting system that minimizes false positives while catching real problems."
9. "How do you handle the tension between feature velocity and system reliability?"
10. "A service consistently shows 99.9% latency compliance, but users complain of slowness. What went wrong?"

## What the Interviewer Tests

When the interviewer brings up SLOs, they are testing several specific skills:

**Operational maturity:** Do you understand the difference between raw metrics and actionable signals? Can you distinguish between measuring everything and knowing what matters?

**Trade-off awareness:** Do you understand the cost of reliability? Can you articulate why 99.99% is not always better than 99.9%?

**System thinking:** Do you understand how SLOs connect to alerting, deployment decisions, and incident response? Can you describe the full feedback loop from measurement to action?

**Practical experience:** Have you worked with SLOs in production? Can you describe real situations where SLOs drove decisions or where they failed?

**Communication clarity:** Can you explain SLO concepts to both technical and non-technical audiences? The ability to explain error budgets to a product manager is a skill the interviewer looks for.

## Level-by-Level Expectations

### L3 / Junior Engineer

At the L3 level, the interviewer expects you to understand the basic SLO concepts. You should define SLI, SLO, and error budget. You should explain that an SLO is a target for an SLI, and the error budget is the allowed failure.

A strong L3 answer covers: what SLIs measure (latency, error rate, availability), what SLOs define (the target threshold), and what error budgets represent (the margin for failure). Mention a 30-day rolling window as the standard measurement period.

A weak L3 answer confuses SLI with SLO, cannot explain error budgets, or proposes monitoring every metric without prioritization.

### L4 / Mid-Level Engineer

At L4, the interviewer expects you to apply SLOs to a specific design scenario. You should be able to define appropriate SLOs for the system being designed and explain how they drive operational decisions.

A strong L4 answer identifies 2-3 key SLOs for the system, explains the rationale for each target, and describes how the SLO feeds into the alerting system. Mention multi-window multi-burn-rate alerting as the standard approach.

A weak L4 answer proposes a single SLO for the entire system, sets targets arbitrarily, or cannot connect SLOs to operational decisions.

### L5 / Senior Engineer

At L5, the interviewer expects you to design a complete SLO system. You should cover instrumentation, aggregation, evaluation, alerting, and the feedback loop into the development process.

A strong L5 answer describes the full pipeline: per-request instrumentation, local and global aggregation, sliding window evaluation, multi-window burn rate alerting, and error-budget-based deployment gating. Discuss trade-offs in sampling rate, window size, and alert sensitivity.

A weak L5 answer focuses only on the dashboard layer and does not address instrumentation, aggregation, or the operational feedback loop.

### L6 / Staff Engineer

At L6, the interviewer expects you to address organizational and strategic aspects of SLOs. You should discuss team-level accountability, SLO negotiation with stakeholders, and the cultural impact of error budget policies.

A strong L6 answer covers: how to introduce SLOs to an organization that has never used them, how to negotiate SLO targets with product and business stakeholders, how the error budget policy affects team culture and engineering velocity, and how to handle SLO violations from dependent services.

A weak L6 answer treats SLOs as a purely technical problem and does not address the organizational dynamics of reliability management.

### L7 / Principal Engineer

At L7, the interviewer expects you to discuss SLOs at an industry-wide level. You should compare SLO approaches across companies, identify emerging trends, and anticipate future developments in reliability engineering.

A strong L7 answer discusses: the evolution from Google SRE SLOs to the open-source ecosystem (Prometheus, Grafana SLO), the differences between cloud provider SLO implementations, the limitations of current SLO systems (the dead metric problem, the budget hoarder anti-pattern), and the future of SLOs with AI-driven anomaly detection and automated remediation.

## FAANG Framework for SLO Answers

Use this structured approach when SLOs come up in an interview:

**Step 1: Define the SLI** — Start by stating what you will measure. Choose something directly observable from the user perspective. "I will measure the fraction of HTTP requests that complete with a 2xx or 3xx status code within 500 milliseconds."

**Step 2: Set the SLO target** — Explain how you choose the target. Reference user needs, business requirements, and system capability. "For a user-facing API, I would target 99.9% based on industry standards for similar services. For an internal service, I might target 99.5% to allow more development velocity."

**Step 3: Calculate the error budget** — Show the math. "A 99.9% SLO over a 30-day window gives an error budget of 0.1% — approximately 43 minutes of allowed downtime per month. This budget resets as a rolling window."

**Step 4: Design the alerting** — Describe multi-window multi-burn-rate alerting. "I will use four alerting thresholds: a burn rate of 14.4 over 5 minutes for immediate page, 6.0 over 30 minutes for ticket creation, 1.0 over 6 hours for notification, and 0.5 over 3 days for trend detection."

**Step 5: Close the feedback loop** — Explain how SLOs drive decisions. "When the error budget drops below 50%, the team enters a reliability hardening phase where only bug fixes and reliability improvements are shipped. Deployment pipelines automatically block non-critical changes when the budget is low."

## Red Flags

These statements or behaviors in an interview signal that you may not have deep SLO experience:

**Confusing SLI and SLO:** If you say "the SLO is 200ms" without specifying the SLI or measurement window, you are confusing the target with the indicator.

**No error budget:** If you describe SLOs without mentioning error budgets, you are missing the most important operational concept. The error budget is what makes SLOs actionable.

**Ignore costs:** If you propose 99.999% SLOs for every service without discussing cost, you lack practical experience. Five-nines requires 10x the infrastructure investment compared to four-nines.

**Single window only:** If you describe a single-window alerting approach (e.g., "alert if error rate exceeds 1% over the last 5 minutes"), you have not encountered the false positive problem that multi-window alerting solves.

**No measurement window:** If you define an SLO without specifying the measurement window (7 days, 30 days, 90 days), your definition is incomplete.

**Tabular data only:** If you cannot draw a diagram of the SLO pipeline, you lack system-level understanding of how SLOs work.

**Perfectionist SLOs:** If you propose 100% SLO targets, you do not understand that error budgets enable velocity. 100% SLO means zero error budget, which means zero tolerance for deployment risk. This is not practical.

**No organizational awareness:** If you describe SLOs purely as a technical mechanism without discussing team culture, stakeholder negotiation, or deployment policies, you lack the organizational perspective needed for senior roles.

**Ignore failure modes:** If you do not mention the dead metric problem, double counting, or clock skew, you have not deployed SLOs in a real production environment.

**No ongoing evaluation:** If you think SLOs are set once and never revisited, you miss the iterative nature of SLO management. Targets must be adjusted as the system and user expectations evolve.

**SLOs as a Blame Tool:** If you describe SLOs as a way to measure team performance or hold people accountable, you reveal a lack of understanding of the blameless SLO culture. SLOs are for learning, not for blaming.

**No Mention of Alert Fatigue:** If you propose a single-window alerting approach without discussing false positives, you have not experienced the real-world problem of alert fatigue. The interviewer will know you lack production SLO experience.

**Ignoring the SLO System's Own Reliability:** If you design an SLO system but do not discuss how to monitor and maintain the SLO system itself, you have an incomplete design. The SLO system must be self-monitoring.

**Over-Engineering the Initial Solution:** If you propose a complete multi-region, multi-cloud, globally distributed SLO system for a service with 1,000 users, you lack judgment. The SLO system should be proportional to the service's scale and criticality.

**No Mention of Sampling:** If you propose measuring 100% of requests for a service processing 100,000 requests per second without discussing the cost, you lack practical experience. Sampling is essential for high-throughput services.

**Incorrect Math:** If you calculate the error budget incorrectly during the interview, the interviewer will notice. Practice the math: 30 days = 43,200 minutes. 99.9% = 43.2 minutes of budget. 99.99% = 4.32 minutes. 99.999% = 25.9 seconds.

## Mock Interview Transcript

The following mock interview shows how an SLO conversation might unfold in a system design interview. The interviewer is designing a video-sharing platform.

**Interviewer:** How would you ensure this video-sharing platform is reliable?

**Candidate:** I would start by defining what reliability means for this platform. For a video-sharing platform, the most important user-facing metrics are video playback success, upload success, and feed loading speed. I would define a Service Level Indicator for each of these.

**Interviewer:** Walk me through the playback SLI.

**Candidate:** The playback SLI is the fraction of play requests where the video starts playing within 3 seconds of the user pressing play and plays for at least 30 seconds without rebuffering. This measures the complete playback experience, not just the start time.

**Interviewer:** What would you set as the SLO target?

**Candidate:** I would recommend 99.9% for the playback SLO. This is the industry standard for video streaming services. It gives us an error budget of 0.1% — approximately 43 minutes per month of allowed playback failures. If our users need higher reliability, we can increase the target later, but starting at 99.9% gives us room to learn.

**Interviewer:** How would you know if we are about to violate the SLO?

**Candidate:** I would use multi-window multi-burn-rate alerting. I would set up four alerting tiers. The first tier would page the on-call engineer if the burn rate exceeds 14.4 over 5 minutes. This catches catastrophic failures immediately. The second tier would create a ticket if the burn rate exceeds 6.0 over 30 minutes. The third tier would send a notification if the burn rate exceeds 1.0 over 6 hours. The fourth tier would display a trend indicator on the dashboard if the burn rate exceeds 0.5 over 3 days.

**Interviewer:** What happens when the error budget is low?

**Candidate:** We would implement an error budget policy. When the budget drops below 50%, the team enters a reliability hardening phase. Only reliability improvements, bug fixes, and performance optimizations can be deployed. New features are blocked. The deployment pipeline automatically checks the budget consumption before allowing a deployment.

**Interviewer:** How would you instrument this system?

**Candidate:** I would use a sidecar agent pattern. Each service instance runs a sidecar process that receives metric events via a Unix socket. The sidecar batches events every 10 seconds or 10,000 events and flushes to an aggregation layer. The sidecar handles retries and backpressure so the service is never blocked by metric instrumentation.

**Interviewer:** What about the SLO system itself? How do you ensure it is reliable?

**Candidate:** The SLO system needs its own SLO. I would define a 99.9% SLO for the SLO evaluation system. The SLI would be the fraction of minutes where the SLO system successfully evaluated all configured SLOs. I would also implement data freshness monitoring — if the SLO system has not received data for 5 minutes, it alerts the operations team.

**Interviewer:** What are the most common failure modes?

**Candidate:** The dead metric failure is the most dangerous. If the instrumentation stops working, the SLO system sees zero errors and shows 100% compliance even though the service may be completely down. The mitigation is data freshness monitoring. Another common failure is the double counter — misconfiguration causes events to be counted twice. The mitigation is deduplication by instance ID and sequence number.

**Interviewer:** Good answer. Let me ask a more specific question: what would you do if the SLO shows 99.95% compliance but users are complaining about the service being slow?

**Candidate:** This is a classic SLO mismatch. The most likely cause is that the SLI is measuring the wrong thing. The SLO might be measuring server-side latency while users experience client-side latency. The difference could be network latency, TLS handshake time, or DNS resolution time. I would investigate the SLI definition and adjust it to measure from the client perspective using synthetic monitoring.

## Common Follow-up Questions

Interviewers often ask follow-up questions to probe deeper. Here are the most common ones with recommended answers:

**Q: How do you set the SLO target for a new service with no historical data?**

A: I would start with a provisional SLO of 99% for the first month. After one month of production data, I would analyze the actual performance and set the permanent SLO. The provisional SLO gives the team time to stabilize the service without the pressure of a permanent violation.

**Q: What is the difference between an SLO and an SLA?**

A: An SLO is an engineering target. It is internal, negotiable, and used for operational decisions. An SLA is a contractual commitment. It is external, legally binding, and has financial penalties. Every SLA contains one or more SLOs, but not every SLO is part of an SLA. Internal services operate on SLOs without SLAs. External customer-facing services have both.

**Q: How do you handle SLOs for services with external dependencies?**

A: I would define two SLOs: one for the end-to-end user experience that includes the external dependency, and one for the service's own performance excluding the dependency. The end-to-end SLO drives the user experience. The internal SLO drives the team's operational decisions. If the external dependency is slow, the end-to-end SLO may be violated, but the internal SLO remains healthy.

**Q: How do you handle planned maintenance in SLOs?**

A: Planned maintenance is either excluded from the SLO measurement or included. If the maintenance is communicated to users and has a fixed window, I exclude it. If the maintenance is not communicated, I include it. The decision depends on whether the user is aware of the maintenance and can plan around it.

**Q: How many SLOs should a service have?**

A: I recommend starting with three SLOs per service: availability, latency, and error rate. These three cover the most important user-facing properties. Additional SLOs are added only when a specific reliability concern justifies a new SLO. More than 5 SLOs per service is usually too many.

**Q: How do you handle SLO violations from dependent services?**

A: Each service has its own SLO based on its own SLI. If Service A depends on Service B and Service B is slow, Service A's SLO should reflect the end-to-end experience. The root cause is attributed to Service B, but Service A's SLO tracks the impact on the user. The dependency is a diagnostic dimension, not an SLO dimension.

## Practice Drills

### Drill 1: Define SLOs for a Video Streaming Service

Given a video streaming service with 50 million users, define three SLOs. Specify the SLI, the SLO target, the measurement window, and the alerting thresholds for each.

**Practice time:** 5 minutes.
**Expected output:** Playback start success, rebuffer rate, and video quality SLOs with specific targets and window sizes.

**Model Answer:**

SLO 1 — Playback Start Success
- SLI: Fraction of play requests where the video starts playing within 3 seconds of the user pressing play.
- SLO target: 99.9% over a 30-day rolling window.
- Error budget: 0.1% — approximately 43 minutes per month.
- Alerting: Burn rate 14.4 over 5 minutes pages the on-call engineer. Burn rate 6.0 over 30 minutes creates a ticket.

SLO 2 — Rebuffer Rate
- SLI: Fraction of streaming sessions that experience zero rebuffering events after the video starts.
- SLO target: 99.5% over a 30-day rolling window.
- Error budget: 0.5% — approximately 3.6 hours per month.
- Alerting: Burn rate 6.0 over 30 minutes pages the on-call engineer. This is lower than the playback SLO because rebuffering is less critical than playback failure.

SLO 3 — Video Quality
- SLI: Fraction of streaming minutes delivered at the highest available bitrate for the user's device and network.
- SLO target: 95% over a 30-day rolling window.
- Error budget: 5% — approximately 36 hours per month.
- Alerting: Burn rate 1.0 over 6 hours creates a ticket. No page. This SLO is for trend monitoring, not incident response.

The three SLOs cover the complete user experience: getting the video to start, keeping it playing, and delivering the best quality. Each SLO has a different target and alerting sensitivity based on its impact on the user experience.

### Drill 2: Design the Alerting System

Given a service with a 99.9% SLO over a 30-day window, design the alerting rules. Specify the burn rates, window sizes, notification channels, and cooldown periods for each alerting tier.

**Practice time:** 10 minutes.
**Expected output:** A multi-window multi-burn-rate alerting configuration with four tiers, each specifying burn rate, window, channel, and cooldown.

**Model Answer:**

| Tier | Burn Rate | Window | Channel | Cooldown | Trigger Condition |
|------|-----------|--------|---------|----------|-------------------|
| Page | 14.4 | 5 min | PagerDuty | 30 min | Error rate 1.44% for 5 min |
| Ticket | 6.0 | 30 min | Jira | 60 min | Error rate 0.6% for 30 min |
| Notify | 1.0 | 6 hours | Email | 4 hours | Error rate 0.1% for 6 hours |
| Trend | 0.5 | 3 days | Dashboard | None | Error rate 0.05% for 3 days |

The Page tier catches catastrophic failures immediately. The 14.4 burn rate over 5 minutes means the budget would be exhausted in 2 days. This requires immediate human response.

The Ticket tier catches sustained degradation. The 6.0 burn rate over 30 minutes means the budget would be exhausted in 5 days. The engineer creates a ticket and investigates within the hour.

The Notify tier catches slow degradation. The 1.0 burn rate over 6 hours means the budget is being consumed at the nominal rate. The engineer schedules an investigation during regular hours.

The Trend tier catches long-term shifts. The 0.5 burn rate over 3 days means the budget is being consumed at half the nominal rate. No notification is sent. The trend is visible on the dashboard for weekly review.

The cooldown periods prevent alert storms. After a page fires, the same alert condition will not fire again for 30 minutes. This gives the engineer time to investigate without being overwhelmed by repeated alerts.

### Drill 3: Handle a Budget Exhaustion Scenario

Your service has a 99.9% SLO with 30-day window. A deployment error causes a 10% error rate for 5 minutes. Calculate the budget consumption. Is the service in violation? What does the team do next?

**Practice time:** 5 minutes.
**Expected output:** Budget consumption calculation, violation status, and the operational response including rollback, incident review, and error budget reconciliation.

**Model Answer:**

Assume the service processes 10,000 requests per second. Total requests over 30 days: 10,000 * 86,400 * 30 = 25,920,000,000 requests. Error budget at 99.9%: 25,920,000,000 * 0.001 = 25,920,000 allowed failures.

Outage: 5 minutes at 10,000 requests/second with 10% error rate. Failed requests: 5 * 60 * 10,000 * 0.10 = 300,000 failed requests. Budget consumption: 300,000 / 25,920,000 = 1.16%.

The service is NOT in violation. Only 1.16% of the budget was consumed. The burn rate during the outage was 10% / 0.1% = 100. The 5-minute burn rate of 100 far exceeds the 14.4 page threshold. The on-call engineer was paged within 5 minutes.

The team should: (1) roll back the deployment immediately, (2) verify the error rate returns to normal, (3) document the incident with the budget consumption and root cause, (4) schedule a post-mortem within 5 business days, and (5) add a canary deployment step to catch similar errors before they reach production.

### Drill 4: Negotiate SLOs with Stakeholders

A product manager wants 99.99% SLO for a new feature. The engineering estimate says this requires 2x infrastructure cost and reduces deployment frequency from daily to weekly. How do you negotiate?

**Practice time:** 5 minutes.
**Expected output:** A structured negotiation that quantifies the cost of each SLO level, identifies the user impact of different reliability levels, and proposes a phased approach.

**Model Answer:**

The negotiation should follow a structured approach: quantify the cost, quantify the benefit, and propose alternatives.

Quantify the cost: 99.99% SLO requires 2x infrastructure cost and reduces deployment frequency from 1x per day to 1x per week. The monthly cost increase is 50,000 dollars. The deployment frequency reduction means features take 5x longer to reach users.

Quantify the benefit: 99.99% SLO provides 8.6 hours per year of allowed downtime versus 99.9% which provides 8.7 hours. The difference is 0.1% of downtime — approximately 52 minutes per year. Ask the product manager: is 52 minutes of additional uptime per year worth 50,000 dollars per month and a 5x reduction in deployment frequency?

Propose alternatives: Start with 99.9% SLO. Monitor the actual error budget consumption. If the service consistently achieves 99.99% with the 99.9% target, consider raising the target. Implement a phased approach: 99.9% for the first 3 months, then evaluate whether to raise to 99.99%. The phased approach defers the cost until the benefit is proven.

### Drill 5: Contrast Cloud Provider SLO Offerings

Compare the SLO offerings of AWS CloudWatch, Datadog, and Google Cloud Monitoring. Which would you choose for a startup versus an enterprise with existing observability investment?

**Practice time:** 5 minutes.
**Expected output:** A comparison table covering integration depth, pricing model, alerting features, and retention periods, with recommendations for different scenarios.

**Model Answer:**

| Feature | AWS CloudWatch | Datadog | Google Cloud Monitoring |
|---------|---------------|---------|------------------------|
| Integration | Deep with AWS services | Broad across platforms | Deep with GCP services |
| SLI Types | Request-based, time-based | Request-based, time-based, monitor-based | Request-based, percentile-based |
| Alerting | Basic threshold | Multi-window burn rate | Multi-window burn rate |
| Pricing | 0.50 dollars per SLO | 0.30 dollars per SLO | 0.30 dollars first 100, 0.10 dollars additional |
| Retention | 30 days | 90 days | 30 days (configurable) |
| Dashboard | CloudWatch dashboards | Datadog dashboards | Cloud Monitoring dashboards |
| API | CloudWatch API | Datadog API | Cloud Monitoring API |

Recommendation for a startup: Choose the provider you are already using. If you are on AWS, use CloudWatch SLOs. If you are on GCP, use Cloud Monitoring SLOs. The integration benefit outweighs the feature differences. Startups should minimize the number of vendors.

Recommendation for an enterprise: Choose Datadog if you already use it for APM and infrastructure monitoring. The monitor-based SLO capability is unique and valuable. The broader platform integration provides a unified view across services. The 90-day retention is better for compliance reporting.

### Drill 6: Write the SLO System Design

Design the complete SLO system for a service handling 100,000 requests per second. Cover instrumentation, aggregation, storage, evaluation, and alerting. Include the data structures, the deployment architecture, and the failure modes.

**Practice time:** 20 minutes.
**Expected output:** A full system design covering the pipeline from instrumentation to alerting, with specific data structures (sliding window, histogram), deployment topology (local aggregators, global aggregators, evaluator shards), and failure mode mitigations (data freshness monitoring, deduplication, NTP, snapshots).

**Model Answer:**

**Instrumentation:** Use the sidecar agent pattern. Each of the 200 service instances runs a sidecar process. The service sends metric events to the sidecar via a Unix socket. The sidecar batches events every 5 seconds or 50,000 events. The batch is sent to the local aggregator via gRPC streaming.

**Local Aggregation:** 10 local aggregator instances in each region. Each aggregator receives events from 20 service instances. The aggregator groups events into 1-minute windows and maintains a latency histogram for each SLO. The aggregator writes the merged window entry to a Kafka topic.

**Global Aggregation:** A global aggregator consumes the Kafka topic from all regions. It merges window entries by summing counters and merging histograms. The global aggregator deduplicates entries by (instance_id, sequence_number). Merged entries are written to the time-series database.

**Storage:** Time-series database with 30-day retention for 1-minute window entries. Entries are stored with a composite key of (service, SLO, timestamp). The database supports range queries for SLO evaluation. Entries older than 30 days are archived to object storage.

**Evaluation:** Three evaluator instances in an active-active configuration. Each evaluator is responsible for a shard of SLOs. The evaluator maintains a circular buffer sliding window of 43,200 entries per SLO. The evaluator reads new window entries from the database, advances the sliding window, computes burn rates, and generates alerts.

**Alerting:** Multi-window multi-burn-rate alerting with four tiers. Alerts are sent to an alert manager that handles deduplication, aggregation, and notification routing.

**Failure Modes:** Data freshness monitoring alerts if no data is received for 5 minutes. Deduplication prevents double counting. NTP keeps clock skew under 100ms. Hourly snapshots enable crash recovery in under 30 seconds. The evaluator reads from the primary database to avoid stale reads from replicas.

### Drill 7: Debug an SLO Anomaly

The SLO dashboard shows the service is at 99.95% compliance and meeting the 99.9% SLO target. However, users are reporting that the service is unresponsive during peak hours. What could explain this discrepancy?

**Practice time:** 5 minutes.
**Expected output:** Hypotheses including: the SLI measures server-side latency but not client-side, the SLO aggregates across all endpoints and hides a specific degraded endpoint, the measurement window includes older good data that masks recent bad data, or the SLO measures availability but not correctness.

**Model Answer:**

There are five possible explanations for the discrepancy:

1. **Client-side versus server-side measurement.** The SLI measures server-side latency, which does not include network latency, TLS handshake time, or DNS resolution time. The user experiences latency from all of these sources combined. The fix is to measure from the client perspective using synthetic monitoring.

2. **Aggregate hides specific endpoint.** The SLO aggregates all endpoints. During peak hours, a specific high-traffic endpoint (like the video upload endpoint) may be degraded, but the aggregate SLO hides this because other healthy endpoints compensate. The fix is to use per-endpoint SLOs for the most critical endpoints.

3. **Measurement window masks recent degradation.** The 30-day rolling window includes the last 30 days of data. If the service has been healthy for 29 days and degraded for the last 1 day, the SLO still shows approximately 96.7% for a 100% degraded day — which is above the 99.9% target. The fix is to add a shorter-window SLO (7 days or 1 day) that detects recent degradation.

4. **SLO measures availability, not performance.** The SLO tracks whether requests return a response. During peak hours, the service may return responses but with high latency. The SLO shows green because the responses are within the latency threshold, but the user experience is degraded because the latency is at the threshold boundary.

5. **SLO measures the wrong percentile.** The SLO uses p50 latency. During peak hours, p50 latency is 200ms but p99 latency is 5 seconds. The p99 users experience a slow service, but the p50 SLO hides this. The fix is to use p99 or p99.9 latency as the SLI.

Each of these explanations points to a mismatch between the SLO measurement and the actual user experience. The correct response is to investigate the SLI definition, adjust it to match the user experience, and validate the fix by comparing the new SLO against user feedback.

## How to Prepare for the Interview

### Pre-Interview Preparation

Set aside 2-3 hours for dedicated interview preparation. Divide the time into three blocks:

**Block 1 (45 minutes): Concept Review.** Review the definitions of SLI, SLO, error budget, burn rate, measurement window, and alerting threshold. Practice explaining each concept in one sentence. If you cannot explain a concept in one sentence, you do not understand it well enough.

**Block 2 (45 minutes): Math Practice.** Practice error budget calculations for different SLO targets and window sizes. Calculate burn rates for different error rates. Practice the mental math until it is automatic. The interviewer should not have to wait while you calculate 0.1% of 43,200 minutes.

**Block 3 (30 minutes): Mock Interview.** Have a friend or colleague ask you one or two of the practice drill questions. Give your answer as if it were a real interview. Ask for feedback on clarity, completeness, and fluency.

Before the interview, review these key concepts until you can explain them without notes:

1. SLI, SLO, and error budget definitions and relationships.
2. The error budget formula: 1.0 - SLO target = error budget.
3. The burn rate formula: error_rate / (1.0 - SLO target) = burn rate.
4. The four tiers of multi-window multi-burn-rate alerting.
5. The sliding window data structure and O(1) compliance computation.
6. The three common SLO failure modes: dead metric, double counter, clock skew.
7. The three anti-patterns: kitchen sink, perfect target, silent SLA.
8. The error budget policy zones: green (over 50%), yellow (20-50%), red (under 20%).

### Mental Math Practice

Practice these calculations until they are automatic:

- 99.9% of 30 days = 43.2 minutes of error budget.
- 99.99% of 30 days = 4.32 minutes of error budget.
- 99.999% of 30 days = 25.9 seconds of error budget.
- 1% error rate at 99.9% SLO = 10x burn rate.
- 0.1% error rate at 99.99% SLO = 10x burn rate.

### Whiteboard Practice

Practice drawing these diagrams on a whiteboard:

1. The four-layer SLO pipeline: instrumentation, aggregation, evaluation, alerting.
2. The sliding window circular buffer with head pointer.
3. The multi-window burn rate cascade with 5-minute, 30-minute, 6-hour, and 3-day windows.
4. The error budget consumption chart showing budget remaining over time.
5. The SLO alert response flow from alert received to post-mortem.

### Storytelling Preparation

Prepare two real-world stories about SLOs:

**Story 1: An SLO that saved the day.** Describe a situation where an SLO alert caught a problem before users noticed. Explain the SLI, the SLO target, the burn rate, and the response. Quantify the error budget consumed.

**Story 2: An SLO that failed.** Describe a situation where the SLO system missed a problem or generated a false positive. Explain the root cause (dead metric, wrong SLI, incorrect threshold) and what the team learned. Show that you understand SLO failure modes from experience.

### Building Your SLO Vocabulary

Practice using SLO terminology naturally in your answers. The interviewer should hear a candidate who is fluent in SLO concepts, not someone who is reading from a script.

Replace vague statements with precise SLO language:
- Instead of "the system should be fast," say "the p99 latency SLO target is 500ms over a 30-day rolling window."
- Instead of "we monitor errors," say "the error rate SLI measures the fraction of requests returning non-2xx status codes."
- Instead of "we alert on problems," say "we use multi-window multi-burn-rate alerting with a page threshold of 14.4 burn rate over 5 minutes."
- Instead of "we slow down deployments when things are bad," say "the error budget policy blocks non-critical deployments when budget consumption exceeds 50%."
- Instead of "the system went down for a while," say "the service consumed 35% of its error budget in a 12-minute outage."

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

On the interview day, avoid these common mistakes:

**Overcomplicating the answer:** Start with the basics and add complexity only when asked. A simple, correct answer is better than a complex, confusing answer.

**Forgetting the error budget:** The error budget is the most important SLO concept. If you forget to mention it, the interviewer will question your SLO experience.

**Using vague numbers:** Always use concrete numbers. Say "43 minutes per month" not "some amount of time." Say "99.9%" not "high availability."

**Neglecting the feedback loop:** Do not stop at measurement. Explain how the SLO drives decisions — the error budget policy, the deployment gate, the incident response.

**Ignoring the business context:** Connect SLOs to business outcomes. Explain how the SLO target was chosen based on user needs and cost constraints.

## Additional Practice Questions

Use these questions for self-study or mock interviews with a partner:

**Question 1:** A service has a 99.9% SLO with a 30-day window. The service processes 1 million requests per day. A 5-minute outage at peak traffic (10,000 requests/second) causes a 100% error rate. Calculate the budget consumption. Is the service in violation?

**Question 2:** Design the SLO system for a real-time chat application. Define the SLI, the SLO target, the measurement window, and the alerting thresholds. Explain how the SLO accounts for the real-time nature of the service.

**Question 3:** A team has 10 SLOs per service. The on-call engineer receives 50 alerts per day, most of which are false positives. How would you reduce the alert volume while maintaining coverage?

**Question 4:** A service has a 99.99% SLO. The team wants to deploy 5 times per day. Each deployment has a 0.01% chance of causing a 5-minute outage. How many deployments can the team safely make per month?

**Question 5:** Compare the SLO approach for a batch processing system versus a real-time system. How would the SLI, the SLO target, and the measurement window differ?

**Question 6:** A team defines an SLO for API latency. The SLO is 99.9% of requests under 500ms. The p50 latency is 50ms, p99 is 450ms, and p99.9 is 950ms. Is the service meeting the SLO? Explain your reasoning.

**Question 7:** Design a composite SLO for a user-facing flow that involves three microservices. Each microservice has a 99.9% SLO. What is the end-to-end SLO? How would you debug a violation?

**Question 8:** A service has a 99.9% SLO. The error budget is fully consumed on day 15 of a 30-day window. The team fixes the root cause. How many days until the SLO recovers? What does the team do during the recovery period?

**Question 9:** The CEO asks for a single number that represents the reliability of the entire platform. The platform has 20 services, each with its own SLO. How would you compute this number? What are the limitations?

**Question 10:** A service uses a time-based SLO (99.9% of minutes are good) instead of a request-based SLO (99.9% of requests are good). During peak hours, the service processes 10,000 requests per second. During off-peak hours, it processes 10 requests per second. A 5-minute outage during off-peak hours affects 3,000 requests. The same outage during peak hours affects 3,000,000 requests. How does the SLO treat these two outages differently?

**Hint for Question 10:** In a time-based SLO, both outages consume exactly 5 minutes of error budget regardless of the number of affected requests. In a request-based SLO, the peak outage consumes 1,000 times more budget than the off-peak outage. This illustrates the fundamental difference between time-based and request-based SLOs. Time-based SLOs treat all minutes equally. Request-based SLOs weight minutes by traffic volume.

## Final Preparation Tips

Review these final tips on the morning of the interview:

1. Sleep well the night before. A tired brain cannot do accurate error budget math.
2. Bring a pen and paper (or a whiteboard marker) for calculations.
3. Speak slowly and clearly. Rushing through an answer makes it harder to follow.
4. Pause before answering. A 5-second pause to collect your thoughts is better than a rambling answer.
5. If you make a math error, correct it immediately. Do not try to hide it.
6. Connect every SLO decision back to the user experience. The SLO is a proxy for user satisfaction.
7. Be honest if you do not know something. "I have not encountered that specific scenario, but here is how I would approach it" is a stronger answer than a confident guess.

## Summary of Key Interview Takeaways

1. Always start with the SLI. Define what you measure before you set the target.
2. Always mention the error budget. It is the most important SLO concept.
3. Always use concrete numbers. "43 minutes per month" not "some time."
4. Always connect SLOs to decisions. The error budget policy, the deployment gate, the incident response.
5. Always discuss failure modes. The dead metric, the double counter, the clock skew.
6. Always mention the SLO system's own SLO. The reliability system must be reliable.
7. Always tailor the answer to the service type. Video streaming, payment processing, and real-time chat have different SLO needs.
8. Always show awareness of cost. Higher SLO targets cost more. Justify the investment with the expected reduction in failure cost.
9. Always ask about the SLO system's own reliability. The meta-SLO is a mark of an experienced SLO practitioner.
10. Always end by connecting back to the user experience. The SLO is a proxy for user satisfaction. Never lose sight of what the SLO represents.
