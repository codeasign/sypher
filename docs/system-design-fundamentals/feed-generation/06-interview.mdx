## How It Appears

The feed generation problem appears in system design interviews under many different phrasings. The interviewer is testing your ability to design a personalized, ordered content delivery system at scale. The specific product name changes, but the core problem remains the same: how do you generate a feed of content for each user efficiently?

### Common Question Phrasings

**Phrasing 1: Design Twitter Timeline**

Design the Twitter timeline system. When a user opens Twitter, they see tweets from people they follow, sorted by recency and relevance. The system must handle 500 million tweets per day and 200 million daily active users. Focus on the timeline generation, not the tweet posting itself.

**Phrasing 2: Design Facebook News Feed**

Design the Facebook News Feed. Users see posts from friends, pages they follow, and groups they are in. The feed is ranked by relevance, not purely chronological. The system must handle 1 billion users and 150 billion feed impressions per day.

**Phrasing 3: Design Instagram Feed**

Design the Instagram feed. Users see photos and videos from accounts they follow, sorted by a combination of recency and engagement. The feed must load in under 500 milliseconds. The system handles 95 million photo uploads per day.

**Phrasing 4: Design LinkedIn Feed**

Design the LinkedIn feed. Users see posts from their network, companies they follow, and topics they are interested in. The feed is ranked by a machine learning model that considers connection strength, content type, and engagement history. The system has 300 million monthly active users.

**Phrasing 5: Design a Personalized News Feed**

Design a personalized news feed for a content aggregation platform. Users subscribe to topics and publishers. The feed displays articles sorted by a combination of recency, popularity, and the user's reading history. The system must handle 10 million daily active users and 100,000 new articles per day.

**Phrasing 6: Design a Video Feed**

Design a video feed for a platform like TikTok. The feed is a single-column scroll of short videos. The ranking is heavily driven by user engagement signals: watch time, likes, shares, comments, and replays. The system must handle 1 billion video plays per day.

**Phrasing 7: Design a Notification Feed**

Design a notification feed for a social platform. Users receive notifications when someone likes, comments, or shares their content. The notifications are sorted by time, but important notifications (follow requests, direct messages) are pinned to the top. The system handles 10 billion notification events per day.

**Phrasing 8: Design a Comments Feed**

Design a comments feed for a content platform like Reddit. Each post has a comments feed. The comments are sorted by a combination of upvotes, recency, and reply depth. The system must handle 100 million comments per day across 10 million posts.

**Phrasing 9: Design an Activity Feed**

Design an activity feed for a collaboration tool like Slack or Discord. Users see messages from channels they are in, sorted by the most recent activity. The feed must update in real time when new messages arrive. The system handles 1 billion messages per day.

**Phrasing 10: Design a Discovery Feed**

Design a discovery feed for an e-commerce platform like Amazon. Users see product recommendations based on their browsing history, purchase history, and similar users' behavior. The feed is ranked by predicted purchase probability. The system handles 100 million daily active users and 500 million products.

### What the Interviewer Actually Wants

Every single phrasing above tests the same core skill set. The interviewer is looking for your ability to:

**Understand the read-write trade-off.** Can you identify that the fundamental tension is between fan-out on write and fan-out on read? Do you know when to use each one? Can you justify the hybrid approach with concrete numbers?

**Design for scale.** Can you estimate the write amplification, storage requirements, and throughput for a platform with millions of users? Do you know how many Redis nodes you need and why?

**Handle the celebrity problem.** Can you identify that a single user with millions of followers breaks the naive fan-out on write approach? Do you have a strategy to handle high-follower accounts?

**Optimize the read path.** Do you know why the feed read is more than just a sorted set lookup? Can you explain hydration, caching, and ranking as separate concerns?

**Discuss consistency and failure modes.** Can you explain why feed systems are eventually consistent and why that is acceptable? Do you know what happens when the fan-out engine crashes, a Redis node fails, or a cache stampede occurs?

## What Interviewer Tests

### Core Concepts

The interviewer tests whether you understand the four core concepts of feed generation:

**Fan-out strategies.** Can you explain the difference between push, pull, and hybrid? Do you know the write amplification factor for each? Can you calculate the storage cost?

**Feed storage.** Do you know why Redis sorted sets are a natural fit? Can you explain the alternative (Cassandra, HBase) and when each is appropriate?

**Ranking and hydration.** Do you understand that the feed list stores lightweight references and the expensive part is hydrating those references into full content objects? Can you explain how ranking models work at scale?

**Pagination.** Do you know why cursor-based pagination is essential for feeds? Can you explain what happens with offset-based pagination when new items are inserted?

### Scoring Criteria

Interviewers use a scoring rubric with these criteria:

**Problem scoping (10 percent).** Did you ask clarifying questions about the number of users, the read-to-write ratio, the required latency, and the consistency requirements? Did you define the scope of the system (what is included and what is out of scope)?

**High-level design (25 percent).** Did you propose a clear architecture with the major components: content ingestion, fan-out engine, feed storage, read path, and ranking engine? Did you explain the data flow between components?

**Deep dive (30 percent).** Did you go deep on the fan-out engine? Did you explain the follower list lookup, the batch write strategy, and the fan-out threshold? Did you discuss the feed storage data structure and the read path optimization?

**Trade-offs (20 percent).** Did you discuss the trade-offs between fan-out on write vs fan-out on read, Redis vs Cassandra, chronological vs ranked, and push vs pull for celebrity accounts? Did you justify your choices with concrete numbers?

**Scale and performance (15 percent).** Did you calculate the storage requirements, write throughput, and read latency? Did you discuss sharding, replication, caching, and multi-region deployment? Did you identify the bottlenecks and how to address them?

## Level-by-Level

### L3 (Entry Level)

**What you need to demonstrate.** You can explain the basic concepts of feed generation. You understand the difference between chronological and ranked feeds. You can describe a simple fan-out on write system with a single Redis instance.

**Sample question.** Design a feed for a photo-sharing app with 50,000 users. Each user follows an average of 50 accounts. The feed is chronological.

**Sample answer.** "I would use fan-out on write. When a user uploads a photo, I store the photo in a database and publish the photo ID to a message queue. A background worker reads the queue, looks up the uploader's followers in a followers table, and writes the photo ID to each follower's feed list in Redis. The feed list is a Redis sorted set with the photo ID as the member and the upload timestamp as the score. When a user opens the app, I read the top 20 items from their feed list using ZREVRANGE, then fetch the full photo metadata from the database. The read latency is under 50 milliseconds because the Redis read is fast and the hydration is a simple batch query."

**Key vocabulary to use.** Fan-out on write, sorted set, ZREVRANGE, hydration, batch query, message queue, background worker.

**Common mistakes.** Proposing a complex architecture before understanding the scale. Using the term "fan-out on read" without understanding the trade-off. Forgetting to mention hydration. Not discussing the message queue for decoupling.

### L4 (Mid Level)

**What you need to demonstrate.** You can handle the hybrid fan-out strategy. You understand the celebrity problem and can propose a solution. You can estimate storage and throughput requirements. You discuss caching and pagination.

**Sample question.** Design a feed for a microblogging platform with 10 million daily active users. The average user follows 200 accounts. Some users have 1 million followers. The feed is ranked by a combination of recency and engagement.

**Sample answer.** "I would use a hybrid fan-out strategy. For users with fewer than 10,000 followers, I use fan-out on write. When a user posts a tweet, I write the tweet ID to each follower's timeline in Redis. For users with more than 10,000 followers, I use fan-out on read. The tweet is not written to individual timelines. Instead, when a follower reads their timeline, the read path checks a separate list of celebrity tweets and merges them in.

"The feed storage is a Redis cluster with 64 shards. Each shard handles approximately 156,000 users. Each feed list stores 800 items, and each item is 40 bytes. The total storage is 10 million times 800 times 40, which equals 320 GB. With replication, the total is approximately 640 GB.

"For ranking, I use a lightweight scoring function: score equals recency weight times recency normalized plus engagement weight times engagement normalized. The recency weight is 0.4, and the engagement weight is 0.6. The scoring function runs in a background job that re-scores each user's feed list every 5 minutes.

"For pagination, I use cursor-based pagination. The cursor is the tweet ID of the last item on the current page. The next page returns items with a score lower than the cursor's score. This handles the insertion of new items between page loads without causing duplicates or missed items.

"The read path has a Memcached cache that stores the first 50 items of the hydrated feed. The cache TTL is 30 seconds. The cache hit rate is approximately 80 percent for active users. On a cache miss, the read path assembles the feed from the Redis feed list, applies ranking, hydrates the content, and returns the response. The P99 read latency target is 300 milliseconds."

**Key vocabulary to use.** Hybrid fan-out, fan-out threshold, pull-based for celebrities, Redis cluster, sharding, cursor-based pagination, Memcached, timeline cache, scoring function, cache hit rate.

**Common mistakes.** Not calculating the storage requirements. Proposing a ranking model that is too complex for the scale. Not discussing the cache layer. Forgetting to mention the fan-out threshold. Not handling the celebrity case.

### L5 (Senior Level)

**What you need to demonstrate.** You can design a multi-region feed system. You understand the ranking model in detail. You discuss the failure modes and their mitigations. You can design the fan-out engine's internal architecture, including batching, backpressure, and throttling.

**Sample question.** Design a feed for a global social platform with 100 million daily active users. Users are distributed across North America, Europe, and Asia. The feed is ranked by a machine learning model with 20 features. The system must have 99.99 percent availability.

**Sample answer.** "I would use a hybrid fan-out strategy with multi-region deployment. The primary region is US-East, where the fan-out engine runs. The fan-out engine is a Kafka consumer group with 128 workers. Each worker processes content events from a Kafka partition. The fan-out engine reads the follower list from a graph database (Cassandra with 256 nodes), batches the feed list writes in groups of 100, and writes to the Redis feed storage cluster using pipeline mode.

"The Redis feed storage cluster has 256 shards (512 nodes with replication). Each shard is a Redis primary with one replica. The cluster uses consistent hashing for shard assignment. The total Redis memory is 8.32 TB for 100 million users with 800 items per feed list.

"For multi-region, each region has its own Redis feed storage cluster. The fan-out engine in the primary region writes to the local Redis cluster and publishes a replication event to a cross-region Kafka topic. A replication worker in each region consumes the replication event and writes to the local Redis cluster. The cross-region replication latency is 60 to 120 milliseconds depending on the geographic distance.

"The ranking model is a gradient-boosted decision tree with 20 features. The features include recency, engagement velocity, creator-viewer affinity, content type, time of day, and device type. The model runs on a GPU cluster with 32 GPUs. Each feed request triggers a model inference in 5 to 10 milliseconds. The ranking pipeline uses multi-stage filtering: retrieve 10,000 candidate items, filter to 500 using lightweight rules, rank the 500 using the ML model, and return the top 50.

"For failure modes, I have three mitigations. First, if the fan-out engine lags by more than 10 seconds, the read path falls back to pull-based assembly. Second, if a Redis node fails, the replica is promoted automatically, and the fan-out engine retries any failed writes. Third, if a cache stampede occurs, the system rate-limits cache-miss requests to 1,000 concurrent and serves stale cache entries with background refresh for the rest.

"Availability is achieved through redundancy at every layer. The fan-out engine has 128 workers across 16 availability zones. The Redis cluster has 256 shards with automatic failover. The read path service has 64 instances behind a load balancer. The timeline cache is cross-region replicated. The P99 availability target is 99.99 percent, which allows 52 minutes of downtime per year."

**Key vocabulary to use.** Multi-region deployment, cross-region replication, gradient-boosted decision tree, GPU inference, multi-stage filtering, pipeline mode, backpressure, availability zone, automatic failover, rate limiting, stale cache with background refresh.

**Common mistakes.** Not discussing cross-region replication latency. Proposing a ranking model that is too slow for real-time inference. Not having a fallback strategy for the fan-out engine. Underestimating the network bandwidth requirements. Not discussing the failure modes in detail.

### L6 (Staff Level)

**What you need to demonstrate.** You can design the feed system from end to end, including the operational aspects. You discuss the trade-offs between consistency models and justify the eventual consistency choice. You design the monitoring and alerting system. You can design the capacity planning model.

**Sample question.** Design a feed system for a platform that is growing from 10 million to 1 billion users. The CEO wants to know the cost and timeline for scaling. The feed must be ranked by relevance. The system must handle a 10x increase in traffic over the next 18 months.

**Sample answer.** "I would design the system with a clear growth model. The system has three phases. Phase one (10 to 50 million users): fan-out on write with a Redis cluster of 32 shards. The ranking is a simple time-decayed engagement score. The timeline cache is Memcached with 30-second TTL. The total infrastructure cost is approximately $50,000 per month.

"Phase two (50 to 200 million users): hybrid fan-out with a fan-out threshold of 10,000 followers. The Redis cluster grows to 128 shards. The ranking model upgrades to a gradient-boosted tree with 10 features. The timeline cache is expanded with regional replicas. The cost is approximately $200,000 per month.

"Phase three (200 million to 1 billion users): multi-region deployment with active-active feed storage. The fan-out engine runs in three regions simultaneously. The ranking model uses a deep neural network with 50 features running on a GPU cluster. The timeline cache is a global distributed cache with cross-region invalidation. The cost is approximately $1.5 million per month.

"The operational model includes automated capacity scaling. The fan-out engine's worker count is controlled by the Kafka consumer lag. If the lag exceeds 10,000 events, the system adds 16 workers. If the lag drops below 1,000 events, the system removes 16 workers. The Redis cluster's shard count is adjusted based on memory utilization. If any shard exceeds 70 percent memory, the system adds 8 new shards and rebalances.

"The consistency model is eventual consistency with a 5-second window. The system uses read-your-write consistency for the content creator: the creator sees their own post immediately. For followers, the post appears within 100 milliseconds to 5 seconds depending on the fan-out engine's load. This is acceptable because feed users expect content to appear gradually, not instantly.

"The cost model shows that the dominant cost is the Redis cluster at 40 percent of total infrastructure cost. The fan-out engine workers are 20 percent. The ranking GPU cluster is 15 percent. The timeline cache is 10 percent. The content service and hydration layer are 15 percent. The total cost at 1 billion users is approximately $1.5 million per month, which is $0.0015 per user per month.

"The timeline for scaling is 18 months. Month 1-3: implement the phase one architecture. Month 4-6: test at 50 million users with synthetic load. Month 7-12: implement the phase two architecture. Month 13-15: test at 200 million users. Month 16-18: implement the phase three architecture and validate at 1 billion users."

**Key vocabulary to use.** Growth model, cost model, infrastructure cost per user, active-active deployment, automated capacity scaling, read-your-write consistency, time-decayed engagement score, global distributed cache, operational model, capacity planning.

**Common mistakes.** Not providing concrete cost numbers. Ignoring the operational complexity of multi-region deployment. Not discussing the scaling timeline. Underestimating the cost of the Redis cluster. Not having a clear growth model with milestones.

### L7 (Principal Level)

**What you need to demonstrate.** You can design the feed system as part of a larger platform architecture. You discuss the business impact of the design decisions. You can design the A/B testing framework for the ranking model. You can design the data pipeline for offline analysis and model training.

**Sample question.** Design the feed system for a platform that is expanding into 10 new countries with different content regulations and user behavior patterns. The feed must be personalized per country while sharing infrastructure. The business requires that the feed engagement rate increases by 20 percent year over year.

**Sample answer.** "I would design a federated feed system with a shared core and per-country customization. The shared core includes the fan-out engine, the feed storage cluster, and the content service. The per-country customization includes the ranking model, the content moderation pipeline, and the timeline cache.

"The ranking model is a multi-task learning model that shares a base embedding layer across all countries and has per-country output heads. The base layer learns universal patterns: recency matters, engagement signals are important, and content affinity is a strong predictor. The per-country heads learn country-specific patterns: users in Japan prefer different content than users in Brazil. The model is trained on a global data lake that aggregates user behavior signals from all countries.

"The A/B testing framework is built into the feed read path. Each feed request is assigned an experiment ID. The experiment ID determines which ranking model version to use, which fan-out strategy to apply, and which content moderation rules to enforce. The system supports 100 concurrent experiments. Each experiment has a minimum sample size of 1 million users to achieve statistical significance. The experiment results are tracked in real time with a dashboard that shows engagement rate, read latency, and error rate per experiment.

"The data pipeline for offline analysis uses Apache Spark. The pipeline processes 10 TB of feed interaction data per day. The data includes: feed impressions, clicks, likes, shares, comments, dwell time, and scroll depth. The pipeline trains a new ranking model every 24 hours. The model training uses AutoML to find the optimal feature combination and hyperparameters. The trained model is validated against a holdout set of 10 percent of users before being deployed to production.

"The business impact metrics are tracked at three levels. Level one: feed engagement rate (likes, shares, comments per feed impression). Target: 20 percent increase year over year. Level two: user retention rate (users who return to the feed within 7 days). Target: 90 percent. Level three: revenue per feed impression (ad revenue generated per feed view). Target: 15 percent increase year over year.

"The cost-benefit analysis shows that the ranking model improvement drives the majority of the engagement increase. A 10 percent improvement in ranking model accuracy translates to a 5 percent increase in engagement rate. The multi-country customization adds 30 percent to the ML infrastructure cost but drives a 15 percent increase in engagement in new markets. The net ROI is positive within 6 months.

"The regulatory compliance is handled by the content moderation pipeline. The pipeline has per-country filters that remove content violating local regulations. The filters are applied after the feed ranking but before the response is returned. The system logs all moderation actions for audit purposes. The logging data is stored for 90 days in the country of origin to comply with data sovereignty requirements."

**Key vocabulary to use.** Federated architecture, multi-task learning, shared embedding layer, per-country customization, A/B testing framework, experiment ID, statistical significance, AutoML, business impact metrics, engagement rate, user retention, revenue per impression, cost-benefit analysis, ROI, regulatory compliance, content moderation pipeline, data sovereignty.

**Common mistakes.** Treating all countries as the same. Not having a data-driven approach to ranking model improvement. Ignoring the business impact of technical decisions. Not discussing the A/B testing framework. Underestimating the complexity of regulatory compliance.

### L8 (Distinguished Level)

**What you need to demonstrate.** You can design the feed system as a platform that other teams build on. You discuss the long-term evolution of the system. You can design the API contracts, the data model, and the SLAs for external consumers. You can design the system to be extensible for future use cases that have not been invented yet.

**Sample question.** Design a feed platform that serves 10 different products within the company. Each product has different feed requirements: some need chronological feeds, some need ranked feeds, some need real-time updates, and some need batch delivery. The platform must be extensible for future products. The SLA is 99.995 percent availability with P99 latency under 200 milliseconds.

**Sample answer.** "I would design a feed platform with a plugin architecture. The core platform provides the feed storage, the fan-out engine, the read path, and the monitoring infrastructure. Each product plugs in its own ranking model, content adapter, and business logic through well-defined interfaces.

"The API contract is defined by a gRPC service definition. The FeedService has three RPCs: WriteFeedItem (content ID, creator ID, feed type, timestamp, metadata map), ReadFeed (user ID, feed type, cursor, limit, ranking config), and AcknowledgeFeed (user ID, feed type, cursor). The feed type parameter allows the platform to support multiple feed types within the same infrastructure: a user can have a home feed, a notification feed, and a discovery feed, each with different storage and ranking configurations.

"The data model uses a generic feed item with extensible metadata. Each feed item has a content ID, a creator ID, a timestamp, a score, a feed type, and a metadata map (string-to-string key-value pairs). The metadata map carries product-specific attributes without requiring schema changes. The feed storage is a generic sorted set where the score determines the sort order. Products that need different scoring can use different score ranges: chronological feeds use the timestamp as the score, ranked feeds use a normalized score between 0 and 1, and real-time feeds use a combination of timestamp and priority.

"The extensibility model uses a plugin system for the ranking model. Each product registers a ranking plugin that implements a simple interface: `function rank(items, userContext): rankedItems`. The plugin receives the candidate items and the user context (user ID, device type, time of day, location, and a product-specific context map). The plugin returns the ranked items with scores. The platform calls the plugin during the feed read path. New products can add a ranking plugin without modifying the core platform.

"The SLA of 99.995 percent availability requires a multi-region active-active architecture. The platform runs in three regions simultaneously. Each region handles its own feed reads and writes. Cross-region replication uses a two-phase commit protocol to ensure consistency. The P99 latency target of 200 milliseconds is achieved through a combination of local cache, regional feed storage, and ranking plugins that must complete within 100 milliseconds.

"The long-term evolution plan includes three phases. Phase one (year 1): support the first 3 products with a monolithic core and plugin ranking. Phase two (year 2-3): extract the plugin system into a separate service with dynamic loading, versioning, and canary deployment. Phase three (year 4-5): open the platform to external developers through a public API with rate limiting, authentication, and billing.

"The cost model for the platform is based on a per-feed-item-written and per-feed-item-read pricing model. Each product pays for its usage. The platform's infrastructure cost is distributed across products based on their feed item volume. The pricing model incentivizes products to optimize their feed write volume and cache hit rate."

**Key vocabulary to use.** Plugin architecture, extensibility, gRPC service definition, generic feed item, metadata map, ranking plugin, plugin system, active-active architecture, two-phase commit, long-term evolution, canary deployment, public API, per-feed-item pricing, usage-based billing.

**Common mistakes.** Designing a rigid system that cannot adapt to new products. Not defining the API contracts upfront. Not considering the cost allocation model. Underestimating the complexity of a multi-tenant feed platform. Not having a clear evolution plan.

## FAANG Framework

The FAANG Framework is a six-step structure for answering feed generation system design questions. Use this framework to organize your response in any interview.

### Step 1: Scope and Requirements

Define the scope of the system. Ask clarifying questions:

**How many users does the platform have?** This determines the scale of the fan-out engine, the feed storage, and the read path. A platform with 10 million users requires a different architecture than a platform with 1 billion users.

**What is the read-to-write ratio?** This determines whether fan-out on write or fan-out on read is more appropriate. A high read-to-write ratio favors fan-out on write. A low ratio favors fan-out on read.

**What is the feed ordering?** Chronological feeds are simpler. Ranked feeds require a ranking engine. Real-time feeds require persistent connections.

**What is the latency requirement?** A feed that must load in 200 milliseconds requires aggressive caching and a highly optimized read path. A feed that can load in 2 seconds allows more flexibility.

**What is the consistency requirement?** Most feed systems are eventually consistent. If the platform requires strong consistency, the feed generation pattern may not be the right choice.

**Sample scoping statement.** "The platform has 100 million daily active users. Each user follows 200 accounts on average. The feed is ranked by a combination of recency and engagement. The P99 read latency must be under 300 milliseconds. The system is eventually consistent. The write volume is 50,000 new content items per second during peak hours."

### Step 2: Core Concepts

Introduce the core concepts of feed generation. This establishes your vocabulary and shows the interviewer that you understand the fundamentals.

**Fan-out strategies.** Explain the three approaches: fan-out on write, fan-out on read, and hybrid. State which one you are using and why.

**Feed storage.** Explain what data structure you are using (Redis sorted set) and why. Mention the alternative (Cassandra, HBase) and when you would use it.

**Ranking and hydration.** Explain that the feed list stores lightweight references and the expensive part is hydrating those references into full content objects.

**Pagination.** Explain why you are using cursor-based pagination.

**Sample concepts statement.** "I am using a hybrid fan-out strategy. Users with fewer than 10,000 followers use fan-out on write. Users with more than 10,000 followers use fan-out on read. The feed storage is Redis sorted sets. The feed list stores lightweight references (content ID, creator ID, timestamp, score) totaling 40 bytes per item. The read path uses cursor-based pagination with the content ID as the cursor. The hydration layer fetches full content metadata in a batch query."

### Step 3: High-Level Design

Draw the architecture diagram (in your head or on the whiteboard). Walk through the major components and the data flow between them.

**Content ingestion.** How does content get into the system? What happens after a user clicks "Post"?

**Fan-out engine.** How does the fan-out engine read content events, look up followers, and write feed list entries?

**Feed storage.** Where are the feed lists stored? How is the storage sharded?

**Read path.** How does the user's feed request flow through the system? What happens on a cache hit vs a cache miss?

**Ranking engine.** How is the feed ranked? When does ranking happen?

**Sample design walkthrough.** "The content ingestion service receives the post, stores the content in a database, and publishes a content event to Kafka. The fan-out engine consumes the event, looks up the creator's followers in the graph database, and writes the post ID to each follower's Redis sorted set. The feed read service handles user requests. On a cache hit, the response is returned from Memcached in 10 milliseconds. On a cache miss, the read path reads the user's feed list from Redis, applies ranking, hydrates the content, and returns the response in 200 milliseconds. The ranking engine runs as a background job that re-scores each user's feed list every 5 minutes."

### Step 4: Deep Dive

Pick the most important component and go deep. The fan-out engine is usually the best choice because it is the most complex and the most critical for performance.

**Fan-out engine internals.** How does the engine handle the follower list lookup? How does it batch writes? How does it handle backpressure? How does it handle failures?

**Feed storage internals.** How is the Redis cluster sharded? How is data replicated? How does the cluster handle node failures?

**Read path internals.** How does the cache work? How does the hydration layer batch fetch content? How does the ranking model compute scores?

**Sample deep dive on the fan-out engine.** "The fan-out engine is a Kafka consumer group with 64 workers. Each worker processes events from one Kafka partition. When a worker receives a content event, it calls the Follower Graph Service via gRPC to get the follower list. The Follower Graph Service stores follower sets in Cassandra, partitioned by creator ID. The lookup returns the first 10,000 follower IDs within 10 milliseconds.

"The worker then writes the content reference to each follower's feed list. The writes are batched in groups of 100 and sent to the Redis cluster using pipeline mode. Pipeline mode sends all 100 commands in a single network round trip, reducing the per-write overhead from 0.5 milliseconds to 0.05 milliseconds.

"The worker handles backpressure by monitoring the Redis write latency. If the latency exceeds 10 milliseconds, the worker slows down the batch rate. If the latency exceeds 50 milliseconds, the worker pauses and waits for the Redis cluster to recover. The content event remains in the Kafka topic and is retried on the next poll cycle.

"For celebrity accounts with more than 10,000 followers, the worker switches to pull-based mode. Instead of writing to individual feed lists, the worker writes a 'new content available' flag to a Redis key. The flag is checked by the read path when a follower reads their feed."

### Step 5: Trade-offs

Discuss the trade-offs you made and why you made them. This shows the interviewer that you understand the design space and can make informed decisions.

**Fan-out on write vs fan-out on read.** Write efficiency vs read efficiency. Why did you choose the hybrid approach?

**Redis vs Cassandra.** Read latency vs write throughput. Why did you choose Redis?

**Chronological vs ranked.** Simplicity vs engagement. Why did you choose ranked?

**Push vs pull for celebrities.** Write amplification vs read latency. Why did you choose a threshold-based approach?

**Single-region vs multi-region.** Cost vs latency. Why did you choose single-region or multi-region?

**Sample trade-off discussion.** "I chose the hybrid fan-out strategy because it balances write efficiency and read efficiency. Fan-out on write provides fast reads for most users. Fan-out on read protects the system from write amplification spikes caused by celebrity accounts. The threshold of 10,000 followers is a starting point that should be tuned based on the fan-out engine's capacity.

"I chose Redis over Cassandra because Redis sorted sets are a natural fit for feed lists. The ZADD and ZREVRANGE commands map directly to the feed list operations. Cassandra would require a custom sorted merge implementation, which adds complexity and latency. The trade-off is that Redis stores all data in RAM, which is more expensive than Cassandra's disk-based storage. I accept this cost because the read latency improvement is critical for the user experience.

"I chose ranked feeds over chronological feeds because ranked feeds drive 30 to 50 percent higher engagement. The trade-off is increased computational cost and complexity. The ranking model must be lightweight enough to run in real time. I use a scoring function with 5 signals, not a deep neural network with 50 signals."

### Step 6: Scale and Performance

Estimate the resources required and discuss how the system scales. Calculate storage, throughput, and latency. Identify bottlenecks and propose solutions.

**Storage calculation.** How much Redis memory is needed? How many shards? How much per shard?

**Throughput calculation.** How many writes per second does the fan-out engine generate? How many reads per second does the read path handle?

**Latency budget.** How is the 300-millisecond latency budget allocated across subsystems?

**Bottleneck analysis.** What is the bottleneck at each scale? How do you address it?

**Sample scale discussion.** "For 100 million users with 800 items per feed list and 40 bytes per item, the feed list storage is 3.2 TB. With Redis replication (2x) and overhead (1.3x), the total is 8.32 TB. With 128 shards, each shard stores 65 GB. Each shard runs on a server with 128 GB RAM.

"The fan-out engine handles 50,000 content creations per second, each with 200 followers on average. The total write throughput is 10 million writes per second. With 128 Redis shards, each shard handles approximately 78,000 writes per second. A single Redis node can handle 100,000 writes per second, so each shard has 22 percent headroom.

"The read path handles 100,000 feed reads per second during peak hours. The timeline cache serves 80 percent of requests from the cache in 10 milliseconds. The remaining 20 percent trigger a full feed assembly that takes 200 milliseconds. The weighted average read latency is 48 milliseconds.

"The bottleneck at this scale is the fan-out engine's write throughput. When the system grows beyond 100 million users, the fan-out engine requires more workers and more Redis shards. ## Red Flags

Avoid these red flags in your interview answer. They signal to the interviewer that you do not understand the feed generation domain.

### Red Flag 1: Proposing Fan-Out on Write for All Users

If you propose fan-out on write for every user including celebrities, the interviewer knows you have not thought about the celebrity problem. A single post from a celebrity with 10 million followers generates 10 million feed list writes. The fan-out engine cannot keep up. The system falls over.

**What to say instead.** "I will use a hybrid fan-out strategy. Users with fewer than 10,000 followers use fan-out on write. Users with more than 10,000 followers use fan-out on read. The threshold is configurable and should be tuned based on the fan-out engine's capacity."

### Red Flag 2: Ignoring Hydration

If you focus only on the feed list storage and forget to mention hydration, the interviewer knows you have not thought about the complete read path. The feed list read is 5 milliseconds. The hydration is 50 to 120 milliseconds. The hydration layer is the bottleneck.

**What to say instead.** "The feed list stores lightweight references. The hydration layer fetches the full content metadata for each reference. The hydration is the most expensive part of the read path, so I optimize it with batch fetching and aggressive caching."

### Red Flag 3: Using Offset-Based Pagination

If you propose offset-based pagination for a feed, the interviewer knows you have not thought about the real-time update problem. New items are inserted at the top of the feed between page loads. Offset-based pagination causes duplicates and missed items.

**What to say instead.** "I use cursor-based pagination. The cursor is the content ID of the last item on the current page. The next page returns items with a score lower than the cursor's score. This handles the insertion of new items between page loads without causing duplicates or missed items."

### Red Flag 4: Proposing a Ranking Model Without a Feature Budget

If you propose a deep neural network with 50 features for a feed system that needs to respond in 200 milliseconds, the interviewer knows you have not thought about the inference latency. A complex model takes 50 to 100 milliseconds per feed request. The remaining time is not enough for the feed list read, hydration, and response assembly.

**What to say instead.** "The ranking model must be lightweight enough for real-time inference. I use a scoring function with 5 to 10 signals. The inference time target is under 10 milliseconds. More complex models can be used for offline ranking, but the online ranking must be fast."

### Red Flag 5: Not Discussing the Fan-Out Threshold

If you do not mention the fan-out threshold, the interviewer knows you have not thought about the transition between push and pull. The fan-out threshold is the key parameter in the hybrid strategy. It determines how the system handles high-follower accounts.

**What to say instead.** "The fan-out threshold is the maximum number of followers that trigger push-based fan-out. Users with more followers than the threshold use pull-based feed delivery. The default threshold is 10,000 followers, but it should be dynamic based on the system load."

### Red Flag 6: Ignoring the Storage Cost

If you propose storing full content objects in the feed list, the interviewer knows you have not calculated the storage cost. A 40-byte reference becomes a 2 KB content object. For 100 million users, the storage grows from 3.2 TB to 160 TB. The Redis cluster becomes prohibitively expensive.

**What to say instead.** "The feed list stores only lightweight references: content ID, creator ID, timestamp, and score. Each reference is approximately 40 bytes. The full content is stored in the content service and hydrated on read. The hydration cache stores content objects separately, keeping the feed list storage efficient."

### Red Flag 7: Not Having a Consistency Model

If you do not discuss consistency, the interviewer knows you have not thought about what happens when the fan-out engine is behind. Users see stale content. The system must have a clear consistency model.

**What to say instead.** "The feed system is eventually consistent. The content creator sees their own post immediately through read-your-write consistency. Followers see the post within 100 milliseconds to 5 seconds depending on the fan-out engine's load. During peak hours, the fan-out lag may increase, and the read path falls back to pull-based assembly to ensure fresh content."

### Red Flag 8: Proposing a Single-Redis-Node for a Large-Scale System

If you propose a single Redis node for a platform with millions of users, the interviewer knows you have not thought about the throughput requirements. A single Redis node can handle approximately 100,000 writes per second. A platform with 10 million users and 50,000 content creations per second generates 10 million feed list writes per second. The single Redis node cannot handle this workload.

**What to say instead.** "The Redis cluster has 128 shards (256 nodes with replication). Each shard handles approximately 78,000 writes per second. The shards are assigned using consistent hashing to distribute the load evenly. The cluster supports adding new shards without downtime."

### Red Flag 9: Not Discussing Cost

If you do not mention the infrastructure cost, the interviewer knows you have not thought about the practical constraints of the design. The CEO cares about the cost. The infrastructure cost determines whether the design is feasible.

**What to say instead.** "The total infrastructure cost for 100 million users is approximately $200,000 per month. The Redis cluster is the dominant cost at 40 percent. The fan-out engine workers are 20 percent. The ranking GPU cluster is 15 percent. The timeline cache is 10 percent. The content service and hydration layer are 15 percent."

### Red Flag 10: Not Having a Fallback Strategy

If you do not discuss what happens when the system fails, the interviewer knows you have not thought about the operational aspects. Every system fails. The question is how gracefully it degrades.

**What to say instead.** "The system has three fallback strategies. First, if the fan-out engine lags, the read path falls back to pull-based assembly. Second, if a Redis node fails, the replica is promoted automatically. Third, if the cache stampede occurs, the system rate-limits cache-miss requests and serves stale cache entries. The system degrades gracefully without going completely down."

## Common Interview Follow-Up Questions

Interviewers often ask follow-up questions after your initial design. Prepare for these:

### Follow-Up 1: "How does your system handle a user who follows 10,000 accounts?"

The fan-out on write approach writes 10,000 entries per post to that user's feed list. This is acceptable because the writes happen in the fan-out engine, not during the user's feed read. However, the user's feed list fills up quickly. With 800 items capacity and 10,000 followed accounts posting an average of 2 times per day, the feed list turns over completely every 2.4 hours. The user misses content that is older than 2.4 hours. The solution is to increase the feed list size for heavy followers to 2,000 items, or to switch to a ranked feed that prioritizes the highest-quality content from the 10,000 accounts.

### Follow-Up 2: "What happens when two users follow exactly the same accounts? Do they see the same feed?"

In a pure chronological fan-out on write system, yes, they see the same feed because the feed lists are written identically. In a ranked system, they see different feeds because the ranking model uses per-user signals: affinity score (how often does this user engage with each creator), content type preferences, and time-of-day context. The feed list contains the same items, but the ranking step re-orders them differently per user. The cache layer stores per-user cache entries, so even if two users have the same feed list, they get different cached responses.

### Follow-Up 3: "How do you handle content that is edited after publication?"

When content is edited, the fan-out engine does not rewrite the feed lists. The feed list stores the content ID as a reference. The content itself is stored in the content service. When a user reads their feed, the hydration layer fetches the latest version of the content. This means edits are reflected automatically on the next read. The trade-off is that the hydration cache may serve stale content for the cache TTL duration (30 seconds). For urgent edits (content that violates policies), the system invalidates the content from the hydration cache immediately.

### Follow-Up 4: "How do you handle content deletion?"

Content deletion follows a two-step process. Step one: mark the content as deleted in the content service. The hydration layer returns a "this content is unavailable" placeholder instead of the full content. This happens within milliseconds and makes the content invisible to all users. Step two: a background sweep job removes the content reference from all feed lists. The sweep job iterates over all feed lists using a Redis SCAN cursor and removes the deleted content ID using ZREM. For a platform with 100 million users, the sweep takes approximately 3 to 6 hours. After the sweep, the content reference is physically removed. The read path uses the placeholders during the sweep window, which is acceptable because the content is already inaccessible.

### Follow-Up 5: "How do you measure feed quality?"

Feed quality is measured through a combination of online metrics and offline analysis. Online metrics: engagement rate (likes, comments, shares per feed impression), scroll depth (what percentage of the feed does the user scroll through), dwell time (how long does the user spend on the feed), and skip rate (how many items does the user scroll past without engaging). Offline analysis: human evaluation (raters score feed quality on a 1-5 scale), A/B test results (does the new ranking model increase engagement?), and diversity metrics (is the user seeing content from a diverse set of creators?). The feed quality score is tracked daily and alerted if it drops below a threshold.

### Follow-Up 6: "How do you handle feed personalization for new users with no history?"

New users with no follow graph, no interaction history, and no preference data present the cold start problem. The system uses a multi-stage approach to bootstrap the feed. Stage one (first session): show the most popular content on the platform (trending posts from the last 24 hours, ranked by engagement velocity). Stage two (onboarding): ask the user to select topics or categories of interest (technology, sports, fashion, etc.). The system uses the selected topics to find relevant creators and content. Stage three (first 7 days): the system observes the user's engagement signals (what they like, share, click on) and gradually transitions from topic-based recommendations to personalized recommendations. After 7 days, the user has enough history for the full personalization model.

### Follow-Up 7: "How do you prevent spam and low-quality content from appearing in feeds?"

Spam prevention happens at two points. Point one: content ingestion. The content service runs spam detection on every new piece of content before it enters the feed pipeline. The spam detector uses a combination of rule-based filters (known spam domains, excessive hashtags, duplicate content) and a machine learning model (trained on reported spam content). Suspicious content is held in a moderation queue. Point two: feed ranking. The ranking model includes a quality score signal. Content with low quality scores (detected spam, clickbait, misinformation) receives a penalty that pushes it below the visible threshold. If a user with high authority (verified account, high engagement rate) creates content that a spammer is copying, the legitimate content ranks higher because of the authority signal.

### Follow-Up 8: "How do you handle real-time updates to the feed while the user is scrolling?"

When a user is scrolling through their feed, new content may arrive. The system supports real-time updates through a combination of polling and push. The mobile app polls the feed service every 30 seconds for new content. The poll sends the cursor of the topmost visible item and receives any new items that arrived after that cursor. For real-time platforms (like Twitter), the app maintains a WebSocket connection. The fan-out engine publishes new content events to a Redis Pub/Sub channel per user. The feed service subscribes to the channels of currently connected users and forwards new items to the client. The client inserts the new item at the top of the feed with a visual indicator ("N new posts"). The user taps the indicator to scroll to the top. This pattern avoids disrupting the user's scroll position.

### Follow-Up 9: "How do you handle the case where a user blocks another user?"

When user A blocks user B, two changes must happen. First, the follower graph service removes the follow relationship (if A followed B) and prevents B from following A. Second, the feed system must remove all of B's content from A's feed list. The removal happens through a targeted sweep: the sweep job scans A's feed list using ZSCAN and removes entries where the creator ID matches B's ID using ZREM. If A has a large feed list (800 items), this takes approximately 50 milliseconds. The feed list is rebuilt on the next read if the user is actively using the feed. For real-time block handling, the system also checks for blocked creators during the deduplication step in the read path. If any content in the feed list is from a blocked creator, it is filtered out immediately, even before the sweep completes.

### Follow-Up 10: "How would you modify your design for a platform where the read-to-write ratio is 1:1 (users read feeds as often as they write)?"

A 1:1 read-to-write ratio changes the trade-off calculation. Fan-out on write becomes less attractive because the write amplification is 200x (for an average of 200 followers per user) while the read benefit is only 1x. The total system cost is dominated by writes, not reads. In this scenario, fan-out on read is the better choice. The feed is assembled on every read by querying the recent content from followed users. The read latency is higher (200 to 500 milliseconds instead of 10 to 30 milliseconds), but the write cost is eliminated. The timeline cache is less effective because users read less frequently (lower cache hit rate). The system can still use a cache with a longer TTL (5 minutes instead of 30 seconds) to catch occasional reads. The storage cost drops to near zero because there are no pre-computed feed lists.

### Follow-Up 11: "How do you handle feed localization for a global audience?"

Feed localization requires three adaptations. First, content ranking must incorporate language and regional preference signals. A post in Spanish should rank higher for Spanish-speaking users. The ranking model includes a language affinity signal: if the user has engaged with Spanish content before, future Spanish content gets a boost. Second, the content moderation pipeline must apply region-specific policies. Content that is acceptable in one country may violate regulations in another. The moderation filters are applied during the hydration step, before the feed response is assembled. Third, the feed storage must support regional partitioning. Users in Europe should read from the European Redis cluster, not the US cluster. The regional routing is handled by the DNS layer: the feed service domain resolves to the nearest regional endpoint. The cross-region replication ensures that fan-out writes reach all regions within 120 milliseconds.

### Follow-Up 12: "How do you handle the case where a user's follow graph changes frequently?"

A user who follows and unfollows accounts frequently creates churn in the feed system. In a fan-out on write system, each follow or unfollow requires updating the feed list. When a user follows a new account, the system backfills the feed list with recent content from that account. When a user unfollows, the system removes content from that account. The backfill and removal operations each take 50 to 200 milliseconds for a single account. If the user performs 100 follow/unfollow operations in a session, the system must process 100 backfill or removal operations. The solution is to batch the follow graph changes: the user's app accumulates changes for 30 seconds and sends them in a single batch request. The system processes the batch as a single transaction, reducing the per-operation overhead.

### Follow-Up 13: "What happens to the feed when a user's account is deleted?"

When a user deletes their account, the feed system must handle two cases. Case one: the deleted user was a content creator. Their content must be removed from all follower feed lists. The removal follows the two-step process: mark content as unavailable in the hydration layer (immediate), then sweep feed lists to remove references (background, 3 to 6 hours). Case two: the deleted user was a follower. Their feed list in Redis is deleted (one Redis DEL command). The fan-out engine stops writing to their feed list because the follow graph no longer includes them. The Redis memory is reclaimed within the next eviction cycle.

### Follow-Up 14: "How do you handle feed rate limiting to prevent abuse?"

Feed rate limiting protects the system from abusive traffic patterns. The rate limiter operates at three levels. Level one: per-user rate limiting. A single user cannot read their feed more than 60 times per minute. If exceeded, the system returns a 429 status code. Level two: per-IP rate limiting. A single IP address cannot send more than 1,000 feed requests per minute. This prevents bot attacks. Level three: global rate limiting. The total feed read throughput is capped at 150 percent of the expected peak load. If the cap is exceeded, excess requests are queued and processed with a delay. The rate limiter uses a token bucket algorithm with Redis as the backing store. Each user has a token bucket with 60 tokens, refilled at 1 token per second. The rate limiter adds 1 to 3 milliseconds to the read path latency.

### Follow-Up 15: "How do you handle cross-region failover?"

Cross-region failover ensures the feed system remains available if an entire region goes down. The failover strategy uses an active-passive model. The primary region (US-East) handles all fan-out writes and serves the majority of read traffic. The secondary region (US-West) serves read traffic only and receives replicated feed data from the primary. If the primary region fails, the secondary region is promoted to primary. The DNS records are updated to route traffic to the secondary region. The promotion takes 60 to 120 seconds: 30 seconds to detect the failure (health checks every 10 seconds, 3 consecutive failures triggers failover), 30 seconds to update DNS, and 30 seconds to stabilize. During the failover window, feed reads continue in the secondary region with the last replicated state (possibly 60 to 120 seconds stale). New content creation is queued in the secondary region's Kafka topic. After the primary recovers, the queued events are processed through the fan-out engine. The failover is tested quarterly to ensure it works correctly.

### Follow-Up 16: "How does your design change if the feed must support push notifications for important content?"

Push notifications require a separate delivery path from the feed. When a followed creator publishes content, the fan-out engine can optionally trigger a push notification to followers. The push notification decision is based on the content's importance score: content from close friends (high affinity), content with high engagement velocity, and content from verified creators triggers notifications. The push notification is sent via Firebase Cloud Messaging or Apple Push Notification Service. The notification includes a deep link that opens the feed scrolled to the specific content item. The push notification path is decoupled from the feed write path to avoid delaying the feed list write. The fan-out engine publishes a notification event to a separate Kafka topic. A notification worker consumes the topic and sends push notifications in batches. The notification worker respects per-user rate limits (no more than 5 notifications per hour) to avoid overwhelming users.

### Follow-Up 17: "How do you estimate the cost of running the feed system?"

The cost estimation covers four categories. Compute: the fan-out engine workers (64 instances at $0.50 per hour equals $768 per day), the read path service (32 instances at $0.50 per hour equals $384 per day), and the ranking engine (16 GPU instances at $3 per hour equals $1,152 per day). Storage: the Redis cluster (128 nodes at 128 GB RAM, each node at $200 per month equals $25,600 per month), and the Cassandra follower graph (256 nodes at $150 per month equals $38,400 per month). Network: cross-region data transfer (10 PB per month at $0.02 per GB equals $200,000 per month). Managed services: Kafka cluster (10 brokers at $500 per month equals $5,000 per month), Memcached cache (32 nodes at $100 per month equals $3,200 per month). The total monthly cost is approximately $320,000 for 100 million active users. This is $0.0032 per user per month.

### Follow-Up 18: "How do you maintain feed freshness during a flash crowd event?"

A flash crowd event is a sudden surge in feed reads caused by an external event (a celebrity posts, breaking news, a product launch). The read path must handle 5x to 10x the normal traffic without degrading latency. The system uses three mechanisms. Mechanism one: aggressive caching. The timeline cache TTL is reduced from 30 seconds to 5 seconds, and the prefetch window is increased to prefetch the next 2 pages instead of 1 page. The cache hit rate drops from 80 percent to 60 percent, but the cache miss path is optimized to handle 2x the normal load. Mechanism two: sacrificial degradation. Non-critical features are disabled during the flash crowd. The ranking model switches from ML-based to a simple chronological sort (saves 30 milliseconds per request). Ad insertion is disabled (saves 15 milliseconds). Content moderation light filters run instead of the full pipeline (saves 20 milliseconds). These degradations save 65 milliseconds per request, allowing the system to handle 3x the traffic. Mechanism three: admission control. If traffic exceeds 5x the normal load, excess requests receive a stale cache entry (5 seconds stale) instead of a fresh feed. The stale entry is returned in 5 milliseconds instead of 200 milliseconds. This sacrifices freshness for availability. Users see slightly stale content but do not see errors.

### Follow-Up 19: "How do you A/B test the fan-out strategy itself?"

Testing the fan-out strategy requires a controlled experiment that measures the impact on both write latency and read latency. The experiment assigns users to one of three groups: control (current hybrid fan-out with 10,000 follower threshold), variant A (fan-out on write for all users, no threshold), and variant B (fan-out on read for all users). Each group has 100,000 users. The experiment measures: write latency (P50 and P99 for content creation), read latency (P50 and P99 for feed reads), fan-out engine CPU utilization, Redis cluster write throughput, and Redis cluster memory utilization. The experiment runs for 7 days to capture weekday and weekend patterns. The results determine whether the current fan-out strategy is optimal or whether the threshold should be adjusted. For example, if variant B (fan-out on read) shows acceptable read latency for users with fewer than 500 followers, the threshold could be raised from 10,000 to 500, saving write amplification for those small-follower accounts.

### Follow-Up 20: "What metrics would you track in production to validate your feed design?"

Track these 12 metrics across three categories. User-facing metrics: feed load time (P50, P99, P999), feed freshness (average age of the top item at load time), feed engagement rate (likes, shares, comments per 100 feed impressions), and feed scroll depth (average number of items scrolled per session). System metrics: fan-out lag (seconds between content creation and feed list write), cache hit ratio (timeline cache and hydration cache), fan-out engine write throughput (writes per second per worker), and Redis cluster latency (P50, P99 for ZADD and ZREVRANGE). Business metrics: daily active users (DAU), feed revenue (ad impressions per feed view), user retention (percentage of users who read their feed on day 1, day 7, and day 30), and content diversity (average number of unique creators per 100 feed items). Alert on any metric that deviates by more than 3 standard deviations from its 7-day rolling average. Review the metrics weekly in a feed system health review.

### Follow-Up 21: "How do you version and roll back the ranking model?"

The ranking model versioning system stores each deployed model version in a model registry. The registry contains the model binary, the feature schema, the training data fingerprint, the evaluation metrics, and the deployment timestamp. Each feed request includes the model version ID in the response headers. The model is deployed using a blue-green deployment pattern: two model servers run simultaneously (blue and green). The blue server runs the current model. The green server runs the new model. When the new model is deployed, traffic is gradually shifted from blue to green over 30 minutes. If the new model causes a regression (engagement drops by more than 5 percent, or latency increases by more than 10 percent), traffic is shifted back to the blue server within 5 minutes. The rollback is instantaneous because the blue server is still running and serving requests. After 24 hours of stable operation, the blue server is updated to the next version.

### Follow-Up 22: "How do you handle the intersection of feed ranking and content moderation?"

Content moderation and feed ranking interact at two points. Point one: during content ingestion. The content moderation pipeline assigns a content safety score (0.0 to 1.0) to every new piece of content. Content with a safety score below 0.3 is rejected and never enters the feed system. Content with a safety score between 0.3 and 0.7 is flagged for human review and is not shown in feeds until reviewed. Content with a safety score above 0.7 is published immediately. Point two: during feed ranking. The ranking model includes the content safety score as a signal. Content with a safety score between 0.7 and 0.8 receives a small ranking penalty (score multiplied by 0.8). Content with a safety score above 0.9 receives a ranking boost (score multiplied by 1.1). This ensures that borderline content is shown less frequently without being censored entirely. The safety score is recomputed every 24 hours using the latest moderation model, and feed lists are re-scored to reflect the updated scores.

### Follow-Up 23: "What is the most common performance bottleneck in a feed system, and how do you detect it?"

The most common performance bottleneck is the hydration layer. The feed list read from Redis takes 5 milliseconds. The ranking computation takes 30 milliseconds. But the hydration of 20 content items takes 100 milliseconds or more, especially if the hydration cache has a low hit rate. You detect hydration bottlenecks by monitoring the hydration latency breakdown: database query time, cache fetch time, and serialization time. If the database query time is high (above 50 milliseconds), the content database is the bottleneck. If the cache fetch time is high (above 30 milliseconds), the Memcached cluster is overloaded. If the serialization time is high (above 20 milliseconds), the content objects are too large (include unnecessary metadata). The fix depends on the detected sub-bottleneck: add more database replicas, increase the Memcached cluster size, or trim the response payload.
