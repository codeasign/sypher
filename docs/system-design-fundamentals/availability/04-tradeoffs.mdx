# Availability Trade-offs

## The Core Tension

Availability is never free. Every redundant component, every health check, every failover mechanism adds cost — in infrastructure, engineering time, operational complexity, and latency. The central question is not "how do we make this 99.999% available?" but "what availability level is appropriate for this specific system at this specific time?"

The tension operates on three axes:

**Availability vs Cost**: Each additional nine multiplies infrastructure cost by 2-3x. A single-server hobby project cannot cost-justify multi-region active-active. A payment processor handling billions in transaction volume cannot afford to be down for 10 minutes.

**Availability vs Consistency**: The CAP theorem states you can have at most two of Consistency, Availability, and Partition Tolerance. In distributed systems, partitions are inevitable. You must choose: prioritize availability (AP systems like DynamoDB and Cassandra) or consistency (CP systems like Spanner and etcd).

**Availability vs Complexity**: Every redundancy mechanism adds moving parts. A simple single-server system has one failure mode: the server is down. A multi-region active-active system has dozens: replication lag, split-brain, DNS propagation delays, certificate expiration on standby, capacity mismatch between regions. More complexity means more things that can break, and incident response gets harder.

## Decision Matrix

Use this matrix to determine the appropriate availability target for different system types.

| System Type | Recommended Availability | Justification | Approximate Annual Budget |
|---|---|---|---|
| Personal blog / portfolio | 99.0% (two nines) | Cost of downtime is near zero. A single $5/month VPS is fine. | 3.65 days |
| Internal dashboard | 99.5% | Used during business hours. An hour of downtime affects a small team. | 1.83 days |
| SaaS application for SMEs | 99.9% (three nines) | Customers rely on it for daily operations. Revenue impact is moderate. | 8.76 hours |
| E-commerce platform | 99.95% | Holiday peak can generate six figures per hour. Every minute of downtime is measurable revenue loss. | 4.38 hours |
| Payment processing | 99.99% (four nines) | Regulatory requirements. Each minute of downtime can trigger SLA penalties and regulatory scrutiny. | 52.56 minutes |
| Air traffic control | 99.9999% (six nines) | Human safety depends on it. Redundancy is military-grade with multiple independent systems. | 31.5 seconds |
| DNS root servers | 99.99999% (seven nines) | If DNS fails, the internet stops. Anycast with dozens of globally distributed nodes. | 3.15 seconds |

The jump from 99.99% to 99.999% costs roughly 2-3x more. The jump from 99.999% to 99.9999% costs another 2-3x. At the extreme end, the cost is so high that only systems where failure directly causes physical harm or global-scale disruption can justify it.

### When to Choose CP

Choose CP (sacrifice availability for consistency) when:

- **Financial transactions**: You cannot have two ATMs dispensing the same $100 bill.
- **Inventory management**: You cannot oversell tickets for a concert with 50,000 seats.
- **Locking systems**: Distributed locks in etcd or Zookeeper must be consistent.
- **User account state**: Account balance reads must return the latest value.

In a CP system during a partition, the minority side of the partition stops accepting writes. If your write-primary database is on the minority side, writes are blocked. This is a partial availability loss — but consistency is preserved.

### When to Choose AP

Choose AP (sacrifice consistency for availability) when:

- **Social feeds**: It is acceptable for a user to see slightly stale posts for a few seconds.
- **Product catalog**: Showing a product as "in stock" when it was just sold is acceptable — the checkout step validates.
- **Content delivery**: CDN caches serve stale content during origin failures. The content is slightly old but the site works.
- **Analytics**: Dropping or duplicating a few events during a partition does not affect aggregate metrics meaningfully.

In an AP system, both sides of a partition accept writes. When the partition heals, conflict resolution (last-writer-wins, CRDTs, or custom merge logic) reconciles the divergent data.

## Five Failure Modes

Understanding failure modes helps you design for the right trade-offs.

### 1. Cascading Failure

A failure in one component causes overload in another, which fails, propagating the failure through the system.

```
[App Server 1 dies]
       │
       ▼
[Load balancer routes all traffic to App Server 2]
       │
       ▼
[App Server 2 gets 2x traffic, runs out of connection pool]
       │
       ▼
[App Server 2 fails → all traffic to App Server 3]
       │
       ▼
[App Server 3 overloaded → slow responses → connection timeouts]
       │
       ▼
[Database connection pool exhausted by slow queries]
       │
       ▼
[Database crashes → complete system outage]
```

Prevention: Circuit breakers, bulkheads (separate connection pools per service), capacity headroom, rate limiting.

### 2. Retry Storm

When a service recovers from a brief outage, all clients retry simultaneously — overwhelming the service and causing it to fail again.

<AsciiDiagram

  content={`
     t=0  Service fails
     t=1  100 clients waiting
     t=2  100 clients waiting
     t=5  100 clients retry AT THE SAME TIME ───▶ Service overloaded
     t=6  Service fails again
     t=11 100 clients retry AT THE SAME TIME ──▶ Service overloaded
     t=12 Service fails again (repeatedly)
     
     With jitter:
     t=0  Service fails

     t=2  ~10 clients retry per 100ms window
     t=3  Service handles load fine
     t=4  All clients retried, service healthy
     
     Jitter formula:
     delay = base_backoff * 2^retry + random(0, base_backoff)
  `}
  caption="Retry storm without jitter vs smooth recovery with jitter."
/>

Prevention: Exponential backoff with jitter. Maximum retry limits. Client-side rate limiting.

### 3. Thundering Herd

When a cached resource expires, every request that misses the cache hits the database simultaneously.

```
Cache key "product_123" expires
       │
       ▼
100 requests arrive simultaneously
       │
       ▼
All 100 miss cache
       │
       ▼
100 identical DB queries execute simultaneously
       │
       ▼
DB CPU spikes to 100%
       │
       ▼
All 100 requests slow down
       │
       ▼
More requests queue, DB connection pool fills
       │
       ▼
Other queries also slow, system degrades
```

Prevention: Cache locking (single request loads the cache, others wait), early recomputation (refresh cache before TTL expires), randomize TTLs.

### 4. Slow Response Cascades

A slow response is often worse than no response. A service that responds in 30 seconds instead of 100ms ties up connection pool slots, threads, and memory.

```
Normal:    ──▶ Request ──▶ 100ms response ──▶ Free slot
Slow:      ──▶ Request ──▶ ...30 seconds... ──▶ Timeout
                  │
                  ▼
            Connection pool fills with slow requests
                  │
                  ▼
            Healthy requests cannot get connections
                  │
                  ▼
            Service becomes effectively unavailable
```

Prevention: Aggressive timeouts (connection: 100ms, read: 500ms), circuit breakers that trip on latency (not just errors), thread pool isolation.

### 5. DNS Propagation Delay

When you fail over to a secondary region, DNS changes must propagate through the global DNS system. During propagation, some clients hit the dead region.

```
             t=0: Primary region fails
             t=30s: DNS record updated to secondary IP
             t=30-300s: Propagation in progress
     
     Client A (ISP cached):     Client B (just resolved):
     ┌──────────────────┐      ┌────────────────────┐
     │"I know primary's │      │"Secondary IP"       │
     │ IP"              │      │ Connect to secondary │
     │ Connect ──▶ BAD  │      │ ──▶ WORKS           │
     └──────────────────┘      └────────────────────┘
     
     Client C (TTL not expired):
     ┌──────────────────┐
     │"Primary IP cached│
     │ for 55 more sec  │
     │(TTL=60)"         │
     │ Connect ──▶ BAD  │
     └──────────────────┘
```

Prevention: Use low TTL values (60 seconds or less). Use anycast routing (BGP withdrawal is faster than DNS TTL expiration). Use a global load balancer with health-checked pools.

## Anti-Patterns

### 1. Assuming Fails Never Happen

The most dangerous anti-pattern is assuming the system will always work. You see this in:
- No health check configuration (default TCP keepalive = 2 hours)
- No circuit breakers for external API calls
- No timeout configuration on HTTP clients
- Single database server with no replica

**Reality**: Components fail. Network partitions happen. Disks fill up. Certificates expire. The question is never "if" but "when."

### 2. No Redundancy in Development or Staging

You run a single server in dev, a single database in staging. The production design includes redundancy but it has never been tested. When the failover mechanism is needed for the first time during a real incident, it does not work.

**Reality**: Test failover regularly. Chaos Engineering (see Netflix Chaos Monkey) is a formalization of this principle: constantly inject failures to verify that availability mechanisms work.

### 3. Ignoring DNS TTLs

You set a 60-second TTL on your DNS record. Your failover script updates the DNS record in 2 seconds. You assume failover completes in 2 seconds.

**Reality**: DNS TTL is the *minimum* time a resolver caches the record. Many ISPs ignore TTLs and cache for longer (5-30 minutes). Some browsers also cache DNS independently. Your 2-second DNS update does not reach users for minutes.

### 4. Symmetric Failure Detection

You configure all three application servers with the same health check interval (5s), failure threshold (3), and timeout (2s). When a transient network glitch drops 3 consecutive pings, all three servers go unhealthy simultaneously.

**Reality**: Add randomization to health check parameters. Server A: 5s interval, Server B: 7s, Server C: 4s. This prevents correlated mis-detections.

### 5. Forgetting to Monitor the Monitor

Your availability monitoring system is itself a SPOF. The monitoring dashboard is on the same server as the application. When the server goes down, you cannot see that it is down.

**Reality**: Run monitoring in a separate environment (different region, different cloud provider, or at minimum a different availability zone). Use an external uptime monitoring service (Pingdom, StatusCake, Checkly) that is independent of your infrastructure.

### 6. Over-Engineering for Day One

You design a five-nines system on day one for a product that has not launched yet. You spend 6 months building infrastructure and 0 months building features. The product fails because it solves no customer problem — but at least it fails with 99.999% uptime.

**Reality**: Start with a simple architecture that meets the current availability requirements. Add redundancy as the product matures and the cost of downtime becomes measurable. Amazon's "two-pizza team" philosophy applies: build what you need now, iterate when the data justifies it.

### 7. Assuming the Cloud Handles Everything

You deploy to a single AWS region, single availability zone, and assume AWS guarantees availability. When AWS us-east-1 had its multi-hour Kinesis outage in 2020, many customers learned the hard way that AWS availability guarantees apply to its infrastructure, not your application running on that infrastructure.

**Reality**: Cloud providers offer building blocks for availability, not availability itself. Multi-AZ RDS still requires you to configure it. ELB cross-zone load balancing is a checkbox you must enable. Read the shared responsibility model carefully.

### 8. No Load Shedding Under Stress

Traffic spikes 10x above normal (flash crowd, viral event, DDoS). Your auto scaling group adds 10 more instances, but the load balancer connection table grows beyond capacity. All connections — legitimate and malicious — are dropped.

**Reality**: Implement load shedding. Prioritize traffic: authenticated users over anonymous users. Serve cached pages for popular content. Return HTTP 503 with a Retry-After header for non-critical requests. A partially functioning system is better than a completely broken one.

## Availability vs Latency: The Hidden Trade-off

High availability mechanisms often increase latency. Every redundancy layer adds a processing step.

     - Active-passive with health checks is acceptable

  `}

The availability-latency trade-off is most acute for:

- **Synchronous replication**: Writing to two regions synchronously doubles the write time for most geographies (US-East to EU-West round trip = 80ms).
- **Health check for slow services**: A deep health check that queries the database adds latency to the health check path. In extreme cases, health check traffic can consume 5-10% of database capacity.
- **Circuit breakers on every call**: Checking the circuit breaker state adds 1-5 microseconds per call. Negligible for most services, but significant at 100,000+ RPS.

## Cost-Benefit Analysis Framework

Use this framework to determine the right availability level for any system.

### Step 1: Quantify the Cost of Downtime

```
C_downtime = DPM * A * D

Where:
DPM = direct cost per minute of downtime (lost revenue, SLA penalties)
A    = additional cost per minute (reputation damage, regulatory fines)
D    = expected downtime in minutes per month

Example:
DPM = $5,000/minute (e-commerce, average transaction value)
A    = $2,000/minute (reputation: 5% of customers churn after a 30-min outage)
D    = expected downtime: depends on architecture

At 99.9% (43.2 min/month):
Total cost = ($5,000 + $2,000) * 43.2 = $302,400/month

At 99.99% (4.32 min/month):
Total cost = ($5,000 + $2,000) * 4.32 = $30,240/month
```

### Step 2: Calculate the Cost of Redundancy

```
C_redundancy = (R - B) * N

Where:
R = cost per redundant instance per month
B = base infrastructure cost per month
N = number of redundant instances

Example:
Base infra:   $10,000/month (20 instances at $500 each)
N+1 (1 spare): $500/month extra
N+2 (2 spares): $1,000/month extra
2N (20 spares): $10,000/month extra
Multi-region: $30,000/month extra (duplicate infra in second region)
```

### Step 3: Find the Optimal Point

```
Optimal availability: where C_redundancy ≈ C_downtime_reduction

If downtime cost savings > redundancy cost → invest more in availability
If redundancy cost > downtime cost savings → you are over-investing

Example:
Current state (99.9%): downtime cost = $302,400/month
Proposed state (99.99%): redundancy cost = $30,000/month
Downtime cost at 99.99%: $30,240/month
Savings: $302,400 - $30,240 = $272,160/month
Net benefit: $272,160 - $30,000 = $242,160/month
→ The investment pays for itself 9x over.

If the proposed state costs $300,000/month (e.g., full multi-region with dedicated team):
Net benefit: $272,160 - $300,000 = -$27,840/month
→ The investment does not pay for itself. Stay at 99.9%.
```

## When NOT to Maximize Availability

High availability is not always the right goal. There are legitimate reasons to accept lower availability:

**Prototyping**: A prototype that costs $50/month with 99% availability is fine. Adding multi-region redundancy for a prototype burns money that could fund product development. The goal of a prototype is learning, not uptime.

**Internal tools**: A build system used by 10 engineers does not need 99.99% availability. If it is down for an hour, the team takes a coffee break. The cost of downtime is low. Invest that engineering time in features that generate revenue.

**Batch processing**: An overnight batch job that runs for 6 hours and fails once a month is acceptable if the job is idempotent and retryable. The availability metric is completion within the SLA window, not continuous uptime. A batch job that finishes in 8 hours (within a 12-hour window) is fine even if it had to restart twice.

**Cost-constrained markets**: A startup serving customers in developing markets where price sensitivity is extreme may need to trade availability for affordability. A 99.5% available service at half the cost is a better product-market fit than a 99.99% available service priced out of reach.

**Read-only vs read-write distinction**: The availability requirement for read operations is often different from write operations. A social media site that cannot accept new posts but can display cached feeds is partially functional. Define availability separately for reads and writes: 99.99% for reads, 99.9% for writes. This reduces cost while maintaining a good user experience.

**The key is intentionality**: Make the trade-off deliberately, with your eyes open, rather than discovering it in a post-incident review. Document the decision and revisit it quarterly. The right availability level changes as the business grows.

## Organizational Trade-offs

Availability is not just a technical decision. It involves organizational dynamics that affect how teams operate.

### Team Size and Skill Level

A small team of 3 engineers cannot maintain a multi-region active-active system. The operational burden (monitoring, incident response, capacity planning, failover drills) requires dedicated SRE support. A team of 3 should target 99.9% with managed services (RDS Multi-AZ, ELB, auto scaling) and accept the limitations.

Availability engineering effort scales with the number of nines:

| Nines | Engineering Overhead | SRE Coverage | Drill Frequency |
|---|---|---|---|
| 99% | 5% of team time | None | None |
| 99.9% | 10% of team time | On-call rotation | Quarterly |
| 99.99% | 20% of team time | Dedicated SRE | Monthly |
| 99.999% | 40%+ of team time | 24/7 SRE coverage | Weekly + Chaos |

### Blameless Postmortem Culture

The single most important organizational factor for availability is a blameless postmortem culture. If engineers fear punishment for mistakes, they will hide failures, not document them, and avoid automation that might fail during their shift.

A blameless culture means:

- Postmortems focus on system weaknesses, not individual mistakes.
- The question is always "what in the system allowed this person to make this mistake?"
- Action items target automation, not training or process changes.
- Incident responders are thanked, not blamed.

### Feature Velocity vs Reliability

Every deployment carries risk. A team deploying 10 times per day accepts more deployment risk than a team deploying once per month. The trade-off between deployment frequency and availability is managed through:

- **Automated testing**: Comprehensive integration tests catch deployment errors before they reach production.
- **Canary deployments**: Roll out changes gradually, monitoring for regression.
- **Feature flags**: Enable and disable features independently of deployments.
- **Rollback automation**: Ensure rollback is faster than forward-fix for critical issues.

Teams that deploy 50+ times per day (Amazon, Netflix, Etsy) maintain high availability through extensive automation and canary testing. Teams deploying once per week with manual testing should expect higher availability from the change management process.
