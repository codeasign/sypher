## The Problem Story

On the evening of November 17, 2022, a major airline's baggage handling system went dark. Bags piled up at carousel 12 in Frankfurt while the tracking dashboard showed every bag as "checked in" — two hours stale. The cause was not a database failure or a crashed microservice. It was a single TCP connection between the sensor gateway and the tracking server that had silently hung. The server was waiting for byte sequence 784,221. The gateway had sent byte 784,221 three times already, but the checksum disagreed each time and the server kept dropping it. Neither side knew they were deadlocked. Meanwhile, a dozen UDP-based fire alarms in the same server room had reported every puff of smoke with zero retransmission overhead and zero baggage disasters — because they could tolerate a lost packet once in a while.

Your video call stutters. The other person freezes for two seconds, then jumps forward in time. Your streaming movie, on the other hand, buffers for half a second and plays perfectly. Your bank transfer either completes fully or fails entirely — there is no in-between. Every single digital interaction you have relies on one of two fundamental transport protocols: TCP or UDP. Choosing the wrong one for your system design means the difference between a conference call that works in a tunnel and a stock trade that settles on the wrong number.

This decision — TCP versus UDP — is the oldest transport-layer choice in networking. It appears in every system design interview, every real-time architecture discussion, and every latency-sensitive implementation you will ever build. Getting it wrong costs you correctness or speed. Getting it right means your system survives at scale.

## The Insight

**TCP (Transmission Control Protocol)** is a connection-oriented, reliable, ordered transport that guarantees every byte arrives exactly once, in the right order, or the connection fails cleanly. **UDP (User Datagram Protocol)** is a connectionless, best-effort transport that sends discrete messages with no guarantees about delivery, ordering, or duplication.

Think of a restaurant kitchen. TCP is the head chef who calls out an order, waits for the line cook to repeat it back, confirms the repeat is correct, fires the dish, and then asks the waiter to confirm the plate reached table 7. Every step requires acknowledgment before the next step begins. If the waiter drops the plate, the head chef knows and fires another one. This is perfectly reliable and painfully slow.

UDP is a food runner shouting "Table 7 needs ketchup!" as they sprint past the kitchen door. They do not stop to hear someone repeat it. They do not wait for confirmation that the ketchup arrived. Most of the time it works fine. If the ketchup does not arrive, table 7 asks again — but that is the application's problem, not the runner's. The runner stays fast because they do not carry any overhead.

## Core Vocabulary

**TCP Connection** — A bidirectional virtual circuit between two hosts established via a three-way handshake. Why it matters: the handshake adds one round trip before any data flows, which sets a floor on your latency regardless of bandwidth.

**Three-Way Handshake** — SYN, SYN-ACK, ACK. The client and server exchange three packets to synchronize sequence numbers and establish state. Why it matters: this single mechanism prevents old duplicate packets from corrupting a new connection, but adds 1.5 round trips of delay to every new connection.

**Sequence Number** — A 32-bit counter that tracks every byte sent. Each byte gets a number. Why it matters: sequence numbers let the receiver detect missing bytes and reassemble data in order. Exhausting the sequence number space (wrap-around) is a real problem on high-speed links.

**Acknowledgment (ACK)** — A packet that says "I received everything up to byte N." Why it matters: selective ACKs (SACK) let TCP recover multiple lost packets in one round trip instead of retransmitting everything after the first loss.

**Flow Control** — The receiver advertises a window size: "I can accept this many more bytes." Why it matters: without it, a fast sender overwhelms a slow receiver and packets drop, triggering retransmissions that make everything worse.

**Congestion Control** — The sender probes the network path and adjusts its sending rate. Slow start, congestion avoidance, fast retransmit, fast recovery. Why it matters: this is why the internet does not collapse under load. Each algorithm is a different theory about how to share bandwidth fairly.

**Checksum** — A 16-bit hash of the TCP or UDP header and payload. Why it matters: a single bit flip in flight turns your JSON "amount": 5000 into "amount": 4000. TCP verifies every segment and drops corrupt ones. UDP checksums are optional in IPv4 and only cover the payload integrity, not ordering.

**UDP Datagram** — A self-contained message with a source port, destination port, length, and checksum. Maximum 65,507 bytes of payload (theoretical). Why it matters: each datagram is atomic — it is either received intact or not at all. The application sees whole messages, not a byte stream.

**Multiplexing** — Both TCP and UDP use 16-bit port numbers (0–65535) to identify which application on a host gets which traffic. Why it matters: port numbers let a single server run a hundred thousand connections by differentiating them by (source IP, source port, dest IP, dest port) tuples.

**MTU (Maximum Transmission Unit)** — The largest packet the underlying network can carry, typically 1500 bytes for Ethernet. Why it matters: TCP performs path MTU discovery to avoid fragmentation. UDP applications must handle fragmentation themselves or stay under ~1400 bytes to avoid it.

## How It Actually Works — Step by Step

### Step 1: A TCP Connection Opens — The Three-Way Handshake

Before a single byte of application data moves, the client and server exchange three packets to agree on sequence numbers and window sizes.

<AsciiDiagram

  │Client│                              │Server│
  └──┬───┘                              └──┬───┘
     │                                     │
     │  1. SYN (seq=1000, flags=SYN)       │
     │ ─────────────────────────────────►  │
     │                                     │ Server allocates
     │  2. SYN-ACK (seq=5000, ack=1001)    │ buffer, sets
     │ ◄─────────────────────────────────  │ initial seq=5000
     │                                     │
     │  3. ACK (seq=1001, ack=5001)        │
     │ ─────────────────────────────────►  │ Connection ESTABLISHED
     │                                     │

     │                                     │
     │  RTT = 1 round trip before data     │
     └─────────────────────────────────────┘
  `}
  caption="Three packets, one round trip before any data. On a 200ms link (transatlantic), that is 200ms of dead time before your HTTP request even leaves."

## Variants and Flavors

### Variant 1: TCP with TLS (TCP+TLS)

Standard TCP with encryption added via TLS handshake after the TCP handshake.

<AsciiDiagram
  id="tcp-vs-udp/tcp-tls"
  title="TCP+TLS Handshake Sequence"
  content={`
  ┌──────┐                                      ┌──────┐
  │Client│                                      │Server│
  └──┬───┘                                      └──┬────┘
     │                                             │
     │  ── TCP Handshake (3 packets) ──            │
     │  SYN ─────────────────────────────►         │
     │  ◄── SYN-ACK ──────────────────────         │
     │  ACK ─────────────────────────────►         │
     │                                             │
     │  ── TLS Handshake (2-3 round trips) ──     │
     │  ClientHello ─────────────────────►         │
     │  ◄── ServerHello + Cert + HelloDone         │
     │  ClientKeyExchange ───────────────►         │
     │  ◄── Finished ─────────────────────         │
     │                                             │
     │  ── Total setup: 3-4 round trips ──        │
     │  RTT 200ms x 4 = 800ms before first byte   │
     │                                             │
     │  HTTP/2 multiplexes over this one TCP       │
     │  + TLS connection (single connection)       │
     └─────────────────────────────────────────────┘
  `}
  caption="TCP+TLS adds 2-3 round trips on top of the TCP handshake. Connection reuse is critical — creating a new HTTPS connection for every request devastates latency."
/>

**When to use**: Any application handling sensitive data (credit cards, passwords, personal data) over TCP. This is the standard for HTTPS.

**When not to use**: When you need connection setup under 100ms. Consider a UDP-based encrypted transport like QUIC (HTTP/3) instead.

The application implements exactly the reliability guarantees it needs on top of UDP.

**When to use**: Real-time multiplayer games (losing a position update is fine; losing a "you picked up the flag" event is catastrophic), video streaming (keyframes reliable, delta frames best-effort).

**When not to use**: Database replication, financial transactions, or any scenario where absolute ordering and exactly-once delivery is required by regulation. Just use TCP.

QUIC is Google's UDP-based transport that implements TCP's reliability and TLS encryption in one handshake.

**When not to use**: Legacy infrastructure that does not support UDP at the load balancer or firewall level. Enterprise networks often block UDP.

## Mental Model Check

**Q1: You are designing a real-time chat application. Messages must arrive in order and cannot be lost. Should you use TCP or UDP?**

A1: TCP. Chat messages require ordered, reliable delivery. A user saying "I quit" followed by "just kidding" arriving in the wrong order reverses the meaning. TCP's sequence numbers and ACKs guarantee ordering. The downside is head-of-line blocking — if one message packet is lost, all later messages wait for its retransmission. For chat at human typing speed (~5 messages per minute), this is irrelevant.

**Q2: You are designing a stock market data feed that pushes 2 million price updates per second. Losing 0.01% of updates is acceptable — brokers can interpolate. What do you choose?**

A2: UDP. Two million TCP connections would overwhelm any receiver with ACK processing. The per-packet overhead of TCP headers (20-60 bytes) versus UDP headers (8 bytes) at this volume wastes 24-104 MB/s of bandwidth. UDP with a thin reliability layer — send each update with a sequence number, the receiver requests a retransmit of lost batches — gives you 99.99% delivery with 1/10th the overhead. This is exactly how NASDAQ's OUCH protocol works.

**Q3: Your video streaming service buffers for 3 seconds before playback. Halfway through, the connection drops for 5 seconds. When it resumes, playback picks up 2 seconds before the drop, skipping 3 seconds. Explain why.**

A3: TCP retransmits the lost segments, which arrive after the connection resumes. The video player has already buffered those bytes, so it resumes from the last decodable keyframe before the loss — which is 3 seconds behind the live point. This is TCP's reliability at work: it delivered the data, just late. A UDP-based streaming protocol (like WebRTC's SCTP-over-DTLS) would skip the lost frames and continue from the latest keyframe, trading perfect delivery for lower latency.

**Q4: A DNS query is 32 bytes. The response is under 512 bytes. Should you use TCP or UDP?**

A4: UDP. DNS uses UDP by default for queries under 512 bytes (extended to 4096 bytes with EDNS0). The overhead of a TCP handshake (3 packets + 2 for teardown = 5 packets) for a single 32-byte query would increase latency by 150% on a local network. The downside: UDP's 8-byte header versus TCP's 20-byte header matters less than the round trips. DNS falls back to TCP when the response exceeds 512 bytes or when the resolver requests it (zone transfers always use TCP for reliability at scale).

**Q5: You are building a multiplayer game with 100 players sending 20 position updates per second. Each update is 50 bytes. Two players have 50ms RTT, eight have 150ms RTT. What transport do you use and why?**

A5: UDP with a thin reliability layer. At 2000 updates/second (100 players x 20 updates), TCP's ACK overhead would add 2000 ACKs/second on every client. A player at 150ms RTT whose ACK for packet 5 is still in flight while packets 6-20 arrive would cause TCP to slow its sending rate due to congestion control. With UDP, each position update is independent — losing 1 of 20 packets per second per player (5% loss) is invisible because the next update overwrites the position 50ms later. Use a separate reliable stream (over the same UDP socket) for game-critical events like weapon pickups or health changes.
