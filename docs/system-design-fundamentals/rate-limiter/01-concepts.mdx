## The Problem

Imagine a coffee shop that serves 50 customers per hour. One morning, 500 people show up at once. The barista tries to brew every order simultaneously, the espresso machine overheats, the milk steamer clogs, and within 10 minutes no one gets coffee. The line is out the door, customers are angry, and the shop loses the entire day's revenue to a single spike.

This is exactly what happens to a server without rate limiting. A viral post links to your API. Traffic jumps from 1,000 requests per minute to 100,000 requests per second. Your database connection pool exhausts. CPU hits 100 percent. Memory consumption climbs until the kernel OOM killer terminates your process. Every legitimate user gets a 500 error. The site is down for 45 minutes. By the time you restart, the traffic spike is gone, but so is your user trust.

The deeper problem is not capacity -- it is control. You might have enough infrastructure to handle 100,000 requests per second during a planned load test. But unplanned spikes are different. They arrive without warmup, without traffic shaping, without the gradual scaling that your auto-scaler depends on. Your auto-scaler provisions new instances based on CPU utilization over 5-minute windows. A traffic spike that lasts 2 minutes does not trigger a scale-up event, but it is enough to melt a single instance. Your system needs a gatekeeper that says "slow down" early, before the chaos propagates.

Rate limiting is that gatekeeper. It is the practice of controlling the rate at which requests enter your system, ensuring each consumer gets a fair share and no single consumer can monopolize resources. It is not about rejecting users -- it is about protecting the experience for all users by shedding excess load at the perimeter. Without rate limiting, your infrastructure is one viral post away from collapse. With rate limiting, you absorb the spike gracefully: some users get 429 responses instead of 500 errors, and the system stays operational for everyone else.

Consider a concrete scenario. Your API serves 1,000 paying customers. Each customer averages 10 requests per second during business hours. Total traffic: 10,000 requests per second. Your backend is provisioned for 15,000 requests per second with 50 percent headroom. One customer runs a batch job that accidentally sends 8,000 requests per second. Without rate limiting, total traffic becomes 17,000 requests per second -- exceeding capacity. All 1,000 customers see degraded performance. With per-customer rate limiting, that one customer is limited to 100 requests per second. The other 999 customers never notice the batch job exists.

Another scenario: a misconfigured mobile app enters an infinite retry loop. Every time the app receives an error, it retries the request immediately. Without rate limiting, the retries compound exponentially: 1 error becomes 2 retries, 2 become 4, 4 become 8. Within 30 seconds, a single phone generates 2 billion requests per second. The rate limiter stops this by rejecting the retries when the client's quota is exhausted, breaking the exponential amplification.

## The Insight

Rate limiting works because traffic patterns follow statistical distributions. Even in a chaotic spike, the distribution of requests across clients is rarely uniform. A single misbehaving client -- a scraper, a buggy mobile app, an aggressive retry loop -- produces a disproportionate share of the traffic. By tracking per-client rates, you can identify and throttle the outliers while allowing well-behaved clients through.

Analogy: a highway on-ramp metering light. Without the light, cars merging from the ramp create turbulence that collapses the mainline flow. With the light, cars enter one at a time at a controlled rate, and the mainline keeps moving at 65 mph. The light adds a few seconds of delay to each merging car but saves everyone else 30 minutes of stop-and-go traffic. This is exactly the trade-off rate limiting makes: a small latency cost for each client in exchange for system-wide reliability.

The mathematical foundation of rate limiting is queueing theory. The relationship between arrival rate, service rate, and queue depth is governed by Little's Law: L = lambda * W, where L is the average number of requests in the system, lambda is the arrival rate, and W is the average time a request spends in the system. When arrival rate exceeds service rate, the queue grows unbounded. Rate limiting caps the arrival rate so it never exceeds the service rate, keeping the queue bounded and latency predictable.

A secondary insight is that rate limiting creates a backpressure signal. When a client receives a 429, it knows the server is under load. The client can use this signal to reduce its request rate, add delay, or switch to a different endpoint. This turns the rate limiter into a communication channel: the server tells the client about its capacity without the client needing to know anything about the server's internal state.

A third insight is that rate limiting enables capacity planning. When you know that your system will never receive more than X requests per second, you can calculate exactly how much infrastructure you need. Without rate limiting, you must provision for worst-case peaks. With rate limiting, you provision for the rate limit threshold, knowing that traffic exceeding that threshold will be rejected gracefully rather than collapsing the system.

## Core Vocabulary

**Rate Limiter**: A component that controls the rate of requests processed by a system, typically by rejecting requests that exceed a defined threshold. It sits between clients and backend services, intercepting every request before it reaches application logic. The rate limiter is transparent to the backend -- the backend sees a smooth, controlled traffic pattern.

**Throttling**: The act of slowing down or rejecting requests when a rate limit is exceeded. Throttling strategies include immediate rejection with an HTTP 429 status code, queuing requests with a delay for later processing, and degrading response quality (returning cached data instead of fresh data). The choice of throttling strategy depends on whether the system can tolerate latency or must respond immediately.

**Token Bucket**: An algorithm where tokens are added to a fixed-capacity bucket at a constant rate. Each request consumes one token. Requests are rejected when the bucket is empty. The bucket accumulates tokens during idle periods, enabling burst handling. This is the most widely used rate limiting algorithm because it naturally separates burst capacity from sustained rate.

**Leaky Bucket**: An algorithm where requests enter a queue (the bucket) and are processed at a fixed rate. If the queue is full, incoming requests are rejected. Unlike the token bucket, the leaky bucket enforces a strict outflow rate and provides no burst capability. It is ideal for smoothing spiky traffic into a steady stream, such as for database write operations that cannot handle sudden surges.

**Sliding Window Log**: An algorithm that maintains a timestamp log of requests per client. When a request arrives, it removes timestamps outside the current window, counts the remaining timestamps, and compares the count against the limit. This provides exact accuracy but uses memory proportional to the number of requests per window period.

**Sliding Window Counter**: An approximation of the sliding window log that uses counters for fixed sub-windows. It computes a weighted estimate of the request count by combining the current sub-window's counter with a fraction of the previous sub-window's counter. This uses O(1) memory per client with an accuracy error of roughly 3-5 percent.

**Fixed Window Counter**: The simplest algorithm. It maintains a counter that resets at fixed time intervals, such as every minute or every hour. The counter increments for each request and resets when the window expires. This algorithm is vulnerable to traffic spikes at window boundaries because clients can send burst traffic just before the reset and again just after.

**Rate Limit Header**: HTTP headers that communicate rate limit status to clients. The standard headers are `X-RateLimit-Limit` (the maximum requests allowed in the window), `X-RateLimit-Remaining` (the number of requests remaining in the current window), and `X-RateLimit-Reset` (the Unix timestamp when the window resets). These headers were popularized by Twitter and GitHub and are now an industry convention.

**Backpressure**: A signal sent from a downstream component to an upstream component indicating it cannot keep up, causing the upstream to slow its output. In rate limiting, the 429 response is a backpressure signal. Backpressure can propagate through a system: when the database is overloaded, the application rate-limits its requests to the database, which reduces the throughput the application can offer to clients.

**Quota**: A maximum number of requests allowed per client over a given time period. Quotas are typically expressed in requests per second, per minute, per hour, or per day. Multi-window quotas combine short-term and long-term limits, such as "100 requests per minute and 10,000 requests per day."

**Burst Capacity**: The ability to handle short spikes above the sustained rate limit. Burst capacity is measured in requests. For example, a rate limiter with a sustained limit of 100 requests per second and a burst capacity of 500 requests can handle a sudden spike of 500 requests, then drop to the sustained rate afterwards.

**Concurrency Limit**: A limit on the number of simultaneous in-flight requests at any moment. This differs from rate limiting, which controls the rate of request arrivals. Concurrency limiting protects against long-running requests that accumulate and exhaust connection pools or thread pools.

**Consistent Hashing**: A technique for distributing keys across a set of servers such that adding or removing a server only requires relocating a fraction of keys. In rate limiting, consistent hashing ensures that all requests for a given client are routed to the same Redis shard, which is necessary for atomic rate limit operations.

**Lua Scripting**: Atomic scripts that execute on the Redis server without interruption from other commands. Lua scripts are the standard mechanism for implementing race-condition-free rate limit checks in distributed systems. A single Lua call replaces the read-check-write round trip.

**Circuit Breaker**: A pattern that detects when a downstream dependency (such as Redis) is failing and prevents further calls until the dependency recovers. In rate limiting, the circuit breaker switches from Redis-backed state to local in-memory state when Redis is unavailable, ensuring the rate limiter continues operating.

**Exponential Backoff**: A retry strategy where the delay between retries increases exponentially (1 second, 2 seconds, 4 seconds, 8 seconds). This prevents retry storms when multiple clients receive 429 responses simultaneously.

**Jitter**: Random variation added to retry delays to prevent synchronized retries. Without jitter, N clients receiving a 429 at the same time would all retry at the same time, recreating the spike. With jitter, the retries spread out across the delay window.

**Request Collapsing**: A technique where multiple identical requests are merged into a single request. If 100 clients request the same resource while rate limited, the rate limiter can forward one request and share the response among all clients. This is used by CDNs and caching proxies to reduce backend load.

**Rate Limit Policy**: A configuration that defines who is limited, by what metric, to what threshold, and with what action. Example policy: "Free tier clients are limited to 100 requests per minute on the /api/data endpoint. When exceeded, return 429 with a 30-second Retry-After."

**Sliding Window**: A time window that slides continuously rather than resetting at fixed boundaries. A 60-second sliding window always contains the last 60 seconds of activity, regardless of when the first request was made. This eliminates the boundary spike problem of fixed windows.

## How It Works -- Step by Step

Rate limiting follows a predictable sequence of operations for every incoming request. Understanding this sequence at a granular level reveals where performance bottlenecks and design decisions live.

### Step 1: Request Arrives at the Edge

Every request to your system first hits an edge component -- a load balancer, API gateway, or reverse proxy. This is the earliest point where you can apply rate limiting. The edge component checks whether the request can be served from cache. If so, the rate limiter is bypassed entirely. If not, the request proceeds to rate limit evaluation. The edge extracts the client identifier from the request: an IP address, API key, authentication token, or session cookie.

The choice of edge component affects rate limiter placement. If you use an API gateway (Kong, Envoy, NGINX), the rate limiter runs as a gateway plugin and intercepts all traffic before it reaches any service. If you use a load balancer (AWS ALB, HAProxy), the rate limiter runs as a separate service that the load balancer forwards requests to. Edge-based rate limiting (Cloudflare, AWS WAF) runs at the CDN level, before traffic even reaches your infrastructure.

### Step 2: Identity Key is Normalized

The rate limiter normalizes the raw client identity into a deterministic lookup key. For IP-based limiting, this might be the raw IP or a /24 CIDR prefix to group users behind NAT. For API key-based limiting, it is the key hashed with SHA-256 to create a fixed-length identifier that does not leak the raw key. For token-based limiting, the JWT sub claim provides a user-level identifier.

The key format determines the granularity of your rate limits and how you shard across Redis nodes. A common format is `rl:{namespace}:{client_id}:{endpoint}`.

```python
def build_rate_limit_key(client_id, endpoint_path, version="v1"):
    normalized_id = hashlib.sha256(client_id.lower().encode()).hexdigest()[:16]
    return f"rl:{version}:{normalized_id}:{endpoint_path}"
```

Key normalization must be deterministic -- the same client always produces the same key. It must also be collision-resistant -- two different clients never produce the same key. SHA-256 truncation to 16 hex characters (64 bits) gives 2^64 possible values, which provides sufficient collision resistance for systems with under 1 billion clients.

### Step 3: Rate Limit State is Retrieved

The rate limiter fetches the current state for this client key from the data store. In a Redis-backed implementation, this is a single `GET` command for a simple counter or an `HGETALL` command for complex state like token bucket parameters (token count, last refill timestamp, bucket capacity). The state retrieval should be fast -- under 5 milliseconds, ideally under 2 milliseconds.

For performance, a multi-tier cache is used:
- **L1 (in-process)**: A local dictionary or LRU cache within the rate limiter process. Zero network latency. Stores recently accessed rate limit states with a 1-second TTL. Hit rate: 50-70 percent.
- **L2 (distributed)**: A shared Memcached or Redis read-only replica cluster. 1-2 millisecond latency. Hit rate: 20-30 percent.
- **L3 (authoritative)**: The main Redis cluster. 3-5 millisecond latency. Hit rate: 10-20 percent.

The L1 cache is the most important optimization. At 50,000 requests per second, cutting the Redis load by 60 percent saves 30,000 operations per second, which translates to roughly 3 fewer Redis CPU cores needed.

### Step 4: Algorithm Check

The core decision: does this request exceed the rate limit? Each algorithm performs a different check:

**Token bucket**: The rate limiter reads the current token count and the last refill timestamp. It calculates the number of tokens that should have been added since the last refill based on elapsed time and refill rate. It adds those tokens to the bucket, capping at the maximum capacity. If the token count is at least 1, the request is allowed and a token is consumed. If the token count is 0, the request is rejected.

**Fixed window**: The rate limiter reads the counter for the current time window (calculated by dividing the current Unix timestamp by the window size). If the counter is below the limit, the request is allowed and the counter is incremented. If the counter equals or exceeds the limit, the request is rejected. If the window has expired, the counter resets.

**Sliding window log**: The rate limiter retrieves the sorted set of timestamps for this client. It removes all timestamps that fall outside the current window using `ZREMRANGEBYSCORE`. It counts the remaining timestamps using `ZCOUNT`. If the count is below the limit, it adds the current timestamp to the set using `ZADD` and allows the request. If the count equals or exceeds the limit, it rejects the request.

**Sliding window counter**: The rate limiter retrieves the counters for the current and previous sub-windows using `HGETALL` or two `HGET` calls. It calculates the weight of the current sub-window by computing `(now - sub_window_start) / sub_window_duration`. It estimates the total request count as `previous_counter * (1 - weight) + current_counter`. If the estimate is below the limit, it allows the request and increments the current sub-window counter. If the estimate exceeds the limit, it rejects the request.

### Step 5: State Mutation with Atomicity

If the request is allowed, the rate limiter mutates the stored state in the data store. The mutation depends on the algorithm. For token bucket: decrement the token count and update the last refill timestamp. For fixed window: increment the counter with `INCR` and set or update the key's TTL. For sliding window log: append the current timestamp to the sorted set with `ZADD`. For sliding window counter: increment the current sub-window's counter with `HINCRBY`.

The critical requirement is atomicity. The state read and the state mutation must happen as a single atomic operation. Without atomicity, two concurrent requests from the same client can both read the same token count of 1, both decide the request is allowed, and both decrement, resulting in a negative token count of -1. This allows 2 requests when only 1 was available.

Redis Lua scripts provide this atomicity by executing the entire read-evaluate-write sequence as a single operation on the Redis server. No other commands interleave during execution. The script runs on the keys-required-to-exist-at-call-time principle, ensuring all keys are known before execution begins.

### Step 6: Backend Forwarding

The allowed request is forwarded to the backend service. The rate limiter may attach rate limit headers to the request or response. Common headers include `X-RateLimit-Limit` (the maximum requests allowed in the window), `X-RateLimit-Remaining` (the number of requests remaining in the current window), and `X-RateLimit-Reset` (the Unix timestamp when the window resets).

The backend receives the request in the normal flow. It has no knowledge that rate limiting occurred. The traffic pattern it sees is smooth and controlled, free of the spikes that would otherwise arrive. This decoupling is intentional -- the backend should not need to know about rate limit policies or current usage levels.

### Step 7: Response Interception

On the return path, the rate limiter may intercept the response to perform post-processing. This includes attaching rate limit headers to the outgoing response, computing updated remaining counts for the headers, and handling response-specific logic such as refunding tokens when the backend returns an error.

Token refund is an important optimization. If the backend returns a 500 error, the rate limiter can refund the consumed token. This means the client is not penalized for errors caused by the server. The refund is also atomic, using a Lua script that checks the current token count against the capacity before incrementing.

```lua
-- Token refund Lua script
local key = KEYS[1]
local capacity = tonumber(ARGV[1])
local state = redis.call("HMGET", key, "tokens", "last_refill")
local tokens = tonumber(state[1]) or 0
tokens = math.min(capacity, tokens + 1)
redis.call("HMSET", key, "tokens", tokens)
return tokens
```

### Step 8: Rejected Request Response

If the request exceeds the limit, the rate limiter returns an HTTP 429 Too Many Requests response without forwarding the request to the backend. The response includes status code 429, a `Retry-After` header with the number of seconds the client should wait, rate limit headers showing the client their current status, and a structured error body explaining the rate limit violation.

### Step 9: Metrics Emission

Every rate limit decision emits metrics for monitoring and alerting. The following metrics are essential for understanding rate limiter behavior and detecting problems:

- `rate_limiter_requests_total`: Total requests processed, tagged by client tier, endpoint, and decision (allow/reject)
- `rate_limiter_allowed_total`: Requests that passed the rate limit check
- `rate_limiter_rejected_total`: Requests that exceeded the limit, tagged by rejection reason (burst, quota, concurrency)
- `rate_limiter_latency_seconds`: Time spent in the rate limit check, broken down by p50, p95, p99
- `rate_limiter_cache_hit_ratio`: Ratio of L1 cache hits to total lookups
- `rate_limiter_tokens_remaining`: Gauge showing remaining tokens per active client key

Metrics are emitted asynchronously using a background thread or a message queue to avoid adding latency to the request path. A rate limiter that blocks request processing to emit a metric adds latency that compounds across the entire system.

### Step 10: Periodic State Cleanup

Background processes must periodically clean up stale rate limit state. Expired token buckets, abandoned client logs, and orphaned counters consume memory in the data store. Without cleanup, Redis memory usage grows without bound.

The cleanup strategy depends on the data structure. TTL-based keys: each rate limit key has a TTL of `window_size * 2`. After `2 * window_size` seconds of inactivity, Redis automatically evicts the key. Lazy expiration: the rate limit check itself removes expired entries. For sliding window log, `ZREMRANGEBYSCORE` removes expired timestamps before counting. Background sweep: a cron job runs every 60 seconds, scanning Redis keys matching the pattern `rl:*` and removing entries with expired TTLs.

## Variants and Flavors

### Variant 1: Global Rate Limiting

Global rate limiting applies a single limit across all clients. This protects the overall system from being overwhelmed by aggregate traffic. It is the simplest form but does not prevent a single client from starving others. A global rate limiter counts all incoming requests against a single counter.

```python
class GlobalRateLimiter:
    def __init__(self, redis_client, limit_per_second):
        self.redis = redis_client
        self.key = "rl:global"
        self.limit = limit_per_second

    def allow(self):
        current = self.redis.get(self.key)
        if current and int(current) >= self.limit:
            return False
        pipe = self.redis.pipeline()
        pipe.incr(self.key)
        pipe.expire(self.key, 2)
        pipe.execute()
        return True
```

### Variant 2: Per-Client Rate Limiting

Each client receives its own rate limit quota. This prevents any single client from overwhelming the system or starving other clients. It is the standard approach for multi-tenant APIs. Each client's state is stored independently and checked independently.

Per-client limits enable tiered pricing models. Free clients get 100 requests per minute. Pro clients get 1,000 requests per minute. Enterprise clients get 10,000 requests per minute. The rate limiter checks the client's tier before evaluating the limit.

```python
class PerClientRateLimiter:
    def __init__(self, redis_client, default_limit=100, tier_limits=None):
        self.redis = redis_client
        self.default_limit = default_limit
        self.tier_limits = tier_limits or {
            "free": 100,
            "pro": 1000,
            "enterprise": 10000
        }

    def allow(self, client_id, tier="free"):
        limit = self.tier_limits.get(tier, self.default_limit)
        key = f"rl:per_client:{client_id}"
        current = self.redis.get(key)
        if current and int(current) >= limit:
            return False
        pipe = self.redis.pipeline()
        pipe.incr(key)
        pipe.expire(key, 60)
        pipe.execute()
        return True
```

### Variant 3: Distributed Rate Limiting

When you have multiple application instances running behind a load balancer, rate limit state must be shared across all instances. Without shared state, each instance has its own counter, and a client can exceed the limit by distributing requests across instances.

Distributed rate limiting uses a centralized data store, typically Redis or Memcached. All instances read and write to the same store, ensuring a consistent view of the rate limit state. Consistent hashing distributes client keys across Redis shards, ensuring all requests for the same client hit the same shard.

### Variant 4: Hierarchical Rate Limiting

Combines global and per-client limits into a hierarchy. A request must pass all applicable limits in the hierarchy to proceed. This provides system-wide protection plus client-level fairness.

A three-level hierarchy example: Level 1 (Global) is 100,000 requests per minute total for the entire system. Level 2 (Tier) is 50,000 req/min for Free tier and unlimited for Enterprise. Level 3 (Per-client) is 100 req/min for each Free client and 10,000 req/min for each Enterprise client.

The request checks level 3 first (fastest, per-client, local cache), then level 2 (tier aggregate, requires Redis), then level 1 (global, single counter). If any level rejects, the rejection is immediate and subsequent levels are not checked.

```python
class HierarchicalRateLimiter:
    def __init__(self, redis_client):
        self.redis = redis_client
        self.global_limiter = GlobalRateLimiter(redis_client, 100000)
        self.tier_limiters = {
            "free": GlobalRateLimiter(redis_client, 50000),
            "pro": GlobalRateLimiter(redis_client, 200000),
            "enterprise": None  # no tier limit
        }

    def allow(self, client_id, tier="free"):
        # Level 1: Global check
        if not self.global_limiter.allow():
            return False
        # Level 2: Tier aggregate check
        tier_limiter = self.tier_limiters.get(tier)
        if tier_limiter and not tier_limiter.allow():
            return False
        # Level 3: Per-client check
        limit = {"free": 100, "pro": 1000, "enterprise": 10000}.get(tier, 100)
        key = f"rl:hierarchical:{tier}:{client_id}"
        current = self.redis.get(key)
        if current and int(current) >= limit:
            return False
        pipe = self.redis.pipeline()
        pipe.incr(key)
        pipe.expire(key, 60)
        pipe.execute()
        return True
```

### Variant 5: Adaptive Rate Limiting

Adaptive rate limiting adjusts limits in real time based on backend health. When the backend is healthy, limits are generous. When the backend is struggling, limits tighten automatically. This closes the control loop, making the rate limiter responsive to actual system capacity rather than static configuration.

The adaptive controller monitors backend p99 latency (if it exceeds 500ms, reduce limits by 20 percent), backend error rate (if it exceeds 5 percent, reduce limits by 50 percent), Redis latency (if it exceeds 50ms, switch to local only), and queue depth (if the backend's request queue exceeds 10,000, reject aggressively).

Adaptive limits are computed every 30 seconds and propagated to all rate limiter instances through a configuration service. The adjustment is gradual -- no single update changes limits by more than 20 percent -- to prevent oscillation.

## Mental Model Check

**Q1: What is the fundamental trade-off in rate limiter algorithm choice?**

A1: The fundamental trade-off is between accuracy and memory or computation cost. Sliding window log is perfectly accurate because it stores every request timestamp, but it consumes O(n) memory per client and requires O(log n) operations for insertion and counting. Fixed window counter uses O(1) memory but allows double bursts at window boundaries because a client can send a full quota just before the reset and another full quota just after. Sliding window counter offers a middle ground with O(1) memory and an approximation error of roughly 3-5 percent. Token bucket preserves burst handling without window boundaries but requires careful configuration of capacity and refill rate. The choice depends on your accuracy requirements, memory budget, and traffic patterns.

**Q2: Why can't you rely on IP address alone for client identity?**

A2: Multiple users behind a NAT share the same public IP address. A single NAT gateway might serve 500 employees in an office building or 10,000 mobile users behind carrier-grade NAT (CGNAT). Rate limiting by IP would throttle all of these users together, blocking legitimate users when just one user exceeds the limit. Mobile carriers compound this with CGNAT where thousands of subscribers share a single public IP. Further, IP addresses change -- mobile clients switch between WiFi and cellular, home routers get reassigned, and VPNs mask the origin. Use API keys or authentication tokens for accurate client identity, and use IP addresses only as a secondary signal or for anonymous traffic. The hybrid approach uses API keys for authenticated requests and per-IP limits for anonymous requests, providing accurate identification where possible and best-effort limiting otherwise.

**Q3: How does rate limiting differ from load shedding?**

A3: Rate limiting operates on request rate per client identity. It is surgical -- it targets specific misbehaving clients based on their individual request rate. Load shedding operates on total system load regardless of source. It is a sledgehammer -- when the system exceeds a load threshold, it drops a percentage of traffic or prioritizes certain request types. They are complementary: rate limit per client first to prevent any single client from creating the overload, then load-shed if the system is still overloaded despite proper per-client limits. A common pattern is to rate limit at the application layer and load-shed at the infrastructure layer (load balancer). The difference in granularity means rate limiting requires per-client state tracking, while load shedding operates on aggregate system metrics like CPU utilization or queue depth.

**Q4: What happens to rejected requests? How should clients handle 429 responses?**

A4: Rejected requests receive an HTTP 429 status code with a `Retry-After` header. The client must respect this header and not retry before the specified time. Aggressive retries without backoff create a thundering herd problem, where all rejected clients retry simultaneously, triggering another wave of rejections. The standard retry strategy is exponential backoff with jitter: start with a delay of 1 second, double on each retry up to a maximum of 60 seconds, and add random jitter of plus or minus 50 percent. The total retry budget should be limited to 3-5 retries to prevent infinite loops. Server-side, the rate limiter should track retry frequency per client and escalate to temporary bans (5 minutes, then 30 minutes, then 24 hours) for clients that ignore Retry-After. The combination of client-side exponential backoff and server-side retry enforcement creates a stable system.

**Q5: When would you choose concurrency limiting over rate limiting?**

A5: Concurrency limiting protects against long-running requests that accumulate and exhaust connection pools or thread pools. Rate limiting protects against a high arrival rate of short requests. A file upload API benefits from both: concurrency limiting to limit simultaneous large uploads (max 10 concurrent 1GB uploads) and rate limiting to limit the arrival rate of upload requests (max 100 upload requests per minute). Concurrency limiting is appropriate when the cost is in simultaneous work -- database connections, thread pool slots, file handles. Rate limiting is appropriate when the cost is in request processing overhead -- parsing, routing, logging. The two are independent and should be configured separately. A typical configuration for a backend service is 100 concurrency slots and 1,000 requests per minute rate limit. The concurrency limit prevents 100 simultaneous slow requests from blocking the system, while the rate limit prevents 10,000 fast requests from overwhelming the CPU.

**Q6: What is the boundary burst problem in fixed window rate limiting?**

A6: The boundary burst problem occurs when a fixed window reset allows a client to send a full quota of requests just before the reset and another full quota just after. For example, with a limit of 100 requests per minute, a client can send 100 requests at 12:59:59 and another 100 at 1:00:00. In 2 seconds, the client sent 200 requests, effectively doubling the intended rate. The sliding window algorithm solves this by maintaining the window relative to the current time, not to fixed clock boundaries. The token bucket algorithm solves it by limiting burst to the bucket capacity regardless of when the burst occurs.

**Q7: How do you choose between token bucket and leaky bucket?**

A7: Choose token bucket when you need to allow burst traffic. The token bucket accumulates unused capacity during idle periods, enabling bursts when demand spikes. This matches most web API traffic patterns where requests arrive in bursts (page loads, batch jobs, data syncs). Choose leaky bucket when you need to enforce a strict output rate regardless of input pattern. The leaky bucket queues requests and processes them at a fixed rate, smoothing out spikes. This matches queue processing systems where the downstream component (a database, a print queue) cannot handle bursts. The token bucket is more common in API rate limiting (used by AWS, GitHub, Stripe). The leaky bucket is more common in data pipeline and job processing systems.

## Key Takeaways

Rate limiting is a fundamental pattern for building resilient distributed systems. It acts as a gatekeeper that protects backend services from traffic spikes, misbehaving clients, and resource exhaustion. The mental model is a bouncer at a club: check each request, maintain a count of who is inside, and turn away excess traffic before it causes chaos.

The core vocabulary -- token bucket, leaky bucket, sliding window, fixed window, 429, backpressure, burst capacity, concurrency limits -- forms the foundation of the pattern. Each variant serves a different purpose: global for system protection, per-client for fairness, distributed for multi-instance deployments, hierarchical for multi-tenant platforms, and adaptive for dynamic environments.

Understanding the request lifecycle at a granular level -- arrival, identity extraction, state read, algorithm check, mutation, forwarding, response, metrics, cleanup -- reveals where design decisions impact performance, consistency, and reliability. The ten steps of the lifecycle provide a framework for reasoning about any rate limiter implementation.

The mental model check questions sharpen understanding of the trade-offs that every rate limiter design must navigate: accuracy versus memory, strictness versus availability, identification versus privacy, and global versus per-client approaches. Mastering these foundations prepares you for the deeper implementation details covered in the next chapter.
