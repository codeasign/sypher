# Reliability — Core Concepts

## The Problem: When Systems Lie

Imagine you transfer $50,000 from your savings account to pay for a house deposit. The banking app shows "Transfer complete." You sign the paperwork. Three days later, the seller's bank says the money never arrived — and your savings account still shows the $50,000. The money vanished into a network timeout. The system told you it succeeded when it did not.

This is not hypothetical. In 2012, a major bank's payment system lost $175 million overnight because a software upgrade introduced a silent data corruption bug. The logs showed clean success. The database showed clean success. The money was gone.

Now imagine a social media feed. You open the app, scroll for five minutes, and see three copies of the same post from your friend. You refresh — now there are five. The "like" counter shows 1,042. You press like. Nothing happens. You press again. Nothing. You press a third time. Now it shows 1,098 and the app crashes.

These are reliability failures. The first destroys trust permanently — not one user loses a banking relationship when money disappears. The second destroys trust gradually — users churn when feeds feel broken. Both damage the business, but the first is catastrophic. Banking systems require absolute correctness. Social feeds require functional availability. Both require reliability, but they define it differently.

A single reliability incident at a payment processor in 2021 caused 11,000+ merchants to lose transaction data. Recovery took 72 hours. The company paid $12 million in SLA penalties and lost 30% of its processing volume within the next quarter. Customers do not forgive data loss.

## Insight: What Reliability Actually Means

Reliability is the probability that a system functions correctly over a defined period under stated conditions. The formal definition comes from reliability engineering: R(t) = P(T > t), where T is the time to first failure.

```
R(t) = e^(-λt)
```

This is the exponential reliability function, where λ (lambda) is the failure rate. If a service has a failure rate of 0.001 per hour, its reliability over 24 hours is e^(-0.001 * 24) = e^(-0.024) = 0.976, or 97.6%. Over 100 hours: 90.5%. Over 1,000 hours: 36.8%. Every system decays toward unreliability over time.

Think about a postal service analogy. You mail 100 letters per year. A 99% reliable postal service loses one letter per year. That might be your tax return. A 99.9% reliable service loses one letter per decade. A 99.99% reliable service loses one letter per century. In software, the difference between 99% and 99.99% is the difference between a service that fails for 3.65 days per year and one that fails for 52 minutes per year.

| Reliability | Downtime per year | Downtime per month | Downtime per week |
|---|---|---|---|
| 90% (one nine) | 36.5 days | 73 hours | 16.8 hours |
| 99% (two nines) | 3.65 days | 7.3 hours | 1.68 hours |
| 99.9% (three nines) | 8.76 hours | 43.8 minutes | 10.1 minutes |
| 99.99% (four nines) | 52.56 minutes | 4.38 minutes | 1.01 minutes |
| 99.999% (five nines) | 5.26 minutes | 26.3 seconds | 6.05 seconds |
| 99.9999% (six nines) | 31.5 seconds | 2.63 seconds | 0.6 seconds |

Google's Borg infrastructure targets five nines (99.999%) for its control plane. AWS S3 Standard targets 99.999999999% durability for objects — eleven nines. That means if you store 10 trillion objects in S3, you expect to lose roughly one object per year. These numbers come from design, not luck.

## Core Vocabulary

**Reliability.** The probability that a system performs its intended function without failure over a given time period. Measured as a percentage of successful operations or uptime.

**Correctness.** The property that a system produces the right output for every valid input, under all conditions. A system can be available but incorrect — it responds quickly with wrong answers.

**Error.** A design flaw or mistake in the system that can produce failures. An error is a defect; a failure is the observable symptom of that defect. The error exists even when no failure occurs.

**Fault.** The internal state of a system component that deviates from correct operation. A disk sector going bad is a fault. A memory bit flipping due to cosmic radiation is a fault.

**Failure.** The deviation of a system's external behavior from its specification. When a fault propagates to the service boundary, it becomes a failure. Not all faults cause failures — fault tolerance prevents propagation.

**Fault Tolerance.** The ability of a system to continue operating correctly in the presence of faults. Achieved through redundancy, isolation, and graceful degradation. A fault-tolerant system masks internal faults from its callers.

**Fail-Safe.** A design property where a system transitions to a safe state when a failure occurs. For a nuclear reactor, fail-safe means control rods insert automatically on power loss. For a payment system, fail-safe means aborting a transaction rather than committing it partially.

**Idempotency.** The property that performing the same operation multiple times produces the same result as performing it once. PUT requests in REST are idempotent. POST requests are not — unless you add an idempotency key. Idempotency prevents duplicate charges, duplicate emails, duplicate database entries.

**Retry.** Repeating a failed operation in the hope that it succeeds on a subsequent attempt. Retries recover from transient failures (network timeouts, temporary overload) but amplify persistent failures (bugs, missing resources).

**Backoff.** Waiting between retries to avoid overwhelming the failing system. Fixed backoff waits the same duration each time. Exponential backoff doubles the wait after each attempt. Jitter adds randomness to prevent thundering herd problems.

**Circuit Breaker.** A pattern that stops calls to a failing service when the failure rate exceeds a threshold, preventing cascading failures. The circuit has three states: closed (normal), open (failing — reject all calls immediately), and half-open (testing recovery).

**Timeout.** A maximum duration a system waits for an operation to complete before treating it as failed. Without timeouts, a hung service causes callers to hang too — cascading resource exhaustion.

**Graceful Degradation.** The ability of a system to provide reduced functionality when some components fail, rather than failing entirely. When the recommendation service is down, show trending items instead. When the database is slow, serve stale cached data.

**Byzantine Fault.** A fault where a component produces arbitrary, potentially malicious behavior. Byzantine components may send conflicting data to different observers, impersonate other components, or lie about their state. Byzantine fault tolerance requires 3f+1 replicas to tolerate f faulty nodes.

The diagram above shows the fundamental architecture pattern. A client sends requests through a retry loop with backoff. The request reaches the service, which checks its circuit breaker before calling dependencies. If the primary database is down, the service falls back to cache or a degraded response. If the circuit breaker detects excessive failures, it stops calls to the failing dependency entirely and the fallback service handles the request.

Every element of this pipeline contributes to reliability in a different way:

- **Retry with backoff** handles transient failures — the database was momentarily overloaded, the network packet was dropped, the garbage collector was running.
- **Circuit breaker** prevents cascading failures — one slow dependency cannot consume all your threads and bring down the entire service.
- **Fallback** provides degraded functionality — users see slightly stale data instead of errors.
- **Caching** reduces load on dependencies, making them less likely to fail.

## Step by Step: How a Reliable System Handles Failure

### Step 1: The Client Sends a Request

A user clicks "Place Order" on an e-commerce site. The frontend sends a POST request to the order service at `POST /api/v1/orders` with an `Idempotency-Key` header. This key is a UUID generated by the frontend when the user first clicked the button. If the user clicks again, the same key is sent — the server deduplicates.

### Step 2: The Request Times Out

The order service needs to check inventory. It sends a gRPC call to the inventory service with a 500-millisecond timeout. The inventory service is running a heavy background job. The 500ms expires. The gRPC client returns `DEADLINE_EXCEEDED`.

Without a timeout, the order service thread blocks indefinitely. With 200 concurrent requests, you exhaust the thread pool in seconds. All threads are waiting — no thread is doing useful work. New requests queue and time out too. Cascading failure begins.

### Step 3: The Retry Loop Activates

The order service has a retry policy: max 3 attempts, exponential backoff starting at 200ms, jitter of ±50ms. The first attempt failed. The client waits a random duration between 150ms and 250ms. The second attempt also fails — the inventory service is still running its background job. Wait 400ms to 600ms. The third attempt succeeds.

The jitter is critical. Without jitter, 50 services that all depend on the same database all retry at exactly the same time — the thundering herd problem. The database, already struggling, receives 50 simultaneous requests instead of one. With jitter, the retries spread across a window, giving the database time to recover.

### Step 4: The Circuit Breaker Opens

The inventory service is not just slow — it is failing. The past 100 calls had 60 failures (60% failure rate). The circuit breaker threshold is 50% over a 10-second sliding window. The circuit breaker opens.

When open, the circuit breaker rejects all calls to the inventory service immediately — no network call is made. The caller gets a `CircuitBreakerOpenException` in less than 1 millisecond instead of waiting 500ms for a timeout. This saves threads, CPU, and network bandwidth. The circuit stays open for 30 seconds, then transitions to half-open.

In the half-open state, the circuit breaker allows a single probe request. If it succeeds, the circuit closes and normal operation resumes. If it fails, the circuit opens again, this time for 60 seconds (doubled backoff at the circuit level).

### Step 5: The Fallback Activates

The order service has a fallback method: if the inventory service is unavailable, check the inventory from the cache. The cache has slightly stale data (updated 2 minutes ago). The order service responds with degraded information — it shows "Low stock: 3 remaining" instead of the exact real-time count.

### Step 6: The Bulkhead Isolates the Damage

The order service uses bulkheads — separate thread pools for each downstream dependency. The database thread pool has 10 threads. The inventory service thread pool has 5 threads. The payment service thread pool has 10 threads.

When the inventory service hangs, it only consumes the 5 inventory threads. The database and payment threads remain available. Without bulkheads, a single hung dependency can consume all 25 threads.

### Step 7: Health Checks and Recovery

The infrastructure runs health checks every 10 seconds against the inventory service. The health check endpoint (`GET /health`) returns 200 with a JSON body: `{"status": "ok"}`. The health check probes CPU usage, memory, connection pool utilization, and recent error rates.

When the inventory service recovers — the background job finishes, memory pressure drops — the health check passes again. The load balancer marks the instance as healthy. The circuit breaker probes succeed. Traffic resumes.

## Variants: Different Tools for Different Failures

### Timeouts and Retries

The most basic reliability tool. Every outbound call must have a timeout. Without a timeout, a slow dependency makes your service slow too. The timeout should be tight enough that users do not notice — typically 100ms to 500ms for internal calls, 1s to 5s for external API calls.

Retries must use exponential backoff with jitter. A common formula is `wait = min(cap, base * 2^attempt) + random(0, jitter)`. For base=100ms, cap=10s: attempt 0 waits ~100ms, attempt 1 waits ~200ms, attempt 2 waits ~400ms, attempt 3 waits ~800ms, attempt 9 waits 10.2s (capped).

Idempotency token — generate a unique key (UUID v4) for each operation. The server checks if it has already processed this key. If yes, return the cached result. If no, process and store the result keyed by the idempotency token.

### Circuit Breakers

Circuit breakers protect downstream services from being overwhelmed by failing callers. They also protect callers from wasting resources on failing downstreams.

Three parameters configure a circuit breaker:
- **Failure threshold**: percentage (50%) or count (10 failures) in a sliding window.
- **Window size**: the duration of the sliding window (10 seconds, 60 seconds).
- **Open duration**: how long the circuit stays open before transitioning to half-open (30 seconds, 60 seconds).

Some implementations support a ring buffer per state: a fixed-size circular buffer of recent call outcomes. When the buffer is full, the oldest result is evicted. The failure rate is calculated over the buffer contents. This avoids time-based window management.

### Bulkheads

Bulkheads isolate system components so a failure in one does not cascade to others. Named after ship hulls — a breach in one compartment floods only that compartment.

In software, bulkheads take several forms:
- **Thread pool isolation**: separate thread pools per dependency, per client, or per operation type.
- **Connection pool isolation**: separate database connection pools per query type (read vs write) or per tenant.
- **Process isolation**: run different services or different tenants in separate processes or containers.
- **Resource quotas**: limit CPU, memory, or IOPS per tenant or per operation.

### Graceful Degradation

When a dependency fails, the system should degrade rather than fail entirely. Strategies include:

- **Cache fallback**: serve stale cached data when the database is slow.
- **Default values**: return safe defaults when real values are unavailable (show "In stock" instead of "Error checking inventory").
- **Feature degradation**: disable non-critical features when under load. Stop showing recommendations; keep search working.
- **Request sampling**: under extreme load, randomly drop a percentage of requests instead of failing all of them.

## Mental Model: Five Questions for Every System

When you design or analyze a system for reliability, ask these questions:

**1. What does "correct" mean for this system?** A payment system and a video streaming service define correctness differently. For payments, correctness means every transaction settles exactly once — no duplicates, no omissions. For video streaming, correctness means every frame renders within 30ms of the playhead. Define your correctness criteria before you design reliability mechanisms.

**2. What are the failure modes?** List every way each component can fail. Disk full? Network partition? Memory exhausted? Process killed by OOM killer? Dependency returns garbage data? Dependency hangs? Dependency returns valid data that violates constraints? Each failure mode requires a different mitigation.

**3. What happens when a retry makes things worse?** Retries amplify load on already-stressed systems. Without circuit breakers, a retry storm can kill a service that was merely slow. With circuit breakers, retries stop before they do damage. Ask: what limits the maximum amplification factor of my retry strategy?

**4. Can the system degrade gracefully?** What is the fallback for every critical dependency? If the recommendation engine is down, show popular items. If the database is unreachable, serve from cache. If the cache is empty, serve static defaults. Every dependency needs a survivable fallback.

**5. How do you know the system is reliable?** Monitoring tells you if your reliability mechanisms are working. Track retry rates, circuit breaker state changes, fallback activations, and timeout rates. Alert on anything that exceeds your reliability budget. Without observability, reliability is hope.

These five questions apply at every scale — from a two-service startup to a 200-service enterprise. The answers change as the system grows, but the questions remain the same.

## Reliability Metrics: What to Measure

You cannot improve reliability you do not measure. These metrics form the minimum viable reliability dashboard:

**Error rate.** The percentage of requests that result in an error (4xx or 5xx). Track per endpoint, per service, and per dependency. Alert when error rate exceeds 1% over 5 minutes.

**Latency percentiles.** p50, p95, p99 response time. A rising p99 signals a degrading dependency before failures occur. Track per operation. Alert when p99 exceeds 2x the baseline.

**Availability.** The percentage of time the service is operational and reachable. Calculated over rolling windows: 24 hours, 7 days, 30 days. Compare against the SLA target.

**Retry rate.** The percentage of requests that require at least one retry. A rising retry rate signals increasing failure frequency. Track per dependency. Alert when retry rate exceeds 5%.

**Circuit breaker state changes.** Count of state transitions (closed-to-open, open-to-half-open, half-open-to-closed). A circuit breaker that opens and closes rapidly (oscillation) indicates a threshold that is too tight.

**Fallback activation rate.** The percentage of requests served by a fallback rather than the primary dependency. A rising fallback rate signals a degrading primary dependency.

**Bulkhead pool utilization.** The percentage of threads or permits in use. Track per pool. Alert when utilization exceeds 80%.

**Dead-letter queue depth.** The number of messages that have exceeded max_retries. A non-zero DLQ indicates errors that retries cannot fix. Investigate every DLQ message.

**Reliability budget consumption.** Track the number of nines consumed against the annual budget. If the budget is 99.99% (52.56 minutes downtime per year) and you have already used 40 minutes in Q1, you are on track to exceed the budget.

## Reliability by System Type

Different systems prioritize different reliability mechanisms:

**Payment systems.** Correctness over availability. Use idempotency keys, transactional outbox, reconciliation jobs. Circuit breaker threshold: 30% — payment failures are serious. Fallback to secondary provider with different infrastructure.

**Real-time communication (chat, WebRTC).** Availability over correctness. Dropped messages are retried; duplicate messages are tolerated. Circuit breaker threshold: 70% — tolerate more failures because real-time depends on availability. Fallback to offline delivery (email, push notification).

**Data pipelines (ETL, analytics).** Completeness over latency. Use queue-based retry with dead-letter queues. Reprocess DLQ on recovery. Circuit breaker threshold: 50% — moderate tolerance. Fallback: pause and resume from last checkpoint.

**Content delivery (CDN, streaming).** Latency over correctness. Use aggressive caching with stale-while-revalidate. Circuit breaker: immediate open on any failure (100% threshold). Fallback: serve from edge cache with stale content.

**Infrastructure control plane (Kubernetes, DNS).** Consistency over everything. Use quorum-based consensus (etcd, ZooKeeper). Byzantine fault tolerance for critical operations. No retry on consistency failures — fail fast and alert.

| System type | Primary concern | Key mechanisms | CB threshold |
|---|---|---|---|
| Payment | Correctness | Idempotency, outbox, reconciliation | 30% |
| Real-time chat | Availability | Queue retry, duplicate tolerance | 70% |
| Data pipeline | Completeness | DLQ, checkpoint, reprocess | 50% |
| CDN / Streaming | Latency | Stale cache, aggressive edge serving | 100% |
| Control plane | Consistency | Consensus, quorum, BFT | N/A |

## Measuring Reliability: SLIs, SLOs, and SLAs

An **SLI (Service Level Indicator)** is a raw measurement: request latency, error rate, throughput. An **SLO (Service Level Objective)** is a target for the SLI: 99.9% of requests complete in under 500ms. An **SLA (Service Level Agreement)** is the business commitment to the SLO, with penalties for violation.

For reliability, the key SLO is availability: the percentage of time the service produces correct responses. A 99.9% availability SLO means the service can be down for 8.76 hours per year. The SLO must be tighter than the SLA — if the SLA promises 99.9%, target 99.95% internally to absorb measurement errors and brief blips.

Track SLO compliance over a rolling 30-day window. If you have been at 99.9% for 20 days and then have a 1-hour outage, your 30-day availability drops from 99.9% to 99.86% — you are violating the SLO for the remainder of the month. This is called SLO burn rate: how fast you consume the error budget.

Error budget = 1 - SLO. For a 99.9% SLO, the error budget is 0.1% of requests. If you serve 1 million requests per day, your error budget is 1,000 errors per day. If you exhaust the error budget in the first week of the month, you freeze all feature deployments for the rest of the month.

Error budget policies create accountability. The team knows that every deployment that increases error rates consumes budget that could have been used for future deployments. This aligns development velocity with reliability.

## Common Failure Scenarios and Mitigations

**Database connection pool exhaustion.** The database runs out of connections because too many services are connecting simultaneously. Mitigation: set a maximum connection pool size per service (10 connections). Monitor pool utilization. Scale the database or add a connection pool proxy (PgBouncer, RDS Proxy).

**Memory leak.** A slow memory leak consumes all available RAM over hours or days. The OOM killer terminates the process. Mitigation: set memory limits in the container or process. Restart the process periodically (every 24-48 hours). Monitor memory usage and alert on trend lines, not absolute values.

**Network partition.** Two services cannot communicate because a switch fails or a firewall rule changes. Mitigation: circuit breakers on both sides prevent thread exhaustion. Health check endpoints on separate network paths. Redundant network interfaces and paths.

**Cascading failure.** Service A fails. Service B depends on A. Service B's calls to A timeout, consuming B's thread pool. Service C depends on B. Service C's calls to B timeout, consuming C's thread pool. The failure propagates up the dependency chain. Mitigation: circuit breakers at each dependency boundary. Bulkheads to isolate dependencies. Timeout hierarchy to ensure Service B's timeout fires before Service C's timeout.

**Retry storm.** A brief failure triggers retries from 100 clients simultaneously. The retries amplify the load on the already-stressed service. The service collapses. Mitigation: jitter in backoff ensures retries are evenly distributed. Retry budget limits total retry volume. Circuit breaker stops all calls when failure rate exceeds threshold.

**Configuration error.** A human operator changes a circuit breaker configuration from "threshold 50%" to "threshold 5%". The circuit breaker now opens on normal traffic fluctuations. The system goes down unnecessarily. Mitigation: configuration changes are reviewed, tested in staging, and rolled out gradually. Automated validation rejects configurations that would cause >1% increase in error rate.
