## Tier 1 -- Foundation

### Challenge 1: Identify the Distributed Monolith (10 minutes)

**Scenario**: A team of 20 engineers operates 8 microservices. The services are deployed on Kubernetes, each with its own container image. Each service has its own CI/CD pipeline. The team reports that deployments require coordination across 3 services on average. A change to the Order Service frequently requires changes to the Payment Service and the Inventory Service. The team has a shared PostgreSQL database -- all 8 services connect to the same instance.

**Question**: Is this a microservices architecture or a distributed monolith? List the specific symptoms that justify your answer.

**Evaluation criteria**:
- Correctly identifies this as a distributed monolith (3 points)
- Identifies the shared database as the primary symptom (3 points)
- Identifies the need for coordinated deployments as a secondary symptom (2 points)
- Identifies the coupling across services as the root cause (2 points)

**Model answer**: This is a distributed monolith. The symptoms are: (1) all 8 services share a single PostgreSQL database, creating coupling at the data layer -- a schema change in one service requires coordination across all services that touch the same tables; (2) deployments require coordination across 3 services on average, which means the service boundaries do not align with change boundaries; (3) a change to the Order Service frequently requires changes to Payment and Inventory, which indicates the services are not independently deployable. The team has the operational complexity of microservices (Kubernetes, CI/CD pipelines, 8 deployable units) but none of the independence benefits.

### Challenge 2: Choose the First Service to Extract (10 minutes)

**Scenario**: A food delivery monolith handles restaurants, menus, orders, payments, delivery tracking, notifications, and reviews. The codebase is 300,000 lines of Python. The team has 25 engineers. The monolith deploys once per week. The deployment window is 4 hours.

**Question**: Which service should be extracted first? Justify your choice with specific reasoning about the extraction risk, the value of extraction, and the dependency profile.

**Evaluation criteria**:
- Identifies a low-risk, high-value candidate (3 points)
- Explains the dependency profile of the chosen service (3 points)
- Describes the extraction process (strangler fig pattern) (2 points)
- Mentions what makes other candidates worse choices (2 points)

**Model answer**: The notification service should be extracted first. It has the lowest risk profile: it has minimal shared state with other domains (it reads notification templates and user preferences, but does not write to shared business tables). It provides high value: offloading notification processing from the monolith reduces the monolith's compute load and enables the notification service to scale independently during peak hours. The extraction process: (1) identify the notification-related code in the monolith, (2) design the notification service API, (3) extract the code to a new repository, (4) deploy the notification service alongside the monolith, (5) dual-write notifications to both the monolith and the new service, (6) cut over traffic incrementally. The payment service is a worse first choice because financial data requires ACID transactions and regulatory compliance, making extraction riskier. The review service is a worse choice because it is heavily coupled to the order and user domains.

### Challenge 3: Design the Infrastructure Prerequisites (15 minutes)

**Scenario**: Your team has decided to adopt microservices. You have 6 months before the first service extraction. You need to establish the infrastructure that will support 10-15 services.

**Question**: List the minimum infrastructure components required before the first service is deployed. For each component, explain why it is essential and provide a specific technology choice.

**Evaluation criteria**:
- Covers containerization and orchestration (2 points)
- Covers service discovery (2 points)
- Covers observability: logging, metrics, tracing (3 points)
- Covers CI/CD pipeline (2 points)
- Covers inter-service communication (1 point)

**Model answer**: The minimum infrastructure components: (1) Containerization with Docker -- packages each service with its dependencies, ensures consistent environments across dev, staging, and production. (2) Container orchestration with Kubernetes -- manages deployment, scaling, service discovery, and self-healing. (3) Service discovery via Kubernetes DNS -- services find each other through DNS names like `payment-service.namespace.svc.cluster.local`. (4) Centralized logging with ELK stack (Elasticsearch, Logstash, Kibana) -- aggregates logs from all services, enables searching across services. (5) Metrics with Prometheus and Grafana -- collects per-service metrics (request rate, error rate, latency), enables dashboards and alerting. (6) Distributed tracing with Jaeger -- traces requests across service boundaries, identifies latency bottlenecks. (7) CI/CD pipeline with GitHub Actions and ArgoCD -- automated build, test, and deploy per service, supports blue/green or rolling deployments. (8) API Gateway (Kong) -- single entry point for client requests, handles authentication, rate limiting, and routing. (9) Message broker (Kafka) -- enables event-driven communication between services, decouples producers and consumers. (10) Configuration management with Kubernetes ConfigMaps and Secrets -- manages per-service configuration without code changes.

## Tier 2 -- Design

### Challenge 4: Design the Migration Plan (30 minutes)

**Scenario**: A ride-sharing company has a 2-million-line Rails monolith. The company has 200 engineers organized into 8 teams: Rider Experience, Driver Experience, Pricing, Payments, Maps, Safety, Operations, and Data. The monolith deploys twice per week with a 60-minute deployment window. The company is growing 30% quarter-over-quarter. The VP of Engineering wants a 12-month migration plan to microservices.

**Scale requirements**: 10 million active riders, 1 million active drivers, 50+ cities globally.

**Question**: Design the 12-month migration plan. Specify the services to extract, the order of extraction, the infrastructure investments, and the risk mitigation strategy. Address how the team structure will evolve.

**Rubric**:
- Identifies the right first service to extract (3 points)
- Proposes the strangler fig pattern (3 points)
- Addresses data migration strategy (3 points)
- Aligns service boundaries with team boundaries (3 points)
- Specifies infrastructure investments with timeline (3 points)
- Identifies and mitigates risks (3 points)
- Provides a realistic timeline (2 points)

**Model answer**: The 12-month plan follows the strangler fig pattern with 3 phases:

**Months 1-2: Infrastructure foundation.** Set up Kubernetes cluster, Istio service mesh, Jaeger tracing, ELK logging, Prometheus/Grafana metrics, and Kafka event bus. Create CI/CD pipeline templates for new services. No service extraction yet -- this is the foundation. Risk: infrastructure delays push the timeline. Mitigation: use managed Kubernetes (EKS) to reduce operational overhead.

**Months 3-4: Extract first service (Notifications).** The Notifications team already owns the notification domain. Extract the notification code into a standalone service with its own PostgreSQL database. Use dual-writes for 2 weeks, then cut over. The monolith shrinks by 5%. Risk: data migration issues. Mitigation: run both systems in parallel and compare notification counts.

**Months 5-6: Extract Pricing and Maps.** These domains have clear boundaries and are owned by dedicated teams. The Pricing Service owns fare calculation algorithms. The Maps Service owns route optimization and ETA calculation. Both services are stateless -- no database extraction needed. The monolith shrinks by 20%.

**Months 7-9: Extract Payments.** This is the highest-risk extraction because it involves financial data. The Payments Service is extracted with its own database. The extraction uses the expand-migrate-contract pattern for database tables. A compliance officer reviews every data flow. The monolith shrinks by 15%.

**Months 10-12: Extract Rider Experience and Driver Experience.** These are the largest domains. Each is split into 3-4 services: User Profile, Ride Matching, Ride History, Notifications (for Rider), and Driver Onboarding, Earnings, Trip Management (for Driver). The monolith shrinks to 40% of its original size. Remaining domains (Safety, Operations, Data) are scheduled for the next 12-month phase.

The team structure evolves with the architecture. Each team becomes a service team. The infrastructure team grows from 3 to 8 engineers to support the platform. A dedicated SRE team handles on-call rotation.

### Challenge 5: Design the Data Consistency Strategy (30 minutes)

**Scenario**: A payment processing platform handles 100,000 transactions per day. The system is being decomposed into microservices: Payment Service (processes payments), Fraud Service (detects fraud), Ledger Service (maintains accounting records), Notification Service (sends receipts), and Reconciliation Service (matches payments to bank statements).

**Scale requirements**: 5 million users, 100,000 transactions/day, financial-grade consistency (no money lost), regulatory compliance (audit trail for every transaction).

**Question**: Design the data consistency strategy across these services. How do you ensure that money is never lost despite eventual consistency across service boundaries? How do you detect and recover from inconsistencies?

**Rubric**:
- Uses the saga pattern for distributed transactions (3 points)
- Implements the outbox pattern for reliable event publishing (3 points)
- Designs reconciliation jobs to detect inconsistencies (3 points)
- Provides compensating actions for each transaction step (3 points)
- Addresses audit trail requirements (3 points)

**Model answer**: The consistency strategy combines the saga pattern, outbox pattern, and reconciliation jobs:

**Payment flow as a saga:**
1. Payment Service: authorize payment (hold funds). If fail, end.
2. Fraud Service: run fraud check. If flagged, compensate: release hold.
3. Ledger Service: record debit entry. If fail, compensate: release hold + notify fraud.
4. Notification Service: send receipt. If fail, no compensation needed (receipt can be retried).
5. Payment Service: capture payment (finalize). If fail, compensate: release hold, record failed transaction.

**Outbox pattern**: Each service writes state changes and events in a single database transaction. A background process (Transaction Outbox Relayer) reads pending events from the outbox table and publishes them to Kafka. This ensures at-least-once delivery.

**Reconciliation jobs**: A nightly reconciliation job compares transaction records across services. It queries the Payment Service's "completed payments" against the Ledger Service's "recorded entries." Mismatches are flagged for manual review. The reconciliation query runs in batch mode: `SELECT p.id, p.status, l.entry_id FROM payments p LEFT JOIN ledger_entries l ON p.id = l.payment_id WHERE l.entry_id IS NULL` -- this finds payments that were processed but not recorded in the ledger.

**Compensating actions**: Each service defines a compensating action for every state-changing operation. The Payment Service's compensate for `authorize_payment` is `release_hold`. The Ledger Service's compensate for `record_debit` is `record_credit`. Compensating actions are idempotent -- they can be retried safely.

**Audit trail**: Every state change is logged to an append-only audit log. The log includes: transaction ID, service name, operation, timestamp, before-state, after-state, and user ID. The audit log is immutable and stored in a separate database (or S3) for regulatory compliance.

### Challenge 6: Design the Observability System (30 minutes)

**Scenario**: A social media platform has 30 microservices. The team struggles to debug production issues. A single user request can span 8 services. The team has no distributed tracing, no centralized logging, and no metrics dashboards. On-call engineers spend 3 hours per incident just identifying which service is failing.

**Scale requirements**: 50 million DAU, 500,000 requests/second at peak, 30 services, 5 regions.

**Question**: Design the observability system. Specify the three pillars (logging, metrics, tracing), the data flow from service to dashboard, and the alerting strategy. Address the cost of storing observability data at this scale.

**Rubric**:
- Covers all three observability pillars (3 points)
- Designs the data pipeline (collection, processing, storage, visualization) (3 points)
- Specifies key metrics and alerting rules (3 points)
- Addresses cost management (3 points)
- Provides a realistic implementation plan (3 points)

**Model answer**: The observability system is structured in three layers:

**Logging**: Each service writes structured JSON logs to stdout. A Fluentd daemon running as a DaemonSet on each Kubernetes node collects logs, adds metadata (pod name, namespace, cluster), and batches them to Elasticsearch. The ELK stack (Elasticsearch for storage, Kibana for visualization) provides log search and aggregation. Log retention: 7 days for all logs, 30 days for error logs, 90 days for audit logs. Cost: approximately $2,000/month for 1 TB/day of log data at 50M DAU.

**Metrics**: Each service exposes a `/metrics` endpoint using the Prometheus client library. Prometheus scrapes metrics every 15 seconds from each service instance. Key metrics per service: `http_requests_total` (counter, by status code, method, path), `http_request_duration_seconds` (histogram, p50/p99/p999), `service_errors_total` (counter, by error type), `db_queries_total` (counter, by query type), `db_query_duration_seconds` (histogram), `queue_depth` (gauge for Kafka consumers). Grafana dashboards aggregate metrics into views: service-level, domain-level, and system-level. Alerting rules: service error rate exceeds 1% for 5 minutes, p99 latency exceeds 500ms for 5 minutes, queue depth exceeds 10,000 for 2 minutes.

**Tracing**: Each service propagates trace context through HTTP headers (`traceparent`, `tracestate` per W3C Trace Context standard) and Kafka message headers. Jaeger receives sampled traces (1% of all requests, 100% of error requests). Traces show end-to-end request flow with per-span timing. The trace view for a single request shows: API Gateway -> Auth Service -> Feed Service -> Post Service -> Media Service -> Notification Service, with each span duration.

**Alerting strategy**: Alert fatigue is prevented by tiered alerting. P0 alerts (page on-call): error rate above 5%, p99 above 2 seconds, service down. P1 alerts (notify during business hours): error rate above 1%, p99 above 500ms, queue backlog. P2 alerts (create ticket): latency increased 20% week-over-week, error rate trending up. All alerts route through PagerDuty with a 15-minute escalation if unacknowledged.

**Cost management**: Sampling reduces tracing storage costs. Dynamic sampling: 100% of error traces, 1% of successful traces. Log retention policies: hot storage (Elasticsearch with 7 days), warm storage (S3 with 30 days), cold storage (Glacier with 90 days). Metrics are aggregated (roll up 1-second data to 1-minute averages after 24 hours) to reduce Prometheus storage requirements.

## Tier 3 -- Expert

### Challenge 7: Design for Multi-Region, Multi-Cloud (45 minutes)

**Scenario**: A global ride-sharing company operates in 200 cities across 30 countries. The company is currently running on a monolith deployed in a single AWS region (us-east-1). Users in Asia experience 300ms latency. The company wants to expand to 500 cities within 2 years. Regulatory requirements in the EU, India, and Brazil require data to be stored locally.

**Question**: Design the migration from a single-region monolith to a multi-region, multi-cloud microservices architecture. Address the following: service decomposition strategy, data residency compliance, multi-region deployment topology, cross-region data synchronization, disaster recovery, and the migration timeline. Consider that the company has 500 engineers and 10 regional teams.

**Multiple valid approaches**:
- **Approach A: Regional deployment with global control plane.** Each region runs its own instance of all services. A global control plane manages service discovery, configuration, and deployment across regions. Data stays within the region. Cross-region data is synchronized through a global event bus. This approach provides strong data sovereignty and independent regional operations.
- **Approach B: Global services + regional data services.** Compute services are global (run in 3-4 regions, serve traffic globally). Data services are per-region (each region stores its own data). Regulatory compliance is handled by routing user data to the appropriate region. This approach is more cost-efficient but requires careful data routing.
- **Approach C: Federated architecture.** Each region operates as an independent deployment with its own services, databases, and infrastructure. A global routing layer (DNS + API gateway) routes users to their nearest region. Data never leaves the region. Cross-region features (e.g., user traveling from one region to another) use a cross-region user lookup service. This approach provides the strongest isolation and compliance guarantees.

**Evaluation criteria**:
- Addresses data residency requirements explicitly (3 points)
- Designs the multi-region topology with specific components (3 points)
- Provides a realistic migration timeline (3 points)
- Handles disaster recovery across regions (3 points)
- Considers cost and operational complexity (3 points)

**Sample approach (Approach A - Regional deployment with global control plane):**

The migration spans 18 months in 3 phases:

**Months 1-6: Infrastructure and foundation.** Set up Kubernetes clusters in 3 regions (US-East, EU-West, AP-Southeast). Deploy the global control plane: a global service registry (Consul with WAN federation), a global configuration system (etcd with cross-region replication), and a global event bus (Kafka MirrorMaker for cross-region event replication). Establish CI/CD pipelines per region with ArgoCD.

**Months 7-12: Service extraction and regional deployment.** Extract the first set of services (Notifications, Pricing, Maps) and deploy them to all 3 regions. Each region runs its own instance of each service. Data stays within the region. The global control plane manages deployment and configuration. Cross-region data synchronization uses Kafka MirrorMaker with 30-second replication lag.

**Months 13-18: Data residency compliance and full migration.** Implement data residency by routing user data to the appropriate region based on regulatory requirements. EU users' data stays in EU-West. Indian users' data stays in AP-Southeast. A global user lookup service (with a small, replicated database) maps user IDs to their home region. The monolith is gradually decommissioned, region by region, as each region's services reach full feature parity.

**Disaster recovery**: Each region operates independently. If a region fails, the global load balancer routes traffic to the nearest healthy region. The failed region's data is recovered from the last known good state. The recovery point objective (RPO) is 30 seconds (asynchronous replication lag). The recovery time objective (RTO) is 5 minutes (DNS propagation + load balancer health check).

**Cost considerations**: Multi-region deployment approximately doubles infrastructure costs. Each region needs its own Kubernetes cluster, database instances, and supporting infrastructure. The trade-off is acceptable for global latency improvement (from 300ms to under 50ms for users in Asia) and regulatory compliance.

### Challenge 8: Design the Organization and Architecture Co-Evolution (45 minutes)

**Scenario**: A 50-person startup has grown from 10 to 50 engineers in 18 months. The codebase is a 500,000-line monolith. The team is organized into 5 functional groups: Frontend, Backend, Data, DevOps, and QA. The monolith is slowing down feature delivery. The CTO wants to transition to microservices but is concerned about the organizational disruption.

**Question**: Design a 24-month plan for co-evolving the organization structure and the software architecture. The plan must address: how to reorganize teams into service-oriented squads, how to sequence the service extraction, how to handle the transition period when both monolith and services coexist, how to manage the cultural shift to "you build it, you run it," and how to measure success.

**Multiple valid approaches**:
- **Approach A: Gradual reorganization with domain discovery.** Start with a 3-month domain discovery phase where teams analyze the monolith and identify bounded contexts. Reorganize teams around domains before extracting services. Extract the first service after 6 months. Full transition in 24 months.
- **Approach B: Service extraction drives reorganization.** Extract the first service (Notifications) with a cross-functional team pulled from the existing groups. The team continues to own the service after extraction. As more services are extracted, more teams form around them. The org structure evolves naturally with the architecture.
- **Approach C: Strike team model.** A dedicated "platform team" extracts services and builds the infrastructure. Domain teams are formed later. This approach separates the extraction work from the feature work, reducing disruption but creating a temporary "us vs them" dynamic.

**Evaluation criteria**:
- Addresses both organizational and technical dimensions (3 points)
- Acknowledges cultural challenges (3 points)
- Provides a realistic timeline with milestones (3 points)
- Includes metrics for measuring success (3 points)
- Anticipates resistance and provides mitigation strategies (3 points)

**Sample approach (Approach B - Service extraction drives reorganization):**

The 24-month plan is structured in 4 phases, each with organizational and technical milestones:

**Months 1-3: Discovery and preparation.** The CTO forms a 3-person architecture team that analyzes the monolith codebase to identify bounded contexts. The team produces a domain map showing the 8-10 bounded contexts and their dependencies. The organization is reorganized from 5 functional groups (Frontend, Backend, Data, DevOps, QA) into 3 cross-functional domain teams: Customer Experience (owning user-facing features), Operations (owning orders, delivery, payments), and Data (owning analytics, reporting). Each team has 12-15 engineers including frontend, backend, and QA. DevOps is distributed across teams. The transition is announced at an all-hands meeting with a clear rationale: faster feature delivery, reduced coordination overhead, and clearer ownership.

**Months 4-9: First service extraction and team formation.** The Operations team extracts the Notifications Service. A 6-person strike team within Operations handles the extraction. The remaining engineers continue feature work on the monolith. The Notifications Service is deployed to production after 8 weeks. The strike team becomes the permanent Notifications Service team. The Operations team now has 2 sub-teams: Monolith Operations and Notifications Service. The extraction is celebrated internally as a success, building momentum for the next phase.

**Months 10-18: Bulk extraction.** The pattern repeats: a strike team forms, extracts a service, and becomes the permanent service team. Extraction order: Notifications, Pricing, Maps, Payments, Order Management, User Management. Each extraction takes 6-8 weeks. The organization evolves: the Customer Experience team splits into 3 squads (User, Content, Social). The Operations team splits into 4 squads (Pricing, Maps, Payments, Orders). A new Platform team of 8 engineers forms to maintain the Kubernetes infrastructure, service mesh, and observability stack. The monolith shrinks to 40% of its original size.

**Months 19-24: Monolith retirement and optimization.** The remaining monolith modules (legacy reporting, admin panel, background jobs) are the last to be extracted. The monolith is replaced by 8 microservices owned by 7 squads. The Platform team grows to 12 engineers. Squads fully own their services end-to-end: they build, deploy, monitor, and are on-call for their services. Deployment frequency increases from once per week to 3 times per day per squad.

**Measuring success:**
- Deployment frequency: from 1/week to 21+/week (3/day x 7 squads)
- Lead time for changes: from 4 hours to 30 minutes
- Mean time to recovery (MTTR): from 2 hours to 20 minutes
- Change failure rate: from 15% to 5%
- Developer satisfaction: measured via quarterly survey (target: 20% improvement)
- Feature delivery velocity: features shipped per engineer per quarter (target: 2x improvement)

**Cultural challenges and mitigations:**
- **Resistance to change**: addressed by starting with a low-risk service (Notifications) and demonstrating success before scaling
- **"You build it, you run it" anxiety**: addressed by providing a 2-week ramp-up period with shadow on-call and comprehensive runbooks
- **Loss of shared context**: addressed by weekly cross-squad demo sessions and a shared architecture wiki
- **Silo formation**: addressed by rotating engineers between squads every 6 months and maintaining a shared code review culture across squads
