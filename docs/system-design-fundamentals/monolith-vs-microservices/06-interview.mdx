## How This Topic Appears

Monolith vs microservices questions appear in system design interviews at every level. The topic is a gateway -- it tests your ability to make architectural trade-offs, not your knowledge of a specific technology. Here are the most common question phrasings, organized by category.

### Architecture Design Questions

1. "Design a video streaming platform like YouTube. Would you use a monolith or microservices? Walk through your reasoning."
2. "Design an e-commerce checkout system. How would you handle the transition from a monolith to microservices, and what would you extract first?"
3. "Design a ride-sharing application. How would you decompose it into services? Show me the service boundaries and data ownership."
4. "Design a real-time chat application. When would a monolith make sense, and when would you split into services?"

### Migration Strategy Questions

5. "Your team has a 500,000-line Rails monolith for a food delivery platform. The codebase is slowing down the team. How would you break it into microservices?"
6. "A startup raised Series B and is hiring 50 engineers. The monolith needs to be decomposed. What is your migration plan? What is the first service you extract, and why?"
7. "Your e-commerce platform had a bad outage -- a change to the search feature broke the checkout flow. The CEO wants microservices. How do you respond?"

### Evaluation and Comparison Questions

8. "Compare monolith and microservices for a social media analytics platform. Which do you recommend and why?"
9. "What are the essential infrastructure components you need before running microservices in production?"
10. "Your team adopted microservices but deployment frequency went down, not up. What went wrong? How do you fix it?"
11. "When is a distributed monolith worse than a plain monolith?"

### Scenario-Based Questions

12. "Your microservices architecture has 15 services. A user request triggers 8 internal calls. Latency p99 is 3 seconds. How do you debug and fix this?"
13. "A junior engineer proposes splitting the User Service into 'UserReadService' and 'UserWriteService.' What do you tell them?"
14. "Your team is building a new product from scratch. You have 6 engineers. Should you use microservices?"

## What the Interviewer Is Actually Testing

When an interviewer asks about monolith vs microservices, they are evaluating five dimensions of your engineering judgment:

**1. Trade-off awareness.** Do you understand that every architecture decision involves trade-offs? Can you articulate the specific costs of each choice? The candidate who says "microservices are always better" fails this dimension. The candidate who says "a monolith is simpler, but here are the conditions where it breaks" passes.

**2. Pragmatic judgment.** Can you match the architecture to the context? A 6-person startup should not use microservices. A 200-person company should not use a monolith. The interviewer wants to see that you consider team size, codebase size, operational maturity, and business requirements.

**3. Operational depth.** Do you understand what it takes to run microservices in production? The candidate who mentions service mesh, circuit breakers, distributed tracing, and the saga pattern demonstrates operational depth. The candidate who only mentions "services communicate over HTTP" does not.

**4. Migration experience.** Have you been through a decomposition? The candidate who can describe the strangler fig pattern, the first service to extract, and the common pitfalls (database coupling, chatty services, distributed monolith) demonstrates real experience. The candidate who describes a big-bang rewrite has not done this before.

**5. Systems thinking.** Can you trace the implications of an architectural choice through the entire system lifecycle? From code organization to deployment frequency to on-call rotation to team structure? The interviewer wants to see that you think about architecture as a socio-technical system, not just a technical diagram.

## Level-by-Level Expectations

### L3 / L4 (Junior / Mid-Level)

**Expectation**: Can describe the basic differences between monolith and microservices. Knows the pros and cons. Can recommend one over the other for a given scenario.

**Sample answer for "Should we use microservices for a new startup?":**
"A startup with 6 engineers should start with a monolith. Microservices add operational complexity: you need container orchestration, service discovery, distributed tracing, and CI/CD pipelines. A monolith lets you move fast, deploy quickly, and debug easily. The key is to build a well-structured modular monolith with clear internal boundaries. When the team grows to 15+ engineers and the codebase passes 100,000 lines, you can extract services using the strangler fig pattern. Starting with microservices at a startup would slow you down with infrastructure management instead of building product features."

**Why this works**: It shows awareness of trade-offs, considers team size, and demonstrates knowledge of the modular monolith and strangler fig pattern.

### L5 (Senior)

**Expectation**: Can design a migration strategy. Can identify the first service to extract. Can describe the infrastructure needed for microservices. Can discuss data migration strategies.

**Sample answer for "How would you decompose a 500K-line monolith?":**
"I would use the strangler fig pattern. The first service to extract is usually the one with the clearest boundary and least shared dependencies. For a food delivery platform, that is often the notification service -- it has minimal shared state, can be extracted quickly, and provides immediate value by offloading work from the monolith.

The extraction follows this sequence: identify the bounded context, design the service API, extract the code to a new repository, deploy the service alongside the monolith, migrate the database tables using dual-writes, and cut over traffic incrementally. I would start with a 10% traffic cut and monitor error rates and latency for 48 hours before increasing to 50%, then 100%.

The critical infrastructure before starting: container orchestration (Kubernetes), service mesh (Istio for mTLS and traffic splitting), distributed tracing (Jaeger), and a message broker (Kafka) for event-driven communication. Without these, the extracted services will be harder to operate than the monolith."

**Why this works**: It demonstrates migration experience, specific tooling knowledge, and a cautious, data-driven approach to cutting over traffic.

### L6 (Staff)

**Expectation**: Can discuss organizational implications. Can design service boundaries aligned with team structures. Can anticipate and prevent common failure modes. Can reason about cost and resource allocation.

**Sample answer for "Design a video platform. How do you decompose into services?":**
"I would start by understanding the team structure. Conway's Law means the service boundaries must align with team boundaries. If the organization has teams for content ingestion, playback, personalization, and monetization, those are the service boundaries.

The decomposition would produce approximately 8-10 services: Content Ingestion, Transcoding, Content Catalog, Playback, Recommendation, Search, User Management, Billing, Analytics, and Notification. Each service owns its database. The catalog service might use PostgreSQL with proper indexing. The recommendation service would use a graph database for relationship modeling. The analytics service would use a column store for time-series data.

The critical failure mode to prevent is the distributed monolith. I would enforce strict service boundaries: no shared databases, no shared caches, no synchronous dependency chains deeper than 3 levels. Every service-to-service call must have a timeout, a circuit breaker, and a fallback. The API gateway enforces these policies at the edge.

For cost efficiency, I would size services inversely to their expected load variance. The recommendation service, which needs to scale during peak hours, would be containerized with horizontal pod autoscaling. The catalog service, which has stable load, would run on reserved instances for 40% cost savings."

**Why this works**: It demonstrates organizational awareness, considers multiple data store types, anticipates failure modes, and shows cost awareness.

### L7 / L8 (Principal / Distinguished)

**Expectation**: Can design for global scale. Can discuss multi-region deployment, data sovereignty, and regulatory compliance. Can design a migration strategy for a company with 2,000+ engineers. Can discuss the evolution of the architecture over a 5-year horizon.

**Sample answer for "Design a global payment platform. How do you handle the monolith-to-microservices transition?":**
"A global payment platform has unique constraints: regulatory compliance varies by region, financial transactions require strong consistency, and latency must be under 200ms for checkout flows. The architecture must be multi-region with data residency guarantees.

The service decomposition would be driven by regulatory boundaries, not just technical ones. Payment processing in the EU requires GDPR-compliant data handling. In India, payments must comply with RBI guidelines. Each region would have its own instance of the payment processing service, hosted in that region, with data stored locally.

The migration strategy for a 2,000+ engineer organization follows a domain-by-domain approach. I would identify 5-6 major domains (payment processing, fraud detection, user management, merchant management, reporting, reconciliation) and create a migration team for each. Each team has 6 months to extract their domain from the monolith. The teams work independently but share infrastructure: the same service mesh, the same observability stack, the same CI/CD platform.

The 5-year architecture vision: the monolith is fully decomposed into 50-80 services organized by domain. A global event bus connects regional deployments. The API gateway handles routing, rate limiting, and compliance checks. A data lake aggregates events from all services for analytics and ML training. The infrastructure is fully automated: infrastructure-as-code, policy-as-code, and automated compliance auditing.

The key risk is regulatory: moving financial data across service boundaries requires careful data governance. Each service's data schema must be auditable. Each data flow must be documented. The migration team includes a compliance officer who reviews every service boundary for regulatory impact."

## The FAANG Framework for Answering

Use the **TRADE** framework to structure your answer to any monolith vs microservices question:

**T - Trade-offs**: Start by stating the fundamental trade-off. "Monoliths are simpler but create coupling. Microservices are flexible but add operational complexity. The choice depends on team size, codebase maturity, and organizational structure."

**R - Requirements**: Clarify the requirements. "What is the current team size? How many engineers will own this system? What is the expected user scale? What is the regulatory environment? Are there latency requirements?" This shows you do not jump to conclusions without understanding context.

**A - Architecture**: Propose the architecture. If monolith, specify the internal structure (modular monolith with clear interfaces). If microservices, list the services, their boundaries, and their data ownership. "I propose 6 services: User, Content, Payment, Notification, Analytics, and Search. Each owns its database. Communication is event-driven through Kafka."

**D - Decision points**: Walk through the critical decisions. "The first extraction is Notification -- it has the clearest boundary. Communication will be event-driven, not synchronous, to avoid tight coupling. The database migration follows the expand-migrate-contract pattern. The infrastructure investment includes Kubernetes, Istio, and Jaeger."

**E - Evaluation**: Evaluate the proposal. "This architecture supports 10 million DAU with 6 services. The bottleneck will be the Content Service, which can be scaled independently to 50 instances. The risk is data consistency across services -- mitigated by the saga pattern and reconciliation jobs. The organizational alignment is good: 6 teams match 6 services."

## Phrase Bank

Use these phrases to sound experienced and precise during the interview:

1. "The fundamental trade-off is simplicity versus flexibility. A monolith is simpler to build, deploy, and debug. Microservices give you independent scaling, team autonomy, and faster deploys -- at the cost of operational complexity."

2. "I would start with a modular monolith. Clear internal interfaces, separate modules, but a single deployment. This preserves the option to extract services later."

3. "The strangler fig pattern is the only safe migration strategy. Extract one service at a time, route traffic incrementally, keep the monolith running until zero traffic remains."

4. "The first service to extract is the one with the clearest bounded context and the least shared dependencies. Typically notifications or authentication."

5. "Service boundaries must align with team boundaries. Conway's Law is not optional -- teams will communicate the way their services communicate."

6. "A distributed monolith is the worst outcome: all the complexity of microservices with none of the independence. Symptoms include shared databases, synchronized deployments, and cross-service feature branches."

7. "Database-per-service is non-negotiable for microservices. A shared database creates coupling that undermines independent deployability."

8. "Event-driven communication reduces coupling. Services emit events; other services react. No service needs to know about the others."

9. "The saga pattern replaces distributed transactions. Each step has a compensating action. If step 3 fails, the system executes compensating actions for steps 1 and 2."

10. "Observability is not optional. You need distributed tracing, centralized logging, and metrics dashboards before you extract your first service."

11. "Circuit breakers prevent cascading failures. If a downstream service is slow, the circuit breaker trips and rejects requests quickly instead of letting them pile up."

12. "The API gateway handles cross-cutting concerns: authentication, rate limiting, routing, and aggregation. Clients talk to one endpoint instead of many."

13. "Service mesh moves inter-service communication concerns to the infrastructure layer. Retries, timeouts, mTLS, and traffic splitting are handled by sidecar proxies, not application code."

14. "The outbox pattern ensures reliable event publishing. Write the event to the database in the same transaction as the state change. A background process publishes pending events."

15. "You cannot bolt microservices onto a team that is not organized for it. The organizational structure must support autonomous teams with end-to-end service ownership."

## Red Flags

Avoid these mistakes in your interview answer. Each one signals inexperience or poor judgment.

**Red Flag 1: "Microservices are always better."** This shows you do not understand trade-offs. The best answer considers the specific context and acknowledges that a monolith is often the right starting point.

**Red Flag 2: "We can do a big-bang rewrite."** This shows you have never done a migration. Big-bang rewrites fail 90% of the time. The strangler fig pattern is the only proven approach.

**Red Flag 3: Ignoring data migration.** "We just extract the code and deploy it separately" -- without addressing the database. Data migration is the hardest part of service extraction. Ignoring it signals inexperience.

**Red Flag 4: "All services should use REST."** Not understanding the role of event-driven communication, gRPC for internal calls, and message queues for async workflows. Different communication patterns serve different purposes.

**Red Flag 5: No mention of infrastructure.** Proposing microservices without mentioning Kubernetes, service mesh, CI/CD, or observability. The infrastructure is the hardest part of running microservices.

**Red Flag 6: "We need 100 services."** Suggesting a large number of services without justification. The trend is toward fewer, larger services. Start with 5-10, extract more as needed.

**Red Flag 7: Ignoring team structure.** Proposing an architecture that does not match the team organization. Conway's Law will assert itself regardless of the architecture diagram.

**Red Flag 8: "We can use a shared database -- it is simpler."** This eliminates the primary benefit of microservices. A shared database creates coupling, coordination overhead, and contention. It is a distributed monolith.

## Practice Drills

### Drill 1: The Migration Pitch (5 minutes)

**Prompt**: "Your CEO has read that Amazon uses microservices and wants you to decompose the 200-person startup's monolith immediately. You have 5 minutes to persuade them of the right approach. What do you say?"

**What to cover**: Explain the strangler fig pattern, the need for infrastructure investment, the 6-12 month timeline, the first service to extract, and the risks of doing it too fast. Emphasize that the goal is faster feature delivery, not architectural purity.

### Drill 2: The Failure Postmortem (5 minutes)

**Prompt**: "Your team extracted 15 microservices over 6 months. Deployment frequency dropped from 10 per week to 2 per week. Engineers are burned out. What went wrong? What do you fix first?"

**What to cover**: Diagnosis: likely a distributed monolith (shared database, chatty services, or poorly aligned boundaries). Fix: merge services back, extract data ownership, introduce event-driven communication, and invest in CI/CD pipelines. The immediate fix: stop extracting and stabilize the current architecture.

### Drill 3: The Greenfield Decision (5 minutes)

**Prompt**: "A new product team of 8 engineers is building a SaaS analytics platform. They want to use microservices because they plan to scale to 1 million users within a year. Do you agree?"

**What to cover**: Recommend a modular monolith. The team of 8 is too small for microservices. The operational overhead will slow them down. Scale can be handled by vertical scaling and read replicas in the short term. Plan to extract services when the team reaches 15+ engineers and the codebase crosses 100,000 lines. The modular monolith preserves the option to extract later.

### Additional Practice: Complete Sample Interview Walkthrough

**Sample question**: "Design a food delivery platform. Should it be a monolith or microservices?"

**Complete answer using the TRADE framework:**

**T - Trade-offs**: "The choice between monolith and microservices for a food delivery platform depends on team size, expected scale, and organizational structure. A monolith is simpler to build initially, but as the platform grows, the tight coupling between restaurants, orders, delivery tracking, and payments creates bottlenecks. Microservices offer independent scaling and team autonomy but require significant infrastructure investment."

**R - Requirements**: "Before deciding, I need to clarify a few things. How large is the engineering team today, and how fast is it growing? What is the expected user scale in the first 12 months? Are there multiple teams that would own different parts of the system? What is the deployment frequency target? For this discussion, let me assume a team of 20 engineers organized into 4 teams, targeting 1 million orders per month, with plans to double within a year."

**A - Architecture**: "I recommend starting with a modular monolith. The monolith has clear internal modules: Restaurant Management, Order Processing, Delivery Tracking, Payments, and Notifications. Each module has a well-defined interface. The monolith is deployed as a single unit on Kubernetes, allowing us to scale the entire application horizontally. Within 6 months, as the team grows and we understand the domain boundaries better, we will extract the first service."

**D - Decision points**: "The first extraction is Payments. It has the clearest bounded context, requires PCI compliance, and benefits from independent scaling during peak hours. The extraction uses the strangler fig pattern: build the Payment Service alongside the monolith, dual-write payments, and cut over incrementally over 4 weeks. The second extraction is Notifications, which has minimal shared state. Each extraction takes 4-6 weeks."

**E - Evaluation**: "This approach gives us the best of both worlds. We start fast with a monolith, avoid premature complexity, and extract services when the team and domain understanding are mature enough. The modular monolith structure ensures that extraction is straightforward -- each module is already a service candidate. The risk is that teams might skip the modular monolith step and create a tightly coupled codebase, making future extraction harder."

**Sample question**: "How do you handle database changes in a microservices architecture without downtime?"

**Complete answer**: "Database changes follow the expand-migrate-contract pattern. Phase 1, expand: add the new column or table alongside existing schema. Both old and new code paths work. Phase 2, migrate: deploy the updated service code that uses the new schema. Old code paths are deprecated but still present. Phase 3, contract: remove the old column or table once all instances are running the new code.

For example, splitting the `users` table into `users` and `user_profiles`: in phase 1, create the `user_profiles` table and a trigger that keeps it in sync with the profile columns in `users`. In phase 2, deploy code that reads from `user_profiles` and writes to both tables. In phase 3, drop the profile columns from `users`.

Large table migrations use chunked processing: `UPDATE videos SET new_format = format WHERE id BETWEEN 0 AND 10000` repeated for each chunk with a 1-second pause between chunks to avoid replication lag. The entire migration runs in the background without locking the table for extended periods."

**Sample question**: "A junior engineer says they want to use WebSockets for all inter-service communication because it is 'faster than HTTP.' How do you respond?"

**Complete answer**: "WebSockets are designed for persistent, bidirectional, real-time communication between a client and a server. They maintain an open TCP connection, which consumes memory on both ends. For inter-service communication, the connection count scales as: number of services x number of instances per service. With 20 services at 10 instances each, that is 200 persistent connections per service, or 20,000 total connections, counting both directions. Each connection consumes approximately 50 KB of kernel buffer memory, totaling 1 GB of memory just for connection overhead.

HTTP/1.1 with keep-alive and connection pooling reuses connections efficiently. gRPC uses HTTP/2 multiplexing, where a single TCP connection carries multiple concurrent requests. For inter-service communication, gRPC is typically the best choice: it uses protocol buffers for efficient serialization, supports streaming, and multiplexes requests over a single connection. WebSockets are better suited for client-facing real-time features like live delivery tracking or chat."

### Drill 4: The Service Boundary Debate (5 minutes)

**Prompt**: "Your team proposes splitting the User Service into four services: `UserReadService`, `UserWriteService`, `UserProfileService`, and `UserSettingsService`. Evaluate this proposal."

**What to cover**: This is nanoservices -- premature decomposition. The four services together own the same data and are tightly coupled. A single feature (update user profile) would require changes to all four. Merge them into one `UserService`. Extract only when the `UserService` grows too large for one team, and then extract by bounded context (profile, settings, authentication), not by CRUD operation.

### Drill 5: The Observability Gap (5 minutes)

**Prompt**: "A user reports that their video transcoding occasionally takes 5 minutes instead of 30 seconds. The Transcoding Service shows 100% CPU. The Video Upload Service shows 0 errors. The Kafka queue shows no backlog. Where do you look next?"

**What to cover**: This requires distributed tracing. Each service looks healthy in isolation. The only way to find the bottleneck is to trace a slow request across all services. The trace might show: a slow S3 download, a resource contention with another transcoding job, or a garbage collection pause in the Transcoding Service. If tracing is not set up, the debugging approach is: add span logs to each step, measure per-step latency, and deploy a tracing infrastructure before the next incident.
