## Why This Matters

AI engineering is one of the fastest-growing roles in software development. Companies of every size are integrating large language models into their products: customer support chatbots, code assistants, document processors, data extraction pipelines, and autonomous agents. The difference between a demo and a production system is what AI engineering is about.

Traditional software engineering treats rules as fixed — you write logic, it executes deterministically. AI engineering introduces probabilistic components where the same input can produce different outputs. This shift changes how you test, monitor, debug, and deploy. Understanding these differences is the foundation of every other skill in this course.

## The Problem

A product manager asks you to build a system that answers customer questions about your company's documentation. Your first instinct might be to write a search function that matches keywords, or a decision tree of common questions. Both approaches fail at scale: keyword search can't handle synonyms or paraphrased questions, and decision trees explode in complexity as your documentation grows.

You could train a custom ML model, but that requires thousands of labeled examples, weeks of training, and ongoing maintenance. What you need is a system that understands natural language, generalizes across topics you haven't explicitly programmed, and can be updated by simply changing a text prompt — without retraining.

Large language models solve exactly this problem, and AI engineering is the discipline of integrating them into reliable, production-ready systems.

## The Concept

AI engineering is the practice of building production software systems that leverage large language models and other AI services as components. It lives at the intersection of three disciplines:

**Software engineering** provides the architecture: APIs, databases, caching, authentication, error handling, monitoring, and deployment pipelines. An AI system is still a software system — it needs tests, CI/CD, logging, and all the usual infrastructure.

**Machine learning operations** provides the model management: prompt versioning, evaluation datasets, A/B testing, performance monitoring, cost tracking, and safety guardrails. LLMs remove the need for most custom training, but they introduce new operational concerns around latency, cost, and output quality.

**Product engineering** provides the user-facing design: how to surface AI responses, when to show uncertainty, how to handle edge cases gracefully, and how to build trust with users who know they are interacting with AI.

The core workflow of AI engineering is a loop: prompt the model, evaluate the output, refine the prompt or the surrounding system, and repeat. Unlike traditional software where bugs are deterministic, here you iterate toward reliability through better prompting, validation layers, and system design.
