## Why This Matters

Large language models are surprisingly good at producing convincing wrong answers. On tasks that require multiple steps of reasoning — math problems, logical deductions, multi-step planning — the model often jumps to the wrong conclusion because it short-circuits the reasoning process. Chain-of-thought prompting forces the model to externalize its reasoning, which dramatically improves accuracy on these tasks.

The improvement is not marginal. On many reasoning benchmarks, chain-of-thought prompting improves accuracy by 20-40 percentage points. The model does not just produce better answers — it produces answers you can audit. You can read the reasoning trace, identify where it went wrong, and fix the prompt accordingly. Chain-of-thought turns the model from a black box into a glass box.

## The Problem

You ask the model: "A farmer has 15 chickens. Each chicken lays 2 eggs per day. How many eggs does the farmer collect in a week?"

The model answers: "210 eggs."

The answer is correct. But when you ask: "A farmer has 15 chickens. Each chicken lays 2 eggs per day. 3 chickens are eaten by a fox on Wednesday. How many eggs does the farmer collect from Monday to Sunday?"

The model might answer: "24 eggs" — wrong. It forgot to account for the reduced chicken count after Wednesday. The model shortcut the reasoning and made a mistake you cannot diagnose because you cannot see the intermediate steps.

Chain-of-thought prompting fixes this by requiring the model to show its work before giving the final answer.

## The Concept

Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting instructs the model to produce intermediate reasoning steps before arriving at the final answer. Instead of jumping from question to answer, the model decomposes the problem into sub-steps, reasons through each one, and produces the final answer only at the end.

There are two main approaches:

**Zero-shot CoT** adds a simple instruction like "Let's think step by step" to the prompt. No reasoning examples are provided — the model generates its own chain of thought spontaneously. This works surprisingly well for many tasks.

**Few-shot CoT** provides examples of problems solved step by step. Each example shows the reasoning chain and the final answer. The model learns the pattern from the examples and applies it to new problems.

Chain-of-thought is most effective for:

- Arithmetic and math word problems
- Logical reasoning and puzzles
- Multi-hop questions (questions requiring multiple facts)
- Planning and strategy problems
- Code debugging and error analysis

It is less effective for tasks that do not require multi-step reasoning, where it adds latency and cost without improving accuracy.
