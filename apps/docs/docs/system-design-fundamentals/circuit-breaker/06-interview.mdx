## Question 1: Explain the circuit breaker pattern

**What the interviewer wants:** A clear explanation of the three states, the transitions between them, and the problem the pattern solves.

**Frame your answer.** Start with the problem: cascading failure caused by thread pool exhaustion when a slow dependency blocks all callers. Use the home electrical breaker analogy -- a 15-amp breaker trips when current exceeds the threshold, preventing a fire. Then describe the three states: CLOSED (normal, all calls pass through with 200-microsecond overhead), OPEN (failing, all calls fast-fail with fallback in under 1 microsecond), and HALF_OPEN (probing, limited calls allowed through to test recovery). Walk through the transitions: CLOSED to OPEN when 5 failures occur in a 10-second window, OPEN to HALF_OPEN after a 30-second reset timeout, HALF_OPEN to CLOSED when 3 probes succeed, or back to OPEN when a probe fails. End with a concrete example: payment service slows, breaker opens after 5 failures, 30 seconds later it probes, recovery is automatic. Total duration: 35 seconds from first failure to full recovery.

**Follow-up:** What happens if the dependency recovers in 2 seconds but the reset timeout is 30 seconds?

**Answer.** The breaker stays OPEN for 30 seconds, rejecting requests that would have succeeded for 28 seconds. This is a false positive. The fix is to reduce the reset timeout to 5-10 seconds or use exponential backoff starting at 5 seconds.

## Question 2: Compare circuit breaker with retry

**What the interviewer wants:** Understanding that these patterns solve different problems and can conflict.

**Frame your answer.** A retry assumes the failure is transient and the same request might succeed on a second attempt. A circuit breaker assumes the dependency is unhealthy and further attempts will waste resources. Retry works for network timeouts lasting 100-500 milliseconds. Circuit breaker works for dependency overload lasting seconds to minutes. They conflict when combined naively: retry sends 3 requests with 3-second timeouts, all fail, the breaker opens, but the caller waited 9 seconds. The fix is to check the circuit breaker before each retry attempt. If the breaker is OPEN, skip retries and go directly to fallback.

## Question 3: How would you design a circuit breaker library?

**What the interviewer wants:** System design thinking for middleware.

**Frame your answer.** Define the public API: `isRequestAllowed()` returns boolean, `recordSuccess()`, `recordFailure()`. Describe the internal components: a state machine using an atomic integer with CAS transitions (10-20 nanoseconds), a metrics collector using a bucketed sliding window of 10 buckets covering 10 seconds, and a threshold evaluator that compares metrics to configuration. Cover thread safety: CAS operations for state transitions and atomic integers for counters. Explain the configuration: failureCount=5, windowSize=10s, resetTimeout=30s, halfOpenMax=3. Discuss the integration pattern: a decorator that wraps the client interface. Mention metrics export: cb_state gauge, cb_transitions counter, cb_rejected counter.

## Question 4: When would you choose count-based versus rate-based circuit breaking?

**What the interviewer wants:** Understanding of the tradeoff between the two approaches.

**Frame your answer.** Count-based breaks after N failures regardless of total request volume. It is simple and works well for steady-state traffic of 50-500 req/s. It fails for burst traffic where 5 failures arrive in 200 milliseconds and sparse traffic where 5 failures take 10 minutes. Rate-based breaks when the failure percentage exceeds a threshold (50 percent of at least 10 minimum requests). It adapts to traffic volume. Choose count-based for simple systems with predictable traffic. Choose rate-based for variable-traffic systems or critical dependencies needing proportional protection.

## Question 5: How do you configure reset timeout?

**What the interviewer wants:** Understanding of the recovery dynamic.

**Frame your answer.** The reset timeout should match the dependency's recovery time plus a safety margin of 1.5x to 2x. If too short (under 3 seconds), the breaker probes before recovery and immediately reopens. If too long (over 5 minutes), the dependency recovers but the breaker stays OPEN unnecessarily. The ideal is 1.5x to 2x the known recovery time. Use exponential backoff starting at 5 seconds, doubling each cycle up to 300 seconds.

## Question 6: Design a circuit breaker for a multi-region deployment

**What the interviewer wants:** Distributed systems thinking.

**Frame your answer.** Each region maintains its own circuit breaker state: payment in us-east-1 is independent from payment in eu-west-1. Cross-region calls need separate breakers with longer timeouts (30 seconds versus 10 seconds) and lower thresholds (3 failures versus 5 failures). For global coordination, use a distributed store like Redis at 5ms latency per check, but prefer local state with eventual consistency to avoid adding latency to every request.

## Question 7: How do you test a circuit breaker implementation?

**What the interviewer wants:** Practical testing knowledge.

**Frame your answer.** Unit tests: verify CLOSED to OPEN after 5 failures, OPEN to HALF_OPEN after 30-second timeout, HALF_OPEN to CLOSED after 3 successful probes, HALF_OPEN to OPEN after a failed probe. Verify thread safety with 50 concurrent threads. Integration tests: start a test dependency, inject failures (503 responses, 5-second latency), verify the breaker opens. Restore health, verify probes and close. End-to-end tests: deploy two services, simulate a dependency crash, verify fallbacks, restore dependency, verify normal operation.

## Question 8: What metrics would you monitor for circuit breakers?

**What the interviewer wants:** Operational thinking.

**Frame your answer.** Track three metrics per circuit breaker. State: CLOSED (0), OPEN (1), or HALF_OPEN (2) as a gauge. Alert when OPEN for more than 5 minutes. Transition count: changes per minute as a counter. Alert when transitions exceed 10 per minute (indicates cycling). Rejected request count: requests rejected while OPEN. Alert when rejected exceeds 1,000 per minute. Additional metrics: failure rate before opening, time-to-detect, time-to-recover.

## Question 9: How does a circuit breaker interact with a bulkhead?

**What the interviewer wants:** Understanding of complementary patterns.

**Frame your answer.** The bulkhead limits concurrent calls using a dedicated thread pool of 10-50 threads per dependency. The circuit breaker prevents calls to unhealthy dependencies by checking state before each request. They work at different layers: bulkhead protects from resource exhaustion (slow but not failing), circuit breaker protects from wasted effort (actively failing). Use both: bulkhead with a small thread pool per dependency, circuit breaker around the bulkhead-protected call. When the circuit is OPEN, the bulkhead sees zero traffic.

## Question 10: What happens when the circuit breaker itself fails?

**What the interviewer wants:** Failure analysis and defensive design.

**Frame your answer.** Three failure modes. Process crash: state resets on restart, breaker starts CLOSED, first 5 requests hit unhealthy dependency. State corruption: metrics collector produces negative counts. Clamp values to sane ranges. Deadlock: use CAS-based transitions instead of locks. Always maintain a forced-closed bypass flag for emergencies.

## Question 11: How would you design a circuit breaker for serverless functions?

**What the interviewer wants:** Adapting patterns to different execution models.

**Frame your answer.** Lambda functions are stateless and short-lived. Use an external state store like DynamoDB or ElastiCache. Each invocation reads state (5-10ms DynamoDB read), makes the call or returns fallback, and writes updated state (5-10ms DynamoDB write). Total overhead: 10-20ms per invocation. Optimize by caching state for the duration of the invocation. Use conditional writes for atomic state transitions across concurrent invocations.

## Question 12: Explain circuit breakers and graceful degradation

**What the interviewer wants:** High-level architectural thinking.

**Frame your answer.** Graceful degradation is the principle that a system continues operating at reduced capacity when parts fail. The circuit breaker enables this by providing a structured mechanism: when a dependency fails, the breaker opens and the fallback returns a degraded response. Your users see a slightly degraded experience (cached balance instead of real-time, default shipping estimate instead of exact date) instead of a complete failure. Without the breaker, they see timeouts, cascading failures, or blank error pages.

## Question 13: Handle circuit breaker state across restarts

**What the interviewer wants:** Operational resilience.

**Frame your answer.** On restart, the breaker starts CLOSED with an empty sliding window. This is safest because you do not know current dependency health. During rolling deployments, new instances have CLOSED breakers while old instances might have OPEN breakers. Mitigation: use a 30-second warm-up period where new instances probe before accepting full traffic.

## Question 14: Can a circuit breaker cause a DDoS?

**What the interviewer wants:** Second-order effects.

**Frame your answer.** Yes, through the retry-and-reset cycle. 100 instances all have 30-second reset timeouts that fire simultaneously. All 100 send probes at the same time. The dependency receives a burst of 100 requests that overwhelms it. Mitigation: add jitter to the reset timeout by plus or minus 25 percent so instances probe at different times.

## Question 15: When would you NOT use a circuit breaker?

**What the interviewer wants:** Understanding of when the pattern adds overhead without benefit.

**Frame your answer.** Four scenarios. Idempotent stateless dependencies: retries with backoff are more appropriate. Local-only operations: local file system or in-memory cache has no cascading failure risk. Batch processing: each work unit is independent. Read-only replicas: failover logic is more appropriate.

## Question 16: Design a distributed circuit breaker with shared state

**What the interviewer wants:** Distributed systems design.

**Frame your answer.** Use Redis-backed circuit breaker. Store state in `cb:{name}:state` string, failures in `cb:{name}:failures` sorted set with timestamps as scores. Each instance reads state from Redis before allowing a request (5ms), writes success or failure after a request (5ms). Total overhead: 10ms per request. Use Redis Lua scripts for atomic state transitions. Tradeoff: shared state eliminates false positive cascades but adds 10ms latency.

## Question 17: Circuit breaker for gRPC streaming calls

**What the interviewer wants:** Extended application of the pattern.

**Frame your answer.** For server streaming, track failures on RPC establishment and on each message received. If stream establishment fails, count as failure. If stream breaks mid-flow, count as failure. Wrap stream creation: `if (!breaker.isRequestAllowed()) return fallbackStream()`. For bidirectional streaming, apply circuit breaking at the stream level. When a stream is established successfully, the circuit is healthy for the stream duration. When the stream ends in error, count one failure.

## Question 18: Explain to a non-technical stakeholder

**What the interviewer wants:** Communication clarity.

**Frame your answer.** Imagine a call center with 200 agents. Each agent handles one call at a time. If the payment department takes 5 minutes to answer, agents spend 5 minutes on hold. Soon all 200 agents are on hold with payment. New calls queue up. Nobody answers any calls. A circuit breaker is like a supervisor who notices after 5 calls where agents waited more than 3 minutes and stops all calls to payment. Agents hang up immediately and tell customers "payment system temporarily unavailable, please try later." Other departments keep working normally. After 30 seconds, the supervisor lets one call through to test. If payment answers quickly, all agents start calling again. Self-healing.

## Question 19: Circuit breaker and fail fast

**What the interviewer wants:** Philosophical connection.

**Frame your answer.** Fail fast is the principle of detecting and reporting errors immediately rather than letting them propagate. The circuit breaker is a concrete implementation of fail fast at the architecture level. When a dependency is unhealthy, the breaker fails fast by returning a fallback immediately instead of waiting for a 2-second timeout. The OPEN state is the ultimate fail-fast mechanism: zero wait time, no network call, immediate response. The breaker trades the possibility of a successful late response for the certainty of a fast failure.

## Question 20: Design a rate-limiting circuit breaker

**What the interviewer wants:** Combining rate limiting with circuit breaking.

**Frame your answer.** A rate-limiting circuit breaker opens not just on failures but also on request rate approaching capacity. Maintain two sliding windows: one for failure rate, one for request rate. When the request rate exceeds 80 percent of the dependency's known capacity of 10,000 req/s, the breaker opens preemptively to prevent overload. This is Amazon's approach. The preemptive open prevents the dependency from entering a degraded state. No failures ever occur because the dependency is never overloaded.

## Question 21: How do you size a bulkhead thread pool?

**What the interviewer wants:** Capacity planning.

**Frame your answer.** Use the formula: pool size = R * L * (1 / U). R is request rate in req/s, L is average latency in seconds, U is target utilization. For payment service with 50 req/s, 200ms latency, 60 percent utilization: pool size = 50 * 0.2 * (1 / 0.6) = 16.6, round up to 17 threads. Queue depth = R * maxWait * (1 - U_avg). For 50 req/s, 5-second max wait, 50 percent average utilization: queue depth = 50 * 5 * (1 - 0.5) = 125 entries.

## Question 22: Cascading circuit breakers in a microservice chain

**What the interviewer wants:** Deep architecture understanding.

**Frame your answer.** In chain A calls B calls C calls D, if D fails, breakers should open in order: D first, then C, then B, then A. Configure deeper services with shorter reset timeouts (D: 5s, C: 10s, B: 15s, A: 30s) so deeper services recover faster and upper services wait for recovery. Use priority: higher-priority traffic gets longer timeouts, lower-priority traffic is rejected first.

## Question 23: What happens during a HALF_OPEN to CLOSED transition when a probe succeeds but then subsequent traffic fails?

**What the interviewer wants:** Understanding of the infinite probe cycle.

**Frame your answer.** This is the infinite probe cycle failure mode. The probe succeeds with minimal load, but full traffic overwhelms the dependency, causing immediate failures that reopen the breaker. The system oscillates between HALF_OPEN and OPEN. Mitigation: use a larger halfOpenSuccessThreshold of 5-10, introduce a stability period of 30 seconds in CLOSED state before allowing full traffic, and gradually increase traffic from probe levels to full levels over a ramp-up period of 30 seconds.

## Question 24: How do you handle a dependency that returns 429 Too Many Requests?

**What the interviewer wants:** Handling rate limits with circuit breakers.

**Frame your answer.** Distinguish 429 from 5xx errors. A 429 response includes a Retry-After header specifying how long to wait (for example, 30 seconds). Do not count 429 responses as failures in the circuit breaker sense -- they indicate rate limiting, not service health. Instead, implement a separate rate limiter that respects the Retry-After header. If the dependency consistently returns 429, increase the backoff or reduce the request rate. If the dependency returns 5xx along with 429, the circuit breaker should consider the 5xx responses as failures.

## Question 25: How does the circuit breaker handle idempotency for retried requests?

**What the interviewer wants:** Understanding of request safety.

**Frame your answer.** If the dependency is idempotent (the same request produces the same result without side effects), the circuit breaker can retry the same request after the half-open probe succeeds. The breaker stores the original request parameters and replays the exact same request on probe success. If the dependency is not idempotent, the breaker should not replay -- it should let the client retry. Payment charges are not idempotent; GET requests for inventory counts are idempotent. Use an idempotencyKey header to deduplicate requests across the circuit breaker lifecycle.
