# Reliability — Interview Practice

Reliability questions test your ability to design systems that survive failures. Interviewers want to see that you understand failure modes, can reason about tradeoffs, and know production patterns. This page covers the question landscape, signals interviewers evaluate, a structured framework, high-signal phrases, red flags, and practice drills.

## The Question Landscape

Reliability questions appear in three forms in system design interviews:

**Primary reliability questions.** The interviewer explicitly asks about reliability: "Design a reliable payment system" or "How would you handle failures in this system?" These test your depth of reliability knowledge. You must cover timeout, retry, circuit breaker, bulkhead, fallback, and idempotency in a structured way.

**Reliability as a cross-cutting concern.** The interviewer asks about another topic (design a chat system, design a URL shortener) and says: "How do you ensure the system is reliable?" or "What happens when the database fails?" These test whether you naturally consider reliability in any design. The interviewer expects you to bring reliability up without prompting.

**Reliability incident analysis.** The whiteboard shows a failed system. "This system had an outage. Why? How would you fix it?" These test your debugging and diagnostic skills. You must trace the failure chain from the root cause to the user-visible symptom.

| Level | Question |

### Probing Questions Interviewers Ask

After you provide your initial answer, interviewers probe deeper with follow-ups:

- "What happens when the retry queue fills up?" — Tests bounded resource understanding.
- "How do you handle the case where the fallback also fails?" — Tests multi-layer fallback thinking.
- "What is your recovery time objective for this design?" — Tests quantitative thinking and SLA awareness.
- "How do you test this in production?" — Tests operational maturity.
- "What if the circuit breaker causes a self-inflicted outage?" — Tests understanding of circuit breaker limitations and conservative configuration.
- "How does this design change if the failure rate is 0.1% versus 10%?" — Tests adaptive thinking based on data.
- "Your retry budget is exhausted. What do you tell the clients?" — Tests error propagation and graceful degradation.

## Signals Interviewers Evaluate

Interviewers look for specific signals. These vary by level:

### Junior Signals (0-2 years)

- **Knows the vocabulary**: can define timeout, retry, circuit breaker, fallback.
- **Can draw a simple state diagram**: closed, open, half-open for circuit breakers.
- **Mentions idempotency**: understands that retries without idempotency cause duplicates.
- **Recognizes failure types**: transient vs persistent, crash vs hang.
- **Asks clarifying questions**: "Is this payment system processing credit cards or cryptocurrency?"
- **Can explain the tradeoff**: "Retries add latency but recover from transient failures."

### Mid-Level Signals (2-5 years)

- **Provides concrete numbers**: timeout at 500ms, retry 3 times, backoff at 200ms with jitter.
- **Compares alternatives**: thread pool vs semaphore bulkhead, retry vs circuit breaker.
- **Considers edge cases**: what happens when memory is full, when the dead-letter queue fills up.
- **Discusses monitoring**: what metrics to track, what alerts to set.
- **Handles interviewer follow-ups**: adjusts the design when the interviewer introduces new constraints.
- **Draws timeline diagrams**: shows request flow with timing annotations for retries and timeouts.
- **Mentions configuration management**: "I would make the retry parameters configurable without redeployment."

### Senior Signals (5-8 years)

- **Tradeoff analysis**: articulates the latency cost of retries and the complexity cost of circuit breakers.
- **Failure-mode enumeration**: lists 5+ specific failure modes and their mitigations.
- **Multi-service reasoning**: traces failure propagation across service boundaries.
- **Configuration discussion**: describes how to configure reliability mechanisms, including specific parameters and how to tune them.
- **Operational experience**: references real incidents, explains what went wrong and how to fix it.
- **Quantitative reasoning**: calculates retry amplification factors, circuit breaker thresholds, bulkhead pool sizes.
- **Budget-aware thinking**: "We have a 99.95% uptime budget for this service. That gives us 4.38 hours of allowed downtime per year. My design targets 99.99% uptime to stay within budget."

### Staff+ Signals (8+ years)

- **System-level thinking**: designs a reliability framework that works across teams and services.
- **Quantitative reasoning**: calculates retry amplification factors, circuit breaker thresholds, bulkhead pool sizes.
- **Business context**: ties reliability design to business metrics (revenue impact of 5-minute vs 1-hour outage).
- **Organizational design**: describes how to enforce reliability standards without blocking development velocity.
- **Past incident analysis**: references a real outage (e.g., the 2017 AWS S3 outage caused by a typo).
- **Standardization vs autonomy tradeoff**: "We provide default reliability configurations but allow teams to override them with documented justification."
- **Evolution path**: describes how reliability design changes as the system grows from 100 to 100,000 requests per second.

## The RSVP Framework for Reliability Answers

Use this structured framework when answering reliability questions in interviews:

**R — Recognize failures.** List the specific failure modes for each component in your design. Do not say "things can fail." Say "the database can fail: connection pool exhaustion, query timeouts, disk full, replication lag, network partition." The more specific you are, the more the interviewer trusts your depth.

**S — Specify mechanisms.** For each failure mode, specify the exact mechanism that handles it. Not "use retries" but "use exponential backoff with jitter, max 3 attempts, base delay 200ms, cap 5s, jitter 25%." The mechanism must match the failure mode — do not use a circuit breaker for a failure that requires idempotency.

**V — Verify the design.** Walk through a failure scenario. Trace a request through the system during each failure mode. Verify that your mechanisms handle the failure correctly and do not create new failure modes. This is where hiring managers separate candidates — the ones who verify find the bugs in their own designs.

**P — Plan for operations.** Describe how you monitor, alert, and debug the reliability mechanisms. What dashboards do you need? What alerts? How do you test the mechanisms? An unmonitored circuit breaker is a liability, not an asset.

### RSVP in Action: Example Walkthrough

Interview question: "Design a reliable notification service that sends email, SMS, and push notifications."

**R — Recognize failures.** Three downstream providers (email, SMS, push). Each can fail independently: network timeout, provider API down, rate-limited, authentication failure, malformed response. The notification service itself can fail: thread pool exhaustion, memory exhaustion, database connection pool full.

**S — Specify mechanisms.** Each provider has a separate bulkhead pool of 10 threads. Each call has a 2-second timeout with 3 retries, exponential backoff at 200ms base, jitter 25%. Circuit breaker per provider: threshold 50% over 30-second window, open for 60 seconds. Fallback: if email fails, do not send SMS as fallback — retry email only. Mutations (marking notifications as sent) use idempotency keys.

**V — Verify the design.** Walk through a scenario: the email provider goes down at t=0. First call times out after 2 seconds. Retry 1 at t=2.2s — also fails. Retry 2 at t=2.8s — also fails. The circuit breaker opens after 10 failures in the 30-second window. From t=30 onward, email calls are rejected in under 1ms. SMS and push continue working. At t=90, the circuit breaker transitions to half-open. The probe succeeds. Normal operation resumes. Verified: email outage does not affect SMS or push.

**P — Plan for operations.** Dashboard shows: circuit breaker state per provider, retry rate, fallback activation count. Alerts: circuit breaker opens, any provider error rate exceeds 5%, retry queue size exceeds 1,000. Runbooks document the procedure for each alert.

## Concrete Interview Scenario: Full Walkthrough

Interview question: "Design the reliability layer for a ride-hailing payment system. The system charges riders after each trip. Duplicate charges are unacceptable."

### Your Answer Using RSVP

**Step 1: Define the architecture.**

The payment system has three components: the ride service (initiates payment), the payment service (processes the charge), and the payment gateway (Stripe external API). The payment service calls Stripe's `POST /v1/charges` endpoint.

**Step 2: Identify failure modes.**

- Network blip between payment service and Stripe: timeout, response lost.
- Stripe returns 5xx: transient overload on Stripe's side.
- Stripe processes the charge but the response is lost: the charge went through, but the payment service does not know.
- Stripe is down for an extended period: minutes to hours.
- Idempotency key collision: two different payments accidentally use the same key.
- Payment service crashes mid-transaction: the charge may or may not have been processed.

**Step 3: Specify mechanisms for each failure mode.**

```
Idempotency key: UUID v4, sent as Idempotency-Key header.
  Storage: Redis cluster with 24-hour TTL.
  Collision: return 409 if same key with different body.

Timeout: 3 seconds per Stripe call.
  Rationale: Stripe's p99 latency is 800ms. 3 seconds is 4x the p99.

Retry: max 3 attempts, base delay 200ms, cap 5s, jitter 25%.
  Retry on: timeout, 502, 503, 504, 429.
  Do not retry on: 400, 401, 402, 403, 404, 409.

Circuit breaker: threshold 30% over 10-second window, open for 60 seconds.
  Rationale: Stripe rarely fails. If it does, fail fast.

Bulkhead: semaphore-based, max 20 concurrent calls to Stripe.
  Rationale: short calls (under 3s), semaphore overhead is minimal.

Fallback: retry with a secondary gateway (Adyen).
  Activation: when Stripe's circuit breaker is open.
  Rate limit: max 10 calls/second to Adyen (avoid overwhelming the fallback).
```

**Step 4: Walk through a complete failure scenario.**

t=0: A rider completes a trip. The ride service sends a charge request to the payment service.
t=0.1s: The payment service generates idempotency key `abc-123` and sends a POST to Stripe with a 3-second timeout.
t=0.2s: Stripe receives the request, processes the charge, and sends a 200 response.
t=0.3s: The response is lost due to a network blip. The payment service hits the 3-second timeout.
t=3.3s: Retry 1 with backoff. Same idempotency key `abc-123`. The payment service sends the POST again.
t=3.5s: Stripe receives the request, checks its idempotency cache, finds the key, and returns the cached result. No duplicate charge.
t=3.6s: The payment service receives the response. The charge succeeded. The transaction is complete.

**Step 5: Verify the design.**

- Duplicate charges: prevented by idempotency keys on the Stripe side.
- Stripe outage: circuit breaker opens after 30% failure rate over 10 seconds. Fallback to Adyen. No charges lost, but Adyen may have different pricing.
- Payment service crash: unprocessed charges are retried by the ride service, which has its own retry policy (3 attempts, 5-second backoff). The idempotency key ensures no duplicates.
- Redis cluster failure: idempotency check falls back to a local in-memory cache with a 60-second TTL. Rare window for duplicates during Redis downtime (60 seconds).
- Stripe-side idempotency failure: Stripe guarantees idempotency within 24 hours. If Stripe's idempotency fails, the system detects duplicate charges through a daily reconciliation batch job comparing Stripe transactions against local transactions.

**Step 6: Plan for operations.**

Metrics: charge success rate, Stripe p50/p95/p99 latency, circuit breaker state, retry rate, fallback rate, idempotency cache hit rate.

Alerts:
- Charge success rate below 99.9% over 5 minutes.
- Circuit breaker opens for Stripe.
- Fallback to Adyen activates.
- Idempotency cache hit rate below 95% (possible Redis issue).
- Reconciliation batch finds unmatched transactions.

Testing:
- Weekly chaos testing: randomly inject Stripe failures in staging.
- Monthly failover drill: switch all traffic to Adyen for 5 minutes.
- Quarterly reconciliation audit: manually review all unmatched transactions from the past 3 months.

## Fifteen High-Signal Phrases

Use these phrases in your answers. Each one signals a specific reliability knowledge area:

1. "This operation is idempotent, so retries are safe — we include an idempotency key header."
2. "The circuit breaker uses a ring buffer of 100 outcomes with a 50% failure threshold over a 10-second window."
3. "Exponential backoff with jitter prevents thundering herd — base delay 200ms, cap 30s, jitter 25%."
4. "The bulkhead isolates the payment service thread pool from the recommendation service thread pool."
5. "The dead-letter queue captures messages that exceed max_retries so operators can inspect them."
6. "We validate external API responses before processing them — do not trust external schema or latency."
7. "The timeout hierarchy ensures inner timeouts fire before outer timeouts — the database query timeout fires before the service's gRPC deadline."
8. "Fallback returns cached data with a header indicating staleness so the client knows the data quality level."
9. "The retry budget limits amplification to 2x normal request volume — when the budget is exhausted, we return errors instead of retrying."
10. "Health checks are independent of application logic — they probe a separate endpoint on a separate thread pool."
11. "Configuration changes to reliability parameters are auditable and rollbackable."
12. "We measure the dependency's normal failure rate for two weeks before setting circuit breaker thresholds."
13. "Profiling — I want to verify that the fallback path has sufficient capacity to handle the failover load."
14. "The semaphore-based bulkhead is appropriate here because the calls are short (under 10ms) and we want to minimize overhead."
15. "Cross-region replication uses asynchronous WAL shipping with data center awareness to minimize latency impact."

## Eight Red Flags

These statements signal weak reliability knowledge. Avoid them:

**Red flag 1: "The system will never fail."** Shows no understanding of distributed systems reality. Every system fails. Reliability is about surviving failure, not preventing it.

**Red flag 2: "We retry three times and it works."** Shows no understanding of backoff, jitter, or retry amplification. Retries without backoff cause thundering herd.

**Red flag 3: "The database handles consistency."** Shows no understanding of application-level reliability. Databases fail. The application must handle database failures.

**Red flag 4: "We use monolith so reliability is easy."** Shows no understanding of reliability patterns. A monolith can still fail — database down, disk full, memory exhausted. Single-process systems need reliability mechanisms too.

**Red flag 5: "Circuit breakers solve all reliability problems."** Shows no understanding of circuit breaker limitations. Circuit breakers protect against cascading failures but do not handle data corruption, Byzantine faults, or configuration errors.

**Red flag 6: "We do not need fallbacks."** Shows no understanding of graceful degradation. Every critical path needs a fallback, even if the fallback is "show an error message" instead of "crash."

**Red flag 7: "Retries are free."** Shows no understanding of retry costs. Retries add latency, consume resources, and can amplify failures.

**Red flag 8: "We set the timeout to 30 seconds."** Shows no understanding of user expectations. A 30-second timeout means users wait 30 seconds before seeing an error. Timeouts should be short — 500ms to 2 seconds for most operations.

## Answering Follow-Up Questions

Interviewers probe your design with follow-ups. Here are common follow-ups and strong responses:

**Follow-up: "What happens when the retry queue fills up?"**

Strong response: "The retry queue is bounded at 10,000 entries. When full, new retries are rejected immediately — the request returns a 503 to the caller. An alert fires when the queue exceeds 5,000 entries. The queue is backed by a write-ahead log on disk, so a process restart does not lose queued retries. If the queue stays full for more than 5 minutes, we scale up the retry worker pool."

**Follow-up: "How do you handle the case where the fallback also fails?"**

Strong response: "The fallback has its own circuit breaker. If both the primary and fallback are failing, the system returns a degraded response — `{"error": "temporarily unavailable", "retry_after": 30}`. The client retries after 30 seconds. An on-call engineer is paged immediately. The runbook documents the manual escalation to the fallback's operations team."

**Follow-up: "How do you set the circuit breaker threshold?"**

Strong response: "I measure the dependency's normal failure rate for two weeks. If the p99 failure rate is 2%, I set the threshold at 10% — 5x the normal rate. This absorbs normal variation while tripping on real outages. I also monitor the false-positive rate: if the circuit breaker opens more than once per week without an actual outage, I raise the threshold."

**Follow-up: "Your idempotency cache (Redis) goes down. What happens?"**

Strong response: "The idempotency cache has a local fallback — an in-memory LRU cache with a 60-second TTL and 10,000 entry capacity. If Redis is down, new idempotency keys are cached locally for 60 seconds. After 60 seconds, the entry expires and the same key could theoretically be reused for a different operation. This creates a 60-second window for potential duplicates. The business accepts this risk because Redis downtime is rare (99.99% uptime) and the window is short."

**Follow-up: "The payment gateway responds in 5 seconds normally. What timeout do you set?"**

Strong response: "I set the timeout at 7 seconds — 1.4x the p99 latency. This is longer than usual because the payment gateway is an external service with higher latency variance. If the timeout were too tight (3 seconds), 5% of legitimate payments would be retried unnecessarily, adding latency and potential confusion. However, I verify that the client's total timeout (10 seconds) is still longer than the payment service's timeout (7 seconds) plus retry time (3 seconds max)."

## Five Drills

These drills train the RSVP framework and help you internalize reliability patterns.

### Drill 1: Failure Mode Brainstorming

Set a timer for 5 minutes. Pick a service (payment processing, file upload, real-time chat). List every failure mode. Aim for 15+ per service.

Sample for a payment service: network timeout, database overload, payment gateway down, idempotency key collision, duplicate charge, lost transaction, corrupted transaction data, disk full on logs, DNS resolution failure, TLS handshake timeout, certificate expiry, load balancer connection limit, thread pool exhaustion, memory leak, OOM killer, disk I/O bottleneck, configuration error, deployment race condition, rate limit exceeded, credit card declined (expected failure), fraudulent transaction detection (false positive), third-party SLA violation.

### Drill 2: Mechanism Specification

Pick a failure mode from Drill 1. Write the exact mechanism for handling it: which mechanism, with what parameters, in what order.

Example: "Failure mode — payment gateway returns 503. Mechanism — retry with exponential backoff: max 3 attempts, base delay 200ms, max delay 5s, jitter 25%. Circuit breaker: threshold 50% over 30-second window, open for 60 seconds. Fallback: charge using secondary gateway (Stripe -> Adyen). Alert if fallback activates more than 10 times per minute."

### Drill 3: Retry Amplification Calculation

You have 10 client services each sending 500 requests per second to Service X. Each client retries 3 times with 200ms base backoff. Service X can handle 8,000 requests per second. At what failure rate does the retry amplification overwhelm Service X?

**Solution:** Normal load: 10 * 500 = 5,000 req/s. Capacity: 8,000 req/s. Headroom: 3,000 req/s. Each failed request generates up to 3 retries. If the failure rate is f, the total load is 5,000 + 5,000 * f * 3 = 5,000 + 15,000f. Set equal to capacity: 8,000 = 5,000 + 15,000f. f = 3,000 / 15,000 = 20%. At a 20% failure rate, retries consume all capacity. Above 20%, Service X's request queue grows unbounded until requests time out.

The fix: circuit breaker opens at 15% failure rate, or retry budget limits retries to 2x normal volume.

### Drill 4: Multi-Request Tracing

Draw the complete path of a request through a system with these reliability mechanisms:

1. Client sends a POST with an idempotency key.
2. API gateway applies rate limiting and timeout.
3. Order service checks the circuit breaker for inventory service.
4. Order service acquires an inventory bulkhead permit.
5. Inventory service call times out (450ms into a 500ms timeout).
6. Order service backoff 200ms plus jitter (random 150-250ms wait).
7. Order service retries the inventory call.
8. Inventory call succeeds (30ms).
9. Order service releases the bulkhead permit.
10. Order service calls payment service.
11. Payment service circuit breaker is open.
12. Order service falls back to the secondary payment provider.
13. Response returns to the client with X-Fallback header indicating degraded data.

Walk the interviewer through this path. Explain what happens at each step. Trace the resource usage (threads, connections, memory) through each step. For step 5: one inventory bulkhead thread is consumed for 450ms and released when the timeout fires. For step 6: the thread is idle during backoff — this is intentional. For step 11: the circuit breaker rejects the call in under 1ms — no thread is consumed. For step 12: the secondary provider uses its own dedicated thread pool of 10 threads.

### Drill 5: Configuration Debate

Your team has two proposals for circuit breaker thresholds on a critical payment service:

- Proposal A: threshold 10%, window 10 seconds, open duration 30 seconds
- Proposal B: threshold 50%, window 60 seconds, open duration 60 seconds

Debate the tradeoffs. Which one is better?

**Proposal A analysis:** Trips easily (10% failure rate). Fast to react (10-second window). Fast to recover (30-second open duration). Risk: false positives from normal blips cause unnecessary outages. Good for: services that rarely fail but must not cascade when they do.

**Proposal B analysis:** Tolerant (50% threshold). Slow to react (60-second window). Slow to recover (60-second open duration). Risk: during the 60-second window, users experience failures before the circuit breaker opens. Good for: services with high baseline failure rates that need smooth, non-oscillating protection.

Answer: depends on the dependency's normal failure rate. Measure for two weeks. If the dependency has a 2% normal failure rate with occasional 20% spikes, Proposal A trips on those spikes but Proposal B does not. If the dependency has a 5% normal failure rate, Proposal A causes constant false positives. The correct approach is to start with conservative values (50% threshold, 30-second window) and tighten based on production data.

## Common Interview Mistakes

Watch for these mistakes in your practice sessions:

**Mistake 1: Starting with mechanisms instead of failures.** You say "I would add a circuit breaker" before listing what can fail. The interviewer cannot evaluate whether the circuit breaker is the right mechanism. Always start with R (Recognize failures) in RSVP.

**Mistake 2: Forgetting idempotency.** You discuss retries but do not mention idempotency. The interviewer knows retries without idempotency cause duplicates. Mentioning idempotency signals production experience.

**Mistake 3: Over-engineering.** You add circuit breakers, bulkheads, and multi-region failover for a system that handles 100 requests per day. The interviewer notes you cannot scope the solution to the problem. Match the mechanism complexity to the system scale.

**Mistake 4: No numbers.** You say "use exponential backoff" without specifying the base delay, cap, or jitter. The interviewer cannot tell if you understand the parameters. Always provide numbers.

**Mistake 5: No verification step.** You describe the mechanisms but do not walk through a failure scenario. The interviewer suspects the design has hidden bugs. Always verify by tracing a failure scenario.

**Mistake 6: Ignoring operational concerns.** You design the perfect retry system but cannot describe how to monitor it. The interviewer knows the design will fail in production without observability. Always include P (Plan for operations) in RSVP.

**Mistake 7: Treating all failures equally.** You apply the same retry policy to every dependency. The interviewer sees you missed that some failures are terminal (404, 400) and should not be retried. Classify failure types explicitly.

## Practice Interview Script

Practice this 2-minute script for the opening of any reliability design question:

"There are three failure categories in this system.

First, network failures: timeouts, packet loss, connection resets. I handle these with retries — exponential backoff with jitter, max 3 attempts, starting at 200ms, and a circuit breaker that opens at 50% failure rate over 10 seconds.

Second, service failures: crashes, OOM, deadlocks. I handle these with redundancy — multiple instances behind a load balancer with health checks every 10 seconds, and an automatic failover to a secondary region if the primary fails.

Third, data failures: corruption, inconsistency, loss. I handle these with idempotency keys on all mutation operations, transactional outbox for critical writes, and a daily reconciliation batch job to detect and fix inconsistencies.

For each mechanism, I will specify the exact configuration parameters, walk through a failure scenario to verify the design, and describe the monitoring and alerting needed to operate it in production."

This script shows the interviewer that you think in categories, have specific numbers ready, know the RSVP framework, and consider all three types of failures. Tailor the specifics to the question, but keep the three-category structure.
