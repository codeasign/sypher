## The Problem Story

You are building a social platform for photographers. The year is 2005. Your app has 500 users, mostly friends and family. You store photos in a folder on a single server, and your homepage shows every new photo sorted by upload time, newest first. The query is trivial: `SELECT * FROM photos ORDER BY created_at DESC LIMIT 20`. Response time is 12 milliseconds. Life is good.

By 2006, you have 50,000 users. Photographers from around the world are uploading 10,000 photos per day. The simple query now takes 800 milliseconds because the `photos` table has 2 million rows. You add an index on `created_at`. The query drops to 30 milliseconds. You breathe a sigh of relief.

By 2007, you have 2 million users. Uploads hit 200,000 per day. The database has 80 million rows. The indexed query takes 200 milliseconds on a good day. Worse, every user sees the same feed. A photographer in Tokyo sees the same photos as a photographer in Brazil. They want personalized feeds: photos from photographers they follow, sorted by a combination of recency and relevance. The global `ORDER BY created_at` query is no longer the right answer.

You try a JOIN. `SELECT p.* FROM photos p JOIN follows f ON p.user_id = f.followee_id WHERE f.follower_id = ? ORDER BY p.created_at DESC LIMIT 20`. This query takes 4 seconds for a user following 200 photographers. The database terminal is crawling. The application is timing out. Users are leaving.

You try caching. You store the results of the JOIN query in Memcached with a 60-second TTL. The cache hit rate is 40 percent. Every cache miss triggers the slow JOIN. Users see content that is up to 60 seconds stale. And the cache does not help when the underlying problem is the query complexity.

You try denormalization. You add a `feed` table with columns `user_id`, `post_id`, and `created_at`. A background job fills this table by joining posts and follows. The feed read becomes a simple `SELECT * FROM feed WHERE user_id = ? ORDER BY created_at DESC LIMIT 20`. The read is fast. The write is the problem. Every new post triggers an INSERT into the feed table for every follower. For a user with 200 followers, one post generates 200 INSERTs. The background job falls behind. The feed shows stale data.

You are now in the classic feed generation trap. Every solution you try shifts the pain from one part of the system to another. Caching shifts the pain to cache misses. Denormalization shifts the pain to write amplification. Pre-computation shifts the pain to storage costs. There is no free lunch. You must choose where to incur the cost.

Your platform has hit the feed generation problem. You need to generate a personalized, ordered list of content for each user. The naive approach — query all content, filter by follow relationships, sort, and paginate — does not scale past tens of thousands of users. You need a fundamentally different architecture.

## The Insight

The core insight of the feed generation pattern is that you must separate the act of creating content from the act of assembling a feed. These two operations have fundamentally different characteristics. Content creation is a write operation. Feed assembly is a read operation. In a naive system, every feed read triggers a costly assembly process. In a well-designed feed system, the assembly happens ahead of time, or it happens with dramatically optimized data structures.

Think of a newspaper delivery route. In the naive approach, every morning a truck drives to the printing plant, waits for the newspaper to be printed, then delivers it to every house on the route. This is like querying the database on every feed load. The delivery person visits 500 houses, but the truck must wait for the press to finish printing each time.

Now imagine a better approach: the printing press runs overnight. At 5:00 AM, the truck picks up a stack of pre-printed newspapers. Each newspaper already has the subscriber's name and address printed on it. The delivery person drives the route and drops off each paper. The printing and the delivery are decoupled. The press runs once. The delivery route is efficient.

Fan-out on write is the overnight printing press. When a photographer uploads a photo, the system immediately writes a reference to that photo into the feed lists of every follower. The feed read becomes a simple sorted scan of a pre-populated list — no JOINs, no complex queries, no per-request assembly.

Fan-out on read is a different approach. The newspaper is not printed until the subscriber opens their door and asks for it. When a user opens the app, the system queries the recent photos from all followed users, merges them, sorts them, and returns the result. This saves the work of pre-computing feeds for inactive users, but it shifts the cost to every feed read.

The hybrid approach is the most sophisticated: pre-compute feeds for active users (fan-out on write), and compute on demand for inactive users (fan-out on read). A delivery truck runs a daily route for regular subscribers, but ad-hoc deliveries are dispatched on request.

## Core Vocabulary

**Feed.** An ordered list of content items presented to a user. Feeds can be chronological (sorted by time), ranked (sorted by relevance score), or hybrid (time-decayed relevance). A feed is the primary consumption surface for most social platforms. Every feed has a target user, a source of content, and an ordering strategy.

**Fan-out on Write.** A strategy where the system writes a reference to each new content item into the feed lists of all potential readers at the time of content creation. The write operation fans out from one content item to N feed lists. This optimizes read performance at the cost of write amplification. Every writer pays the cost so that every reader sees results instantly.

**Fan-out on Read.** A strategy where the system does not pre-compute feeds. When a user requests their feed, the system queries the recent content from all followed sources, merges the results, applies sorting, and returns the paginated list. This optimizes write performance at the cost of read latency. The reader pays the cost of assembling the feed on every request.

**Write Amplification Factor.** The ratio of total writes performed to the number of original content items created. If one photo upload triggers 1,000 feed list writes (one per follower), the write amplification factor is 1,000. High amplification factors strain write capacity, storage, and network bandwidth. The fan-out strategy directly determines this factor.

**Feed Ranking.** The algorithm that determines the order of items in a feed. Chronological ranking is simple but can miss important content. Relevance-based ranking uses signals like engagement, recency, relationship strength, content type, and user preferences. Most production systems use a combination of signals with time-decayed scoring.

**Feed Storage.** The data store that holds pre-computed feed lists. Feed storage requires fast point reads (get feed for user X), sorted iteration (paginate through items), and efficient writes (prepend new items). Redis sorted sets, Apache Cassandra, and specialized feed stores like Apache Pulsar are common choices. The storage layer must handle hundreds of thousands of feed lists, each potentially containing thousands of items.

**Pagination.** The mechanism for fetching a subset of a feed. Cursor-based pagination is standard for feeds because it handles real-time updates gracefully. The cursor points to a specific item in the feed, and the system returns the next N items after that cursor. Offset-based pagination breaks when new items are inserted at the top of the feed.

**Hydration.** The process of converting a feed entry from a reference (user ID + post ID) to a fully rendered content item with all metadata, media URLs, engagement counts, and user information. Feed lists store lightweight references. Hydration joins those references with the actual content data. This is often the most expensive part of serving a feed.

**Ranking Signals.** The raw data points used to compute a feed score. Signals include: time since publication, author reputation, content type affinity, engagement velocity (likes per minute), relationship strength (close friend vs casual acquaintance), geographic proximity, and device-specific behavior. Each signal is a dimension in the ranking model.

**Cold Start.** The problem of generating relevant feeds for new users who have no follow graph, no interaction history, and no preference data. Cold start strategies include: showing popular content, showing content from influential accounts, asking the user to select interests during onboarding, and using demographic data to infer preferences.

**Feed Slice.** A logical subset of the feed used for parallel processing or experimentation. Feed slices can be user segments (power users, new users, users in a specific region), content categories (photos only, videos only, text only), or time slices (last hour, last day, last week). The fan-out engine can process each slice independently, enabling parallel fan-out and A/B testing within slices.

**Engagement Velocity.** The rate at which a content item accumulates engagement signals (likes, comments, shares, saves) in a given time window. Engagement velocity is a strong signal for feed ranking because it indicates which content is resonating with the audience right now. A post with 100 likes in the first 5 minutes has higher velocity than a post with 200 likes over 24 hours.

**Feed Diversity.** The distribution of content sources, content types, and topics in a user's feed. A feed with low diversity shows content from only a few creators. A feed with high diversity shows content from many creators across different topics. Diversity is a tuning parameter: too much diversity dilutes personalization; too little diversity creates filter bubbles.

**Scoring Function.** The mathematical formula that computes a relevance score for each feed item. A typical scoring function is a weighted sum of normalized signals: `score = w1 * recency_norm + w2 * engagement_norm + w3 * affinity_norm + w4 * diversity_bonus`. The weights are tuned through A/B testing. The normalization ensures each signal contributes within a comparable range (typically 0.0 to 1.0).

## Feed Types

Not all feeds are the same. The feed generation pattern applies to several distinct feed types, each with different requirements.

**Chronological Feed.** Items are ordered by creation time, newest first. This is the simplest feed type. The scoring function is just the timestamp. Implementation uses a sorted set with the timestamp as the score. The advantage is predictability: users know what to expect. The disadvantage is that high-quality content from 12 hours ago is buried under low-quality content from 2 seconds ago. Twitter's earliest feed was purely chronological. Users complained that they missed important tweets while seeing low-effort content.

**Ranked Feed.** Items are ordered by a relevance score that combines multiple signals. The scoring function balances recency, engagement, affinity, and diversity. Ranked feeds improve engagement by 30 to 50 percent compared to chronological feeds. The disadvantage is opacity: users cannot predict why they see certain items. Facebook's News Feed introduced ranking in 2009 and faced backlash from users who wanted chronological ordering.

**Algorithmic Feed.** Items are ranked by a machine learning model that predicts the likelihood of user engagement. The model is trained on historical user behavior: which items the user clicked, liked, commented on, shared, or hid. Algorithmic feeds require a data pipeline for feature computation, a model training pipeline, and a real-time inference service. TikTok's For You feed is the most famous example of an algorithmic feed.

**Hybrid Feed.** Items are ordered by a combination of chronological and ranked signals. The feed shows a mix of "top content" (ranked) and "recent content" (chronological) in an interleaved pattern. This provides the freshness of chronological ordering with the quality filtering of ranked ordering. Many platforms use a hybrid approach: the first 10 items are ranked, and the remaining items are chronological.

**Topic Feed.** Items are filtered to a specific topic or category before ordering. A user might have separate feeds for "Photography," "Cooking," and "Technology." Topic feeds require a content classification step that tags each item with one or more topics. The fan-out engine writes the item to the topic feeds of followers who have opted into that topic.

**Activity Feed.** Items represent user actions rather than user-created content. "User A liked your post," "User B started following you," "User C commented on your photo." Activity feeds use fan-out on write because the audience per activity is small (the affected user only). The feed list has a shorter retention (200 items) because activities are ephemeral.

## How It Works

### Step 1: Content Creation

A content creator performs an action that generates a feed item. This could be posting a photo, writing a status update, sharing a link, or uploading a video. The content is stored in a content database (often a blob store for media and a relational or document database for metadata). The content creation service returns a unique content ID.

### Step 2: Content Published to Event Stream

The content creation service publishes an event to a stream or message queue. The event contains the content ID, the creator's user ID, the content type, and a timestamp. This decouples the content creation from the feed fan-out. The event stream acts as a buffer: if the fan-out engine is under load, events queue up without dropping.

### Step 3: Fan-out Engine Reads the Event

The fan-out engine subscribes to the event stream. It reads the new content event and retrieves the creator's follower list. For a user with 10,000 followers, the fan-out engine must write the content reference to 10,000 feed lists. The follower list is typically stored in a graph database or a key-value store with a follower set per user.

### Step 4: Fan-out Strategy Decision

The fan-out engine decides which feed lists to write to. Under a pure fan-out on write strategy, it writes to every follower's feed list. Under a hybrid strategy, it writes only to the feed lists of active followers. Inactive followers (users who have not opened the app in 30 days) receive the content reference on demand when they next request their feed. This decision is the key optimization point.

### Step 5: Feed List Write

The fan-out engine writes a lightweight reference to each follower's feed list. The reference is typically a tuple of (content ID, creator ID, timestamp, score). The feed list is stored in a data structure that supports sorted insertion and efficient range queries. Redis sorted sets are a popular choice: `ZADD user:feed:{follower_id} {score} {content_id}`. The write must be fast and durable.

### Step 6: Feed List Truncation

Feed lists cannot grow indefinitely. A user who follows 1,000 active posters would accumulate thousands of feed items per day. The fan-out engine applies a truncation policy: after inserting the new item, it removes items beyond the retention limit (typically 500 to 1,000 items). Truncation keeps the feed list small enough for fast reads and prevents unbounded storage growth.

### Step 7: User Requests Feed

The user opens their app and requests their feed. The request includes a cursor (the last seen content ID for pagination) and a page size (typically 20 to 50 items). The feed service receives the request and looks up the user's feed list in feed storage.

### Step 8: Feed List Read

The feed service reads a range of items from the user's feed list. Using a Redis sorted set, this is a `ZREVRANGEBYSCORE` query that returns the top N items after the cursor. The read returns lightweight references: an array of content IDs with associated metadata. This operation is fast because the feed list is a sorted data structure, not a relational query.

### Step 9: Ranking and Filtering

The feed service applies ranking and filtering. Chronological feeds are already sorted by the `ZREVRANGEBYSCORE` operation. Ranked feeds require a scoring pass. The ranking engine computes a score for each content item using signals like recency, engagement velocity, author affinity, and content type. Filtering removes blocked users, muted topics, content the user has already seen, and content that violates platform policies.

### Step 10: Hydration

The ranked content IDs are passed to the hydration layer. The hydration layer fetches the full content metadata from the content database, including the content text, media URLs, like count, comment count, share count, author display name, author avatar URL, and any embedded media. This is a batch operation: the hydration layer fetches all content items in a single request, not one at a time.

### Step 11: Response Assembly

The feed service assembles the final feed response. Each item is a fully hydrated content object with all metadata. The response includes a cursor for the next page. The feed service returns the response to the user's app. The total latency target is under 200 milliseconds for the read path.

### Step 12: Background Re-ranking

Periodically, the background ranking service re-computes the feed for active users. New signals arrive continuously: a post gets likes, a comment thread grows, a user's interest profile changes. The background re-ranking job reads the current feed, applies the latest ranking model, and writes a new sorted order. This can happen every few minutes for high-activity users and every few hours for casual users.

### Step 13: Chronological Feed Assembly

The simplest feed ordering is chronological. Items are sorted by creation time, newest first. This is the default for most systems because it is simple, predictable, and easy to implement. The feed list is a sorted set keyed by timestamp. Pagination is a range query over the sorted set. The disadvantage is that high-quality content from 12 hours ago is buried under low-quality content from 2 seconds ago.

### Step 14: Ranked Feed Assembly

Ranked feeds use a scoring function that combines multiple signals. A common approach is a time-decayed score: `score = (signal_sum) / (time_hours + 2) ^ 1.5`. A post with 500 likes in the first hour scores higher than a post with 50 likes in the same hour. The scoring function is tuned to balance recency and relevance. The ranking engine computes scores in a background job and writes the sorted order to the feed list.

### Step 15: Deduplication and Filtering

The feed pipeline must handle deduplication. A user might follow both Alice and Bob. If Alice shares Bob's post, the post appears in the feed twice. The fan-out engine or the read path must deduplicate by content ID. Filtering removes content the user has already seen. The feed service maintains a set of recently viewed content IDs per user and filters them out before returning the response.

### Step 16: Feed Ranking with Time Decay

For ranked feeds, the scoring function applies a time decay. A common formula uses exponential decay: `score = signal_sum * 2 ^ (-age_hours / half_life_hours)`. A half-life of 2 hours means a post's score drops by half every 2 hours. A post with 500 likes at hour 0 has score 500. At hour 2, its score is 250. At hour 4, its score is 125. At hour 24, its score is less than 1. This ensures that old content eventually falls out of the top of the feed, even if it has high engagement.

The half-life is a tunable parameter. News feeds use a short half-life (30 minutes) because news is time-sensitive. Photo feeds use a longer half-life (4 hours) because photos have a longer relevance window. Video feeds use a very short half-life (15 minutes) because video engagement is driven by recency.

### Step 17: Content Blending

Some feeds blend different content types into a single stream. A social feed might include photos, videos, text posts, shared links, and promoted content. Each content type has its own scoring function. The blender normalizes the scores across content types and interleaves the items to ensure diversity.

Content blending must handle the case where one content type dominates. If videos score consistently higher than photos, the feed becomes all videos. The blender applies a content-type diversity constraint: no more than 60 percent of items in a page can be the same content type. This constraint is applied during the ranking pass.

### Step 18: Promoted and Sponsored Content Insertion

Monetized feeds insert promoted content at specific positions. The standard pattern is to insert one sponsored item every N organic items. The sponsored items are scored by a separate ranking model that optimizes for advertiser goals (click-through rate, conversion rate) rather than user engagement.

Sponsored content insertion happens after the organic ranking pass. The feed service reserves positions 3, 8, 13, and 18 for sponsored items. The sponsored ranking model selects the highest-value sponsored item for each position, subject to frequency caps (no more than 3 sponsored items from the same advertiser per session).

### Step 19: Throttling and Rate Limiting

The feed system must protect itself from abusive request patterns. A user who refreshes their feed 100 times per second should be throttled. The read path applies rate limiting at the user level: no more than 10 feed read requests per second per user. Excessive requests return a 429 status code.

Rate limiting also applies to the fan-out engine. If a single creator posts 50 times in 1 minute, the fan-out engine batches the posts into a single fan-out operation. The creator's posts are treated as a group, reducing the write amplification from 50 times N followers to 1 times N followers.

### Step 20: Feed Refresh and Background Polling

The client app periodically refreshes the feed to check for new content. The refresh interval is a trade-off: shorter intervals mean fresher content but more server load. Standard intervals are 30 seconds for mobile apps and 60 seconds for web apps.

The refresh request uses the current cursor. The server returns only items newer than the cursor. This is an incremental feed update, not a full feed assembly. The incremental update is cheaper than a full read because the feed list is already cached and the hydration layer only needs to fetch new items.

## Variants

### Variant 1: Push-Based (Fan-out on Write)

In the push-based variant, every content creation triggers immediate writes to all follower feed lists. This is the classic fan-out on write approach. It delivers the fastest read performance: a feed read is a single sorted-set lookup. The cost is write amplification. A celebrity with 10 million followers triggers 10 million feed list writes per post. The fan-out engine must be highly scalable and resilient.

This variant is ideal for platforms where the read-to-write ratio is high. Users check their feeds many times per day but post infrequently. Twitter uses a variant of this approach for most users, with special handling for celebrity accounts with millions of followers.

### Variant 2: Pull-Based (Fan-out on Read)

In the pull-based variant, no feed lists are pre-computed. When a user requests their feed, the system queries the recent posts from all followed users, merges the results, sorts them, and returns the paginated list. This eliminates write amplification entirely. The cost is read latency. A user following 500 active accounts requires 500 queries, a merge, and a sort on every feed request.

This variant is ideal for platforms where users follow few accounts, the read-to-write ratio is low, or the system cannot afford the storage cost of pre-computed feed lists. RSS readers and early social networks used this approach.

### Variant 3: Hybrid (Fan-out for Active Users, Pull for Inactive)

The hybrid variant combines push and pull. The system maintains a user activity score. Active users (opened the app in the last 7 days) get push-based feeds. Inactive users get pull-based feeds on their next request. This reduces the write amplification by 60 to 80 percent because most users are inactive on any given day.

The hybrid variant requires a user activity tracking system and a mechanism to switch between push and pull. When an inactive user becomes active, the system backfills their feed list with recent content from followed users. The backfill operation is a one-time pull that populates the feed list, after which the system switches to push mode.

### Variant 4: Topic-Specific Feed Partitioning

Some platforms partition feeds by topic. A user following a photographer on a photo platform sees the photographer's photos in the "Photography" feed tab and their occasional text posts in a "General" feed tab. This requires the fan-out engine to know the content type of each post and write to the appropriate topic feed.

The implementation stores one sorted set per topic per user: `user:feed:photo:{user_id}`, `user:feed:video:{user_id}`, `user:feed:text:{user_id}`. The fan-out engine checks the content type and writes to the correct sorted set. The read path reads from the user's selected topic feed.

This variant increases storage because each user has multiple feed lists. A user with 5 active topics has 5 feed lists, each requiring 800 items. The storage cost is 5 times higher than a single feed list. The trade-off is that each topic feed shows more relevant content: the Photography feed shows only photos, not text posts from the same creator.

### Variant 5: Rewind and Replay Feed

In this variant, the user can rewind to any point in time and see their feed as it appeared at that moment. This is useful for platforms where users want to catch up on missed content. The implementation stores feed list snapshots at regular intervals (every hour). Each snapshot is a copy of the feed list at that time.

The storage cost is significant: 24 snapshots per day times 800 items per snapshot equals 19,200 items stored per user per day. The practical implementation stores snapshots only for users who explicitly request rewind capability. Snapshots are compressed and stored in a cost-effective storage tier like S3, with only the most recent snapshot kept in Redis.

### Variant 6: Pull with Local Cache

This variant adds a local cache layer to the pull-based approach. The feed service caches the merged feed for each user in a local in-memory cache. When a user requests their feed, the service checks the cache first. If the cache is stale (older than 30 seconds), the service performs a full pull, merge, and sort, then updates the cache.

This variant reduces the read latency for users who check their feed frequently. The cache hit rate is typically 60 to 80 percent for active users. The cache TTL is a trade-off: shorter TTL means fresher content but more cache misses; longer TTL means faster responses but potentially stale feeds.

## Mental Model Check

### Question 1

You are designing a feed for a microblogging platform with 100 million daily active users. The average user follows 200 accounts. The platform has 10,000 celebrity accounts with over 1 million followers each. Which fan-out strategy should you use?

**Answer.** A hybrid strategy. Use fan-out on write for regular users (followers < 10,000) and fan-out on read for celebrity accounts with large follower counts. Celebrity posts would cause write amplification of 1 million+ per post under pure push. By switching to pull for celebrity followers, you avoid the write amplification spike while keeping regular feeds fast. The threshold is determined by the fan-out engine's capacity and the acceptable latency for feed reads.

### Question 2

Your feed storage is using Redis sorted sets. A user follows 500 accounts. Each account posts 5 times per day. You store 800 items per feed list. What is the total storage required for 10 million active users, assuming each feed entry is 40 bytes?

**Answer.** 10 million feed lists times 800 items times 40 bytes equals 320 gigabytes. This is manageable for a Redis cluster with 16 shards of 32 GB each. However, you must account for replication (2x for Redis with a replica) and overhead (RDB snapshots, AOF logs, connection overhead). Budget approximately 1 TB of total Redis memory. The alternative is to reduce the feed list size to 500 items, which drops storage to 200 GB (600 GB with replication).

### Question 3

You notice that feed reads are slow for users who follow more than 1,000 accounts. The feed read time increases linearly with the number of followed accounts. What is the likely cause, and how do you fix it?

**Answer.** The system is likely using fan-out on read. Each feed request triggers N queries (one per followed account), and the merge operation scales with N. The fix is to switch to fan-out on write for these users. Pre-compute the feed list so that the read is a single sorted-set lookup regardless of how many accounts the user follows. The trade-off is increased write amplification, but for users with many followed accounts, the read performance improvement is worth the cost.

### Question 4

Your ranked feed is showing 3-hour-old content at the top while fresh content is buried on page 3. Users are complaining about stale feeds. What is wrong?

**Answer.** The ranking score is too heavily weighted toward engagement signals and not enough toward recency. A post with high engagement continues to score well even as it ages. The fix is to increase the time-decay factor in the scoring function. Use a half-life of 30 minutes instead of 2 hours: `score = engagement / (1 + age_minutes / 30) ^ 2`. This ensures that fresh content has a natural advantage over older content, even if the older content has more total engagement.

### Question 5

Your fan-out on write system is failing during peak hours. The write latency for the fan-out engine has increased from 50 milliseconds to 5 seconds. The feed lists are growing faster than the fan-out engine can write. What is the bottleneck, and what are three solutions?

**Answer.** The bottleneck is the write throughput of the feed storage layer. The fan-out engine is issuing too many concurrent writes, overwhelming the storage cluster. Three solutions: (1) Batch the fan-out writes into groups of 100 and write them in pipeline mode, reducing round-trip overhead. (2) Shard the feed storage by user ID so that writes are distributed across more nodes. (3) Implement a hybrid strategy with a fan-out threshold: if a user has more than 10,000 followers, switch to pull-based feed for those followers, reducing the write amplification for high-follower accounts.
