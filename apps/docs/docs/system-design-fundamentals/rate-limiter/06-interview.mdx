## How This Topic Appears

Rate limiting appears in system design interviews across three question formats: design questions, architecture questions, and behavioral questions. The following are the most common questions you will encounter, organized by format.

### Design Questions

**Q1**: Design a rate limiter for a distributed system.

This is the most common rate limiter question. The interviewer wants to see you move from a single-instance solution to a distributed architecture with Redis, discuss algorithm trade-offs, and handle failure modes. Expect to spend 35-40 minutes on this question. The interviewer evaluates your ability to reason about scale, consistency, and fault tolerance.

**Q2**: Design a rate limiter for a multi-tenant SaaS API.

This question adds tiered pricing and per-tenant isolation. You need to discuss per-client limits, hierarchical limiting, and configuration management. The multi-tenant aspect forces you to think about fairness and noisy neighbors. The interviewer will ask about how you handle a tenant that exceeds their quota without affecting other tenants.

**Q3**: Design an API rate limiter that handles 100,000 requests per second.

This is a scale-focused variant. The interviewer expects you to discuss sharding, caching, Lua scripts, and the difference between burst and sustained limits. Performance numbers and capacity planning are expected. The interviewer will press you on the bottlenecks: is it Redis CPU, network bandwidth, or application thread pool?

**Q4**: Design a rate limiter for a real-time messaging system.

This variant adds concurrency control to rate limiting. Messages per second limits combined with simultaneous connection limits. WebSocket considerations add complexity. The interviewer wants to see how you handle persistent connections with rate limiting, where the rate limit is on actions per time period rather than connections per time period.

### Architecture Questions

**Q5**: How would you integrate a rate limiter into an existing microservices architecture?

The interviewer wants to hear about API gateway patterns, sidecar proxies, and the trade-offs between centralization and per-service limits. Discuss Envoy, Kong, and service mesh approaches. The interviewer evaluates your understanding of deployment patterns and operational complexity.

**Q6**: How does GitHub implement its secondary rate limits?

This tests your knowledge of real-world implementations. Discuss the primary (hourly quota) and secondary (rate-based) tiers, and how they independently enforce limits. The interviewer wants to see that you follow industry practices and understand why two tiers are better than one.

**Q7**: Compare and contrast rate limiting at the API gateway versus the application layer.

The interviewer is testing your understanding of architectural layers. Discuss latency, operational complexity, flexibility, and when each approach is appropriate. Gateway-based limiting is simpler but less flexible. Application-layer limiting is more flexible but requires changes to every service.

### Behavioral Questions

**Q8**: Tell me about a time you had to deal with a traffic spike that overwhelmed your system.

This is an opportunity to discuss rate limiting as a solution. Structure your answer around the situation, the problem, the solution (implementing rate limiting), and the result. Be specific about the traffic numbers and the algorithm you chose. The interviewer wants to see that you can diagnose a problem and implement a solution under pressure.

**Q9**: How would you handle a situation where a customer complains about being rate limited unfairly?

The interviewer tests your customer-facing communication skills and your understanding of rate limiter tuning. Discuss adjusting limits, checking for false positives, and communicating retry strategies. Show empathy for the customer while explaining the need for rate limiting.

**Q10**: Your team disagrees on which rate limiting algorithm to use. How do you decide?

The interviewer tests your decision-making framework. Discuss the trade-off matrix, the specific requirements of the system, and how to prototype and measure before committing. Show that you can lead a technical decision without creating conflict.

## What Interviewer Tests

When you answer a rate limiter question, the interviewer evaluates the following:

**Depth of algorithmic knowledge**: Can you explain token bucket, leaky bucket, sliding window, and fixed window without being prompted? Do you know the memory and accuracy characteristics of each? Do you understand the difference between exact and approximate algorithms?

**Systems thinking**: Do you consider failure modes, race conditions, clock drift, and network partitions? Do you have a plan for what happens when Redis goes down? Do you think about monitoring and metrics?

**Performance awareness**: Do you know what 50,000 requests per second means for Redis throughput? Do you understand Lua script atomicity and why it matters? Can you calculate the number of Redis shards needed for a given throughput?

**Trade-off reasoning**: Can you articulate why you chose one algorithm over another with specific, measurable reasons? Not just "token bucket is popular" but "token bucket because our traffic has 10x burst-to-steady ratio and we need sub-millisecond latency."

**Communication clarity**: Can you explain rate limiting to a non-technical stakeholder? Can you use diagrams and analogies effectively? Can you structure your answer so the interviewer can follow your reasoning?

## Level-by-Level Expectations

### L3 (Entry-Level / New Grad)

**Expectation**: Understand the basic concept of rate limiting. Describe token bucket algorithm at a high level. Implement a simple in-memory rate limiter.

**Sample answer**: "A rate limiter controls how many requests a client can make in a given time period. The token bucket algorithm adds tokens at a fixed rate up to a maximum capacity. Each request consumes a token. If the bucket is empty, the request is rejected with a 429 status code. I would implement this with a dictionary mapping client IDs to token counts and timestamps."

**What to add**: Mention the sliding window variant. Show awareness that a single-instance solution does not scale. Discuss the `X-RateLimit-*` headers. Mention that you need thread safety for concurrent access.

### L4 (Mid-Level)

**Expectation**: Compare multiple algorithms. Design for distribution. Address race conditions.

**Sample answer**: "I would compare three algorithms: token bucket for burst tolerance, sliding window log for exact accuracy, and fixed window for simplicity. For a distributed system, I would use Redis with Lua scripts to ensure atomic read-evaluate-write operations. The token bucket would be my default choice because most APIs need to handle burst traffic. The Lua script handles the race condition where two concurrent requests both read the same token count. I would use Redis Cluster with consistent hashing for horizontal scaling."

**What to add**: Discuss the specific Redis commands. Mention the memory overhead of sliding window log. Talk about cache TTLs and cleanup. Show familiarity with Redis data structures.

### L5 (Senior)

**Expectation**: Design for scale with numbers. Discuss failure modes and recovery. Address multi-tenancy.

**Sample answer**: "For 100,000 requests per second, I would shard Redis across 6-8 nodes. Each request hits the L1 in-process cache first (60 percent hit rate, 0.1ms). Cache misses query Redis sharded by client ID. The Lua script runs in 3-5ms. I would use a hybrid local-global approach: local token buckets with fast decisions, periodic sync to Redis for global coordination. For multi-tenancy, I would implement hierarchical limits: global per-account limit, per-endpoint limit, and per-region limit. For failure modes, I would implement a circuit breaker with three states: closed (normal), half-open (local fallback), and open (degraded mode)."

**What to add**: Provide concrete numbers. Discuss the circuit breaker pattern for Redis failures. Mention clock drift mitigation. Talk about the CAP trade-off. Show that you have thought about operational concerns.

### L6 (Staff)

**Expectation**: Design a system that handles edge cases automatically. Discuss adaptive rate limiting. Address operational concerns.

**Sample answer**: "I would implement adaptive rate limiting that adjusts limits based on backend health. The rate limiter monitors p99 latency and error rates of the services it protects. When latency exceeds 500ms, the rate limiter automatically reduces limits by 20 percent. This closes the control loop, making the rate limiter responsive to actual system capacity. For operations, I would implement gradual limit changes through feature flags, shadow mode for testing new limits, and autonomous rollback if rejection rates spike. The system would also detect and mitigate the thundering herd of retries by tracking retry frequency per client and escalating to temporary bans."

**What to add**: Discuss the feedback loop architecture. Talk about gradual rollout of limit changes. Mention autonomous recovery and rollback strategies. Show experience with production incidents.

### L7 (Principal)

**Expectation**: Design a system that serves as a competitive advantage. Discuss industry trends and trade-offs at the highest level.

**Sample answer**: "Rate limiting is a commodity capability, but the differentiation comes from how it integrates with the developer experience and the business model. I would design a rate limiter that exposes real-time usage analytics to API consumers, enabling them to optimize their usage patterns. The limiter would support quota rollover, burst pools, and prepaid request units -- turning rate limiting from a cost center into a monetization tool. The architecture would be pluggable: different algorithms for different use cases, with the ability to switch algorithms per endpoint without deploying code. I would also invest in the global coordination layer for multi-region consistency, knowing that the trade-off between tight consistency and cross-region latency is a business decision that evolves over time."

**What to add**: Discuss the business implications. Talk about the developer experience. Show awareness of how rate limiting affects the API economy. Connect technical decisions to business outcomes.

### L8 (Distinguished / Fellow)

**Expectation**: Define the long-term technical strategy. Influence industry standards. Mentor the organization.

**Sample answer**: "Rate limiting is evolving from static rules to ML-driven dynamic throttling. I would lead the organization to adopt predictive rate limiting that uses traffic pattern analysis to anticipate spikes and pre-allocate capacity. At this level, the focus is on the rate limiter's role in the broader reliability strategy: it is one component of a multi-layered defense that includes load shedding, circuit breakers, and autoscaling. The architectural vision is self-healing infrastructure where the rate limiter learns from past incidents, continuously adjusts its parameters, and feeds insights back into the capacity planning process. The standards question is equally important: pushing for consistent rate limit headers across the industry (RFC 6585, which defines 429, is just the start) and contributing to open-source reference implementations that raise the bar for everyone."

**What to add**: Discuss the future of rate limiting. Talk about ML-driven approaches. Show industry leadership perspective. Connect to broader industry trends and standards.

## FAANG Framework

Use this 6-step framework to structure your rate limiter design answer in an interview. Following this structure ensures you cover all the important aspects without forgetting key components.

**Step 1: Requirements Clarification (2-3 minutes)**

Ask clarifying questions to scope the problem:
- What is the scale? (10 req/s or 10M req/s?)
- What type of limits? (per-user, per-IP, per-endpoint?)
- What is the latency budget? (50ms added or 5ms added?)
- What is the accuracy requirement? (exact or approximate?)
- What happens on failure? (fail open or fail closed?)
- Are there tenant tiers? (free, pro, enterprise?)
- Is this for HTTP APIs or WebSocket connections?

**Step 2: Traffic and Capacity Estimation (3-4 minutes)**

Calculate the numbers that will drive your design:
- 1 million API calls per day = roughly 12 requests per second average
- 100 million API calls per day = roughly 1,160 requests per second average
- Peak-to-average ratio: typically 3:1 to 10:1 for most APIs
- Data points: 10 million active clients * 96 bytes per client = 960 MB of rate limit state
- Redis shards needed: expected_throughput / 25,000

**Step 3: Core Algorithm Selection (3-5 minutes)**

Choose one algorithm and justify it:
- Default to token bucket unless you have a specific reason for another algorithm
- Justify with: "Token bucket because it handles burst traffic naturally, uses minimal memory (48 bytes per client), and performs well with a single Lua script on Redis"
- Mention alternatives: "If we needed exact accuracy, I would use sliding window log. If we needed to minimize memory, I would use sliding window counter."

**Step 4: High-Level Architecture Design (10-15 minutes)**

Draw the architecture and explain each component:
- Client layer, CDN, API gateway, rate limiter service, Redis cluster, backend services
- Explain the data flow for each request (the 12-phase lifecycle)
- Identify the L1/L2/L3 caching strategy
- Show the circuit breaker for Redis failures
- Discuss the monitoring and metrics collection

**Step 5: Deep Dive Into Key Components (10-15 minutes)**

Explore the details the interviewer is most interested in:
- Write the Lua script for token bucket
- Explain the multi-tier cache coherence with TTL-based invalidation
- Discuss the consistent hashing strategy for Redis sharding
- Show the retry-after mechanism with Retry-After header
- Explain the metrics collection and alerting

**Step 6: Failure Modes and Trade-offs (5-7 minutes)**

Proactively address the weaknesses of your design:
- What happens when Redis is down? (circuit breaker to local mode)
- What happens during clock skew? (use Redis TIME command)
- What happens with retry storms? (enforce Retry-After, track retry rate)
- What happens at 10x scale? (more Redis shards, more local caching)
- What is the CAP trade-off? (AP for standard, CP for critical)

## Phrase Bank

Use these phrases in your interview answers to demonstrate depth.

**On algorithms**: "I would default to the token bucket algorithm because it decouples burst capacity from sustained rate, which matches the traffic patterns of most web APIs. The sliding window log would be my choice for billing-critical limits where exact accuracy is required."

**On distribution**: "Consistent hashing across Redis shards ensures that all requests for a given client hit the same shard, which is critical for the Lua script's atomicity guarantees."

**On race conditions**: "The read-evaluate-write sequence must be atomic. I would use a Redis Lua script rather than a transaction because Lua executes without yielding to other commands, eliminating the race condition entirely."

**On caching**: "A multi-tier cache with the L1 in-process cache absorbing 60 percent of checks reduces the Redis load by 2.5x. The stale cache risk is acceptable within a 1-second TTL because the overage is bounded by the cache lifetime."

**On failure handling**: "I would implement a circuit breaker with three states: closed for normal operation, half-open for local fallback during Redis unavailability, and open for degraded mode with conservative limits after extended downtime."

**On multi-tenancy**: "Hierarchical rate limiting with per-tenant, per-tier, per-endpoint, and global limits provides defense in depth. A request must pass all applicable limits to proceed."

**On performance**: "At 50,000 requests per second, the rate limiter adds a p50 latency of 0.5 milliseconds and a p99 latency of 12 milliseconds. The bottleneck is Redis Lua execution, which handles roughly 25,000 evaluations per second per core."

**On monitoring**: "I would emit three critical metrics: allowed requests per second, rejected requests per second, and rate limiter latency. An alert would fire if the rejection rate exceeds 10 percent or if rate limiter latency exceeds 50ms at p99."

**On adaptive limiting**: "The rate limiter should monitor the backend's health and dynamically adjust limits. If the backend's p99 latency exceeds 500ms, the rate limiter reduces limits by 20 percent automatically."

**On retry handling**: "The Retry-After header is the contract between the rate limiter and the client. The server must enforce this by tracking retry frequency and escalating to temporary blocks for non-compliant clients."

**On the CAP trade-off**: "For standard API limits, I choose AP -- availability and partition tolerance. The cost of a few extra requests during a partition is lower than the cost of rejecting legitimate traffic. For billing limits, I choose CP and fail closed."

**On the sliding window counter**: "The sliding window counter approximates the exact window with two sub-window counters. The approximation error is roughly 3-5 percent with 6 sub-windows, which is acceptable for most use cases."

**On Redis sharding**: "I would use Redis Cluster with 8 shards, giving us roughly 200,000 evaluations per second. Each shard handles a subset of clients determined by consistent hashing of the client identifier."

**On idempotency**: "Idempotency-aware rate limiting prevents double-counting when clients retry with the same idempotency key. This is critical for payment APIs where a retry storm could cause double charges."

**On global rate limits**: "A global rate limit is the safety net that catches what per-client limits miss: coordinated attacks using rotated identities, or a sudden surge of legitimate traffic from many clients."

**On the leaky bucket**: "The leaky bucket enforces a strict output rate, which makes it ideal for protecting downstream components that cannot handle bursts, such as databases with limited write capacity."

**On Lua scripting**: "Redis Lua scripts are the standard mechanism for atomic rate limit operations. The script is registered with SCRIPT LOAD and executed with EVALSHA, minimizing network overhead."

**On the circuit breaker**: "The circuit breaker transitions through three states: closed for normal Redis-backed operation, half-open for local fallback, and open for degraded mode. The transition back to closed happens after Redis responds to a health check."

## Red Flags

Avoid these mistakes in your interview answers.

**Red Flag 1: Not mentioning race conditions at all.**

If you design a distributed rate limiter without discussing atomic operations, the interviewer will assume you have not thought about concurrency. Always mention the read-evaluate-write sequence and how you make it atomic using Redis Lua scripts.

**Red Flag 2: Proposing a single-instance solution for a distributed system.**

If the interviewer asks for a system that handles 100,000 requests per second and you describe in-process counters, you have failed the scale aspect. Always clarify the scale requirements and design accordingly. Move to shared state as soon as multiple instances are involved.

**Red Flag 3: Not knowing the HTTP 429 status code.**

Rate limiting is intimately tied to HTTP. If you do not know that 429 is the status code for "Too Many Requests," you lose credibility. Also know the Retry-After header and the X-RateLimit-* headers.

**Red Flag 4: Ignoring failure modes.**

A design that assumes Redis is always available is incomplete. Always discuss what happens when the data store is down, when the network partitions, or when clients retry aggressively. The circuit breaker pattern is the standard solution.

**Red Flag 5: Not providing concrete numbers.**

Saying "high throughput" is weaker than saying "50,000 requests per second with p99 latency under 12ms." Concrete numbers demonstrate that you understand the engineering reality of the system. Know the key numbers: 25K Lua evaluations per second per Redis core, 48 bytes per token bucket client, 60 percent L1 cache hit rate.

**Red Flag 6: Choosing the wrong algorithm for the stated requirements.**

If the requirements emphasize burst tolerance and you choose fixed window, the interviewer will question your judgment. Match the algorithm to the requirements explicitly. If the requirements mention exact accuracy, choose sliding window log. If they mention low memory, choose sliding window counter.

**Red Flag 7: Not discussing monitoring and observability.**

A rate limiter without metrics is a black box. Always discuss the metrics you would collect, the dashboards you would build, and the alerts you would configure. Mention Prometheus, Grafana, and the specific metrics: allowed, rejected, latency, cache hit ratio.

**Red Flag 8: Forgetting about the retry-after mechanism.**

Rate limiting is a two-way contract between the server and client. If you only discuss the server side and ignore how clients should respond to 429s, you are missing half the system. Discuss Retry-After headers, exponential backoff, jitter, and retry budgets.

**Red Flag 9: Not discussing identity extraction.**

A rate limiter is only as good as its client identification mechanism. If you do not discuss how you extract and normalize the client identity, you are missing a critical component. Discuss API keys, IP addresses, and the trade-offs between them.

**Red Flag 10: Confusing rate limiting with authentication.**

Rate limiting is not a security mechanism. If you present rate limiting as a solution to DDoS attacks or authentication bypass, the interviewer will correct you. Rate limiting manages traffic volume; security tools handle threats.

## Practice Drills

**Drill 1: Token Bucket Implementation (15 minutes)**

Implement a token bucket rate limiter in any language. Include refill logic, burst handling, and thread safety. Test it with concurrent requests from multiple threads. Verify that the bucket never goes negative and that it respects the capacity limit. Use `time.monotonic()` instead of `time.time()` to avoid clock drift issues.

**Drill 2: Redis Lua Script (20 minutes)**

Write a Redis Lua script for the token bucket algorithm. Test it with `redis-cli --eval`. Verify that concurrent calls from multiple terminals produce correct results. Measure the throughput with `redis-benchmark`. Try breaking the script by sending concurrent requests from 10 terminals simultaneously.

**Drill 3: Architecture Whiteboard (30 minutes)**

Without looking at notes, design a distributed rate limiter on a whiteboard or paper. Include: load balancer, API gateway, Redis cluster with 6 shards, L1 cache, L2 cache, circuit breaker, and monitoring. Label latency budgets for each component. Time yourself and aim for under 30 minutes.

**Drill 4: Trade-off Table (15 minutes)**

Create a table comparing token bucket, sliding window log, and sliding window counter across these dimensions: memory per client, accuracy, latency, Redis operations, implementation complexity, and burst handling. Memorize the key numbers: 48 bytes, 96 bytes per request, 96 bytes fixed.

**Drill 5: Mock Interview (45 minutes)**

Have a peer ask you the "Design a rate limiter for a distributed system" question. Record yourself. Review your answer for clarity, completeness, and the ten red flags above. Repeat until you can deliver a structured answer in under 35 minutes without notes. Focus on the 6-step FAANG framework.

## Mock Interview Walkthrough

The following is a complete mock interview transcript for the question "Design a rate limiter for a distributed system." Study the structure, not the specific words. Your delivery will differ based on your experience level.

**Interviewer**: Design a rate limiter for a distributed system.

**Candidate** (Step 1: Requirements): Before I design, I need to clarify the requirements. What scale are we targeting? What kind of rate limits -- per user, per IP, per endpoint? What is the latency budget for the rate limit check? And what happens when the rate limiter's data store is unavailable?

**Interviewer**: Let's say 100,000 requests per second. Per user limits with API keys. Latency budget is under 10ms added. The system should tolerate the data store being down for up to 30 seconds.

**Candidate** (Step 2: Estimation): At 100,000 requests per second, assuming 10 million active users each making 10 requests per second average, we need a rate limiter that handles 100,000 decisions per second. Using the token bucket algorithm at 48 bytes per client, we need 48 MB for token bucket state for 1 million active clients. For Redis, each core handles 25,000 Lua evaluations per second, so we need 4 Redis shards minimum. With 30 percent headroom, 6 shards.

**Candidate** (Step 3: Algorithm): I choose the token bucket algorithm. My reasoning: the traffic pattern for most APIs is bursty -- clients are idle then send requests in bursts. The token bucket handles this naturally by accumulating tokens during idle periods. It uses minimal memory (48 bytes per client) and performs well with a single Lua script on Redis. For comparison, the sliding window log would be more accurate but would use 96 bytes per request, which at 100 requests per client is 9,600 bytes per client -- 200x more memory.

**Candidate** (Step 4: Architecture): The architecture has four layers. Layer 1: clients connect through a CDN that caches 429 responses at the edge. Layer 2: an API gateway (Envoy or Kong) runs the rate limiter as a plugin. Layer 3: the rate limiter plugin checks an L1 in-process cache, then an L2 Memcached cluster, then an L3 Redis cluster. Layer 4: the Redis cluster stores the token bucket state with consistent hashing for client key distribution.

**Candidate** (Step 5: Deep Dive): The Redis Lua script for the token bucket is the core of the system. The script accepts the client key, capacity, refill rate, current timestamp, and cost. It reads the current token count and last refill timestamp from a Redis hash. It calculates the elapsed time and adds the appropriate number of tokens. If enough tokens are available, it decrements and returns allowed. If not, it returns rejected. The entire operation is atomic because Redis Lua executes without interruption.

**Candidate** (Step 6: Failure Modes): For Redis unavailability, I implement a circuit breaker with three states. Closed: normal operation with Redis. Half-open: after 500ms of Redis failures, switch to local in-memory token buckets with 50 percent of normal limits. Open: after 10 seconds of Redis failures, allow all requests but log every decision for audit. The circuit breaker automatically reconnects when Redis responds to health checks.

**Interviewer**: How would you handle a client that rotates through multiple API keys to bypass per-key limits?

**Candidate**: I would implement two additional protections. First, a global rate limit that aggregates all traffic regardless of key. This catches the total volume. Second, I would add a client fingerprint that combines the IP range, user agent, and request pattern. If 10 keys from the same /24 IP range all hit exactly 99 percent of their limit simultaneously, I would flag them as a coordinated group and apply a shared limit. The global limit is the primary defense; the fingerprint is the secondary.

## Advanced Interview Scenarios

### Scenario: Rate Limiter for a GraphQL API

GraphQL APIs pose a unique challenge because a single request can query multiple resources, each with different costs. The rate limiter must account for the total cost of the query, not just the request count.

**Interview approach**: Explain that you would implement cost-based rate limiting. Each GraphQL field has a cost weight. The rate limiter computes the total cost of the query by traversing the AST (abstract syntax tree) and summing the field costs. The token bucket consumes cost tokens instead of request tokens. Complex queries that request 10 fields and 3 nested relations cost more than simple queries that request 1 field.

**Sample answer**: "For GraphQL, I would implement cost-based rate limiting. Each field in the schema has a configured cost. The rate limiter parses the GraphQL query, computes the total cost by summing the requested field costs, and consumes that many tokens from the client's bucket. A query requesting id and name costs 2 tokens. A query requesting id, name, posts, comments, likes, and nested user data costs 50 tokens. This ensures that expensive queries are limited proportionally."

### Scenario: Rate Limiter for a File Upload API

File upload APIs have different characteristics than standard APIs. Upload requests are large (100 MB+), long-running (10+ seconds), and resource-intensive (bandwidth, disk I/O).

**Interview approach**: Explain that you would combine rate limiting (uploads per minute) with concurrency limiting (simultaneous uploads). The rate limiter limits the number of upload requests per client. A separate concurrency limiter limits the number of simultaneous in-flight uploads across all clients. The concurrency limit is based on available bandwidth and disk I/O capacity.

**Sample answer**: "For file uploads, I would combine rate limiting with concurrency limiting. The rate limiter controls the arrival rate: 10 upload requests per minute per client. The concurrency limiter controls the simultaneous upload count: 50 uploads in progress at any time, based on 1 Gbps bandwidth divided by 20 Mbps per upload. The concurrency limiter uses a semaphore stored in Redis. When a client starts an upload, it acquires a permit. When the upload completes, it releases the permit. If no permits are available, the upload is queued or rejected."

### Scenario: Rate Limiter with Dynamic Limit Adjustment

Some environments require rate limits that adjust dynamically based on time of day, day of week, or current system load. A rate limiter for a stock trading API needs higher limits during market hours and lower limits after hours.

**Interview approach**: Explain that you would store rate limit configuration in a central configuration service with time-based rules. The rate limiter fetches the current configuration every 60 seconds. The configuration includes time-expressed limits: "Monday-Friday 9:30-16:00: 100 req/min, other times: 20 req/min."

**Sample answer**: "I would implement time-based rate limits using a configuration service. The rate limiter fetches its configuration every 60 seconds from a central store. The configuration contains multiple limit rules with time expressions. The rate limiter evaluates the current time against the rules and selects the active rule. For example, a stock trading API might have limits of 100 req/min during market hours, 20 req/min after hours, and 5 req/min on weekends. The configuration is versioned and supports gradual rollouts through feature flags."

## Expanded Phrase Bank

Additional phrases for specific interview scenarios:

**On global limits**: "The global limit acts as a circuit breaker for the entire system. It is set at 80 percent of the backend's measured capacity and catches coordinated attacks or traffic surges that per-client limits miss."

**On local caching**: "The L1 cache uses a TTL of 1 second. This means the maximum overage is 1 second of traffic at the client's peak rate. For a client limited to 100 req/s, the overage is at most 100 requests during the cache TTL window."

**On rate limit headers**: "The Retry-After header is more important than the rate limit headers for client behavior. Clients that respect Retry-After avoid retry storms. I would design the client SDK to read and respect Retry-After before considering the rate limit headers."

**On algorithm selection for a specific use case**: "For a payment API, I would choose sliding window log for exact accuracy because a single overage could result in a double charge. For a social media API, I would choose token bucket for burst tolerance because users expect to send bursts of activity after being idle."

**On Redis cluster sizing**: "The Redis cluster is sized based on the peak throughput divided by 25,000 evaluations per second per shard, plus 30 percent headroom. For 500,000 req/s peak, that is 500,000 / (25,000 * 0.7) = 28.6, so 30 shards."

**On monitoring the rate limiter**: "I monitor three critical metrics: the rejection rate (alerts above 10 percent), the rate limiter latency (alerts above 50ms p99), and the cache hit ratio (alerts below 50 percent). These three metrics give a complete picture of rate limiter health."

**On testing rate limit changes**: "I use shadow mode to test new rate limit policies before enforcement. The shadow mode logs what would have been rejected without actually rejecting. I compare the shadow rejection rate to the actual rejection rate for at least 24 hours before enabling enforcement."

**On handling misconfigured clients**: "If a client exceeds their limit consistently for more than 1 hour, I flag their account for review. The rate limit is temporarily doubled to prevent false positives, and the account team contacts the client to understand their use case."

**On the difference between rate limiting and throttling**: "Rate limiting is the policy: 'max 100 requests per minute.' Throttling is the action: 'reject this request because the client has exceeded 100 requests this minute.' The rate limiter implements the policy by applying throttling actions."

**On rate limit cost per request**: "The cost of a rate limit check is roughly 0.05 micro-cents per request for the L1 cache hit, 0.5 micro-cents for the L2 cache hit, and 5 micro-cents for the Redis hit. At 100,000 req/s, the total cost is roughly $10-15 per month for rate limit infrastructure."

## Expanded Red Flags

Additional red flags to avoid:

**Red Flag 11: Not discussing the CDN layer.**

The CDN can cache 429 responses and serve them without reaching the origin. This reduces the load on the rate limiter during traffic spikes. Always mention CDN caching as the first defense layer.

**Red Flag 12: Proposing Redis without discussing persistence.**

Redis without persistence loses all rate limit state on restart. Always discuss AOF persistence with `appendfsync everysec`. Mention that Redis is configured to survive restarts without losing critical state.

**Red Flag 13: Not explaining the retry budget.**

Clients should not retry indefinitely. A retry budget of 3-5 attempts with exponential backoff is standard. The server should also enforce a retry budget. Discuss both sides.

**Red Flag 14: Confusing capacity with refill rate.**

Capacity is the maximum burst size. Refill rate is the sustained throughput. Mixing them up shows a fundamental misunderstanding of the token bucket algorithm. Clearly explain both parameters.

**Red Flag 15: Ignoring the warm-up problem.**

After a Redis restart or a cold start, the rate limiter has no state. All clients get a full quota simultaneously. Discuss pre-warming Redis or applying conservative limits during the first window after restart.

**Red Flag 16: Not discussing the client identification strategy.**

A rate limiter is useless without accurate client identification. Discuss the trade-offs between IP-based, API-key-based, and token-based identification. Show awareness of NAT and carrier-grade NAT issues.

**Red Flag 17: Designing a system that requires all-or-nothing Redis availability.**

A single Redis node failure should not take down the entire rate limiter. Discuss Redis Cluster with `cluster-require-full-coverage no` so the cluster continues operating with degraded capacity.

**Red Flag 18: Proposing synchronous cross-region writes.**

Synchronous cross-region writes add 50-100ms of latency. Always propose asynchronous coordination with local-first decisions for multi-region deployments. Mention the overage bound calculation.

## Behavioral Question Deep Dive

Behavioral questions about rate limiting require specific, structured responses. Use the STAR format (Situation, Task, Action, Result) with concrete technical details.

### Question: Tell me about a time you handled a traffic spike that overwhelmed your system.

**Situation**: "Our API was averaging 10,000 requests per second with occasional spikes to 30,000 during product launches. We had no rate limiter. During one launch, a partner integration went into a retry loop and sent 200,000 requests per second. The API gateway CPU hit 100 percent, and all 50 backend instances returned 503 errors for 15 minutes."

**Task**: "I needed to prevent a single misbehaving partner from taking down the entire platform. The solution had to work within 2 weeks and could not require changes to partner applications."

**Action**: "I implemented a token bucket rate limiter using Redis Lua scripts. Each partner API key got a bucket with capacity 10,000 and refill rate 5,000 per second. I deployed it as a plugin on the Kong API gateway, which intercepted all traffic before it reached the backend services. The Lua script ran atomically on Redis, ensuring no race conditions. I added L1 in-process caching with a 1-second TTL to keep p99 latency under 5ms. I also implemented a circuit breaker that fell back to local limiting if Redis was unreachable."

**Result**: "The rate limiter handled the next product launch without issues. The same partner integration failed again, but this time the rate limiter rejected 95 percent of their requests (HTTP 429) while allowing all other partners through. Backend CPU stayed under 40 percent. The partner fixed their retry logic after we shared the rejection logs. The rate limiter added less than 1ms to p50 latency and 8ms to p99 latency."

### Question: How would you handle a customer complaining about being rate limited unfairly?

**Situation**: "A premium enterprise customer contacts support saying their requests are being rejected with 429 errors. They claim they are within their 10,000 req/min limit."

**Task**: "I need to diagnose the issue, determine if the rate limiter is incorrectly configured, and resolve the customer's complaint."

**Action**: "I check the rate limiter logs for the customer's API key. I look at the actual request count for the last 60 minutes versus their limit. I check if the customer is hitting a different limit than expected -- perhaps a per-endpoint limit instead of the global per-client limit. I check if the customer has multiple services sharing the same API key, each unaware of the others' usage. I check the Retry-After headers in the 429 responses to see if the customer is ignoring retry guidance. I check if the customer's traffic pattern shows a burst that exceeded their capacity. I also check the rate limiter metrics for clock drift or state corruption that could cause false positives."

**Result**: "Based on the logs, I identify the root cause. For a legitimate false positive, I adjust the rate limit configuration and credit the customer's quota. For a misconfigured client, I share the usage patterns and recommend best practices. For a genuine overuse, I discuss upgrading their tier. In every case, I provide a written summary of the findings and the resolution."

### Question: Your team disagrees on which rate limiting algorithm to use. How do you decide?

**Situation**: "Our team is designing rate limiting for a new API platform. Half the team favors token bucket. The other half favors sliding window log."

**Task**: "I need to lead the decision-making process and get consensus on the algorithm choice."

**Action**: "I organize a decision framework based on four criteria: accuracy requirements, memory budget, latency requirements, and burst handling. I create a comparison table with each algorithm's characteristics. I propose a prototype phase where we implement both algorithms and measure p50/p99 latency, memory consumption, and accuracy against real traffic patterns. I schedule a decision meeting after the prototype phase, where we review the data and make a choice."

**Result**: "The prototype showed that token bucket had 0.5ms p50 latency versus 1.5ms for sliding window log, used 48 bytes per client versus 9,600 bytes, and handled bursts correctly. The sliding window log offered exact accuracy, but our use case tolerates up to 5 percent overage. The team agreed on token bucket. The decision document is used as a reference for future API designs."

## Common Interviewer Pushbacks and How to Handle Them

**Pushback 1**: "Your solution only works if Redis is available. What if Redis goes down?"

Your response: "I designed a circuit breaker with three states. In the half-open state, the rate limiter falls back to local in-memory token buckets with 50 percent of normal limits. This keeps the system operational during a Redis outage. The circuit breaker automatically transitions back to closed state when Redis responds to health checks."

**Pushback 2**: "Redis Lua scripts block the server. How does this affect performance?"

Your response: "Lua scripts execute atomically and block other commands, but the execution time is under 50 microseconds for a token bucket check. At 25,000 evaluations per second, the script uses roughly 1.25 seconds of CPU time per second per core. This leaves 75 percent CPU capacity for other Redis operations. If the script becomes a bottleneck, I shard Redis across more nodes."

**Pushback 3**: "Your L1 cache allows overage. Why not use strict consistency?"

Your response: "The L1 cache TTL is 1 second, so the maximum overage is 1 second of traffic at the client's peak rate. For a client limited to 100 req/s, the overage is at most 100 requests. This is acceptable for standard API rate limiting. The trade-off is that 60 percent of checks avoid Redis entirely, reducing Redis load by 2.5x and keeping p99 latency under 12ms."

**Pushback 4**: "How do you handle clients that rotate through API keys?"

Your response: "I implement a two-tier defense. First, a global rate limit that aggregates all traffic regardless of key. The global limit is set at 80 percent of backend capacity. Second, I cluster API keys by IP range and user agent. If 10 keys from the same /24 IP range all hit their individual limits simultaneously, I flag them as a coordinated group and apply a shared limit."

**Pushback 5**: "Your design seems complex. Why not use a simple in-memory counter?"

Your response: "An in-memory counter works for a single instance, but our system has 50 instances behind a load balancer. Without shared state, each instance has its own counter, and a client can send 50x their limit by distributing requests across instances. The Redis-backed design adds complexity but is necessary for consistent rate limiting across a distributed system."

## Rapid Fire Questions

Practice answering these rapid-fire questions in under 30 seconds each:

**Q1**: What is the HTTP status code for rate limiting?
A: 429 Too Many Requests (RFC 6585).

**Q2**: What is the difference between a token bucket and a leaky bucket?
A: Token bucket allows bursts by accumulating tokens. Leaky bucket enforces a strict output rate with no burst.

**Q3**: What is the boundary burst problem?
A: In fixed window rate limiting, a client can send a full quota just before the window resets and another full quota just after, effectively doubling the limit.

**Q4**: How many requests per second can a single Redis core handle for Lua rate limit checks?
A: Roughly 25,000 evaluations per second.

**Q5**: What is the memory per client for token bucket?
A: 48 bytes (two float64 values: tokens and last_refill).

**Q6**: What is the purpose of the Retry-After header?
A: It tells the client how many seconds to wait before retrying a rejected request.

**Q7**: What is the difference between rate limiting and load shedding?
A: Rate limiting operates per client identity. Load shedding operates on total system load regardless of source.

**Q8**: What is the sliding window counter approximation error?
A: Roughly 3-5 percent with 6 sub-windows, 0.5 percent with 60 sub-windows.

**Q9**: What is the L1 cache hit rate in a typical production rate limiter?
A: 50-70 percent of requests are served from the in-process cache.

**Q10**: What is the circuit breaker pattern for rate limiters?
A: Three states: closed (Redis normal), half-open (local fallback), open (degraded mode).
