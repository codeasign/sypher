## The Problem Story

Your cache has 4 GB of memory. You have 20 GB of hot data. Something has to go.

This is the fundamental tension in every cache: memory is finite, data is infinite. Every second, your application writes new data into the cache -- user sessions, API responses, database query results, rendered HTML fragments. But the cache does not grow. It stays at 4 GB. When the 4 GB is full and a new item arrives, something must be removed to make room.

The wrong decision here is catastrophic.

If you evict data that is about to be requested again, you pay the penalty of a cache miss. The request goes to the database, which takes 10-100 milliseconds instead of 0.5 milliseconds. On a high-traffic system with 100,000 requests per second, this turns a 50% cache hit ratio into a 10% hit ratio. Response times skyrocket from 5 milliseconds to 200 milliseconds. Databases that were coasting at 20% CPU spike to 95% CPU. Users refresh, compounding the load. The system cascades.

The pain points of poor cache eviction:

- **Cache thrashing.** The cache constantly evicts and reloads the same data. Every evicted item gets requested again seconds later, requiring another database fetch. The cache becomes a revolving door.

- **Cache pollution.** One-time-use data floods the cache, crowding out frequently accessed data. A batch job that scans 1 million records fills the cache with data that will never be requested again, evicting the 10,000 hot records your users actually need.

- **Memory pressure spikes.** When the eviction policy falls behind (too many writes, too few evictions), memory usage climbs past the configured limit. The operating system starts swapping. The cache process gets OOM-killed.

- **Uneven shard distribution.** In a clustered cache, one shard receives more writes than others. Its eviction rate jumps, triggering a higher miss rate on that shard. The slow shard becomes the bottleneck for the entire cluster.

- **Cold start storms.** After a cache restart or deployment, the cache is empty. Every request is a miss. Without a proper eviction policy, the first wave of traffic fills the cache with whatever arrives first -- even if that data is never requested again.

The core observation: eviction is not a garbage collection detail. It is the primary control knob for cache performance. The eviction policy determines what stays in the cache, and what stays in the cache determines the hit ratio.

## The Insight

Cache eviction is the strategy for deciding which data to remove when the cache runs out of space. Every cache has a fixed memory budget. Eviction is the mechanism that enforces that budget.

The mental model is a crowded bookshelf. Your bookshelf has space for 50 books. You own 200 books. Every time you buy a new book, you must decide which book to remove. Do you remove the book you read longest ago (LRU)? Do you remove the book you read least often (LFU)? Do you remove the oldest book on the shelf (FIFO)? Do you remove a random book? The answer depends on how you use your books.

```
Full bookshelf (50 books) ──> New book arrives ──> Must remove one
    │
    ├── LRU: Remove the book you touched longest ago
    ├── LFU: Remove the book you read least often
    ├── FIFO: Remove the book that has been on the shelf longest
    ├── MRU: Remove the book you just touched (you are done with it)
    ├── Random: Remove any book
    └── TTL: Remove books that expired (like a library loan)
```

The insight is that no single policy works for every access pattern. A cache that serves user profiles (where a small set of profiles gets 90% of traffic) benefits from LFU or LRU. A cache that serves CDN assets (where each asset gets a burst of traffic then is never requested again) benefits from TTL-based eviction. A cache that stores database query results (where access is unpredictable) might work fine with random eviction.

The goal of eviction is to maximize the cache hit ratio -- the fraction of requests that find their data in the cache. The optimal eviction policy would evict the item that will be requested farthest in the future. This is Belady's optimal algorithm, and it requires knowledge of the future. Real eviction policies approximate Belady by using past access patterns to predict future access.

## Core Vocabulary

**Eviction policy.** The algorithm that decides which cache entry to remove when the cache is full. The policy is the single most important determinant of cache performance. A good policy matches the access pattern of the workload.

**LRU (Least Recently Used).** Evicts the entry that was accessed farthest in the past. Based on the principle of temporal locality: if you accessed data recently, you are likely to access it again soon. LRU is the default policy in most caching systems. It works well for workloads with strong temporal locality (user sessions, trending content).

**LFU (Least Frequently Used).** Evicts the entry with the lowest access frequency. Based on the principle of frequency: data accessed often is more valuable than data accessed rarely. LFU works well for workloads with stable popularity distributions (top-N product listings, reference data). LFU struggles with changing access patterns -- recently popular data may take time to accumulate enough frequency to stay in the cache.

**FIFO (First In First Out).** Evicts the entry that was inserted longest ago. Simple, low overhead. No consideration of access patterns. FIFO is useful when data has a natural lifespan and access frequency is not relevant (event logs, time-series data). It performs poorly on most general workloads because it evicts hot data that happens to be old.

**TTL-based eviction.** Evicts entries whose time-to-live has expired. Every entry has a fixed expiration time. TTL is the primary mechanism for data freshness. It is not a true eviction policy (it does not respond to memory pressure), but it reduces the burden on the eviction policy by removing stale data proactively.

**Random eviction.** Evicts a randomly selected entry. Surprising effective in practice. Random eviction is scan-resistant (a sequential scan does not pollute the cache). It is simple to implement and has O(1) overhead. Used in early versions of Memcached.

**MRU (Most Recently Used).** Evicts the most recently accessed entry. Useful for workloads where the most recently accessed data is least likely to be accessed again (scan-like patterns, sequential access). MRU is the opposite of LRU and is rarely the right choice for standard caching.

**Clock algorithm.** An approximation of LRU that uses a circular buffer with reference bits. Each entry has a reference bit that is set on access. The clock hand sweeps through the buffer, clearing reference bits. When it finds a cleared bit, it evicts that entry. The clock algorithm avoids the overhead of maintaining a doubly-linked list. Used in the Linux kernel page cache and many database buffer pools.

**SLRU (Segmented LRU).** A two-tier LRU that separates probationary and protected segments. New entries go into the probationary segment. If accessed again, they are promoted to the protected segment. SLRU prevents one-hit-wonders from evicting frequently accessed data. Used in Varnish, Nginx, and many HTTP caches.

**2Q (Two Queue).** A variant of LRU that maintains two queues: a FIFO queue for new entries and an LRU queue for frequently accessed entries. Entries that survive the FIFO queue are promoted to the LRU queue. 2Q is scan-resistant and approximates the optimal replacement policy better than plain LRU. Used in the IBM DB2 buffer pool.

**ARC (Adaptive Replacement Cache).** A self-tuning eviction policy that dynamically balances between recency and frequency. ARC maintains two LRU lists (recently accessed, frequently accessed) and two ghost lists (recently evicted entries). It adapts to changing access patterns by adjusting the balance between the two lists. Developed at IBM Almaden Research Center. Used in ZFS, PostgreSQL, and Varnish.

**LIRS (Low Inter-reference Recency Set).** An eviction policy that uses the reuse distance (number of distinct accesses between two accesses of the same item) rather than recency. LIRS performs better than LRU on workloads with large working sets and limited temporal locality. Used in the Linux kernel page cache (as an alternative to the clock algorithm).

**Cache pollution.** The phenomenon where low-value data fills the cache at the expense of high-value data. One-hit-wonders (data accessed once and never again) are the primary cause. Scan-resistant policies (ARC, 2Q, SLRU) explicitly defend against pollution.

**Working set.** The set of data that the application accesses in a given time window. The ideal cache is sized to hold the working set. If the working set exceeds the cache size, the eviction rate rises and the hit ratio drops. Understanding the working set is essential for capacity planning.

**Scan resistance.** The ability of an eviction policy to survive a sequential scan without polluting the cache. A sequential scan of 1 million records should not evict hot data. LRU is not scan-resistant: a single sequential scan can evict the entire hot set. 2Q, ARC, and SLRU are scan-resistant.

**Ghost entry.** A metadata-only record of an evicted entry. Ghost entries track which data was recently evicted. Used by adaptive policies (ARC, 2Q) to detect changes in access patterns without storing the actual data. If a ghost entry is hit, the policy adjusts its balance toward including more of that type of data.

**Eviction rate.** The number of evictions per unit time. A high eviction rate indicates that the cache is too small for the working set, or that the eviction policy is poorly matched to the access pattern. Monitoring the eviction rate is the primary diagnostic for cache health.

**Hit ratio.** The fraction of cache accesses that find their target in the cache. The hit ratio is the primary metric of cache effectiveness. A 90% hit ratio means 90% of requests are served from cache (fast) and 10% go to the backing store (slow). The eviction policy directly impacts the hit ratio.

**Belady's optimal algorithm.** The theoretical optimal eviction policy: evict the entry that will be accessed farthest in the future. Belady is the gold standard against which real policies are measured. It cannot be implemented in practice because it requires knowledge of future access patterns.

## How It Actually Works

The eviction lifecycle involves a sequence of checks and operations that happen on every write to a full cache.

### Eviction Lifecycle Steps

1. **Write request arrives.** The application sends a SET key/value command. The cache engine receives the command.

2. **Size check.** The engine checks whether the new entry fits within the current memory budget. This involves computing the total memory usage and comparing it against the `maxmemory` limit.

3. **Threshold evaluation.** If the current memory usage plus the new entry size exceeds the limit, eviction is triggered. Some systems (Redis) use a watermark: eviction triggers at `maxmemory` but does not stop until memory drops below a lower watermark.

4. **Policy selection.** The engine consults the configured eviction policy. This determines which entries are candidates for eviction and how the victim is selected.

5. **Candidate enumeration.** The engine iterates over a sample of entries (or the full entry set) to find eviction candidates. The sample size determines accuracy versus performance trade-off.

6. **Victim selection.** Using the policy algorithm, the engine selects a victim entry. LRU picks the entry with the oldest last-access time. LFU picks the entry with the lowest frequency count. FIFO picks the entry with the oldest insertion time.

7. **Cleanup and notification.** If the victim entry has pending operations (write-back dirty data, open connections), the engine performs cleanup. Some systems fire eviction notification events to listeners.

8. **Memory reclamation.** The victim entry is removed from the hash table and its memory is freed. Linked list pointers are updated (for LRU). Frequency counters are adjusted (for LFU).

9. **New entry insertion.** The new entry is inserted into the hash table, linked list (for LRU), and frequency bucket (for LFU).

10. **Post-eviction check.** The engine checks whether memory is now below the limit. If not, it repeats the eviction cycle. This can lead to cascading evictions on large writes.

## Variants

### LRU (Least Recently Used)

LRU evicts the entry that was accessed farthest in the past. It is the most widely used eviction policy and the default in most caching systems (Redis, Memcached, browser caches).

The implementation uses a doubly-linked list plus a hash map. On every access, the entry is moved to the head of the list. On eviction, the tail entry is removed. Both operations are O(1).

LRU works well when access has temporal locality: data accessed recently is likely to be accessed again. This describes most user-facing workloads (sessions, feeds, profiles).

LRU fails badly on scan workloads. A single sequential scan of 1 million records will traverse every entry through the linked list, evicting the entire hot set.

**When to use:** User sessions, API response caching, database query result caching, content delivery networks.

**When to avoid:** Batch processing workloads, data analytics pipelines, any workload with frequent sequential scans.

### LFU (Least Frequently Used)

LFU evicts the entry with the lowest access frequency. Each entry maintains a counter that increments on every access. On eviction, the entry with the lowest count is removed.

The naive implementation requires O(log n) to find the minimum frequency. Optimized implementations use frequency partitioning: a hash map of frequency buckets, where each bucket contains all entries with that frequency. This gives O(1) eviction.

LFU excels at workloads with stable popularity: the most popular items stay in the cache indefinitely, while rarely accessed items are evicted. This makes LFU ideal for reference data, configuration values, and top-N listings.

LFU has a critical weakness: it cannot adapt to changing access patterns. An entry that was popular yesterday but is cold today still has a high frequency count and will remain in the cache, blocking new hot data.

**When to use:** Reference data, configuration values, top-N product listings, media libraries with stable popularity.

**When to avoid:** News feeds, trending content, any workload where popularity changes rapidly.

### FIFO (First In First Out)

FIFO evicts the entry that was inserted longest ago. It maintains a simple queue of entries in insertion order. On eviction, the front of the queue is removed. On insertion, the new entry is added to the back.

FIFO is the simplest eviction policy. It has O(1) overhead and requires no access tracking. There is no per-entry metadata beyond insertion time.

FIFO performs poorly on most workloads because it ignores access patterns entirely. A hot entry that happens to be old will be evicted regardless of how frequently it is accessed.

**When to use:** Event caches, logging buffers, any workload where data has a natural lifespan and access frequency is irrelevant.

**When to avoid:** Most general-purpose caching workloads.

### MRU (Most Recently Used)

MRU evicts the most recently accessed entry. It is the opposite of LRU and is useful for specific access patterns.

MRU is effective when the most recently accessed data is least likely to be accessed again. This describes sequential access patterns (video streaming, paginated results). In a video stream, the currently playing segment is being consumed right now and will not be requested again -- but the next segment will be.

**When to use:** Video streaming, sequential page navigation, playlist processing.

**When to avoid:** General user-facing caches where recency correlates with re-access.

### Random Eviction

Random eviction picks a random entry to evict. Despite its simplicity, it performs surprisingly well in practice. It is scan-resistant by definition: a sequential scan does not preferentially evict hot data.

Random eviction has O(1) overhead and requires no per-entry metadata. This makes it ideal for memory-constrained environments.

The performance of random eviction is typically within 10-20% of LRU on most workloads. For workloads with weak temporal locality, random eviction can outperform LRU.

**When to use:** Memory-constrained caches, workloads without strong access patterns, as a fallback when other policies are too expensive.

**When to avoid:** Workloads with strong temporal locality where every percentage point of hit ratio matters.

### ARC (Adaptive Replacement Cache)

ARC maintains two LRU lists: one for recently accessed entries and one for frequently accessed entries. It also maintains two ghost lists: recently evicted entries from each list.

ARC dynamically adjusts the balance between the two lists based on access patterns. If recently evicted entries from the recent list are being accessed again (indicating a scan-resistant workload), ARC shifts balance toward the recent list. If evicted entries from the frequent list are being accessed, ARC shifts toward the frequent list.

ARC is self-tuning and requires no manual configuration. It adapts to changing access patterns over time. It has O(1) overhead but requires more memory per entry (dual list pointers, ghost list entries).

**When to use:** General-purpose caching with unknown or changing access patterns, production systems where manual tuning is impractical.

**When to avoid:** Memory-constrained environments (ghost entries add overhead), or when the access pattern is well understood and a simpler policy suffices.

### 2Q (Two Queue)

2Q splits the cache into two queues: a FIFO queue for new entries ("Am" queue) and an LRU queue for frequently accessed entries ("A1" queue). New entries enter the FIFO queue. If an entry is accessed again while in the FIFO queue, it is promoted to the LRU queue. Eviction targets the FIFO queue first.

2Q provides scan resistance: a sequential scan fills the FIFO queue but never promotes to the LRU queue, protecting the hot entries in the LRU queue.

**When to use:** Workloads with occasional scans, CDN caching, proxy caching.

**When to avoid:** Workloads where every entry is accessed multiple times (the FIFO queue adds unnecessary complexity).

### SLRU (Segmented LRU)

SLRU divides the cache into two segments: a probationary segment (typically 20% of cache) and a protected segment (typically 80%). New entries enter the probationary segment. If accessed again, they are promoted to the protected segment. Eviction targets the probationary segment first.

SLRU prevents one-hit-wonders from reaching the protected segment. Only data that demonstrates re-access value reaches the protected area.

**When to use:** HTTP caches (Varnish, Nginx), proxy caches, workloads with high one-hit-wonder rates.

**When to avoid:** Workloads where every entry is accessed multiple times (the probationary segment adds latency without benefit).

## Mental Model Check

### Question 1

You have a cache that stores user session data. Each user accesses their session approximately 10 times per minute during a 30-minute session, then never again. Which eviction policy works best?

**Answer:** LRU. User sessions have strong temporal locality: within a session, you access the same session data repeatedly. LRU keeps active sessions in the cache. When the session ends (no more accesses), the session data ages out naturally. LFU would keep expired session data for too long because each session accumulated 300+ accesses before ending.

### Question 2

Your cache receives 10,000 writes per second but only 5,000 reads per second. The cache hit ratio is 20%. What is happening?

**Answer:** The write-to-read ratio is 2:1, meaning more data enters the cache than is read. The cache is likely being flooded with data that is never read again (one-hit-wonders). The eviction policy is constantly removing data to make room for new writes, but the new writes are not being read. You need a scan-resistant eviction policy (2Q, SLRU, ARC) that prevents one-hit-wonders from evicting the small amount of data that is actually read.

### Question 3

You switch your cache from LRU to LFU. The hit ratio drops from 85% to 40%. Why?

**Answer:** Your workload is dominated by rapidly changing popular content (news, trending videos). LRU captures the current hot set because it responds to recency. LFU retains entries with high historical frequency even after they become cold. The LFU cache is filled with yesterday's popular content, leaving no room for today's trending content. LFU favors the accumulated past; LRU favors the immediate present.

### Question 4

Your cache has 8 GB of memory. Your monitoring shows 500 evictions per second. Is this a problem?

**Answer:** It depends on the cache size relative to the working set. If your working set is 6 GB and the cache is 8 GB, 500 evictions per second is normal -- the cache is large enough to hold the working set with room to spare. If your working set is 20 GB and the cache is 8 GB, 500 evictions per second means the cache is thrashing: every evicted item will be requested again shortly, causing a database hit. The key metric is not the raw eviction rate but the combination of eviction rate and cache hit ratio. If hit ratio is above 90%, evictions are fine. If hit ratio is below 50%, evictions are a problem.

### Question 5

You need to choose an eviction policy for a new product, but you do not know the access pattern. The workload could be session-oriented, popularity-oriented, or scan-oriented depending on how customers use the product. What do you do?

**Answer:** Use ARC (Adaptive Replacement Cache). ARC is self-tuning and adapts to changing access patterns without manual configuration. It provides scan resistance (defending against pollution), adapts between recency and frequency, and performs within 5% of the optimal static policy on most workloads. If ARC is not available (it requires specific library support), use 2Q or SLRU as a good compromise. If you must use a simple policy, start with LRU and monitor the eviction rate and hit ratio closely. Be prepared to switch policies when you understand the actual access pattern.
