## The Problem Story

Your team just deployed a new microservice that handles payment processing. The first week goes smoothly. Then, at 2:47 AM on a Tuesday, your phone buzzes. And buzzes again. And again. By the time you stumble to your laptop, five different alerts have fired: p99 latency crossed 2 seconds, error rate jumped to 12%, CPU is at 94%, memory is climbing, and the database connection pool is nearly exhausted.

You open five different monitoring tools. One shows latency. Another shows errors. A third shows infrastructure metrics. A fourth shows business metrics. The fifth shows logs. None of them talk to each other. You spend 18 minutes cross-referencing timestamps across browser tabs, trying to figure out whether the database spike caused the latency or the latency caused the connection pool to fill.

This is the problem that dashboards solve. A dashboard is a single pane of glass that brings together signals from every layer of your system: infrastructure, application, database, network, and business. It correlates metrics in time so you can see cause and effect at a glance. When your phone buzzes at 2:47 AM, you open one dashboard, not five tools.

Now imagine the opposite scenario. You open a single dashboard. The top row shows three large panels: system health (green checkmark), error rate (0.4%, well under the 1% threshold marked as a red line), and p99 latency (280ms, under the 500ms threshold). The second row shows a per-service breakdown with sparklines that reveal the payments service error rate is rising but still acceptable. You drill into the payments dashboard, see that one host has elevated CPU, and navigate to the host dashboard to find the Java garbage collection log. Total time: 3 minutes, not 18. That is the power of a well-designed dashboard.

## The Insight

A dashboard is not a collection of charts. A dashboard is a **hypothesis about what matters** rendered as a visual layout. Every chart, every number, every sparkline on the page represents a decision you made about what to watch. If you put a metric on a dashboard, you are saying: "This metric, right now, tells me whether my system is healthy." If you leave a metric off, you are saying: "I trust this not to break without warning."

The fundamental insight is that a good dashboard answers three questions in under 10 seconds:

1. **Is the system healthy right now?** A single glance at the top row tells you green, yellow, or red.
2. **What changed?** The second row shows trends — did something just start degrading?
3. **Where do I go to fix it?** The dashboard links to runbooks, logs, and traces.

Bad dashboards fail because they violate this hierarchy. They show 50 charts on one page, all equally sized, with no prioritization. They use rainbow color palettes that make every line equally important — which means nothing is important. They are firehoses of data, not filters for signal.

The hierarchy is not just a design preference. It reflects how the human brain processes visual information. The pre-attentive processing stage (the first 200-300 milliseconds of looking at a page) can only handle 3-5 visual chunks. If you present 50 equally-sized panels, the brain cannot determine which one matters. The on-call engineer scans the page, sees nothing standing out, and starts reading panels one by one. This takes 30 seconds instead of 3. In an incident, 30 seconds is the difference between catching a problem early and missing the window for mitigation.

## Core Vocabulary

**Metric** — A numeric measurement collected at a point in time. CPU utilization at 08:00:00 UTC. Request count for the last minute. Error rate as a percentage of total requests. Metrics are the atomic unit of dashboard data. Every metric has a name, a value, a timestamp, and a set of labels that identify its source.

**Time Series** — A sequence of data points indexed by time, each with a timestamp and a value. A time series is the core data structure that powers every line chart, area chart, and sparkline on a dashboard. A single metric produces one time series per unique combination of label values. If you have a metric called `http_requests_total` with labels `service` and `status`, and you have 10 services each returning 5 status codes, you get 50 time series.

**Label** — A key-value pair attached to a metric that identifies its source. Common labels include `service=auth-api`, `host=web-03`, `region=us-east-1`, `status=200`. Labels enable slicing and filtering: you can show the error rate for a specific service across all hosts, or the error rate for all services in a specific region.

**Gauge** — A single numeric value displayed as a dial, number, or progress bar. Gauges show the current state of a metric: current memory usage, active connections, queue depth. They are useful for at-a-glance health checks but convey no trend information. A gauge showing 80% memory usage does not tell you whether memory is rising or falling.

**Sparkline** — A tiny line chart without axes or labels, embedded in a table or card. Sparklines show trend direction — up, down, flat — without taking up the space of a full chart. They are excellent for dense dashboards where every pixel matters. A sparkline that trends upward over 15 minutes tells you more than a gauge showing the current value.

**Heatmap** — A two-dimensional grid where color intensity represents the value of a metric across two dimensions, typically time on one axis and a category (like HTTP status code or host) on the other. Heatmaps reveal patterns that line charts hide, such as periodic spikes or distribution shifts. A heatmap of latency by hour of day might reveal that the system is slow every day at 9:00 AM — a pattern invisible in the average latency chart.

**SLO (Service Level Objective)** — A target reliability level expressed as a percentage over a time window. "99.9% of requests complete in under 500ms over the trailing 30 days." Dashboards track SLO attainment with burn-rate alerts and error budgets. The SLO panel is typically the most important panel on an operational dashboard.

**Drill-Down** — A navigation pattern where clicking a chart element opens a more detailed view. Clicking a latency spike on the overview dashboard opens the per-service breakdown. Clicking a service opens the per-host breakdown. Good drill-down paths let you go from 30,000 feet to the kernel thread in three clicks.

**Aggregation** — The process of combining multiple data points into a single value. Common aggregations include sum, average, minimum, maximum, percentile (p50, p95, p99), and count. The choice of aggregation dramatically changes what a chart communicates: averaging across all hosts hides the one host that is failing.

**Rollup** — The reduction of metric resolution over time by aggregating older data points. Raw data at 10-second resolution is kept for 7 days. Rollups to 1-minute resolution are kept for 30 days. Rollups to 1-hour resolution are kept for 13 months. Rollups reduce storage costs while preserving long-term trend visibility.

**Annotation** — A marker on a dashboard timeline that records an event: a deployment, a configuration change, a scaling event, an incident. Annotations are the most powerful debugging tool on a dashboard because they correlate system changes with metric changes. A latency spike that coincides with a deployment annotation tells you exactly where to look.

**Threshold** — A horizontal line on a chart that marks the boundary between acceptable and unacceptable performance. Thresholds convert a chart from "look at this data" to "is this data OK?" A line that crosses the threshold is a problem. A line that stays below the threshold is fine. Thresholds make dashboards decision-support tools, not data displays.

**Cardinality** — The number of unique label value combinations for a metric. A metric with labels `service` (10 values), `host` (50 values), and `status` (5 values) has a cardinality of 10 x 50 x 5 = 2,500. High cardinality (millions of unique combinations) is the most common cause of TSDB performance problems because the inverted index must be held in memory.

**Burn Rate** — The rate at which an error budget is being consumed, expressed as a multiplier of the target error rate. A burn rate of 1 means you are consuming error budget exactly as expected. A burn rate of 10 means you are consuming it 10x faster than expected and will exhaust the budget in 1/10th of the window.

**Recording Rule** — A pre-computed expression that runs periodically and stores its result as a new time series. Recording rules accelerate dashboard queries by doing the expensive computation ahead of time. For example, a recording rule can compute the hourly error rate every 5 minutes, so dashboard queries read the pre-computed value instead of scanning raw data.

**Downsampling** — The process of reducing the resolution of time series data by selecting or aggregating points at a lower frequency. Downsampling is distinct from rollups: downsampling selects a subset of points (e.g., one per hour) while rollups aggregate (e.g., average, max, min of all points in hour). Downsampling is faster but loses information about the distribution within the window.

**Service-Level Indicator (SLI)** — A specific metric that measures a dimension of service quality. "P99 latency of the login endpoint" is an SLI. "Availability measured as the fraction of successful requests" is an SLI. SLIs are the raw ingredients that feed SLO calculations.

**Cardinality Explosion** — A rapid, uncontrolled increase in the number of unique time series caused by adding high-cardinality labels (user IDs, session IDs, request IDs) to a metric. Cardinality explosion is one of the most common causes of TSDB crashes because it fills memory with index entries, triggers GC pauses, and eventually causes OOM kills.

## Step by Step

### Step 1: Instrument the application

Every service exports metrics through a well-known interface. For a web server, this means exposing a metrics endpoint at `/metrics` that returns a snapshot of all counters, gauges, and histograms in a text format that a metrics agent can parse.

```python
# Example: instrumenting a Python web service with Prometheus client
from prometheus_client import Counter, Histogram, Gauge, generate_latest
from flask import Flask, Response

app = Flask(__name__)
REQUEST_COUNT = Counter(
    'http_requests_total',
    'Total HTTP requests',
    ['method', 'endpoint', 'status']
)
REQUEST_LATENCY = Histogram(
    'http_request_duration_seconds',
    'HTTP request latency in seconds',
    ['method', 'endpoint'],
    buckets=[0.01, 0.025, 0.05, 0.1, 0.25, 0.5, 1.0, 2.5, 5.0, 10.0]
)
IN_FLIGHT = Gauge(
    'http_requests_in_flight',
    'Current number of in-flight HTTP requests'
)

@app.route('/hello')
def hello():
    IN_FLIGHT.inc()
    with REQUEST_LATENCY.labels(method='GET', endpoint='/hello').time():
        result = "Hello, world!"
    REQUEST_COUNT.labels(method='GET', endpoint='/hello', status=200).inc()
    IN_FLIGHT.dec()
    return result

@app.route('/metrics')
def metrics():
    return Response(generate_latest(), mimetype='text/plain')
```

The three metric types serve different purposes. A Counter measures a value that only increases: total requests, total errors, total bytes sent. A Histogram measures the distribution of values: request latency, response sizes. A Gauge measures a value that goes up and down: current memory, active connections, queue depth. Choosing the right type is important because the dashboard query language treats each type differently. Counter rates are computed as `rate(metric[5m])`. Histogram percentiles are computed as `histogram_quantile(0.99, ...)`. Gauges are read directly.

### Step 2: Scrape metrics at a regular interval

A metrics agent (Prometheus, Telegraf, or a cloud provider agent) polls each service's `/metrics` endpoint every 15 to 60 seconds. The agent stores the raw samples in a local buffer and forwards them to the time series database.

### Step 3: Store time series with compression

The time series database writes each incoming sample to a write-ahead log (WAL) for durability, then to an in-memory data structure (MemTable) for fast ingestion. Periodically, the MemTable is flushed to disk as a sorted block. Time series compression is extremely efficient because adjacent data points are similar: Gorilla compression reduces the average sample from 16 bytes to 1.37 bytes.

The Gorilla compression algorithm works in two parts. For timestamps, it stores the first timestamp as a full 64-bit integer, then stores each subsequent timestamp as the difference from the previous difference (delta-of-delta). Since timestamps are evenly spaced (every 15 seconds), the delta-of-delta is almost always zero, which compresses to a single bit. For values, it stores the first value as a full 64-bit float, then stores each subsequent value as the XOR of the current value and the previous value. When values change slowly, the XOR has long runs of zero bits, which compress efficiently.

### Step 4: Apply labels and indexing

Each time series is identified by its metric name and a set of labels. The database builds an inverted index mapping label values to time series IDs. When you query "show me the p99 latency for service=auth-api in region=us-east-1," the inverted index finds the matching time series in microseconds, then scans the relevant data blocks.

### Step 5: Execute the query

When you load a dashboard, the frontend sends a query to the dashboard backend. The backend parses the query, resolves metric names and label matchers through the inverted index, reads the matching time series from disk, applies the requested aggregation (average, p99, sum rate), and returns the result as a JSON array of data points.

Queries are expressed in a domain-specific language. Prometheus uses PromQL, which supports functions like `rate()`, `avg()`, `histogram_quantile()`, and `sum()`. A typical dashboard query looks like this:

```promql
# Get the p99 latency for the auth-api service over the last hour
histogram_quantile(
  0.99,
  sum(
    rate(http_request_duration_seconds_bucket{service="auth-api"}[5m])
  ) by (le)
)
```

This query reads the histogram buckets for `auth-api`, computes the per-second rate over a 5-minute window, sums across all instances, and interpolates the 99th percentile from the bucket boundaries.

### Step 6: Render the chart

The frontend charting library (D3.js, Chart.js, ECharts, or a custom canvas renderer) takes the data points and draws the chart. For a line chart, it maps each timestamp to an x-coordinate and each value to a y-coordinate, then draws a path through the points. The entire process from query to rendered chart should complete in under 500 milliseconds for a single panel.

Canvas-based renderers (ECharts, D3 with canvas) are faster than SVG-based renderers for dashboards with 12+ panels because canvas does not create DOM nodes for each data point. A 12-panel canvas dashboard renders in 60-180 milliseconds. The same dashboard using SVG takes 200-600 milliseconds because each data point becomes a DOM element that the browser must layout and paint.

### Step 7: Apply refresh and auto-polling

The dashboard panel refreshes on a timer — typically every 30 to 60 seconds. Each refresh re-executes the query against the time series database. To avoid overwhelming the database, the dashboard backend caches query results for a configurable interval. Panel-level caching means that if 10 users are looking at the same dashboard, the database receives one query, not 10.

The refresh cycle has a significant impact on database load. A dashboard with 12 panels and a 30-second refresh rate generates 24 queries per minute per viewer. With 50 concurrent viewers, that is 1,200 queries per minute. Without caching, the database would need to handle 1,200 queries per minute for a single dashboard. With a 30-second cache TTL, the first viewer's queries are cached, and subsequent viewers read from the cache. The database handles 24 queries per minute regardless of how many viewers are watching.

### Step 8: Enable drill-down navigation

When you click on a chart element, the dashboard navigates to a more detailed view. This is typically implemented as a URL parameter change: `/dashboard/overview?service=auth-api` loads the service-level dashboard, which shows per-host metrics for that service. The drill-down path is configured in the dashboard definition file, mapping each chart to a linked dashboard with a pre-filtered query.

## Variants

### Variant 1: Operational Dashboard (Real-Time Focus)

The operational dashboard is designed for monitoring and incident response. It shows the current state of the system with minimal delay — typically 15 to 60 seconds of latency. The layout prioritizes at-a-glance health indicators: big red-green-yellow status panels, sparkline trend arrows, and prominently displayed SLO attainment percentages.

### Variant 2: Business Dashboard (Trend Focus)

The business dashboard tracks metrics that matter to the organization: revenue, active users, conversion rates, churn. These metrics change slowly — hourly or daily — so the dashboard does not need real-time data. The layout emphasizes comparisons: this week versus last week, this month versus last month, actual versus forecast.

## Mental Model Check

### Question 1
You are designing a dashboard for a team of on-call engineers. The system has 40 microservices, each with 20 metrics. What is the minimum number of panels you should show on the top row?

**Answer:** Three. The top row should show system-wide health indicators: overall error rate, p99 latency, and a composite health score. Everything else goes below. If you show 40 service panels on the top row, the on-call engineer cannot tell at a glance whether the system is healthy. The top row must be scannable in under 3 seconds.

### Question 2
Your dashboard shows a CPU utilization chart that averages across all 50 hosts. The chart shows a flat line at 45%. Users report that the service is slow. What is happening?

**Answer:** Averaging hides outliers. One host at 100% CPU is invisible when averaged with 49 hosts at 44%. The chart should show p99 CPU utilization across hosts, or show a heatmap where each host is a row. Averaging metrics that are not normally distributed — latency, CPU, queue depth — masks the failures that matter.

### Question 3
You add a dashboard panel that shows the number of requests per second. The chart is a line that goes up and to the right. Your team celebrates. Should they?

**Answer:** Not necessarily. Requests per second going up means more traffic, which could be good (more users) or bad (a DDoS attack). A dashboard panel without context is noise. Pair request count with error rate, success rate, and latency. If requests go up 3x and error rate stays flat, that is good. If requests go up 3x and latency goes up 10x, that is bad.

### Question 4
Your dashboard has 12 panels. Each panel uses a different color scheme. The on-call engineer takes 30 seconds to find the error rate panel. What is the fix?

**Answer:** Standardize colors. Use a single color scheme across all panels: green for good, yellow for warning, red for critical. Use the same color for the same metric type across all panels. Place the most important panels (error rate, latency, throughput) in the same position on every dashboard. Consistent layout creates muscle memory — your engineers find the error rate panel without looking.

### Question 5
You are building a dashboard that tracks SLO attainment for a 99.9% availability target over a 30-day window. The dashboard shows 99.89% — just below the target. What should the dashboard also show?

**Answer:** The dashboard should show the error budget remaining (in minutes or requests), the burn rate (how fast the error budget is being consumed), and a projection of when the budget will be exhausted at the current burn rate. A single number at 99.89% tells you that you are below target. The error budget tells you how much room you have left. The burn rate tells you whether the situation is stable or getting worse.

## Good Dashboard vs Bad Dashboard: A Side-by-Side Comparison

Understanding what makes a dashboard good is easier when you see the difference side by side.

### Bad Dashboard Design

A bad dashboard has 25 panels crammed into a single page. The top row shows: CPU (average across all hosts), memory (average across all hosts), disk I/O (each host as a separate line), network throughput (stacked area chart), and a pie chart showing error distribution by endpoint (17 slices). Below that are 20 more panels showing every metric the system emits, with no prioritization, no threshold lines, and no previous-period comparisons. The colors are a rainbow: each panel uses a different color scheme. The dashboard takes 20 seconds to load and 60 seconds to scan.

When an incident occurs, the on-call engineer opens this dashboard, sees 25 equally-sized panels, and cannot find the error rate panel. They spend 30 seconds scanning the page, then give up and run a terminal command to check the error rate directly. The dashboard is useless during an incident.

### Good Dashboard Design

A good dashboard has 12 panels organized in a 4x3 grid. The top row (3 panels) shows system-wide health: error rate (0.4%, green), p99 latency (280ms, green), and request rate (12,400 req/s). Each panel has a threshold line and a sparkline showing the last hour. The second row (4 panels) shows per-service breakdowns: the 5 services with the highest error rates, each with a sparkline. The third row (3 panels) shows infrastructure metrics: CPU (p99 across hosts, not average), memory, and active connections. The bottom row (2 panels) shows deployment annotations and a link to the runbook.

When an incident occurs, the on-call engineer opens this dashboard, sees the top row is green (no critical errors), and checks the second row for any service with an elevated error rate. They find the payments service at 2.1% errors (yellow), click on it, and drill into the payments service dashboard. Total time from opening the dashboard to identifying the affected service: 5 seconds.

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Using the wrong aggregation.** Averaging latency across all requests hides the long tail. A service with p50 latency of 50ms and p99 latency of 5 seconds has an average latency of approximately 150ms — which looks fine. The average hides the fact that 1% of users experience 5-second load times. Always show percentiles (p50, p95, p99) alongside or instead of averages for latency metrics.

**Mistake 2: No time comparison.** A single time series tells you the current value. A panel showing "CPU: 72%" tells you nothing about whether that is normal. The same panel with an overlay of last week's data tells you that CPU is 10% higher than usual. Every panel should overlay a previous period as a dimmed or dashed series.

**Mistake 3: Too many colors.** A dashboard that uses 15 different colors for 15 different lines creates visual noise. The brain cannot track 15 colors simultaneously. Use 3-4 colors maximum. Use shades of the same color for related series (light blue, medium blue, dark blue for different instances of the same service).

The mental model for dashboards is simple: they are decision-support tools that filter signal from noise. Every design choice — which metrics to show, how to aggregate them, what colors to use, how often to refresh — should be made with that principle in mind.

## Design Patterns for Specific Scenarios

### Pattern: The Four-Golden-Signals Dashboard

Google's SRE book defines four golden signals: latency, traffic, errors, and saturation. Every service should have a dashboard that shows these four signals as the top row. This pattern ensures that any on-call engineer, regardless of their familiarity with the service, can quickly assess its health.

- **Latency:** p50, p95, and p99 response times with threshold lines at 200ms (p50), 500ms (p95), and 1 second (p99).
- **Traffic:** Requests per second with a comparison to the same time last week.
- **Errors:** Error rate as a percentage of total requests, broken down by HTTP status code group (4xx, 5xx).
- **Saturation:** CPU, memory, and connection pool utilization as percentages with threshold lines at 80% (warning) and 95% (critical).

### Pattern: The USE Method Dashboard

Brendan Gregg's USE method (Utilization, Saturation, Errors) applies to every resource in the system. For each resource (CPU, memory, disk, network), the dashboard shows three metrics:

- **Utilization:** What percentage of the resource is being used? CPU at 72%, memory at 65%, disk at 40%.
- **Saturation:** How much work is queued waiting for the resource? CPU run queue length, memory swap usage, disk I/O queue depth.
- **Errors:** How many errors has the resource reported? CPU correctable errors, disk SMART errors, network interface errors.

The USE method dashboard is particularly effective for infrastructure-level monitoring because it tells you not just that something is wrong, but also what is wrong. High utilization with no saturation means the system is at capacity but working. High utilization with high saturation means the system is overloaded and requests are waiting.

### Pattern: The RED Method Dashboard

Tom Wilkie's RED method (Rate, Errors, Duration) applies to every request-handling service. For each service, the dashboard shows:

- **Rate:** How many requests per second is the service receiving? This tells you the traffic level.
- **Errors:** How many requests are failing? This tells you the service health.
- **Duration:** How long are requests taking? This tells you the service performance.

The RED method dashboard is the most common pattern for microservice dashboards because it maps directly to what each service does: handle requests. The RED dashboard is easy to automate: instrument each service with three metrics (request count, error count, latency histogram), and the dashboard auto-discovers the metrics and displays them.

## Dashboard Lifecycle Management

Dashboards have a lifecycle. They are created, used, maintained, and eventually retired. Organizations that neglect the lifecycle end up with hundreds of orphaned dashboards that show "No data" in every panel.

**Creation.** A new dashboard is created when a new service is deployed or a new monitoring requirement emerges. The dashboard should be created from a template that includes the four golden signals, deployment annotations, and a previous-period comparison. The template ensures consistency across all dashboards.

**Active use.** During its active period, the dashboard is viewed daily by the service team and the on-call rotation. Metrics change, thresholds are adjusted, and panels are added or removed. The dashboard owner reviews the dashboard weekly.

**Maintenance.** Every month, the dashboard platform runs a freshness check: are all metrics still emitting data? Are all queries still valid? Are the threshold lines still appropriate? Dashboards that fail the freshness check are flagged and the owner is notified.

**Retirement.** A dashboard that has not been viewed in 90 days is automatically moved to the archive. Archived dashboards are read-only and cannot be edited. After 12 months in the archive, the dashboard is deleted. This prevents dashboard rot and keeps the dashboard catalog navigable.

## Dashboard Layout Principles

### Principle 1: The F-Pattern Layout

Eye-tracking studies show that dashboard viewers scan in an F-pattern: they look at the top row left-to-right, then the second row left-to-right, and rarely look at the bottom-right of the page. The F-pattern means the most important panels must be in the top-left, the second most important in the top-center, and the least important in the bottom-right.

Apply the F-pattern by placing the most critical metric (error rate) in the top-left panel, the second most critical (latency) in the top-center, and the third most critical (throughput) in the top-right. Below that, place per-service breakdowns in the same left-to-right priority order. The bottom-right quadrant should contain reference information (links to runbooks, team contact info) that is rarely needed but available.

### Principle 2: The 3-Second Rule

A dashboard must communicate system health in under 3 seconds. If an on-call engineer cannot tell whether the system is healthy in 3 seconds, the dashboard has failed. To achieve the 3-second rule: limit the top row to 3-4 panels, use color as the primary signal (green = healthy, red = critical), and use single stat panels with sparklines for the top row.

The 3-second rule is enforced by the dashboard platform: if a dashboard does not load in under 3 seconds, the platform shows a loading indicator and the engineer knows the dashboard is degraded. The target for dashboard page load is 2 seconds total (API + rendering).

### Principle 3: The Left-to-Right Deterioration

Panel importance should decrease from left to right and top to bottom. The most important metric (error rate) is top-left. The least important (disk usage on rarely-used hosts) is bottom-right. This means the engineer always finds the most critical information in the same location, regardless of which dashboard they are viewing.

### Principle 4: The 5-Line Maximum

No line chart should show more than 5 lines. When a chart has 6+ lines, the human brain cannot distinguish between them and the chart becomes noise. If you need to show more than 5 metrics, use a heatmap, a table with sparklines, or split the metrics across multiple panels.

## Dashboard Anti-Patterns in Practice

### Anti-Pattern: The Christmas Tree Dashboard

A dashboard where every panel uses a different color, every chart type is different (line, bar, pie, gauge, table), and there is no visual hierarchy. The dashboard looks festive but is impossible to scan.

**Real-world example:** A startup's engineering dashboard had a 3D pie chart showing request distribution by endpoint (12 slices, 12 colors), a stacked area chart of memory usage by host (20 overlapping areas in 20 colors), a bar chart of error counts by service (alternating red and green bars), and a table with alternating row colors. The on-call engineer spent 15 seconds per panel just understanding what they were looking at.

**Fix:** Standardize to 3 chart types maximum (line, single stat, table). Use a consistent 4-color palette. Place key panels in the same position on every dashboard.

### Anti-Pattern: The No-Threshold Dashboard

A dashboard where every panel shows a time series but no panel has a threshold line. The on-call engineer sees a chart with a line going up and must decide from memory whether that value is acceptable.

**Real-world example:** A latency chart showed p99 latency at 250ms. The on-call engineer did not know whether 250ms was good or bad because the SLO was not displayed on the chart. They had to open a separate document to check the SLO. By the time they checked, the latency had risen to 800ms.

**Fix:** Every panel must have a threshold line showing the SLO boundary. The threshold line should be a different style (dashed, a different color) from the data line. The SLO value should be displayed in the panel legend.

### Anti-Pattern: The Absolute Value Dashboard

A dashboard that shows only absolute values (total requests, total errors, total bytes) without rates. A chart that always goes up is impossible to read for trends. The on-call engineer cannot tell whether the system is improving or degrading because the line only goes in one direction.

**Fix:** Show rates (requests per second, errors per second) as line charts. Show absolute values only in single stat panels that reset daily or weekly. Every line chart should have a y-axis that fluctuates around a stable baseline.

## Dashboards and the Observability Trinity

Dashboards are one pillar of the observability trinity: metrics, logs, and traces. A dashboard that shows only metrics is incomplete because it shows the symptom (elevated latency) but not the cause (a specific slow database query visible in the trace) or the context (the error message visible in the log).

The observability trinity is integrated into the dashboard through drill-down links. When an engineer sees a latency spike on the dashboard, they click the spike and see two options: "View logs" (opens the log viewer filtered to the time of the spike) and "View traces" (opens the trace viewer filtered to the affected service). The engineer follows the trace to find the slow database query, then checks the log to see the exact query that was slow.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Should I use a single stat panel or a line chart for the top row?** Use single stat panels with sparklines. The top row must be scannable in under 3 seconds. A single stat panel shows the current value (big number) and the trend (sparkline) in one compact panel. The line chart goes in the second row where the engineer has more time to examine the trend.

**Q: How often should I refresh the dashboard?** Match the refresh interval to the scrape interval. If your metrics are scraped every 15 seconds, refresh the dashboard every 30 seconds. This gives the scrape agent time to collect the data and the TSDB time to index it before the dashboard reads it.

**Q: What is the ideal number of panels per dashboard?** 12-15 panels for operational dashboards. 6-8 panels for executive dashboards. 20-30 panels for debugging dashboards. The key is the top row: never more than 4 panels, and the most important metric must be in the top-left position.

**Q: Should I use a dark or light theme for dashboards?** Dark theme is preferred for operational dashboards because it reduces eye strain during night shifts. Light theme is preferred for executive dashboards because it is easier to read on projectors. Most dashboard tools support both themes; let the user choose.

**Q: How do I handle dashboards for multiple time zones?** Always use UTC for the dashboard time axis. Convert to local time in the browser using the viewer's time zone setting. The dashboard should display the time zone in the time axis label: "14:00 UTC" or "10:00 ET."
