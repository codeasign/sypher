# Monolith vs Microservices in Production

## Company Case Studies

### Netflix: The Pioneer of Microservices at Scale

Netflix began as a monolithic Ruby on Rails application running on a single Oracle database. In 2008, a major database corruption took the entire DVD rental site offline for three days. The incident convinced Netflix to migrate to a cloud-native, microservices architecture on AWS.

Netflix now operates approximately 700 microservices. The migration took seven years (2008-2015). The architecture is organized around business capabilities: content ingestion, encoding, recommendation, personalization, streaming, billing, and customer support.

Netflix's key innovations emerged from the microservices architecture:

- **Chaos Monkey**: A tool that randomly terminates production service instances. This forces teams to build services that tolerate instance failures without user impact. If a service cannot survive losing one instance, it is not production-ready.
- **Hystrix Circuit Breaker**: An open-source library implementing the circuit breaker pattern. Each service call is wrapped in a Hystrix command with a timeout, thread pool isolation, and fallback. Hystrix was the foundation for the circuit breaker pattern described in the deep-dive section.
- **Zuul API Gateway**: A gateway that handles routing, rate limiting, authentication, and request filtering for all client traffic. Zuul processes millions of requests per minute during peak hours.

The migration was not a big-bang rewrite. Netflix used the strangler fig pattern: new features were built as microservices, and the monolith was gradually decomissioned. The last monolith component was retired in 2015, seven years after the migration began.

**Netflix-specific challenges and solutions:**

- **Database decomposition**: The original Oracle database was replaced with a mix of Cassandra (for high-write workloads like viewing history), EVCache (memcached-based for session state), and S3 (for video storage). Each microservice chose its own data store based on workload patterns. The recommendation service used Cassandra for its write scalability. The billing service used MySQL for ACID compliance.
- **Chaos engineering**: Netflix pioneered chaos engineering with Chaos Monkey, which randomly terminates production instances. This forced every service to handle instance failures gracefully. The practice was extended to the Simian Army: Chaos Gorilla (simulate an AWS region failure), Latency Monkey (introduce artificial delays), and Conformity Monkey (find instances that violate best practices).
- **Global CDN**: Netflix built Open Connect, a CDN deployed inside ISP networks. This serves 95% of streaming traffic from edge caches, reducing dependency on the origin infrastructure. Each Open Connect appliance caches the most popular content for its region, updated during off-peak hours.
- **CI/CD pipeline**: Netflix developed Spinnaker, a multi-cloud continuous delivery platform. Spinnaker supports canary deployments, blue/green deployments, and automated rollbacks. Netflix deploys code hundreds of times per day across 700 services, with each deployment going through automated canary analysis before reaching production.

**Measurable outcomes**: Deployment frequency increased from once per week to hundreds per day. Mean time to recovery (MTTR) dropped from hours to minutes. Team velocity increased 10x, measured by features shipped per engineer per quarter.

### Uber: The Monolith That Outgrew Itself

Uber started with a single monolithic Python application (called "Maggie") that handled dispatching, billing, payments, notifications, and driver management. The monolith worked for the first 2 years and 1,000 cities.

By 2014, Uber had 100+ engineers committing to the same codebase. The monolith had 2 million lines of code. Deployments took 30 minutes. A single bad commit could bring down the entire dispatch system globally. The monolith was deployed on 500+ servers, but they all ran the same code -- scaling was wasteful.

Uber's migration decomposed the monolith into approximately 2,200 microservices. The decomposition was organized by domain: dispatch, pricing, payments, notifications, maps, and rider/driver management.

Uber's key challenges were unique to their scale:

- **Service proliferation**: 2,200 services created a dependency graph so complex that no single engineer understood the full system. Uber introduced service templates and standardized communication patterns (RingPop, Thrift) to reduce cognitive load.
- **Language diversity**: Teams chose languages independently: Python, Go, Java, Node.js, and C++. This created operational complexity in managing 40+ runtimes. Uber later standardized on Go and Java for new services.
- **Data consistency**: With 2,200 services, each owning its data, cross-service data consistency became a major challenge. Uber invested heavily in event-driven architectures and the saga pattern for distributed transactions.
- **Deployment coordination**: Despite having 2,200 services, Uber found that 30% of deployments still required coordination across 2+ services. The solution was to enforce stricter service boundaries and invest in consumer-driven contract testing. Each service published its API contract, and downstream services were automatically tested against contract changes.
- **Observability at scale**: Uber built its own observability platform called M3 (a distributed metrics platform) and Jaeger (distributed tracing, later open-sourced). M3 processes 10 billion metrics per day across the 2,200 services. Jaeger traces 100% of requests to identify latency hotspots in the service dependency graph.

**Measurable outcomes**: Deployment time decreased from 30 minutes to under 5 minutes per service. The monolith was reduced from 2 million lines to under 200,000 lines (the remaining shared code). Individual team velocity increased 3x, as measured by features shipped per engineer per month. The architecture enabled Uber to expand from 1,000 cities to 20,000+ cities globally.

### Amazon: The Big Bang That Worked

Amazon's microservices migration is the most famous in the industry. In 2002, CEO Jeff Bezos issued a mandate: all teams must expose their data and functionality through service interfaces, and communicate only through these interfaces. No direct database access, no shared memory, no point-to-point integration. Every team became a service provider.

The mandate was enforced ruthlessly. Teams that violated the rule were publicly called out in weekly meetings. The entire company -- 500+ engineers at the time -- was forced to decompose the monolith into services.

The result was AWS. The internal services (S3, DynamoDB, SQS, SimpleDB) were refined and offered as external products. Amazon's microservices architecture directly led to the cloud computing industry.

## Open Source Implementations

### Kubernetes

Kubernetes is the de facto standard for deploying and managing microservices. It provides service discovery, load balancing, scaling, rolling updates, and self-healing. Every microservices architecture in production runs on Kubernetes or a similar orchestrator.

Key concepts for microservices on Kubernetes:
- **Pod**: The smallest deployable unit. One or more containers. Each microservice instance runs in a pod.
- **Service**: A stable network endpoint backed by a set of pods. Internal DNS name resolves to pod IPs. Example: `video-service.namespace.svc.cluster.local`.
- **Deployment**: Declares the desired state: 5 replicas, container image `streamflow/video-service:v2.1.3`, resource limits (2 vCPUs, 4 GB RAM). Kubernetes ensures actual state matches desired state.
- **ConfigMap/Secret**: Configuration and secrets injected into pods. Environment variables, configuration files, database credentials.

### Docker

Docker containers package each microservice with its dependencies into a single deployable artifact. A Go microservice becomes a 15 MB container image. A Python microservice becomes a 150 MB image. Containers ensure consistent environments across development, staging, and production.

### Spring Boot and Spring Cloud

For Java microservices, Spring Boot provides the application framework (embedded Tomcat, auto-configuration, health checks). Spring Cloud adds service discovery (Eureka), circuit breakers (Resilience4j), API gateways (Spring Cloud Gateway), and distributed configuration (Spring Cloud Config).

### Istio

Istio is the most popular service mesh implementation. It deploys an Envoy sidecar proxy alongside each service instance. The sidecar handles all inter-service traffic, implementing retries, timeouts, circuit breaking, traffic splitting, fault injection, and mutual TLS -- all without modifying application code.

## Cloud Provider Implementations

### AWS: ECS and EKS

AWS offers two container orchestration services:
- **ECS (Elastic Container Service)**: AWS-native, simpler to set up, integrates with ALB, CloudWatch, IAM, and VPC. No Kubernetes control plane to manage. Preferred for teams that want minimal operational overhead.
- **EKS (Elastic Kubernetes Service)**: Managed Kubernetes. Full Kubernetes API compatibility. Supports all Kubernetes tooling (Helm, Istio, Prometheus). Preferred for teams that need Kubernetes API or are migrating from self-managed Kubernetes.

### GCP: GKE

Google Kubernetes Engine (GKE) is the most mature managed Kubernetes offering. Google invented Kubernetes, and GKE benefits from years of operational experience. GKE features include:
- **Autopilot**: Fully managed cluster -- Google handles node provisioning, scaling, and upgrades. Teams only manage pods.
- **Workload Identity**: Fine-grained IAM permissions per pod (each pod gets a distinct GCP service account).
- **Traffic Director**: Managed service mesh built on Envoy, integrated with GKE.

### Azure: AKS

Azure Kubernetes Service (AKS) integrates with Azure Active Directory, Azure Monitor, and Azure DevOps. AKS is the primary choice for organizations already invested in the Microsoft ecosystem.

## Lessons Learned

**Lesson 1: Start with a monolith, extract when needed.** Every successful microservices migration began with a working monolith. Netflix operated for 11 years as a monolith before migrating. Amazon had a 7-year-old monolith. Uber had a 4-year-old monolith. The monolith is not a mistake -- it is the foundation. Extract services when the monolith creates measurable pain: deployment coordination, team bottlenecks, or scaling limits.

**Lesson 2: Service boundaries must match team boundaries.** Conway's Law is not negotiable. If you draw service boundaries that do not align with team structures, teams will work around them. Every case study -- Netflix, Uber, Amazon, Spotify -- aligned service ownership with team ownership. A service owned by two teams is a service owned by no one.

**Lesson 3: Invest in infrastructure before extracting services.** Do not extract a single service until you have container orchestration, service discovery, logging, metrics, and tracing in place. The worst time to set up observability is after you have 20 services that are failing in production. The second-worst time is after you have extracted 3 services and cannot debug a slow request.

**Lesson 4: The strangler fig pattern is the only safe migration strategy.** Big-bang rewrites fail. The strangler fig pattern -- extract one service at a time, route traffic incrementally, keep the monolith running -- is the only proven approach. Amazon's 2002 mandate was the exception, not the rule, and it required a CEO-level mandate to enforce.

**Lesson 5: Microservices are an organizational decision, not a technical one.** The question "should we use microservices?" is primarily an organizational question. Do you have 15+ engineers? Can you organize them into autonomous teams aligned with business domains? Can you invest in the operational infrastructure? If the answer to any of these is no, a modular monolith is the better choice. The technical benefits of microservices exist only when the organizational conditions are met.
