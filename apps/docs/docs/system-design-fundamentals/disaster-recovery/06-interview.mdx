# Disaster Recovery -- Interview Playbook

## How This Topic Appears in System Design Interviews

Disaster recovery is rarely the primary topic of a system design interview, but it is almost always a secondary consideration. The interviewer wants to see that you think about failure modes, not just happy-path functionality. Here are the common ways disaster recovery comes up.

### Category 1: Direct Questions (the topic IS the question)

1. "Design a disaster recovery system for a global e-commerce platform."
2. "How would you ensure zero data loss in a multi-region database setup?"
3. "Design a failover system for a payment processing service."
4. "What is your approach to disaster recovery for a SaaS application with customers in North America and Europe?"
5. "Design a system that can survive a full region failure."

### Category 2: Embedded Questions (the topic is part of a larger design)

6. "You are designing a ride-sharing platform. How do you handle the database if the primary region goes down?"
7. "Design a real-time messaging system. What happens when a data center fails?"
8. "Your video streaming service is about to launch in Asia. How do you ensure availability if the primary data center in us-east-1 fails?"
9. "Design a distributed key-value store. How does it handle network partitions between data centers?"
10. "Your banking application needs to process transactions globally. How do you ensure no transactions are lost during a regional outage?"
11. "Design a notification delivery system. What happens to undelivered notifications when a region fails?"
12. "You are building a global file storage service. How do you handle data replication and recovery across regions?"

### Category 3: Behavioral Questions (the topic evaluates your experience)

13. "Tell me about a time you dealt with a production outage."
14. "Describe a disaster recovery incident you were involved in. What went wrong and what did you learn?"
15. "How do you test disaster recovery in your current role?"
16. "Your team has a DR plan that has never been tested. What do you do?"
17. "What metrics do you track for disaster recovery readiness?"

## Common Question Phrasings by Company

Different companies approach DR questions differently. Here are typical phrasings by company tier:

**FAANG (Facebook/Meta, Apple, Amazon, Netflix, Google)**:
- "Design a system that survives a full region outage." (Amazon/AWS)
- "How would you make our streaming service resilient to a data center failure?" (Netflix)
- "You have a database in us-east-1. It fails. What happens next?" (Google)
- "Design a globally distributed database that works even if two regions go down." (Meta)

**Large SaaS companies (Stripe, Uber, Airbnb, Twitter/X)**:
- "A payment is in flight when the primary region goes down. How do you ensure the payment is not lost?"
- "Design a system that can fail over from one region to another in under 30 seconds."
- "How do you test whether your disaster recovery plan actually works?"
- "Your secondary region is running a different version of the application code. What goes wrong?"

**Startups and growth-stage companies**:
- "We are a single-region company. What is the cheapest way to get DR?"
- "Our database is 2 TB and growing. How do we back it up and restore it efficiently?"
- "We have 10 microservices. Which ones need DR and which ones do not?"
- "How do we convince our CTO to invest in disaster recovery?"

## What the Interviewer Is Actually Testing

When an interviewer asks about disaster recovery, they are evaluating specific skills and mindsets:

**Failure awareness**: Do you assume systems will fail? A junior engineer designs for the happy path. A senior engineer designs for the failure path. The interviewer wants to see that you naturally consider what happens when things go wrong.

**Trade-off thinking**: Can you articulate the cost of different DR strategies? A candidate who says "we should use active-active everywhere" does not understand the cost implications. The interviewer wants to see that you can match the DR strategy to the business requirements.

**Rigor and precision**: Do you throw around terms like RPO and RTO without understanding them? The interviewer will ask you to calculate the cost of a given RPO or the time budget for a given RTO. They want to see numbers, not hand-waving.

**Systematic thinking**: Can you trace through a failure scenario step by step? The interviewer wants to see that you understand the full lifecycle — detection, decision, execution, validation, and recovery — not just the failover button.

**Experience**: Have you actually done this? The interviewer will probe with follow-up questions about specific failure modes, configuration details, and real-world challenges. Candidates who have operated DR systems in production give concrete examples. Candidates who have only read about DR give textbook answers.

## Sample Interview Dialogues

### Dialogue 1: E-Commerce Warm Standby

**Interviewer**: "Design a disaster recovery system for a global e-commerce platform."

**You**: "Let me start by understanding the requirements. What is the business impact of downtime? What are the RPO and RTO targets?"

**Interviewer**: "The platform processes $100,000 per hour in revenue. We want to recover in under 15 minutes. Losing some recent orders is acceptable but we should minimize it."

**You**: "Based on that, I would target an RTO of 10 minutes and an RPO of 30 seconds. The cost of a 10-minute outage is approximately $16,700, which justifies a warm standby setup. I would not recommend active-active because the cost would be 2x, and the RTO requirement is achievable with a warm standby at 1.6-1.8x."

**Interviewer**: "How would you set up the warm standby?"

**You**: "The primary region runs the full stack: application auto-scaling group, Aurora PostgreSQL database, ElastiCache Redis, and S3 for static assets. The secondary region runs a reduced footprint: 10-20% of the application instances in a warm pool, an Aurora read replica, and a minimal Redis cluster. Data replicates asynchronously. On failover, the read replica is promoted to primary, the application instances scale up, and DNS records are updated. The total failover time is approximately 8 minutes under normal conditions."

### Dialogue 2: Zero Data Loss Requirement

**Interviewer**: "I need zero data loss for my payment system. How do I achieve that?"

**You**: "Zero data loss requires synchronous replication. Every write must be acknowledged by both the primary and the secondary before the transaction is committed. This adds latency equal to the round-trip time between the regions."

**Interviewer**: "The primary is in us-east-1. Where should the secondary be?"

**You**: "I would place the secondary in us-west-2. The round-trip time is approximately 60 milliseconds, which is acceptable for payment transactions that already take 200-500ms. I would not recommend an intercontinental secondary for synchronous replication — the 150-250ms latency would be too high for most payment systems."

**Interviewer**: "What happens if the secondary region is unreachable during a write?"

**You**: "If the secondary is unreachable, the write cannot complete because it cannot achieve quorum. The system must either queue the transaction for retry or return an error to the client. This is the fundamental trade-off of synchronous replication: you get zero data loss, but you sacrifice write availability during a network partition. If write availability is more important than zero data loss, you should use asynchronous replication and accept a small RPO."

### Dialogue 3: The Cost-Conscious Startup

**Interviewer**: "We are a startup with 10,000 users. We have no DR. What is the cheapest thing we can do?"

**You**: "The cheapest DR strategy is automated backups to a different region with a documented restore procedure. Here is my recommendation:

1. Enable automated daily snapshots of your database with a 30-day retention.
2. Configure cross-region copy of those snapshots to a different AWS region or GCP region.
3. Write a Terraform script that provisions a new environment from the snapshot.
4. Document the restore procedure in a README file stored in your Git repository.
5. Test the restore procedure once per quarter.

The cost is approximately 10-20% of your infrastructure budget — mostly the storage cost for the cross-region backup copy. The RTO will be 4-8 hours because provisioning and restoring takes time. The RPO will be 24 hours. For a startup with 10,000 users, this is acceptable.

When you grow to 100,000 users, revisit this strategy and consider moving to a pilot light setup with a 30-minute RTO."

## Level-by-Level Expectations

### L3 (Entry-Level) -- Define the Basics

**What you should know**: The definitions of RPO and RTO. The difference between backup and replication. The concept of multi-region deployment.

**Model answer**: "Disaster recovery is about preparing for when a system fails completely. The two key metrics are RPO, which is how much data you can afford to lose, and RTO, which is how long you can afford to be down. For a simple system, I would set up automated backups to a different region and document a restore procedure. For a more critical system, I would use database replication to a secondary region."

**What the interviewer is looking for**: Correct definitions. Understanding that DR requires planning, not just infrastructure. Awareness that backup and restore is the simplest starting point.

### L4 (Mid-Level) -- Compare Strategies

**What you should know**: The spectrum of DR strategies (backup and restore, pilot light, warm standby, active-active). When to use each one. How to calculate the cost of a given RPO or RTO.

**Model answer**: "For a system with an RTO of 5 minutes, I would use a warm standby in a secondary region. The secondary region runs a scaled-down copy of the infrastructure with a database replica. When the primary fails, the secondary scales up, the database is promoted, and DNS is updated. The cost is about 1.8x single-region cost. The complexity is moderate -- you need automated failover orchestration, health monitoring, and regular testing."

**What the interviewer is looking for**: Ability to compare strategies on cost, complexity, and recovery speed. Understanding of the trade-offs. Connection between business requirements and technical decisions.

### L5 (Senior) -- Design and Justify

**What you should know**: How to design a complete multi-region DR system. How to handle split-brain scenarios. How to implement fencing. How to test DR in production. How to calculate capacity for the secondary region.

**Model answer**: "I would design an active-active system for the payment processing tier because it requires zero RPO and sub-minute RTO. The database uses synchronous replication with a quorum of 3 nodes across 3 regions. The application tier runs in both regions simultaneously with traffic distributed by latency-based DNS routing. The key challenge is avoiding split-brain during network partitions. I use a distributed lock system with a STONITH mechanism that terminates the primary's compute instances before promoting the secondary. The total cost is approximately 2.2x single-region, but the payment processing volume justifies it."

**What the interviewer is looking for**: Deep understanding of the failure modes. Practical experience with the design decisions. Ability to articulate the trade-offs and justify the decisions with business context.

### L6 (Staff) -- Multi-System and Organizational

**What you should know**: How to design DR for a system of systems. How to prioritize DR investment across multiple services. How to build a culture of reliability. How to design chaos engineering programs.

**Model answer**: "I would categorize our services into tiers based on revenue impact and data criticality. Tier 1 (payment processing, authentication) gets active-active multi-region DR with synchronous replication. Tier 2 (order management, inventory) gets warm standby with async replication. Tier 3 (recommendations, analytics) gets backup and restore. I would build a DR orchestration platform that all services can use, rather than each service building its own. I would establish a quarterly game day program where every service's DR is tested. I would track DR readiness as a published metric, visible to the entire organization."

**What the interviewer is looking for**: Ability to think across systems. Understanding of organizational change management. Metrics-driven approach to reliability. Strategic thinking about DR investment.

### L7 (Principal) -- Industry-Leading

**What you should know**: Cutting-edge DR practices. Experience designing DR for systems that process billions of transactions. Ability to design DR that works across cloud providers. Deep understanding of consistency models and their implications for DR.

**Model answer**: "I would design the system to be region-independent from the start. Data is partitioned by user region using a geo-aware sharding strategy. Each shard is replicated across three regions within the same continent using a Raft consensus protocol. Writes require a quorum of 2 regions. If a region fails, the remaining two regions continue with a reduced quorum. The system does not have a concept of primary and secondary -- every region is equal. This eliminates the need for failover entirely. The system degrades gracefully under failure: throughput drops by 33% when one region goes down, but availability remains at 100%."

**What the interviewer is looking for**: Novel thinking. Deep understanding of distributed systems theory applied to DR. Experience designing systems that go beyond the standard patterns.

### L8 (Distinguished) -- Transformational

**What you should know**: History of DR failures at internet scale. Ability to design fundamental improvements to how organizations approach reliability. Thought leadership that influences the industry.

**Model answer**: "I would approach DR not as a technical problem but as an organizational one. The root cause of most DR failures is not the infrastructure -- it is the gap between what the team thinks will happen during a disaster and what actually happens. I would build a 'reliability curriculum' that every engineer goes through: quarterly game days, rotating on-call responsibilities, and post-mortem-driven improvement. The DR system itself is a first-class product with its own SLAs, its own on-call rotation, and its own test suite. The real metric is not RTO or RPO -- it is the team's confidence in their ability to handle a disaster, which we measure through surveys after each game day."

**What the interviewer is looking for**: Transformational thinking. Focus on the human and organizational factors that determine DR success. Track record of influencing engineering culture.

## FAANG Framework for Answering

When a disaster recovery question comes up in an interview, use this six-step framework:

### Step 1: Clarify Requirements

Ask questions to understand the system's criticality and constraints before jumping into the design.

"What are the RPO and RTO targets? What is the cost of downtime per minute? Are there regulatory requirements for data retention? Is this a greenfield design or are we evolving an existing system? What is the budget for DR infrastructure? What is the traffic profile -- read-heavy, write-heavy, or balanced?"

The interviewer will give you a set of constraints. Your job is to extract them and use them to guide your design choices.

### Step 2: Define the Recovery Targets

State the RPO and RTO explicitly. Justify them based on the business requirements.

"Based on the payment processing volume of $2 million per hour, I would target an RPO of zero seconds for financial transactions and an RTO of 60 seconds. The cost of a 5-minute outage is $166,000, which justifies the investment in active-active infrastructure."

### Step 3: Choose the DR Strategy

Select the appropriate strategy based on the recovery targets. Explain why you chose it over the alternatives.

"For an RPO of zero and an RTO of 60 seconds, I choose active-active multi-region deployment. Pilot light would not meet the RTO because the scale-up time alone exceeds 60 seconds. Warm standby would not meet the RPO because async replication has a 5-30 second lag. Only active-active with synchronous replication meets both targets."

### Step 4: Design the Architecture

Describe the components and their interactions. Use an architectural diagram (draw it on the whiteboard or describe it verbally).

"The architecture has two regions: us-east-1 and us-west-2. Each region runs a full application stack behind a load balancer. DNS routes traffic to both regions using latency-based routing. The database uses synchronous replication between regions with a quorum of 3 nodes across 2 regions plus a witness in a third region. Application traffic is 50% in each region during normal operation. If one region fails, the remaining region handles 100% of traffic."

### Step 5: Walk Through a Failure Scenario

Trace through the exact sequence of events during a disaster. Show that you understand the detection, decision, and execution phases.

"When the primary region fails, the health check probes in three different regions detect the failure within 10 seconds. The composite trigger evaluates the signals: primary unreachable from all probe regions, secondary healthy. The trigger fires after 30 seconds of sustained failure. The DR orchestrator acquires a distributed lock, disconnects the primary region's database by terminating its compute instances (fencing), promotes the secondary database, and updates DNS. The entire process takes 45 seconds. The remaining region handles 100% of traffic. No data is lost because the synchronous replication ensured all committed writes were present on both regions."

### Step 6: Discuss Trade-offs and Alternatives

Show that you understand the costs and limitations of your design.

"The main trade-off is write latency. Synchronous replication adds 60-80 milliseconds to every write because the write must be acknowledged by both regions. For a read-heavy workload, this is acceptable. For a write-heavy workload, I would consider a different approach: regional sharding with async replication, accepting a 5-second RPO. The cost is also a factor -- active-active doubles the infrastructure cost. If the budget is limited, I would compromise on RTO by using warm standby instead, accepting a 10-minute recovery window."

## Phrase Bank

Use these phrases to sound confident and precise during the interview:

**Setting context**: "Before I design the DR system, I need to understand the RPO and RTO targets. Can you tell me what the business requires?"

**Explaining the trade-off**: "The choice between synchronous and asynchronous replication is a direct trade-off between data consistency and write latency. Synchronous replication gives us zero RPO but adds 60-80ms per write."

**Describing the strategy**: "I recommend a warm standby architecture. The secondary region runs a reduced footprint that can scale up on demand. This balances cost and recovery speed."

**Discussing failure modes**: "The most dangerous failure mode is split-brain, where both regions believe they are primary. To prevent this, I use a fencing mechanism that ensures the primary cannot accept writes before promoting the secondary."

**Talking about testing**: "The DR plan is only as good as the last test. I would schedule quarterly game days where we simulate a full region failure and measure actual RTO and RPO against targets."

**Handling edge cases**: "During the failover, we need to handle the replication lag gap. The last few seconds of writes that were in-flight but not replicated are lost. We need to either accept this loss or implement a reconciliation process."

**Discussing cost**: "The cost of DR is proportional to the RTO. A 5-minute RTO costs about 2x single-region infrastructure. A 4-hour RTO costs about 1.1x because we can use backup and restore."

**Explaining the monitoring**: "We use synthetic transactions that run from three different geographic locations to detect failures. If the transaction fails from all three locations, the composite trigger fires."

**Talking about recovery**: "After the failover, we validate the secondary site by running a synthetic transaction through the full stack. If validation passes, we declare the failover complete. If it fails, we roll back and investigate."

**Describing the organizational approach**: "DR is not a project with a deadline. It is an ongoing practice. Every deployment, every infrastructure change, every team rotation requires a DR review."

## Red Flags

Signal to the interviewer that you recognize these warning signs:

**"We have a DR plan somewhere."** -- A plan that is not known and accessible to the on-call team is not a plan. The DR plan should be the first thing every new team member reads.

**"Our backups are fine."** -- Without validation, backups are a hope, not a guarantee. Every backup should be automatically restored to a test environment and verified.

**"We only test in staging."** -- Staging and production are different. A staging test proves only that staging works. Production DR tests reveal real issues.

**"We use a single cloud provider."** -- A single provider is a valid choice for most companies, but it means you are vulnerable to a provider-level outage. Multi-cloud is not a requirement for most systems, but the risk of a single-provider dependency should be acknowledged.

**"Failover is automatic."** -- Full automation without manual override is dangerous. A false positive failover can be as damaging as a real outage. The automation should handle the common case; the uncommon case should escalate to a human.

**"We never tested failback."** -- Failover is only half the job. If you cannot fail back, you are stuck in the secondary region. Failback has its own set of challenges that must be tested separately.

**"Our RTO is 5 minutes."** -- "And the last time you tested it, what was the actual RTO?" If the answer is "we have not tested it," then the RTO is a guess, not a target.

**"We use a single database."** -- A single database is a single point of failure. Even with replication, the database schema, the connection pool, and the query patterns are a shared dependency that can fail in unexpected ways.

## Practice Drills

### Drill 1: The 5-Minute Warm Standby (5 minutes)

**Prompt**: Design a warm standby disaster recovery system for a SaaS application with 500,000 users. The application uses PostgreSQL, Redis, and S3. The target RTO is 5 minutes. The target RPO is 30 seconds.

**Time yourself**: 5 minutes to sketch the architecture, identify the failover steps, and estimate the RTO budget.

**Check your answer**: Did you include the replication configuration? Did you account for DNS propagation time? Did you plan for cache warm-up? Did you identify the single points of failure?

### Drill 2: The Zero-RPO Challenge (5 minutes)

**Prompt**: A payment processing system requires zero data loss (RPO = 0) and an RTO of 30 seconds. The system processes 10,000 transactions per second. The primary and secondary regions are 3,000 km apart. Design the database replication strategy.

**Time yourself**: 5 minutes to design the solution and identify the performance implications.

**Check your answer**: Did you consider synchronous replication? Did you calculate the added latency? Did you consider the quorum size? Did you discuss the trade-off between consistency and throughput?

### Drill 3: The Post-Mortem (5 minutes)

**Prompt**: You are the incident commander for a disaster recovery failure. The system failed over to the secondary region, but the secondary was running a different database schema version. The failover took 45 minutes instead of the targeted 5 minutes. Write a post-mortem with root cause, impact, and action items.

**Time yourself**: 5 minutes to write a structured post-mortem.

**Check your answer**: Did you identify the root cause (configuration drift between regions)? Did you quantify the impact? Did your action items address both the immediate fix and the systemic prevention?

### Drill 4: The Budget Constraint (5 minutes)

**Prompt**: A startup with 10,000 users has a budget of $500 per month for disaster recovery. Currently, they have no DR. They run on a single EC2 instance with a single RDS database. Design a DR strategy that fits their budget.

**Time yourself**: 5 minutes to design a realistic strategy.

**Check your answer**: Did you recommend backup and restore (the only option in this budget)? Did you specify the backup frequency, retention, and storage location? Did you document the manual restore procedure? Did you acknowledge the RTO (likely 4-8 hours)?

### Drill 5: The Multi-Service Failover (5 minutes)

**Prompt**: A system has 10 microservices, each with its own database. The authentication service is the most critical. The payment service requires zero data loss. The recommendation service can tolerate hours of downtime. Design a tiered DR strategy.

**Time yourself**: 5 minutes to categorize the services and assign DR strategies.

**Check your answer**: Did you categorize services by criticality? Did you assign different DR strategies to different tiers? Did you consider the dependencies between services (the recommendation service depends on the authentication service)? Did you account for the cost of each tier's DR implementation?

### Drill 6: The RPO Violation (5 minutes)

**Prompt**: An SRE receives an alert that database replication lag between the primary and secondary regions has exceeded 60 seconds — well above the 5-second RPO target. The lag is growing, not shrinking. What do you do? You have 5 minutes to make a decision.

**Time yourself**: 5 minutes to decide whether to fail over, pause writes, or wait for the lag to recover.

**Check your answer**: Did you consider the trend? (Growing lag is more dangerous than stable lag.) Did you consider the root cause? (If lag is caused by a large batch job, pausing the batch is better than failing over.) Did you consider the impact of a failover? (If the secondary database is 60 seconds behind, failing over means losing 60 seconds of data.) Did you account for the cost of pausing writes? (If the system stops accepting writes, the business is losing money each second.)

### Drill 7: The Cross-Cloud Question (5 minutes)

**Prompt**: You are building infrastructure for a hospital system that cannot tolerate any single cloud provider outage. Design a DR strategy across two cloud providers. The system has a PostgreSQL database, a Java application, and static file storage. The budget is 3x the single-cloud cost.

**Time yourself**: 5 minutes to produce a high-level architecture.

**Check your answer**: Did you acknowledge the complexity of multi-cloud DR? Did you consider how to replicate PostgreSQL across providers? Did you decide between database-level replication (WAL streaming via VPN) and application-level replication (dual-write)? Did you account for the different APIs, authentication, and networking models? Did you include a third-party DNS provider that lives outside both clouds?

### Drill 8: The Post-Interview Case Analysis (5 minutes)

**Prompt**: You just designed a DR system for a payment processor. The interviewer asks: "What happens when you fail back to the primary region after the disaster is resolved?" Walk through the failback process step by step.

**Time yourself**: 5 minutes to describe the failback process.

**Check your answer**: Did you mention reverse replication? (Changes made on the secondary during failover must be replicated back to the primary.) Did you mention validation? (Before switching traffic, validate that the primary is healthy and data is consistent.) Did you mention the controlled cutover? (Do not switch all traffic at once — route 10% first, then 50%, then 100%.) Did you mention the post-failback monitoring period? (The primary needs close monitoring for the first hour after failback to catch any issues.)

## How to Answer Follow-Up Questions

Interviewers often ask follow-ups that probe deeper into your DR design. Here are common follow-ups and how to handle them.

### Follow-Up: "What if the secondary region also fails?"

This is a test of depth. The interviewer wants to see if you have considered multi-layer failure scenarios.

**How to answer**: "If both regions fail, we have lost both our primary and secondary infrastructure. At this point, we must fall back to our backup and restore strategy. We restore from the most recent validated backup, which is stored in a third region or in an independent storage service (Glacier, cold storage). The RTO for this scenario is 4-8 hours, and the RPO is the backup interval (typically 24 hours). We accept this as the worst-case scenario because the probability of two regions failing simultaneously is extremely low."

### Follow-Up: "How do you know the failover worked?"

This tests your understanding of validation and observability.

**How to answer**: "The failover is validated in three phases. Phase 1: Automated health checks confirm that all services in the secondary region are returning HTTP 200. Phase 2: A synthetic transaction runs through the full application stack — it creates a test record, reads it back, and deletes it. Phase 3: The monitoring system compares key metrics (error rate, p99 latency, throughput) against the pre-failover baseline. If any phase fails, the failover is not declared complete, and the operations team is alerted."

### Follow-Up: "What is the specific API call to promote the database?"

This tests whether you have real experience or just textbook knowledge.

**How to answer**: For AWS RDS, the API call is `rds_client.promote_read_replica(DBInstanceIdentifier='my-replica')`. For Aurora, `rds_client.failover_db_cluster(DBClusterIdentifier='my-cluster', TargetDBInstanceIdentifier='my-replica')`. For a self-managed PostgreSQL, you connect to the replica and run `pg_ctl promote` or the SQL function `pg_promote()`.

### Follow-Up: "What is the DNS TTL?"

This tests your attention to detail in the failover mechanism.

**How to answer**: "I would set the DNS TTL to 60 seconds. This gives the best balance between fast DNS propagation during failover and reasonable DNS query volume during normal operation. A TTL of 60 seconds means DNS changes propagate within 1 minute. However, some DNS resolvers ignore TTLs below a minimum threshold (typically 30 seconds for enterprise resolvers), so I would verify this with the actual DNS provider."

### Follow-Up: "Your team is in different time zones. Who approves the failover at 3 AM?"

This tests the human and organizational aspects of DR.

**How to answer**: "The failover is automated for clearly defined scenarios. A health check failure that meets the composite trigger criteria triggers automatic failover without human approval. However, there is a manual override. The on-call engineer receives an alert and can stop the failover within 30 seconds by pressing a 'cancel' button. If the engineer does not intervene, the failover proceeds automatically. This approach balances speed and safety: the automatic system handles the common case, and the human handles the exceptions."

## Follow-Up Depth by Level

The depth of follow-up questions indicates the level the interviewer is testing for.

**L3/L4 follow-ups**: "What is RPO?" "What happens if the backup fails?" "How often do you test?"

**L5 follow-ups**: "What is the network latency between regions?" "How do you prevent split-brain?" "What are the specific API calls?" "How do you handle replication lag?"

**L6+ follow-ups**: "How do you measure DR readiness across 50 services?" "What is your recovery time for a cascading failover?" "How do you handle regulatory compliance across 10 countries?" "What metrics do you use to convince the board to invest more in DR?"

## Recommended Preparation

To prepare for DR questions in system design interviews:

1. **Know the key metrics cold**: RPO, RTO, MTBF, MTTR. Be able to define them, calculate them, and explain the trade-offs between them. Practice with concrete numbers: "An RPO of 5 seconds with async replication across 800 km costs approximately 60ms of write latency."

2. **Know the four DR strategies**: Backup and restore, pilot light, warm standby, active-active. Be able to compare them on cost, complexity, RPO, and RTO. Be able to recommend one for a given scenario and justify your choice.

3. **Know the failure modes**: Split-brain, replication lag, cascading failover, configuration drift, cache stampede. Be able to explain how each one happens and how to prevent or mitigate it.

4. **Know the replication options**: Synchronous vs. asynchronous, statement-based vs. row-based vs. logical replication. Be able to choose the right option for a given scenario.

5. **Know the cloud services**: Understand how AWS (Route53, RDS Multi-AZ/Global, Aurora, S3 CRR, DRS), Azure (Site Recovery, Traffic Manager), and GCP (Cloud DNS, Cloud SQL replicas, Spanner) handle DR. Interviewers expect you to be familiar with at least one cloud provider's DR services.

6. **Prepare real-world examples**: Have 2-3 concrete stories about DR incidents you have experienced or studied. The Netflix Chaos Monkey story is well-known; go beyond it with Discord, GitLab, or an incident from your own experience.

7. **Practice the math**: Be able to calculate RTO budgets, replication costs, and capacity requirements. Practice questions like: "How many instances do we need in the secondary region to handle 100,000 req/s with a 200ms processing time?"

## Common Mistakes in DR Interviews

### Mistake 1: Jumping to Active-Active Too Fast

Many candidates immediately propose active-active multi-region deployment as the solution to every DR problem. This signals that you do not understand the cost and complexity of active-active.

**Better approach**: Ask about the business requirements first. Then propose the cheapest strategy that meets those requirements. If the RTO is 15 minutes, propose warm standby — not active-active. If the RTO is 4 hours, propose backup and restore.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring the Human Factor

Candidates focus entirely on the technology — the database replication, the DNS failover, the auto-scaling — but ignore the human factors: who decides to fail over, who communicates with customers, who coordinates the response.

**Better approach**: Include the human factor in your design. Mention the communication tree, the decision matrix, the runbook, and the training program. Show that you understand that DR is not just a technical problem.

### Mistake 3: Not Mentioning Testing

The interviewer asks you to design a DR system. You describe the architecture in detail. But you never mention how you would test it.

**Better approach**: After describing the architecture, say: "This system achieves a theoretical RTO of 5 minutes and an RPO of 5 seconds. However, those targets are only valid if the DR system is tested regularly. I would implement a quarterly game day program that simulates a full region failure and measures actual RTO and RPO against targets. I would also implement automated DR validation that runs after every deployment."

### Mistake 4: Confusing Backup with Replication

"You need to back up your database to a different region." This is a common mistake. Backups are point-in-time copies. Replication is continuous. They serve different purposes. Backups protect against data corruption and human error. Replication protects against hardware failure and region outages.

**Better approach**: Discuss both. "I would use cross-region replication for the application data to achieve a 5-second RPO. I would also implement daily backups to a different region with a 30-day retention to protect against data corruption."

### Mistake 5: Not Specifying Numbers

"We need fast recovery" or "we should minimize data loss" are hand-wavy phrases that signal inexperience. The interviewer wants to hear specific RPO and RTO targets with justification.

**Better approach**: "I recommend a 5-minute RTO and a 5-second RPO. These targets are based on the business impact: every minute of downtime costs $10,000 in lost revenue, so a 5-minute recovery limits the financial impact to $50,000. A 5-second RPO limits data loss to approximately 50 transactions at 10 writes per second."

### Mistake 6: Forgetting the Network

Candidates design DR systems that assume perfect network connectivity between regions. In reality, network issues are a common cause of both the disaster itself and the failure of the DR system.

**Better approach**: Discuss network considerations. "The network between us-east-1 and us-west-2 has 60ms of latency and 99.9% availability. For synchronous replication, the write latency is 60ms plus the database commit time. I need to ensure the network bandwidth is sufficient for the WAL stream — approximately 10 MB/sec at peak writes. I would also configure a VPN connection between the regions with a failover path through a different network provider."

### Mistake 7: Single-Point-of-Failure in the DR System

"I will use a Lambda function to orchestrate the failover." But where is that Lambda function deployed? If it is in the primary region, it fails when the primary region fails.

**Better approach**: "The DR orchestrator is deployed in both regions in active-passive mode. The orchestrator in the secondary region monitors the health of the primary orchestrator. If the primary orchestrator stops responding, the secondary orchestrator takes over. The orchestrator state is stored in a DynamoDB table that is replicated across regions."

### Mistake 8: Not Discussing Rollback

You describe a detailed failover plan. The interviewer asks: "What happens if the failover causes more problems than it solves?" If you do not have an answer, you have not thought through the complete system.

**Better approach**: "Every failover step has a rollback procedure. If the DNS update causes routing issues, the DNS record is reverted to the original primary. If the database promotion fails, the secondary database is kept in read-only mode and the primary is not disconnected. The rollback is automated and can be triggered within 10 seconds of detecting an issue."

## Key Concepts to Memorize for Interviews

Memorize these specific numbers and relationships. They will help you sound confident and precise.

**Availability numbers**:
- 99.9% (three nines) = 8.76 hours of downtime per year
- 99.99% (four nines) = 52.56 minutes per year
- 99.999% (five nines) = 5.26 minutes per year

**Replication latency**:
- Same-region async replication: 2-5ms
- Cross-region (800km) async replication: 20-40ms
- Cross-region (800km) sync replication: 60-80ms added to each write
- Intercontinental sync replication: 150-250ms added

**RTO by strategy**:
- Backup and restore: 4-24 hours
- Pilot light: 15-60 minutes
- Warm standby: 5-15 minutes
- Active-active: 0 seconds

**Cost multiplier by strategy**:
- Backup and restore: 1.1x
- Pilot light: 1.3-1.5x
- Warm standby: 1.6-1.8x
- Active-active: 2.0-2.5x

**Capacity planning rule of thumb**:
- Each CPU core handles approximately 100-200 req/sec for a typical web application
- Each database writer handles approximately 5,000-10,000 writes/sec for a typical OLTP workload
- Each database reader handles approximately 15,000-30,000 reads/sec
- Cross-region data transfer costs approximately $0.02/GB

## How to Structure Your Answer

When the interviewer asks a DR question, use this structure:

**1. State your assumptions**: "I assume this system requires an RTO of 15 minutes and an RPO of 30 seconds. Please correct me if these targets are different."

**2. Name the strategy**: "Based on these targets, I recommend a warm standby in a secondary region."

**3. Describe the normal operation**: "During normal operation, the primary region in us-east-1 serves all traffic. Data is replicated asynchronously to a read replica in us-west-2."

**4. Describe the failover process**: "When a disaster is detected, the failover orchestrator promotes the read replica, scales up the application fleet, updates DNS, and validates the new primary."

**5. Describe the recovery process**: "After the primary region is restored, we establish reverse replication from the secondary to the restored primary, validate consistency, and fail back."

**6. Discuss testing**: "The DR system is tested quarterly through game day exercises. Each test measures actual RTO and RPO against targets."

**7. Discuss trade-offs**: "The main trade-off is cost. This warm standby setup costs 1.6x the single-region cost. If the budget is limited, we can reduce to pilot light but the RTO increases to 30 minutes."

## Recommended Preparation

To prepare for DR questions in system design interviews:

1. **Know the key metrics cold**: RPO, RTO, MTBF, MTTR. Be able to define them, calculate them, and explain the trade-offs between them. Practice with concrete numbers.

2. **Know the four DR strategies**: Backup and restore, pilot light, warm standby, active-active. Be able to compare them on cost, complexity, RPO, and RTO.

3. **Know the failure modes**: Split-brain, replication lag, cascading failover, configuration drift, cache stampede. Be able to explain how each one happens and how to prevent or mitigate it.

4. **Know the replication options**: Synchronous vs. asynchronous, statement-based vs. row-based vs. logical replication. Be able to choose the right option for a given scenario.

5. **Know the cloud services**: Understand how AWS, Azure, and GCP handle DR. Interviewers expect you to be familiar with at least one cloud provider's DR services.

6. **Prepare real-world examples**: Have 2-3 concrete stories about DR incidents you have experienced or studied.

7. **Practice the math**: Be able to calculate RTO budgets, replication costs, and capacity requirements.

## Sample Answers for Common Questions

### Question: "Design a DR system for a social media app with 100M users."

**Sample answer**: "I would use a warm standby strategy. The primary region runs the full application stack with a database cluster. The secondary region runs a scaled-down copy with a database read replica. The target RTO is 5 minutes, and the target RPO is 10 seconds. The failover is automated: the health check system detects the failure, the orchestrator promotes the database, scales the application instances, and updates DNS. The cost is approximately 1.6x the single-region cost. The key trade-off is that we lose up to 10 seconds of posts and likes during the failover, which is acceptable for a social media application."

### Question: "How do you handle database replication in a DR setup?"

**Sample answer**: "I use asynchronous replication for the database. The primary database streams its write-ahead log to a read replica in the secondary region. The replica applies changes as they arrive, typically within 2-5 seconds. During a failover, the replica is promoted to primary, and we lose the last 2-5 seconds of writes. For critical data (financial transactions), I use synchronous replication with a separate mechanism to ensure zero data loss."

### Question: "What is the difference between high availability and disaster recovery?"

**Sample answer**: "High availability (HA) protects against component failures within a single region. HA mechanisms include redundant servers, load balancers, and database replicas. Disaster recovery protects against catastrophic failures that affect an entire site. DR mechanisms include cross-region replication, backup and restore, and failover automation. HA operates within a single region and typically achieves RTO under 60 seconds. DR operates across regions and typically achieves RTO of 5-60 minutes."

### Question: "Your primary region is down. The secondary region is 30 seconds behind in replication. Do you fail over?"

**Sample answer**: "Yes, I fail over. The 30-second data loss is acceptable because the RPO target is 30 seconds. The alternative would extend the RTO beyond the target. After the failover, I document the data loss and assess whether the lost data needs to be reconstructed from application logs."

### Question: "How do you test a DR plan without causing an outage?"

**Sample answer**: "I use a staged testing approach. Phase 1 is a tabletop exercise. Phase 2 tests in staging. Phase 3 runs a read-only production test with a small percentage of traffic. Phase 4 is a scheduled full failover test during low-traffic hours with a rollback plan. Each phase validates different aspects without risking the production user experience."

### Question: "What happens if the failover automation fails?"

**Sample answer**: "If the failover automation fails, we fall back to a manual runbook. The runbook is stored in a location independent of both regions — a GitHub repository accessible from anywhere. It contains the exact commands and API calls to execute each step of the failover manually. We practice the manual procedure during game days every quarter, so the on-call engineer is familiar with it. If the manual procedure also fails, we escalate to the senior infrastructure engineer who designed the DR system."

### Question: "How do you calculate the cost of a DR system?"

**Sample answer**: "The cost of a DR system includes four components. First, the infrastructure cost: the compute, storage, and networking resources in the secondary region, which is typically 1.3-2.5x the primary region cost depending on the strategy. Second, the data transfer cost: cross-region replication bandwidth, which is approximately $0.02/GB. Third, the engineering cost: building and maintaining the DR automation, runbooks, and testing infrastructure. Fourth, the testing cost: the engineering time for quarterly game days and the infrastructure cost for staging environments. For a system spending $100,000/month on infrastructure, a warm standby DR system costs approximately $20,000-40,000/month in additional infrastructure and $5,000-10,000/month in engineering time."

## Phrases to Avoid in DR Interviews

Certain phrases signal inexperience or lack of depth. Avoid these:

**"We will just use multi-AZ."** -- Multi-AZ protects against an availability zone failure, not a region failure. The interviewer is asking about disaster recovery, not high availability.

**"The cloud handles that."** -- The cloud provider handles infrastructure failures, not application-level failures. If your application is not designed for multi-region operation, the cloud provider will not save you.

**"We will fix it in post-mortem."** -- The post-mortem is for learning, not for first-time discovery. The DR system should be tested before the disaster, not analyzed after it.

**"We have 100% uptime."** -- No one has 100% uptime. The interviewer will immediately question your credibility. Use precise numbers: "We target 99.99% uptime."

**"That never happens."** -- In DR, everything happens eventually. The interviewer wants to see that you plan for failure, not that you assume it away.

## Key Takeaways

1. Disaster recovery interviews test your ability to think about failure, not your knowledge of specific tools. Focus on the principles, not the implementation details.

2. Always start with RPO and RTO targets. Every DR decision follows from these two numbers. If you do not know the targets, ask the interviewer.

3. Compare strategies by cost, not just by speed. The interviewer wants to see that you understand the trade-offs, not just the ideal solution.

4. Include testing in your answer. A DR system that is not tested is not a DR system. Mention game days, automated validation, and measured RTO/RPO.

5. Include the human factor. DR is not just about technology. It is about communication, decision-making, and escalation. Mention the on-call rotation, the communication tree, and the decision matrix.

6. Be specific about failure modes. Mention split-brain, replication lag, configuration drift, and cache stampede. Show that you understand what can go wrong, not just what should go right.

7. Connect DR to the business. DR is a business decision, not a technical one. The cost of DR must be justified by the cost of downtime. Show that you understand the business context.
