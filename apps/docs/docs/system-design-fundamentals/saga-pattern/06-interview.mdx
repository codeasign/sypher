# Saga Pattern — Interview Playbook

Saga pattern questions test a candidate's understanding of distributed consistency, failure recovery, and trade-off reasoning. These questions appear in system design interviews for mid-level and senior roles, particularly at companies with microservice architectures.

## How This Topic Appears

Saga questions come in four categories: core concept, system design with saga emphasis, debugging, and comparative evaluation.

### Core Concept Questions

1. "What is the saga pattern and when would you use it?"

2. "Explain the difference between choreography and orchestration in the saga pattern. When would you choose each?"

3. "What is a compensating transaction? Give three examples of operations and their corresponding compensations."

4. "How does a saga provide atomicity without isolation?"

5. "What is an idempotency key and why is it critical for sagas?"

### System Design Questions with Saga Emphasis

6. "Design an e-commerce order processing system that handles inventory reservation, payment, and shipping. How do you ensure consistency across these three services?"

7. "Design a travel booking system that coordinates flights, hotels, and car rentals across different vendors. How do you handle the case where one vendor's API is down during the booking?"

8. "Design a payment processing system that supports international money transfers with fraud checks, currency conversion, and settlement. How do you guarantee that money is never lost?"

9. "Design a multi-region saga coordinator that can survive a region-level outage."

### Debugging and Troubleshooting Questions

10. "Your saga logs show that step 2 compensation failed, but step 1 compensation succeeded. The saga is in COMPENSATION_FAILED state. What do you do?"

11. "A customer was charged twice for the same order. The saga log shows the payment step executed twice with different step IDs. How did this happen and how do you prevent it?"

12. "Your saga coordinator crashes and restarts. How does it know which sagas were in progress and what to do next?"

### Comparative Evaluation Questions

13. "Compare the saga pattern with two-phase commit. When would you use each?"

14. "Compare choreography with orchestration sagas in terms of coupling, observability, and operational complexity."

15. "Compare the saga pattern with the Outbox pattern. Can they be combined?"

## What the Interviewer Is Testing

| Skill | What the Interviewer Looks For |
|---|---|
| Saga fundamentals | Precise definition, understanding of local vs compensating transactions |
| Choreography vs orchestration | Clear trade-off reasoning, not just memorized advantages |
| Compensation design | Ability to design correct compensating actions with idempotency |
| Failure understanding | Deep knowledge of failure modes, recovery, and dead-letter handling |
| Isolation awareness | Recognition that sagas do not provide isolation and what countermeasures exist |
| Real-world judgment | Knowing when sagas are appropriate and when they add unnecessary complexity |
| CAP intuition | Connecting saga design to CAP theorem trade-offs |

## Level Expectations

### L3 / Entry Level

The candidate should explain what a saga is and describe the basic idea of compensation. They should differentiate choreography from orchestration at a surface level. They should identify that sagas handle distributed transactions across services.

**Sample answer for "What is the saga pattern":**

> The saga pattern manages data consistency across multiple services without using distributed transactions. It breaks the operation into a sequence of local transactions, each committed to its own database. If one transaction fails, the saga runs compensating transactions to undo the previous ones. For example, in a travel booking system, you book a flight, then a hotel, then a car. If the car is unavailable, you cancel the hotel and flight. Each cancel is a compensating transaction.

**Sample answer for "Choreography vs orchestration":**

> In choreography, each service emits events after completing its work, and other services listen and react. There is no central coordinator. In orchestration, a central coordinator tells each service what to do and tracks the progress. Choreography has no single point of failure but is harder to debug. Orchestration is easier to manage but the coordinator can be a bottleneck.

### L4 / Mid-Level

The candidate should describe compensation design with idempotency details. They should discuss failure modes like partial compensation failure and saga timeout. They should explain the isolation problem and describe countermeasures. They should compare saga with 2PC in detail.

**Sample answer for "How does a saga provide atomicity without isolation":**

> A saga achieves atomicity through compensation — every forward transaction has a corresponding compensating transaction that semantically undoes it. If any step fails, all previous steps are compensated in reverse order. The system reaches a consistent state either through the forward path (all steps succeed) or the backward path (all steps are undone). However, a saga does not provide isolation. After step 1 commits, the flight reservation is visible to other transactions even though the saga might later compensate and cancel it. This means other parts of the system can observe intermediate states — a dirty read at the saga level. To handle this, you use countermeasures like semantic locks, reread values before compensating, and commutative update design.

**Sample answer for "Design an e-commerce order processing system":**

> I would design an orchestrated saga with three steps: reserve inventory, process payment, and create shipment. The saga coordinator receives the order and calls each service in sequence through a message queue. Each service processes its step, writes to its own database, and publishes a result event. The coordinator listens for result events and advances the state machine. If any step fails, the coordinator publishes compensation commands in reverse order. The inventory reserve is compensated by adding the quantity back. The payment is compensated by voiding the charge — using the Stripe void API with the original charge ID. Each command carries an idempotency key to handle duplicate delivery. The coordinator state is persisted to a saga log database so it can recover if it crashes. I would set up a dead-letter queue for compensations that fail after exhausting retries, with alerting for the operations team.

### L5 / Senior / Staff

The candidate should design multi-region saga deployments with active-active coordinators and global idempotency keys. They should discuss trade-offs in saga granularity, partial compensation handling, and integration with legacy systems. They should describe the limitations of sagas in complex business domains and propose hybrid approaches.

**Sample answer for "Design a multi-region saga coordinator":**

> I would deploy the saga coordinator as a stateless service in each region behind a regional load balancer. The saga log database is replicated across regions using asynchronous CDC (change data capture). Each saga instance is owned by exactly one coordinator through a lease mechanism stored in the saga log. The idempotency key includes a region prefix: "use1_ord_789_pay_2" for US East, "euw1_ord_789_pay_2" for EU West. On region failover, the secondary region's coordinator scans for sagas whose leases have expired — those are sagas whose owning coordinator is presumed dead. It acquires the lease and resumes the saga from the last logged step. A critical detail is fencing: the new coordinator must verify that the old coordinator cannot interfere. I achieve this by incrementing a generation number on the saga instance each time a lease is acquired. The old coordinator's lease check includes the generation number, so if it reconnects, its operations are rejected because its generation is stale.

**Sample answer for "Hybrid approaches — when a pure saga is not enough":**

> Some workflows need a mix of saga and 2PC. Consider a payment workflow where the core payment must be exactly-once (use 2PC for the payment and ledger services within the same trust boundary) while the notification and analytics are best-effort (use saga pattern for the broader workflow). This hybrid approach gives strong consistency where it matters and eventual consistency elsewhere. Another hybrid pattern: use the saga pattern as the outer workflow, but within each saga step use local ACID transactions for strong consistency within the service. The saga provides cross-service coordination; local transactions provide intra-service correctness.

## Common Interview Drills

### Drill 1: The Compensation Design Drill

For each operation below, design the compensating action. Consider idempotency and failure modes.

| Forward Operation | Compensation | Edge Case |
|---|---|---|
| Reserve inventory item | Release inventory (add quantity back) | Item was already shipped |
| Charge credit card $100 | Void the charge | Charge was already voided |
| Send welcome email | Cannot unsend; send apology email | None |
| Reserve hotel room | Cancel reservation | Guest already checked in |
| Create shipping label | Void the label | Package already in transit |
| Transfer money from A to B | Reverse the transfer | A's balance is now negative |

### Drill 2: The Recovery Drill

Your saga coordinator was down for 30 seconds. On restart, it finds these sagas in the saga log. What does it do for each?

1. `saga_001`: Status = ACTIVE, step_1 = COMPLETED, step_2 = IN_PROGRESS, step_3 = PENDING
2. `saga_002`: Status = COMPENSATING, step_2 compensation = COMPLETED, step_1 compensation = PENDING
3. `saga_003`: Status = PENDING, steps = none
4. `saga_004`: Status = COMPLETED

**Answers:**
1. For saga_001, the coordinator checks with the step 2 participant using the idempotency key. If the participant processed step 2, the coordinator marks it COMPLETED and proceeds to step 3. If not, the coordinator reissues step 2.
2. For saga_002, the coordinator sends step 1 compensation. The participant handles idempotency and either applies the compensation or returns a cached success response.
3. For saga_003, the coordinator starts executing from step 1.
4. For saga_004, the coordinator takes no action — the saga is already complete.

### Drill 3: The Trade-Off Drill

You are designing a payment system for a cryptocurrency exchange. The system must:
- Coordinate deposits, exchange, and withdrawals across three services.
- Process 10,000 transactions per second at peak.
- Never lose user funds.
- Guarantee that a withdrawal only happens after the deposit is confirmed and the exchange is executed.

Which variant of the saga pattern would you choose? What countermeasures do you need? What external systems (queues, databases) do you use?

**Model approach:**
Choose orchestration for precise control over ordering and compensation. Use a shared saga log database (DynamoDB for throughput) and SQS for command/event queues. Key countermeasure: semaphore locks on user balances that prevent concurrent modification by different sagas. Compensations must be proven to auditors via the saga log.

The throughput of 10,000 TPS may exceed a single coordinator's capacity. Solution: shard sagas by user ID so that all sagas for the same user go to the same coordinator, avoiding contention on shared state. Multiple coordinator instances handle different user shards independently.

### Drill 4: The Countermeasure Drill

You are designing a saga for a concert ticket booking system. Ten thousand users are trying to buy 500 tickets for a popular show. The saga has two steps: reserve ticket and process payment. The ticket reservation creates a 15-minute hold.

Your compensation releases the hold. A user starts the saga, reserves a ticket, starts the payment, but the payment takes 12 minutes because their bank requires 3D Secure authentication. Meanwhile, another user's saga sees the first reservation and cannot book.

What countermeasures do you deploy?

**Model approach:**
- Use a semantic lock: mark the ticket as HELD with a TTL of 15 minutes. Other sagas see the held ticket and skip it.
- Use a re-read pattern: before compensating, check if the ticket was already sold. If another path sold it while the hold was active, the compensation should fail and require manual review.
- Use a queue position: instead of a true hold, put the user in a queue for the ticket. Multiple users can queue for the same ticket. The first to pay gets it. Others receive "ticket sold out" as they dequeue.

## Detailed Answer Templates

Use these templates as a structured guide for system design interview questions involving sagas.

### Template: Designing a Saga-Based System

When asked to design a system that uses the saga pattern, cover these points in order:

```
1. IDENTIFY THE SERVICES
   - List each service involved
   - Note which database each service owns
   - Confirm there is no shared transaction coordinator

2. DEFINE THE STEPS
   - Step 1: Service A does X, compensates by undoing X
   - Step 2: Service B does Y, compensates by undoing Y
   - Step 3: Service C does Z, compensates by undoing Z

3. CHOOSE COORDINATION MODEL
   - Choreography or orchestration?
   - Justify based on number of services, coupling requirements,
     observability needs, and team structure

4. DESIGN THE INFRASTRUCTURE
   - Command queue: what each message looks like
   - Event queue: what each response looks like
   - Saga log: schema and storage engine
   - Coordinator: stateless, replicated, load-balanced

5. HANDLE FAILURES
   - Network timeout during a step
   - Service unavailability (circuit breaker)
   - Compensation failure (retry policy)
   - Coordinator crash (saga log recovery)

6. ENSURE IDEMPOTENCY
   - Key format: {saga_id}_{step_seq}_{attempt}
   - Participant: check before execute, persist key first
   - Compensation: use same key or correlated key

7. ADDRESS ISOLATION
   - Concede that sagas do not provide isolation
   - Describe countermeasures deployed for this specific system
```

### Template: Explaining How Sagas Handle Failures

When asked about failure handling in sagas, structure the answer by failure type:

```
FAILURE TYPE 1: STEP FAILURE
- Step 3 of 4 fails (business logic error, not timeout)
- Coordinator marks step as FAILED without retry (deterministic failure)
- Coordinator transitions saga to COMPENSATING
- Compensates steps 2 and 1 in reverse order
- Each compensation request includes the original step's output data

FAILURE TYPE 2: TIMEOUT
- Step 2 does not respond within the configured timeout
- Coordinator marks step as FAILED with retry scheduled
- Retry happens after exponential backoff: 100ms, 200ms, 400ms
- If all retries exhausted, begin compensation
- Special case: the participant may have actually processed the step
  before the timeout — the compensation must handle this gracefully

FAILURE TYPE 3: COORDINATOR CRASH
- Saga log stores durable state
- On restart, coordinator reads all ACTIVE and COMPENSATING sagas
- For each, determines the current step and resumes
- Uses idempotency keys to check with participants whether
  in-flight steps were executed

FAILURE TYPE 4: COMPENSATION FAILURE
- A compensation action exhausts all retries
- Saga enters COMPENSATION_FAILED state
- Saga is moved to dead-letter queue for operator review
- Operator can retry, force-complete, or escalate
```

### Template: Answering "When Would You Use a Saga?"

```
USE A SAGA WHEN:
- The operation spans multiple independent services
- Each service has its own database
- The services cannot participate in a global distributed transaction
- You can tolerate temporary visibility of intermediate states
- Every service can provide a compensating action

DO NOT USE A SAGA WHEN:
- The operation is within a single service with a single database
  (use local ACID transaction instead)
- You need strict serializable isolation with no intermediate states visible
- External systems cannot provide compensation endpoints
- The operation is read-only (no data modification needed)
```

## Common Interviewer Follow-Ups

Interviewers often follow up initial answers with deeper questions. Here are common follow-ups and how to handle them.

### Follow-Up 1: "What happens if the compensation itself fails?"

The interviewer wants to know that you understand cascading failures and operational escalation.

**Strong answer:**
If a compensation action fails, the coordinator retries it with exponential backoff. The retry count for compensations is typically higher than for forward steps because compensation is the only path back to consistency. If the compensation exhausts all retries, the saga enters COMPENSATION_FAILED state. The saga is moved to a dead-letter queue. An operator is alerted. The operator reviews the saga log to understand what failed and can:
- Manually retry the compensation from the admin panel.
- Execute the compensation manually through the service's API.
- Accept the inconsistency and resolve it through a batch reconciliation process later.

The key is that the system does not silently drop the failure — it persists the saga in COMPENSATION_FAILED state and alerts proactively.

### Follow-Up 2: "How do you test saga compensation paths?"

The interviewer wants to know that you validate the failure path, not just the happy path.

**Strong answer:**
Compensation paths are tested in three ways:

1. **Unit tests**: Each compensation action is unit-tested in isolation, verifying that it correctly reverses the forward action. Tests include idempotency — calling the compensation twice produces the same result.

2. **Integration tests**: A test harness creates a saga, completes the first two steps, and then forces step 3 to fail. The test verifies that compensations are called in reverse order and that the final state is equivalent to the initial state.

3. **Chaos engineering**: In staging, random failures are injected at each step to exercise every compensation path. This catches hidden dependencies — for example, a compensation that requires a database lookup that was not indexed, or a compensation that calls a service endpoint that does not exist.

The testing goal: every compensation path must be exercised before every production deployment.

### Follow-Up 3: "How does your saga coordinator handle duplicate events?"

The interviewer wants to know that you understand at-least-once delivery semantics and idempotency enforcement.

**Strong answer:**
The saga coordinator processes events from the event queue and deduplicates them using a combination of the event's unique ID and the saga step's idempotency key. The deduplication table stores processed event IDs:

```python
def handle_step_event(event):
    # Check deduplication table
    already_processed = db.query("""
        SELECT 1 FROM processed_events
        WHERE event_id = %s
    """, [event.event_id])

    if already_processed:
        return  # Duplicate event, skip

    # Process the event
    saga = load_saga(event.saga_id)
    saga.process_step_event(event)

    # Record as processed
    db.execute("""
        INSERT INTO processed_events (event_id, saga_id, processed_at)
        VALUES (%s, %s, NOW())
    """, [event.event_id, event.saga_id])
```

The deduplication table is pruned periodically — events older than 48 hours are removed because sagas should not take longer than that to complete.

### Follow-Up 4: "What metrics do you monitor for sagas in production?"

The interviewer wants operational maturity — monitoring and alerting.

**Strong answer:**
I monitor these metrics:

1. **Saga creation rate**: Total sagas created per minute, broken down by saga type. A sudden drop may indicate an upstream failure preventing saga creation.

2. **Saga completion rate**: Total sagas reaching COMPLETED status. This should track closely with the creation rate.

3. **Saga compensation rate**: Sagas entering COMPENSATED status. A significant increase indicates a problem in a specific step — if compensation rate jumps from 1% to 5%, the Payment Service's downstream dependencies may be failing.

4. **Saga step latency (p50, p95, p99)**: Step execution time for each service. A p99 increase from 500ms to 3s indicates a service performance regression.

5. **Active saga count**: Sagas currently in ACTIVE state. A growing count without growth in creation rate indicates stuck sagas.

6. **Compensation failure rate**: Sagas entering COMPENSATION_FAILED. This should be zero in normal operation. Any non-zero value requires immediate investigation.

7. **Saga log database IOPS and storage**: Trending metrics that predict when the saga log will need partitioning or archival.

## Advanced Interview Topics

### Topic 1: Saga Granularity Trade-offs

Interviewers at senior level may ask about saga granularity — how fine-grained or coarse-grained should a saga be?

**Fine-grained saga** (each microservice operation is a saga step):
- Pros: Precise compensation, easy to see exactly which step failed.
- Cons: Many steps, high coordinator overhead, complex saga definitions.
- Best for: Systems where each step has a different compensation action.

**Coarse-grained saga** (group related operations into a single step):
- Pros: Fewer steps, simpler coordinator, less overhead.
- Cons: Less precise compensation (may need to undo a group of operations).
- Best for: Systems where steps are naturally grouped by service boundary.

**Sample answer when asked about granularity:**

> I determine saga granularity by the compensation boundary. If two operations can be undone by the same compensation action, they belong in the same step. If they require different compensations, they should be separate steps. For example, in an e-commerce saga, "reserve inventory" and "allocate warehouse" both require separate compensations — one releases inventory, the other cancels warehouse allocation — so they are separate steps. But "validate address" and "calculate shipping cost" can be undone by a single "revert shipping calculation" compensation, so they can be grouped into one step.

### Topic 2: Saga Versioning and Migration

In production, saga definitions change over time. New services are added, existing services change their APIs, and compensation logic evolves. How do you manage these changes without breaking in-flight sagas?

**Sample answer:**

> I use a versioned saga definition approach. Each saga definition has a version number. When a new version is deployed, existing in-flight sagas complete using the old version. New sagas use the new version. The schema is:
>
> ```sql
> CREATE TABLE saga_definitions (
>     saga_type   VARCHAR(128) NOT NULL,
>     version     INT NOT NULL,
>     definition  JSONB NOT NULL,
>     created_at  TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL DEFAULT NOW(),
>     PRIMARY KEY (saga_type, version)
> );
> ```
>
> When a saga instance is created, it records the saga definition version. The coordinator always loads the saga definition by the version stored in the saga instance, not by the latest version.
>
> This becomes critical when a service changes its API. If the Payment Service v2 changes its void endpoint from `POST /void` to `POST /cancel`, the saga definition for version 1 still references `POST /void`. Sagas that started before the change use the old endpoint. New sagas use the new endpoint. The old endpoint is kept operational until all version-1 sagas complete.

### Topic 3: Sagas in Event-Driven Architectures

Some interviewers ask how sagas interact with event sourcing and CQRS systems.

**Sample answer:**

> Sagas and event sourcing work well together. In an event-sourced system, each saga step produces events that are stored in the event store. The compensation for a step is to append a "reverse" event to the event store. The event store provides the audit trail automatically — every forward operation and every compensation is recorded as an event.
>
> For CQRS systems, the saga coordinator reads from the command side (handles write requests) and the query side provides the current state. The saga log is part of the write side — it records commands and their outcomes. The query side reads the saga log to provide saga status to operators and users.
>
> A common pattern: use the saga coordinator to manage the command side, and let the query side be eventually consistent. The saga does not need to wait for the query side to be updated — it only needs the command side to confirm the step was executed.

### Topic 4: Sagas with Idempotent Offline Processing

A rarely discussed but high-signal topic: how do you handle a saga step that involves a batch process that runs offline?

**Sample answer:**

> Some saga steps, like generating a monthly statement or running a credit check, are not synchronous API calls. They are batch jobs that run on a schedule. For these steps, the saga coordinator does not call the service directly. Instead, it publishes a "request" event that the batch system picks up. The batch system processes the request and publishes a "result" event when done.
>
> The coordinator polls or subscribes to the result event. The step timeout is set to the expected batch processing time plus a buffer. The compensation for the batch step is a "cancel" event that the batch system processes before starting the batch.
>
> The challenge is that the batch system may have already started processing by the time the cancel event arrives. The compensation must handle the case where the batch partially completed. The solution is to design the batch process to be idempotent and check for cancellation events at each processing stage.

## Quick Reference: Interview Answer Templates by Question Type

### For "What is the Saga Pattern?"

Structure: Definition, problem statement, analogy, two outcomes.

"The saga pattern is a way to maintain data consistency across multiple independent services without using distributed transactions. The problem it solves is that when an operation spans three services — like booking a flight, a hotel, and a car — and one fails, the system must undo the work that was already done. A saga breaks this into local transactions with compensating actions. Think of it like planning a road trip: if the last hotel is unavailable, you cancel the earlier bookings. A saga guarantees either all steps succeed or all steps are compensated."

### For "Explain Choreography vs Orchestration"

Structure: Each model defined, comparison table, when to choose each.

"Choreography is decentralized — each service emits events and reacts to events from others. Orchestration has a central coordinator that tells each service what to do. Choreography has no single point of failure but is harder to debug beyond 3-4 services. Orchestration is easier to manage and observe but the coordinator can be a bottleneck. I choose choreography when services are independently owned by separate teams and adding new services should not require modifying existing ones. I choose orchestration when the workflow is complex, has many steps or branching, or when I need a single place to query saga status."

### For "Design a System That Uses Sagas"

Structure: Services, steps, coordination model, infrastructure, failure handling, idempotency, isolation.

Walk through each of the seven design elements in order. Use the e-commerce or travel booking example. Show that you understand both the happy path and the failure path. Mention the saga log for recovery. Discuss idempotency keys for duplicate handling. Acknowledge the isolation limitation and describe countermeasures relevant to your specific system.

### For "Compare Saga with X Pattern"

Structure: Pattern definitions, comparison on 3-4 axes, concrete example of when to choose each.

Pick 2-3 comparison points meaningful to the question: consistency model, latency, throughput, coupling, participant autonomy, failure handling. Give a concrete scenario where pattern A is better and another where pattern B is better. End with a recommendation for the specific use case being discussed.

## Pre-Interview Warmup

Run through this checklist 30 minutes before the interview:

1. Define saga in one sentence: "A sequence of local transactions with compensating actions for rollback."

2. Name the two variants: Choreography (event-driven, no coordinator) and Orchestration (central coordinator).

3. State the key limitation: "Sagas provide atomicity but NOT isolation."

4. Name three countermeasures: Semantic lock, commutative updates, reread value before compensation.

5. Name three failure modes: Missing compensation, non-idempotent operations, saga timeout.

6. Give one concrete compensation example: "Reserve inventory is compensated by releasing inventory. Charge payment is compensated by voiding the charge."

7. Mention the recovery mechanism: "The saga log persists state. On coordinator restart, in-flight sagas are read from the log and resumed."

8. Compare with 2PC: "2PC provides strict ACID but holds locks and reduces availability. Sagas provide eventual consistency with higher availability."

9. State when NOT to use sagas: "Single-database operations, operations needing strict serializability, systems without compensation APIs."

10. Mention one real-world tool: "AWS Step Functions, Temporal, Axon, or Camunda."
