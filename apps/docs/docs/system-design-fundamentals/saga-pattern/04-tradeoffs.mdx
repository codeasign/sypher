# Saga Pattern — Trade-offs & Failure Modes

## Core Tension: ACID vs Availability

The fundamental trade-off in the saga pattern is between strict consistency and high availability. ACID transactions guarantee strong consistency at the cost of holding locks and requiring a coordinator. Sagas sacrifice isolation for availability — they allow intermediate states to be visible in exchange for not holding long-lived locks across service boundaries.

## Decision Matrix: Saga vs Alternatives

Not every multi-service workflow needs a saga. This decision matrix helps choose the right pattern.

| Decision Factor | Saga | Two-Phase Commit (2PC) | Try-Confirm/Cancel (TCC) | Best Effort |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Resource locking | No long-lived locks | Locks held during prepare | Tentative hold with confirm | No locking |
| Isolation | None — intermediate states visible | Serializable | Limited — held state is visible | No guarantees |
| Latency | Low (per-step commits) | High (prepare + commit phases) | Medium (try + confirm phases) | Lowest |
| Participant autonomy | High (each owns its database) | Low (must support XA protocol) | Medium (must implement TCC API) | High |
| Crash recovery | Saga log replay | Heuristic outcomes possible | Manual intervention often needed | Application must handle |
| Compensation complexity | High (must write compensating logic) | None (automatic rollback) | Medium (cancel phase) | None |
| Throughput | High (no global locks) | Low (coordinator bottleneck) | Medium | Highest |
| Suitable for external APIs | Yes | No (requires XA) | Partially (if API supports cancel) | Yes |
| Suitable for long-running ops | Yes | No (locks held too long) | Yes | No |
| Implementation cost | Medium | Low (database built-in) | High (three-phase API design) | Very low |

### When to Use Each Pattern

**Use a saga when:**
- The workflow spans multiple independent services with their own databases.
- The operations are long-running (seconds to hours).
- You need atomicity but can accept eventual consistency.
- You cannot hold locks across service boundaries.

**Use Two-Phase Commit when:**
- All participants are within the same trust boundary (same database cluster or organizational domain).
- The transaction is short-lived (under 1 second).
- You need strict ACID guarantees with serializable isolation.
- All participants support the XA protocol.

**Use TCC when:**
- You need a cleaner compensation model with explicit try/confirm/cancel phases.
- The resources support tentative reservations.
- You want to avoid the coordinator co-dependency of two-phase commit.

**Use Best Effort when:**
- The operation is fire-and-forget and eventual consistency is acceptable.
- You have manual reconciliation processes in place.
- The failure cost is low and infrequent.

## CAP Theorem and Sagas

The CAP theorem states that a distributed data system can provide at most two of three guarantees: Consistency, Availability, and Partition Tolerance. Sagas operate in the AP (Available, Partition Tolerant) space.

During a network partition between the saga coordinator and a participant:

1. The coordinator cannot tell whether the participant received and processed the command.
2. The coordinator must decide: wait for the partition to heal (reducing availability) or proceed (potentially losing consistency).
3. The saga pattern chooses availability: the coordinator times out, marks the step as failed, and begins compensation.
4. After the partition heals, the participant may process the original command (it was in the queue). When the compensation arrives, the participant may see a conflict — the original command was processed but the compensation does not find the resource in the expected state.

This is the CAP-induced inconsistency that saga implementations must handle through idempotency and careful compensation design.

### CAP Violation Examples in Sagas

**Scenario A — Network partition during step execution:**
Two coordinators, C1 and C2, both think they own saga S1 (split-brain scenario from a network partition). C1 runs step 1 successfully. C2, unaware of C1's work, also runs step 1. The participant receives two commands with different idempotency keys. Without proper fencing, the participant executes both, creating two reservations for the same saga.

**Resolution:** The saga log must have a strict update ordering mechanism — each write to the saga log checks the current saga generation. C2's write of step 1 fails because the generation number has already been incremented by C1.

**Scenario B — Participant goes down mid-command:**
The coordinator sends a "reserve inventory" command. The participant starts processing but crashes after decrementing inventory but before persisting the response. The queue redelivers the command. The participant, on restart, sees the idempotency key and returns the cached response (which does not exist because the first attempt never completed). The participant must detect this case and re-execute the logic.

**Resolution:** The participant persists the idempotency key at the start of processing, not at the end. If it crashes mid-processing, the idempotency key exists in the table but the result field is NULL. On retry, the participant sees the key exists but the result is NULL and re-executes the logic. After the second execution, it updates the result in place.

## Five Failure Modes

### Failure Mode 1: Missing Compensation

A saga participant books a hotel room. The saga fails later and sends a compensation request. But the participant does not implement the compensation endpoint — or the compensation does not fully undo the forward operation.

**Example:**
The Hotel Service's "book room" action also sends a confirmation email. The compensation cancels the booking but does not send a cancellation email. The customer receives a booking confirmation but the booking is actually cancelled. The customer shows up at the hotel and finds no reservation.

**Prevention:**
- Every forward action must have a corresponding compensation action.
- The compensation action must fully undo the forward action, including all side effects (emails, notifications, third-party API calls).
- Test compensation actions in staging environments with the same conditions as production.

### Failure Mode 2: Non-Idempotent Operations

A participant receives the same command twice (due to queue redelivery) and processes it both times. The booking is created twice. The compensation only cancels the first booking.

**Example:**
```
Attempt 1: Coordinator sends "Reserve hotel" -> Hotel reserves room 101. Returns success.
           (Queue redelivers the message)
Attempt 2: Coordinator processes redelivery -> Hotel reserves room 101 again.
           (Same room, different booking ID)
Compensation: Coordinator sends "Cancel booking" -> Hotel cancels booking ID 1.
              Booking ID 2 remains active. Room 101 is double-booked.
```

**Prevention:**
- Every request must carry a unique idempotency key.
- The participant must check for duplicate keys before processing.
- The compensation must use the same idempotency key or correlate with the original request to ensure the correct resource is being uncompensated.

### Failure Mode 3: Saga Timeout

A saga times out while waiting for a participant response. The coordinator marks the step as failed and begins compensation. The participant then completes the step successfully — the original request was slow but processed.

**Example:**
```
T+0s: Coordinator sends "Charge payment" to Payment Service.
T+8s: Coordinator's step timeout (10s) has not yet fired.
T+12s: Payment Service responds with success, but the coordinator's
       network layer already declared the connection dead.
T+15s: Coordinator marks step as FAILED and begins compensation.
T+20s: Payment Service has charged the customer.
T+25s: Coordinator sends "Void charge" as compensation.
T+27s: Payment Service voids the charge. Customer is not charged.
       (Correct outcome, but the payment was briefly captured)
```

The system eventually reaches a consistent state (the customer is not charged) but the payment was briefly captured. If the Payment Service did not support void, this would be a permanent inconsistency.

**Prevention:**
- Set step timeouts based on the service's p99 latency, not p50.
- Implement a "step status check" endpoint on each participant so the coordinator can verify whether a timed-out step actually executed.
- Use compensating actions that are guaranteed to work even if the original operation is later discovered to have succeeded.

### Failure Mode 4: Partial Failure Cascade

A saga fails, and compensation begins. One compensation action fails. The coordinator retries. The retry also fails. Now the saga is in a COMPENSATION_FAILED state — some steps are compensated, some are not.

**Example:**
```
Step 1: Reserve inventory  [SUCCESS]
Step 2: Charge payment     [SUCCESS]
Step 3: Ship order         [FAILED — address invalid]

Compensation begins:
Step 2 compensation: Void payment.     [SUCCESS]
Step 1 compensation: Release inventory.  [FAILED — inventory system down]

Retry 1: Release inventory.  [FAILED — still down]
Retry 2: Release inventory.  [FAILED — timeout]
Retry 3 (final): [FAILED]

Result: Saga = COMPENSATION_FAILED
Status: Payment voided (customer not charged), but inventory is still reserved.
```

The inventory is now stuck in a "reserved" state. It cannot be sold to other customers until an operator manually releases it. If the reservation has a TTL, the inventory is eventually released when the TTL expires — but the inventory is unavailable to other sagas until then, potentially causing revenue loss.

**Prevention:**
- Implement TTL-based expiration on reservations so they auto-release after a timeout.
- Have an operator monitoring system that alerts on COMPENSATION_FAILED sagas.
- Build a "retry later" mechanism that reattempts failed compensations on a schedule.

### Failure Mode 5: Zombie Saga

A saga instance continues executing steps despite the coordinator having decided the saga is complete or compensated. This happens when two coordinators believe they own the same saga due to a network partition or lease expiration.

**Example:**
```
Coordinator A owns saga ord_789. Network partition isolates Coordinator A.
Coordinator B picks up saga ord_789 after A's lease expires.
Coordinator B runs the saga to completion.
Coordinator A reconnects and continues executing steps,
unaware that the saga has already been completed by B.

Now: Two sets of bookings exist for the same customer.
```

**Prevention:**
- Each saga step checks the current saga status before executing. If the saga is already in a terminal state (COMPLETED or COMPENSATED), the step rejects the command.
- The saga log is the source of truth — the coordinator checks the database, not its in-memory state, before each step.
- Use fencing tokens or version numbers on the saga instance that invalidate stale coordinator leases.

## Five Anti-Patterns

### Anti-Pattern 1: No Compensating Transaction

A developer implements a saga with a "Send Welcome Email" step that cannot be undone. If a later step fails, the email was already sent, and there is no "unsend" action.

**Problem:** The saga cannot fully compensate. The customer received a welcome email for an account that was never created. This is a permanent inconsistency.

**Fix:** Every step must have a corresponding compensation action, even if the compensation is purely informational (e.g., "Send apology email reversing the welcome message"). If a true compensation is impossible (email cannot be unsent), the step must be redesigned — move the email to the end of the saga so it only fires after all other steps succeed.

### Anti-Pattern 2: Using Sagas for Simple Operations

A team implements a saga for a two-step operation: update user profile and send notification. Both steps operate on the same database.

**Problem:** The saga adds unnecessary complexity. A local ACID transaction would handle this case with zero additional infrastructure. The saga introduces a coordinator, message queues, and compensation logic where none is needed.

**Fix:** Use local ACID transactions for operations within a single service boundary. Use sagas only when the operation spans multiple independent services with independent databases.

### Anti-Pattern 3: Ignoring Idempotency

A developer knows about idempotency but assumes it is not needed because "the message queue guarantees at-most-once delivery."

**Problem:** No message queue guarantees at-most-once delivery across all failure scenarios. Network timeouts, client crashes, and broker failover can all cause duplicate delivery. Without idempotency, a single duplicate can cause double booking, double charging, or double inventory allocation.

**Fix:** Always implement idempotency, regardless of the message delivery guarantees. The cost of an idempotency key check (one database read) is negligible compared to the cost of a data inconsistency.

### Anti-Pattern 4: Sagas Spanning Unrelated Domains

A saga coordinates the creation of a user account, a support ticket, and a marketing email subscription. These operations have no business relationship — the support ticket and email subscription are independent of the account creation.

**Problem:** The compensation logic is complex and compensates operations that have nothing to do with each other. If the account creation fails, the compensation cancels a support ticket that was never needed. The saga couples unrelated business domains.

**Fix:** Use sagas only for operations that form a single business transaction. If the operations are independent, let each succeed or fail on its own. The support ticket system should not be part of the account creation saga — let the user create a ticket after their account is active.

### Anti-Pattern 5: Synchronous Compensation

The saga coordinator blocks waiting for each compensation action to complete. If the hotel service is slow, all other compensation actions are delayed. The saga remains in COMPENSATING state longer than necessary.

**Problem:** Blocking compensation delays increase the window of inconsistency. Other parts of the system cannot act on the freed resources until the compensation completes. Long-running sagas consume coordinator memory and database connections.

**Fix:** Use asynchronous compensation with a "compensation worker" that processes compensation requests independently. The coordinator marks the saga as COMPENSATING and then processes compensation events as they arrive, similar to how it processes forward-step events.

## When NOT to Use the Saga Pattern

1. **Single-database operations**: Use local ACID transactions. A saga adds complexity with no benefit.
2. **Short-lived, high-frequency operations**: If the operation takes under 10ms and runs 100,000 times per second, the saga coordinator becomes a bottleneck. Consider alternatives like batch processing or event sourcing.
3. **Operations requiring strict serializability**: If the business requires that no intermediate state is ever visible, sagas cannot provide this guarantee. Use distributed locking or 2PC instead.
4. **External systems without compensation APIs**: If a third-party API does not support idempotent cancellation or void operations, you cannot build reliable compensation. Either wrap the API with a compensating layer or find another approach.
5. **Read-only workflows**: If the operation only reads data without modifying state, no saga is needed. Use direct service calls.

## Cost Analysis: Saga vs Alternatives

Understanding the operational cost of each pattern helps make the right choice for a given workload.

### Development Cost

| Pattern | Development Effort | Key Effort Areas |
|---|---|---|
| Saga (orchestration) | 12-18 engineering-weeks | Coordinator, saga log, compensation logic, idempotency, retry |
| Saga (choreography) | 8-12 engineering-weeks | Event schema design, compensation logic in each service, tracing |
| 2PC | 2-4 engineering-weeks | Database configuration, XA driver setup, connection pooling |
| TCC | 10-16 engineering-weeks | Three-phase API design, try/confirm/cancel implementation |
| Best Effort | 1-2 engineering-weeks | Error handling, manual reconciliation tools |

### Runtime Cost

| Pattern | Infrastructure | Latency Impact | Throughput Impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| Saga (orchestration) | Coordinator service, saga log DB, 2 queues | +200-500ms per step | Coordinator bottleneck at 5,000-10,000 TPS |
| Saga (choreography) | Event bus, per-service event handling | +50-100ms per step (event propagation) | Limited by event bus throughput |
| PC | Transaction coordinator, XA logs | +500-2000ms per transaction (prepare phase) | 10-100x throughput reduction |
| TCC | Confirm/cancel endpoints | +200-400ms per transaction | 2-5x throughput reduction |
| Best Effort | None | No impact | No impact |

### Maintenance Cost

| Pattern | Ongoing Maintenance | Monitoring Needs |
|---|---|---|
| Saga (orchestration) | Coordinator updates, saga definition changes, compensation testing | Saga completion rate, compensation rate, step latency, DB stress |
| Saga (choreography) | Event schema versioning, tracing setup, per-service monitoring | Event flow completion, per-service success rates |
| 2PC | Database tuning, coordinator HA, XA log cleanup | Transaction abort rate, lock contention, coordinator health |
| TCC | Phase timeout tuning, cancel path reliability | Cancel rate, timeout rate, phase duration |
| Best Effort | Reconciliation script maintenance | Reconciliation failure rate |

### Hidden Costs

**Saga-specific hidden costs:**
- Debugging distributed sagas requires distributed tracing infrastructure (Jaeger, Zipkin, or similar) — approximately 2-4 engineering-weeks to set up.
- Testing compensation paths requires a chaos engineering framework — approximately 3-5 engineering-weeks to build.
- Operator training for dead-letter queue handling — approximately 1-2 engineering-weeks to document and train.
- Compliance audit for financial systems — the saga log must be queryable by auditors, requiring reporting tools and data retention policies.

**2PC-specific hidden costs:**
- Database connection pooling increase — 2PC holds connections for the duration of the prepare phase, requiring 2-3x more connections.
- Lock contention under load — 2PC holds locks across the prepare and commit phases, increasing deadlock probability.
- Coordinator failover complexity — 2PC coordinators require consensus-based replication (Paxos or Raft) for high availability, adding significant infrastructure complexity.

## Operational Overhead Comparison

Beyond code and infrastructure, each pattern imposes different operational overhead on the teams running the system.

### Saga Pattern Operational Overhead

**Daily operations:**
- Monitor saga completion rate and compensation rate on dashboards.
- Review dead-letter queue for COMPENSATION_FAILED sagas.
- Check saga log database storage growth and plan archival.
- Verify that all compensation paths are exercised in staging deployments.

**Weekly operations:**
- Review compensation failure trends and identify services with increasing failure rates.
- Update saga definitions for new service versions or API changes.
- Test compensation paths with chaos injection in staging.

**Incident response:**
- When a saga enters COMPENSATION_FAILED, the operator reviews the saga log, identifies which compensation failed, and either retries it or manually executes the compensation.
- The operator then updates the incident postmortem with the root cause and any changes needed to prevent recurrence.

### 2PC Operational Overhead

**Daily operations:**
- Monitor transaction abort rate and lock contention.
- Check XA log storage and plan cleanup.
- Monitor coordinator health and replication lag.

**Weekly operations:**
- Review deadlock logs and identify query patterns causing contention.
- Tune timeout values and connection pool sizes.

**Incident response:**
- When a 2PC coordinator fails, the operator must determine which transactions were in the prepared state and manually resolve them. This is critical because prepared transactions hold locks until resolved.
- Postmortem focuses on coordinator availability and failover mechanisms.

### Choosing Based on Team Maturity

| Team Maturity | Recommended Pattern | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Small team (< 5 engineers), new system | Local ACID transactions first, sagas only when needed | Avoid distributed complexity until cross-service consistency is required |
| Medium team (5-15 engineers), microservices | Orchestrated saga with managed coordinator (AWS Step Functions or Temporal) | Managed services reduce operational overhead of coordinator maintenance |
| Large team (15+ engineers), complex workflows | Orchestrated saga with custom coordinator or Camunda | Full control over saga definitions, compensation logic, and monitoring |
| High compliance industry (fintech, healthcare) | Saga with outbox pattern and audit-verified saga log | Proof of consistency for auditors requires the saga log to be tamper-evident |

## Real-World Anti-Pattern Case Studies

### Case: The Non-Idempotent Payment Step

A ride-sharing company implemented a saga for trip payment: reserve funds, charge upon completion, split payment with driver. The Payment Service did not implement idempotency because "the saga coordinator guarantees exactly-once delivery." A network blip caused the queue to redeliver the "charge" command. The Payment Service processed the duplicate, charging the customer twice. By the time the error was detected, 3,400 customers had been double-charged over a 45-minute period.

**Root cause:** The saga coordinator's queue provided at-least-once delivery, not exactly-once. The team assumed duplicate delivery would never happen under normal conditions. Network blips make duplicate delivery a normal condition, not an exceptional one.

**Resolution:** The team added idempotency key checking to every saga step. Each participant checks for the key before executing. Double-charges were refunded manually. The incident cost $28,000 in customer credits and three engineering-weeks of work.

### Case: The Missing Compensation

An online marketplace built a seller onboarding saga: create seller account, verify identity, set up payment routing, activate storefront. The "activate storefront" step failed because the seller's store name was already taken. The saga compensated: removed payment routing, reversed identity verification, deleted the account. But the identity verification step had sent a confirmation email that the saga did not track. The seller received a "Your identity has been verified" email but the account was deleted. The seller called support, confused and frustrated.

**Root cause:** The identity verification step had a side effect (sending an email) that was not covered by the compensation action. The compensation deleted the verification record but did not revoke the email.

**Resolution:** The team added a "verification_revoked" email to the compensation action. They also changed the email sending policy: verification emails are sent only after the saga reaches COMPLETED status, not during the identity verification step itself. This required moving the email step to the end of the saga.

## Summary: Trade-Off Cheat Sheet

| Decision | Choose Saga | Choose Alternative |
|---|---|---|
| Consistency model | Eventual with bounded recovery | ACID strict consistency |
| Service coupling | Loose (orchestration) or tight (choreography) | None (monolith) or XA (2PC) |
| Failure handling | Compensation with retry + dead letter | Automatic rollback (2PC) |
| Latency impact | +200-500ms per step | +500-2000ms per prepare phase |
| Throughput limit | 5,000-10,000 sagas/sec (coordinator-bound) | 500-2,000 txns/sec (coordinator-bound) |
| Testing effort | High (every compensation path) | Low (built-in rollback) |
| Debugging | Requires distributed tracing | Centralized (single database) |
| Team maturity needed | Medium-high | Low-medium |
| Compliance audit | Saga log must be tamper-evident | Standard database audit log |
| Operational alerts | Compensation failure rate, stuck sagas | Abort rate, lock contention |
