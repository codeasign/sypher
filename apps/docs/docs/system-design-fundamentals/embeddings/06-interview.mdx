## How This Topic Appears

Embedding questions at FAANG fall into four categories:

### Direct Questions ("How does X work?")

1. "Explain how word embeddings capture semantic meaning."
2. "What's the difference between sparse and dense embeddings?"
3. "How does cosine similarity work and why is it preferred for embeddings?"
4. "Walk me through how you'd build a semantic search system."

### Design Questions ("Design a system that uses X")

5. "Design a semantic search engine for a document repository."
6. "Design a recommendation system that uses embeddings."
7. "Design a system that detects duplicate content using embeddings."
8. "Design a system that finds similar products in an e-commerce catalog."

### Debugging Questions ("Our X is showing high latency — walk me through debugging")

9. "Our semantic search is returning poor results. How would you debug it?"
10. "Vector search latency jumped from 50ms to 500ms. What do you check?"

### Trade-off Questions ("Why would you choose X over Y?")

11. "When would you use embeddings vs. keyword search?"
12. "HNSW vs. IVF — which one do you choose and why?"
13. "Would you self-host your embedding model or use an API?"

## What the Interviewer Is Actually Testing

For each question type, the interviewer is looking for specific signals:

**Direct questions:** They want to see that you understand the fundamentals, not just that you've heard the terms. A 3/4 answer defines embeddings; a 4/4 answer explains why they work (the distributional hypothesis, the training objective, the geometry of high-dimensional spaces).

**Design questions:** They're testing your ability to decompose a problem. The embedding model is just one component. A strong candidate discusses data ingestion, chunking strategies, index selection, query flow, caching, re-ranking, and monitoring. A weak candidate jumps straight to "we'll use FAISS."

**Debugging questions:** These separate candidates who have actual production experience from those who've only read about embeddings. Strong candidates have a systematic debugging process: check cache hit rate, check embedding model version, check index size and skew, check query normalization, A/B test with a simpler search.

**Trade-off questions:** They're evaluating your engineering judgment — when to use a more complex solution vs. a simpler one. Over-engineering is a red flag. Strong candidates can articulate the exact thresholds (scale, latency, accuracy) at which embeddings become worth the complexity.

## Level-by-Level Expectations

### L3/L4 — Junior/New Grad

**Minimum expected knowledge:**
- What embeddings are and why they're useful
- The difference between embeddings and keyword search
- Basic understanding of cosine similarity
- Awareness that FAISS exists

**Vocabulary they must know:** embedding, vector, cosine similarity, dimension, dense, sparse, ANN, semantic search

**Concepts they should explain clearly:** The problem embeddings solve (computers don't understand meaning from raw text). The analogy of a map or coordinate system. A simple 3-step pipeline: embed → index → search.

**What a passing answer looks like:**

Interviewer: "Explain how embeddings work."

Candidate: "Embeddings convert text into lists of numbers — vectors — where the meaning of the text determines its position in a high-dimensional space. If two pieces of text have similar meaning, their vectors are close together; if they have different meanings, they're far apart. The distance is measured using cosine similarity, which looks at the angle between vectors rather than their absolute positions. This works because the embedding model was trained on billions of text examples to learn patterns — words that appear in similar contexts end up with similar vectors."

### L5 — Mid-level

**Expected depth of understanding:**
- Detailed knowledge of ANN index types (HNSW, IVF, PQ)
- Understanding of embedding model architectures (bi-encoder vs cross-encoder)
- Awareness of trade-offs: dimensionality vs. cost, indexing vs. search speed
- Experience with at least one production embedding system

**Trade-off awareness required:** A mid-level candidate should articulate why you might choose HNSW over IVF (HNSW is faster but uses more memory), or 1536-dimensional vectors over 768-dimensional ones (better recall vs. higher storage cost).

**Example question + model answer at this level:**

Interviewer: "Design a semantic search system for a 10-million document repository."

Candidate: "Let me break this down systematically. First, I need to decide on the embedding model and dimensionality. For 10M documents, I'd start with text-embedding-3-small at 1536 dimensions — it's cost-effective and handles well at this scale.

For ingestion, I'd chunk each document into 512-token segments with 128-token overlap to preserve context across chunk boundaries. I'd batch-embed them on GPU, L2-normalize the vectors, and store both the vectors and the chunk-to-document mapping in a sharded HNSW index with efConstruction=200 and M=16.

For the query path, I'd add a Redis cache that stores query vectors and their results with a 5-minute TTL. Cache misses are embedded using the same model, then searched against the index with efSearch=128 for the top-100 candidates.

For quality, I'd add a second-stage re-ranker — a cross-encoder model that scores the top-100 candidates — to push final accuracy above 90% recall. The full pipeline should achieve p50 latency of 50-100ms and p99 of under 500ms."

The interviewer is checking: (1) Do you know how chunking works? (2) Do you mention normalization? (3) Do you separate indexing from querying? (4) Do you consider caching and re-ranking? (5) Do you give specific parameters?

### L6 — Senior

**Expected to drive the conversation proactively:** A senior candidate doesn't wait for the interviewer to ask about trade-offs — they bring them up. They talk about failure modes before being asked. They quantify everything.

**Must raise concerns the interviewer didn't ask about:**
- "One thing I'd want to watch for is index skew — if one category has much more data, it'll slow down all global queries. I'd use consistent hashing with virtual nodes to mitigate this."
- "An often-overlooked detail is model drift during embedding model upgrades. I'd architect the system to support dual-indexing so we can A/B test new models without downtime."
- "For compliance, I'd note that embedding vectors can encode PII. We'd need a data retention policy and the ability to delete specific vectors from the index."

**Must quantify — numbers, estimates, capacity:**
- "At 10M documents with 1536-d vectors, I need about 60 GB for raw vectors and about 120 GB for the HNSW graph. That's 6 c6i.8xlarge nodes."
- "Query latency breaks down to: model inference 30ms, index search 10ms, document retrieval 5ms, re-ranking 100ms. Total ~150ms typical."
- "With a 40% cache hit rate, effective QPS capacity is about 1,700 on 4 GPU nodes."

**Example question + model answer at this level:**

Interviewer: "We're seeing poor search results after upgrading our embedding model. Diagnose this."

Candidate: "There are several things I'd check in order:

First, is the new model compatible with our existing index? If we index using text-embedding-3-small and query with text-embedding-3-large, the vectors are in different spaces and similarity scores are meaningless. This is the most common cause of sudden quality drops — a model version mismatch.

Second, is the query normalization correct? Different models sometimes use different normalization strategies. If the old model used L2-normalization and the new one doesn't, our cosine similarity calculations are comparing normalized to unnormalized vectors, which breaks the metric.

Third, are we using the same tokenizer? A different tokenizer could produce different token boundaries, especially for code-like content or mixed-language text.

Fourth, have we checked the distribution shift? The new model might be better overall but worse for specific types of content. I'd segment our queries by category and check if the recall drop is concentrated. If it is, we might need a hybrid approach — use the new model for most content but fall back to the old model for specific categories.

Finally, I'd A/B test the new model vs the old model with a manual relevance judgment set of 500-1000 queries. This gives us ground-truth recall numbers rather than relying on proxy metrics.

If none of those reveal the issue, I'd look at the index configuration — the new model might produce vectors with different statistical properties that require different ANN parameters. I'd tune efSearch and efConstruction specifically for the new model's vector distribution."

### L7/L8 — Staff/Principal

**Expected to discuss org-level impact:** A staff engineer talks about how this choice affects the entire organization. They discuss team ownership, operational burden, and business outcomes.

**Must connect this to business outcomes:**
- "Choosing a managed embedding API vs self-hosting isn't just about cost per query. It's about whether our team can support a GPU infrastructure — which requires specialized hiring and on-call rotations."
- "The recall target shouldn't be 'as high as possible.' It should be the recall level that moves our business metrics. If going from 85% to 90% recall increases search-to-purchase conversion by 2%, that's worth $X million."

**Must address operational complexity and team ownership:**
- "I'd recommend starting with an API-based embedding service and querying via a managed vector database. This gets us to market in weeks instead of months. When we hit a scale where the cost or latency of the managed service becomes a problem (typically at 100M+ vectors), we can invest in self-hosting."
- "The embedding pipeline would be owned by the ML platform team. The search relevance team owns the query-side logic. There's a shared ownership boundary at the vector store."

**Example question + model answer at this level:**

Interviewer: "We're building an embedding-based recommendation system for a platform with 50 million monthly active users. What's your approach?"

Candidate: "Let me start by clarifying the business goal — is this about increasing engagement, or about monetization through affiliate links? The answer changes the optimization target, and I want to make sure we're aligning the technical choice with the business objective.

Assuming engagement, here's my approach. I'd build a three-tier embedding strategy:

Tier 1 — Content embeddings: Every item (product, article, video) gets a content embedding from its text, images, and metadata. This captures what the item IS.

Tier 2 — Behavioral embeddings: Every user session is embedded into a vector space where the position is determined by what they viewed, liked, and purchased. This captures user INTENT.

Tier 3 — Hybrid embeddings: A neural network that combines content and behavioral embeddings through a fusion layer. This captures the interaction between user intent and item characteristics.

For the timeline, I'd phase this: Tier 1 in the first month (simplest, high impact). Tier 2 in month two (requires instrumenting user sessions and building the training pipeline). Tier 3 in month three (requires labeled data from A/B tests).

For team structure, I'd assign two ML engineers to build and maintain the embedding models, one platform engineer to own the vector infrastructure, and one data engineer to build the ingestion pipelines. The search relevance team consumes the embeddings — they own the query-side logic and relevance tuning.

For operational aspects, I'd set SLOs: recall@10 above 85%, p99 latency below 500ms, and index freshness within 5 minutes of data ingestion. We'd monitor these with an error budget that allows one hour of p99 violations per week.

The biggest risk is the cold-start problem for new items. I'd mitigate this by using content embeddings alone for new items (since we don't have behavioral data yet), then gradually blending in behavioral embeddings as we collect interaction data."

## The FAANG Framework for Answering

### Step 1: Clarify Requirements

**What to ask:**
- "What kind of data are we embedding? Text, images, or both?"
- "What's the scale? Number of documents, queries per second?"
- "What are the latency requirements?"
- "Is this real-time or batch?"
- "What accuracy/recall do we need?"

**What NOT to ask:** Questions that show you're not thinking. Don't ask "should we use GPUs?" — that's your call. Don't ask "what index should we use?" — that's your design decision.

### Step 2: Estimate Scale

For embeddings, the key estimates are:
- Total vector count = number of documents x chunks per document
- Storage per vector = dimensions x bytes per dimension (4 for float32, 2 for float16)
- Index overhead = 2x-5x for HNSW, 1.1x for IVF
- Query throughput = daily active users x queries per user / peak seconds
- GPU nodes = peak QPS / QPS per GPU

### Step 3: High-Level Design

Draw the pipeline:
- Ingestion: Data source → chunking → embedding → normalizing → indexing
- Query: User → query embedding → ANN search → document retrieval → results

### Step 4: Deep Dive

Pick the hardest component and dive deep. Usually this is the index selection (HNSW vs IVF vs PQ) or the embedding model selection (small vs large, self-hosted vs API).

### Step 5: Trade-offs

Volunteer these without being asked:
- "The trade-off with chunk size is: smaller chunks give more precise matching but increase index size by 2-5x."
- "The trade-off with embedding dimensionality is: more dimensions capture more nuance but cost proportionally more to store and compare."
- "The trade-off with ANN recall is: going from 90% to 95% recall typically doubles the index size and increases query latency by 50%."

### Step 6: Wrap Up

Summarize your design in 2-3 sentences: "We're building a semantic search system using 1536-dimensional embeddings from text-embedding-3-small, stored in a sharded HNSW index on 6 GPU nodes. The query pipeline adds Redis caching and a cross-encoder re-ranker for production accuracy. The key risks are index skew and model drift during upgrades."

## Phrase Bank

1. "A key property of embedding spaces is that they're continuous — similar items have similar vectors, and you can interpolate between them. This is what enables analogical reasoning like 'king - man + woman = queen.'"

2. "The distributional hypothesis is the theoretical foundation: words that appear in similar contexts have similar meanings. Embedding models learn this from data."

3. "One advantage of cosine similarity over Euclidean distance is that it's scale-invariant. A long document and a short query about the same topic have different magnitude vectors but similar direction."

4. "HNSW is the default choice for most production systems because it gives the best recall-vs-latency trade-off for in-memory indexes. But it's memory-hungry — the graph connections add 2-5x overhead."

5. "For the query pipeline, I'd add a cache layer because embedding inference is expensive. A Redis cache with TTL-based eviction typically achieves 30-50% hit rate."

6. "The trade-off between bi-encoder and cross-encoder is speed vs accuracy. Bi-encoders pre-compute document embeddings, so they scale to millions of documents. Cross-encoders compute relevance for each query-document pair, which is more accurate but 10-100x slower."

7. "I'd use a two-stage retrieval pipeline: fast ANN search to get top-100 candidates, then a cross-encoder re-ranker to refine the ranking. This gives us the speed of the bi-encoder with the accuracy of the cross-encoder."

8. "The cold-start problem for new documents can be mitigated by keeping a buffer index for recent items and merging it into the main index periodically."

9. "One often-overlooked detail is tokenization consistency. If the embedding model uses a subword tokenizer, long documents get truncated at the max token limit. I need to chunk documents to ensure no information is lost."

10. "For monitoring, the most important metrics are recall@10 (are we returning relevant results?), cache hit rate (are we wasting GPU cycles?), and p99 latency (are users waiting?)."

11. "Index rebuild is a significant operational cost. With a 24-hour rebuild cycle, new documents take up to a day to appear in search results. For faster freshness, I'd use a streaming update pipeline."

12. "The curse of dimensionality means that in very high-dimensional spaces, all vectors are roughly equally distant from each other. That's why dimensionality reduction techniques like PCA are sometimes applied to embeddings before indexing."

13. "When comparing embedding models, I'd evaluate on a domain-specific test set. General-purpose benchmarks like MTEB give an overview, but domain-specific relevance is what matters for our application."

14. "A hybrid search approach — combining BM25 keyword matching with embedding-based semantic search — typically outperforms either approach alone. The weights between the two scores need to be tuned per use case."

15. "Vector search is particularly sensitive to data distribution shifts. If the content being indexed changes over time (new categories, new languages), recall degrades silently. Continuous monitoring and periodic index rebuilds are essential."

## Red Flags

1. **Not mentioning normalization.** If you don't mention L2-normalization of both index and query vectors, the interviewer will note the gap.

2. **Only discussing one index type.** A candidate who only knows HNSW or only knows IVF hasn't done their homework. Mention at least two and compare them.

3. **No mention of the caching layer.** Every senior engineer knows that model inference is expensive. Not caching is a strong signal of inexperience.

4. **Treating embedding accuracy as a binary property.** Embeddings aren't "accurate" or "not accurate" — they trade off recall for speed at every layer. Good candidates discuss this trade-off.

5. **Ignoring the cold start problem.** Index is empty → queries return nothing. Not addressing this signals you haven't deployed an embedding system.

6. **No model upgrade strategy.** The question isn't "will you upgrade the model?" — it's "how will you upgrade without downtime and without breaking the index?" Not having a plan is a red flag.

7. **Proposing embeddings for everything.** Not recognizing when keyword search is a better choice signals a lack of judgment.

8. **No discussion of cost.** Embedding APIs charge per token. GPU instances are expensive. A candidate who doesn't address cost hasn't operated at scale.

### Mock Interview Walkthrough

What follows is a full mock interview conversation for the question "Design a semantic search engine for a knowledge base." The interviewer pushes back multiple times. Pay attention to how the candidate responds under pressure.

**Interviewer:** "Let's start with a system design question. Design a semantic search engine for a knowledge base containing internal company documentation, engineering wikis, and support articles. Walk me through your approach."

**Candidate:** "Before I sketch the architecture, I'd like to clarify a few requirements. First, what scale are we talking about — how many documents, what's the average document length, and how many queries per second do we expect?"

**Interviewer:** "Good question. About 2 million documents total, average length is roughly 2000 words. Query volume is about 500 QPS during peak hours, mostly from internal employees."

**Candidate:** "And what are the latency requirements? Is this a real-time search-as-you-type experience, or is it acceptable to have a few hundred milliseconds of delay?"

**Interviewer:** "It needs to feel instant. Under 200 milliseconds p99."

**Candidate:** "One more clarification — do we need real-time freshness? If someone edits a wiki page, should the updated version appear in search results immediately?"

**Interviewer:** "Within 5 minutes of the edit is acceptable."

**Candidate:** "Alright, let me estimate the scale. Two million documents at 2000 words each. At roughly 1.3 tokens per word, that's about 2600 tokens per document. With a 512-token chunk size and 128-token overlap, each document breaks into about 7 chunks. So we're looking at roughly 14 million vectors. Using text-embedding-3-small at 1536 dimensions with float32 storage, each vector is 6 KB. The raw vectors require about 84 GB. With HNSW index overhead of about 2x for the graph connections, we need roughly 170 GB of memory. For 500 QPS peak, allowing for cache hits reducing effective load by 40 percent, we have about 300 raw queries per second. A single modern GPU can handle about 100 embedding inferences per second, so we need 3 GPUs for embed. The ANN search itself is CPU-bound at this scale, so I'd allocate 4 application nodes with enough RAM to hold the full index in memory, plus 2 GPU nodes for embedding. Total: 6 nodes."

**Interviewer:** "Why HNSW and not IVF?"

**Candidate:** "For a knowledge base this size, recall quality matters more than index build speed, because incorrect search results mean wasted employee time. HNSW gives better recall at the same latency compared to IVF. Specifically, HNSW with efSearch=128 achieves roughly 95 percent recall@10, while IVF with 4096 centroids needs about 2x the probes to match that recall, which eats into the latency budget. The downside of HNSW is memory — the hierarchical graph adds about 2x overhead — but 170 GB fits comfortably across 4 nodes at 43 GB each. If we were at 100 million vectors, IVF would become attractive because the graph would exceed available memory."

**Interviewer:** "Walk me through the high-level architecture."

**Candidate:** "There are two pipelines. The ingestion pipeline: documents arrive from the content management system. A chunker splits them into 512-token segments with overlap. Each chunk passes through the embedding model running on GPU, producing a 1536-dimensional vector. The vector is L2-normalized and inserted into the HNSW index. The raw text and metadata go into a document store for retrieval. The query pipeline: user types a query, which is normalized and embedded using the same model. The query vector goes to the ANN search, which returns the top-100 nearest neighbors. A cross-encoder re-ranker scores those 100 against the original query text, producing a refined ranking. We fetch the top-10 document fragments from the document store and return them to the user."

**Interviewer:** "Why a cross-encoder re-ranker? Isn't that expensive?"

**Candidate:** "It is — roughly 10 to 50 milliseconds per query-document pair. But we only run it on the top-100 from ANN search, so it adds about 200 to 500 milliseconds total. That sounds high, so I'd add a caveat: this step runs asynchronously. The user gets the ANN results immediately in under 50 milliseconds, and the re-ranked results replace them within another 300 milliseconds. Users perceive this as 'instant first result, refined shortly after.' For queries where latency is critical — like autocomplete — we skip re-ranking entirely and use raw ANN output."

**Interviewer:** "Now let's talk about handling updates. How do you handle a document that's been edited?"

**Candidate:** "There are two strategies, and I'd choose based on the edit frequency. If edits are rare — say fewer than 1000 per day — I'd use a delete-and-reinsert approach. We store a mapping from document ID to chunk IDs. When a document is edited, we look up its chunk IDs, delete those vectors from the HNSW index using a tombstone mechanism, recompute the embeddings for the new version, and insert them. This is operationally simpler but causes index fragmentation over time. If edits are frequent, I'd use a two-tier approach: a main index that rebuilds every 24 hours and a delta index for recent changes. Queries merge results from both indexes. The delta index is small enough to rebuild quickly."

**Interviewer:** "The interviewer pushes back — 'Two-tier sounds complex. Why not just rebuild the whole index every 24 hours?'"

**Candidate:** "That works if the 5-minute freshness requirement is flexible. But you stated edits should appear within 5 minutes. A 24-hour rebuild cycle means a wiki edit made at 9 AM doesn't appear in search until the next day. That's unacceptable for a knowledge base where engineers rely on up-to-date documentation. If we relax the freshness requirement to hourly, then a full rebuild every hour with an incremental indexer becomes viable. But with the stated 5-minute SLA, the delta index approach is the right call."

**Interviewer:** "What about failures? What happens if the embedding model goes down?"

**Candidate:** "If the embedding model fails, the ingestion pipeline stops — new documents can't be indexed. The query pipeline degrades: we can't embed new queries. I'd implement a circuit breaker that detects embedding failures and falls back to keyword search using Elasticsearch. The fallback should be transparent to the user. For the ingestion side, I'd use a dead-letter queue. Failed embeddings go to the DLQ with the original chunk text and a timestamp. When the model recovers, we replay the DLQ. This ensures no data loss."

**Interviewer:** "How do you monitor whether the system is actually producing good results?"

**Candidate:** "Three tiers of monitoring. Tier 1 — operational: index size, memory usage, query latency, cache hit rate, embedding throughput. Tier 2 — quality: recall@10 against a manually labeled relevance set of 1000 queries. We compute this daily and alert if recall drops below 90 percent. Tier 3 — business: search-to-click rate, search-to-edit rate for the knowledge base. If users search but don't click results, something is wrong even if recall looks fine. The most dangerous metric is recall@10 holding steady while click-through drops — that indicates the embedding space drifted toward a different semantic interpretation that technically matches but is practically useless. I'd also log all queries with zero results and review them weekly for edge cases the embedding model misses."

### Follow-Up Questions

Interviewers use follow-up questions to probe depth beyond the rehearsed answer. Here are the most common ones and what an L5+ candidate should say.

**"How would you handle 100x the scale?"**

At 200 million documents and 50,000 QPS, in-memory HNSW becomes infeasible — the memory cost alone exceeds $100,000 per month. I'd switch to DiskANN, which stores the graph on SSD with memory-mapped files, reducing cost by 10x at the expense of higher p99 latency. I'd also quantize vectors to float16 or int8, accepting a small recall hit to cut storage in half. The embedding pipeline would move from single-GPU serving to a distributed inference cluster with load balancing and autoscaling. The cache layer becomes critical — at this scale, even a 10 percent cache miss rate reduction saves significant GPU cost.

**"What happens if we need to re-index everything?"**

Full re-index is expensive. With 14 million vectors, re-embedding all chunks takes about 39 GPU-hours. I'd plan this as a maintenance window but implement a blue-green index rotation: build the new index on a separate cluster while the old index serves traffic. When the new index is ready, switch traffic with a DNS or load-balancer change. This avoids downtime entirely. The operational lesson is to test the re-index pipeline regularly, not just during emergencies, so we know exactly how long it takes.

**"How do you handle documents that are updated frequently?"**

Frequently updated documents present a consistency challenge. If a page is edited every few minutes, the delete-and-reinsert approach creates high churn — the index rebuilds its graph connections on every update, which is expensive. I'd use a versioned chunking strategy: each chunk carries a document version number. The query pipeline retrieves the latest version of each document and presents only the most recent chunks. Stale chunks remain in the index but are filtered at retrieval time. This trades index space for write throughput, which is the right trade-off for high-churn content.

**"What if the embedding API goes down?"**

Embedding APIs are a single point of failure. I'd implement a fallback chain: primary embedding API with a timeout of 500 milliseconds, then a fallback model running locally on CPU using ONNX Runtime with a quantized version of the model, and finally a keyword search fallback if both fail. The local fallback is slower and less accurate — typically 80 percent of the primary model's quality — but it keeps the system running. This is a standard graceful degradation pattern: degrade accuracy before availability.

**"How do you measure whether the embedding system is actually better than keyword search?"**

This is the most important question because it separates hype from substance. I'd run an A/B test where 50 percent of traffic gets embedding search and 50 percent gets keyword search. The metrics to compare are: search-to-click rate, search-to-conversion rate if applicable, query abandonment rate, and time to first result. I'd also compute the overlap — what percentage of queries return the same top result under both systems. Low overlap with higher engagement on the embedding side is strong evidence the embedding system is finding relevant results keyword search misses. I'd also manually review the cases where one system succeeds and the other fails, to understand the qualitative difference. If embedding search doesn't move the business metrics meaningfully, I'd recommend keeping keyword search and investing engineering resources elsewhere.

### System Design Scoring Rubric

Interviewers score embedding design questions along four axes: fundamentals, architecture, trade-offs, and operations. Here is how those scores translate to hiring decisions.

**Strong Hire at L4:** Defines embeddings clearly with a real analogy. Mentions cosine similarity and why it's better than Euclidean distance. Names one ANN index type. Draws a correct ingestion-to-query pipeline. Understands that embedding inference is expensive and mentions caching.

**Pass at L5:** Quantifies storage, latency, and node count. Explains why HNSW over IVF with specific parameters. Discusses chunking strategy with overlap. Mentions two-stage retrieval with re-ranking. Discusses the trade-off between recall and latency. Has a plan for model upgrades and index rebuilding.

**Strong Hire at L5:** Same as Pass, plus discusses monitoring (recall@10, cache hit rate, latency percentiles). Addresses failure modes before being asked. Compares at least two embedding models (text-embedding-3-small vs large, or self-hosted vs API). Discusses tokenization consistency. Mentions the cold-start problem for new documents.

**Pass at L6:** Quantifies everything with specific numbers. Discusses index skew and consistent hashing. Has a model upgrade strategy with dual-indexing for A/B testing. Addresses PII and data retention. Designs the monitoring and alerting system. Proactively raises trade-offs the interviewer hasn't asked about.

**Strong Hire at L6:** Same as Pass, plus draws on production experience — recounts a specific incident where something went wrong and how it was fixed. Discusses team ownership boundaries. Quantifies the cost of the system in dollar terms. Discusses the business impact of different recall levels.

**Strong Hire at L7:** Everything at L6, plus discusses org-level impact: team structure, hiring needs, operational burden. Connects technical decisions to business outcomes with dollar figures. Discusses phased rollout strategy. Shows awareness of compliance, security, and data governance.

**Most common reason candidates fail:** They propose embeddings without understanding the trade-offs. A candidate who says "we'll use embeddings" without discussing when keyword search would be sufficient signals inexperience. The second most common failure is not quantifying anything — abstract architecture without concrete numbers is seen as a lack of production experience.

**Examples of weak, average, and strong responses to the same question:**

Question: "What embedding model would you use for a semantic search system?"

Weak: "I'd use text-embedding-3-large because it's the most accurate." (Shows no awareness of cost, latency, or scale trade-offs.)

Average: "I'd use text-embedding-3-small at 1536 dimensions. It's cost-effective and the recall is close to the large model for most use cases. If we need higher accuracy on domain-specific queries, I'd switch to the large model or fine-tune our own." (Shows awareness of trade-offs but no quantification.)

Strong: "I'd start with text-embedding-3-small at 1536 dimensions because it gives 90 percent of the recall of the large model at 20 percent of the cost. For our scale of 14 million chunks, the small model costs roughly $40 per full re-index versus $200 for the large model. I'd monitor recall@10 against a domain-specific test set. If our domain has specialized terminology — like internal product codenames — the small model's general-purpose training may miss those nuances. In that case, I'd move to the large model, which has shown 5-10 percent higher recall on specialized domains in published benchmarks. The trade-off is $160 per re-index for an expected 7 percent recall improvement. I'd let the business decide whether that's worth it based on the value of improved search results." (Quantifies cost, mentions domain specificity, benchmarks against a test set, connects to business value, and lets metrics drive the decision.)

## Practice Drills

### Drill 1: 2-Minute Explain

**Prompt:** "Explain what an embedding is to a non-technical product manager."

**Time limit:** 2 minutes.

**Evaluation criteria:** The explanation must use an analogy (maps, coordinates, ingredients), define only 3 terms (vector, similarity, dimension), and be completely jargon-free. A passing answer gets the PM to nod along. A failing answer gets a blank stare.

### Drill 2: 5-Minute Design

**Prompt:** "Here's our scenario: We have 5 million product descriptions. Users search for products using natural language. Currently we use Elasticsearch keyword search. Users complain that searching 'comfy chair' doesn't find 'ergonomic recliner.' Design an embedding-based improvement."

**Time limit:** 5 minutes.

**Evaluation criteria:** Must mention hybrid search (keep Elasticsearch + add embeddings), chunking strategy, index selection, and caching. Bonus points for mentioning re-ranking and A/B testing.

### Drill 3: 10-Minute Deep Dive

**Prompt:** "Design a real-time duplicate content detection system for a content platform. 50 million articles. Must detect near-duplicates within seconds of publication."
