## How This Topic Appears

DNS questions in system design interviews rarely surface as standalone topics. They appear as a sub-problem within a larger system design question, or as a focused deep-dive when you mention DNS in your architecture. Here are the most common phrasings, categorized by how they surface.

### Category 1: "How would you design..." (Full System Design)

These questions are complete system design problems where DNS is one component of the solution. Interviewers watch to see if you mention DNS at all.

1. "Design YouTube. Walk me through the full request flow from URL to video playing on the screen."
2. "Design a global CDN. How does a user in Tokyo request an image and get it from the Tokyo edge?"
3. "Design the notification system for WhatsApp. How does a message reach the recipient in under 100ms?"
4. "Design a URL shortener like bit.ly. When someone clicks a link, what happens?"

### Category 2: "How does X work?" (Deep Dive)

These questions test whether you understand DNS at the protocol level, not just the concept level.

5. "Walk me through what happens when you type google.com into a browser and press Enter."
6. "How does DNS work at the protocol level? I want the wire format."
7. "What happens when a DNS query exceeds 512 bytes?"
8. "How does DNSSEC work under the hood? Walk me through the cryptographic chain."

### Category 3: "Why did this fail?" (Debugging / Root Cause)

These questions present a failure scenario and ask you to diagnose the root cause.

9. "We changed our web server IP 24 hours ago. Half our users still see the old IP. Why?"
10. "Our mobile app works on WiFi but not on cellular data. Both use the same DNS provider. What could be different?"
11. "The site is down in Japan but works everywhere else. DNS resolver logs show SERVFAIL from Japanese resolvers but success from everywhere else. What happened?"

### Category 4: "Compare X and Y" (Trade-off Analysis)

These questions test your understanding of DNS alternatives and trade-offs.

12. "Compare DNS-based load balancing with a hardware load balancer. When would you use each?"
13. "Would you use Route53 or self-host BIND for a 10M DAU application? Why?"
14. "What are the trade-offs between a TTL of 60 seconds versus 3600 seconds?"

## What the Interviewer Is Actually Testing

**For Category 1 (System Design):** The interviewer wants to see if DNS is part of your mental model. Candidates who skip DNS in a "design YouTube" answer and jump straight to database sharding reveal a gap in their full-stack understanding. The bar is not deep DNS expertise — it is awareness that DNS exists as the first hop in the request flow.

**For Category 2 (Deep Dive):** The interviewer wants to know if you have ever actually worked with DNS at the protocol level. Candidates who can describe the four parts of a DNS message (header, question, answer, authority) distinguish themselves from candidates who only know the concept. The bar is understanding the request flow end-to-end, including referral chains and caching.

**For Category 3 (Debugging):** The interviewer wants to see your troubleshooting process. The answer is often "TTL too high" or "resolver caching," but the interviewer cares about your reasoning path: what data you would collect, what tools you would use (dig, nslookup, tcpdump), and how you would isolate the variable.

**For Category 4 (Trade-offs):** The interviewer wants to see systematic comparison, not just memorized facts. A strong answer identifies dimensions (latency, consistency, cost, operational complexity), evaluates each option along those dimensions, and makes a recommendation with specific reasoning.

## Level-by-Level Expectations

### L3/L4 (Entry Level / Early Career)

**Expectation**: You understand the concept of DNS and can describe what happens at a high level when a URL is entered.

**Strong answer**: "The browser checks its cache, then the OS resolver, then queries the recursive resolver configured in /etc/resolv.conf. The recursive resolver walks the root server, TLD server, and authoritative nameserver to get the IP. The response is cached at each level for the TTL duration."

**Differentiator**: Mentioning that the order is browser cache, OS cache, router cache, then recursive resolver demonstrates you understand the caching hierarchy. Mentioning TTL shows you understand a key operational parameter.

### L5 (Senior)

**Expectation**: You understand DNS trade-offs, can diagnose common issues, and design systems that incorporate DNS appropriately.

**Strong answer**: "For the CDN design, I would use Route53 with latency-based routing and a TTL of 60 seconds. The authoritative nameserver would return A records pointing to the nearest edge location based on the user's resolver subnet from EDNS0. This gives us geographic routing with 60-second failover. If an edge location goes down, the health checks remove its IP from responses within 30 seconds, and the TTL ensures all resolvers see the updated response within 90 seconds. For the backend, I would NOT rely on DNS for server-level health — I would use an internal load balancer with sub-second health checks."

**Differentiator**: Giving concrete numbers (60-second TTL, 30-second health check interval, 90-second total failover) demonstrates real operational experience. Explaining when NOT to use DNS shows mature judgment.

### L6 (Staff)

**Expectation**: You can design DNS infrastructure at global scale, anticipate failure modes, and design for operational excellence.

**Strong answer**: "For a global streaming service at 100M+ users, I would run a three-layer DNS architecture. Layer 1 is a managed DNS provider (Route53 or CloudDNS) as the authoritative source of truth with anycast at 4+ regions. Layer 2 is our own recursive resolver fleet in each region, configured with aggressive prefetch and stale-answer-serving on failure. Layer 3 is a traffic management layer that combines DNS with a health-check-based steering system. The key insight is that DNS alone cannot provide sub-10-second failover, so we need a layer-4 load balancer that does health-based routing and only uses DNS for geographic steering. I would set TTL to 300 seconds for most records, 60 seconds for active migration windows, and implement pre-fetching so the resolver refreshes cache before TTL expiry."

**Differentiator**: Distinguishing between geographic routing (DNS) and health-based routing (load balancer) shows architectural maturity. Mentioning pre-fetching and stale-answer-serving shows deep operational knowledge.

### L7/L8 (Principal / Distinguished)

**Expectation**: You can design DNS architecture for an entire organization, including multi-cloud strategy, security posture, and cost optimization at planetary scale.

**Strong answer**: "I would design a multi-provider DNS strategy with Route53 as primary and NS1 as secondary, each configured as authoritative for the same zones. Zone transfers run every 60 seconds with active monitoring of serial numbers — if the secondary diverges, we page immediately. We operate our own recursive resolver fleet using Unbound, configured with DNSSEC validation and aggressive negative caching to reduce auth NS load by 30%. For internal service discovery, we use CoreDNS backed by our service registry, with separate zones for internal-only services. The total DNS operations cost for 100M DAU would be approximately USD 12,000/month in DNS provider fees plus USD 20,000/month in resolver infrastructure. The failure mode I am most concerned about is registrar failure — if the TLD registry goes down or the domain expires, nothing else matters. I would maintain a backup domain on a separate TLD with a completely independent registrar and DNS infrastructure, tested quarterly."

**Differentiator**: Quoting specific costs and showing multi-cloud DNS strategy at organizational scale separates L7+ answers. The backup domain on a separate TLD concept demonstrates anticipating a failure mode most engineers never consider.

## The FAANG Framework for Answering

### Step 1: Clarify Scope (15 seconds)

Before answering, confirm what the interviewer wants. For a "design YouTube" question: "Do you want me to focus on the video serving path specifically, or cover the full system including upload, encoding, recommendations, and search?" This prevents you from spending 5 minutes on DNS when the interviewer wants storage.

### Step 2: Start from the Request (30 seconds)

Always start the answer at the DNS resolution step, even for full system design. "A user types youtube.com and presses Enter. The first thing that happens is DNS resolution..."

### Step 3: State the Caching Layers (15 seconds)

List the caching layers explicitly: "DNS is resolved at four levels: browser cache, OS cache, resolver cache, and the full recursive walk."

### Step 4: Describe the Recursive Walk (60 seconds)

Walk through root, TLD, and authoritative nameserver queries. Include the referral mechanism. Mention that the result is cached at each level.

### Step 5: Connect DNS to the Rest of the System (30 seconds)

"Once we have the IP address, the browser opens a TCP connection to port 443, and the TLS handshake begins. The DNS resolution took approximately 50ms out of the 200ms total time to first byte."

### Step 6: Discuss Trade-offs (60 seconds)

If the interviewer probes deeper: "The key trade-off in DNS is TTL vs. propagation speed. Short TTLs give fast change propagation but increase authoritative server load. For this system, I would use a 60-second TTL during normal operations and reduce to 10 seconds during active failover — but I would only do that for a limited set of critical records."

## Phrase Bank

**When introducing DNS in a system design answer:**
- "The first step in the request flow is DNS resolution. The browser needs to translate the human-readable domain name into an IP address before it can open any connection."
- "DNS is the first network request the browser makes, so it sets the floor for the user's perceived latency."

**When describing the recursive resolution process:**
- "The recursive resolver walks the delegation chain: root, TLD, then authoritative nameserver. Each step is a referral, not the final answer."
- "The cache hierarchy means a popular domain might resolve in under 1ms while a cold domain takes 100-200ms for the full walk."
- "Every response includes a TTL that tells the resolver how long to cache the result. This is the single most important operational parameter in DNS."

**When discussing trade-offs:**
- "Short TTLs are a double-edged sword: they give fast propagation but increase query load on authoritative servers."
- "DNS is an eventually consistent system. There is no transaction log, no consensus protocol — just TTLs and cache expiry."
- "If you need sub-second failover, DNS is the wrong tool. Use a load balancer with active health checks instead."
- "The cost of DNS is dominated by query volume at scale. At 10M DAU, DNS provider fees are around USD 1000/month."

**When diagnosing a DNS issue:**
- "The first thing I would check is TTL. What is the TTL on the record, and when did the change happen? If the TTL is 86400 seconds and the change was 12 hours ago, we are still in the propagation window."
- "I would run dig with +trace to see exactly which nameserver returns what, and at which step the response diverges."
- "I would check whether the recursive resolver seen by the client is the same one I can reach from my test machine. Different resolvers have different cache states."

**When comparing DNS providers:**
- "Route53 charges by the query, so at 100M queries/day the bill is about USD 1200/month in query charges alone. Self-hosting Unbound on EC2 would cost about USD 500/month in instance costs."
- "Multi-provider DNS is essential at scale, but you must actively monitor zone serial numbers across providers. Silent misconfiguration is common."

**When concluding a DNS discussion:**
- "DNS is the first thing that happens and the first thing that breaks. Getting it right means understanding that availability matters more than consistency, and that caching is simultaneously the best and worst feature."
- "The three things I always check for any DNS setup: TTL values, nameserver redundancy, and DNSSEC configuration."

## Red Flags

**Red Flag 1: You say "DNS just resolves a name to an IP."** This is technically correct but shows no depth. Expand immediately to describe the recursive walk, caching layers, or TTL.

**Red Flag 2: You ignore DNS in a system design answer.** You designed a global video streaming platform but started your answer with "the client sends an HTTP request." An interviewer will ask you to back up: "But how does the client know the IP?"

**Red Flag 3: You recommend TTL = 0 for "instant propagation."** A TTL of 0 means every query goes to the authoritative server, which means your authoritative server must handle every single request from every resolver worldwide. For a site with 100K visitors, that is 400K+ QPS. The authoritative server will collapse.

**Red Flag 4: You say "DNS is always UDP."** DNS supports TCP for responses over 512 bytes (or 1232 with EDNS0). Zone transfers (AXFR/IXFR) always use TCP. About 5% of all DNS queries fall back to TCP.

**Red Flag 5: You say you can "flush DNS globally."** There is no button to clear every resolver's cache worldwide. Cache entries expire based on their TTL. You can flush your own resolver's cache, but you cannot control Google's cache, your ISP's cache, or your users' browser caches.

**Red Flag 6: You recommend a single nameserver.** A single point of failure for the most critical service in your stack. ICANN requires at least two NS records. In production, four on different networks is standard.

**Red Flag 7: You confuse DNS with a load balancer.** "DNS will route traffic away from the unhealthy server." DNS has no health awareness. It returns the same records regardless of backend health. You need a separate health-checking system to remove unhealthy records from DNS responses.

**Red Flag 8: You say "DNSSEC is not necessary."** For an internal service, fine. For any public-facing domain, DNSSEC prevents the cache poisoning attacks described in the trade-offs chapter. The operational cost is real, but the security risk of operating without it at scale is well documented.

## Practice Drills

### Drill 1: The 2-Minute Question

**Prompt**: "Walk me through what happens when I type amazon.com into my browser and press Enter. Focus only on DNS. You have 2 minutes."

**Expected structure**: Browser cache check, OS cache check, recursive resolver query, root server referral, TLD server referral, authoritative server response, caching at each level, TCP connection start. Total time: 50-200ms.

**Timer strategy**: 0:00-0:15 state the caching layers. 0:15-1:00 describe the recursive walk. 1:00-1:30 mention TTL and caching. 1:30-2:00 connect to TCP connection and mention total time.

### Drill 2: The 5-Minute Question

**Prompt**: "Design the DNS infrastructure for a global social media platform with 100 million daily active users. You have 5 minutes."

**Expected structure**: Managed DNS provider choice (Route53), anycast nameservers in 3+ regions, TTL strategy (300s default, 60s for critical records), recursive resolver fleet sizing (10 nodes, regional distribution), health-check integration, DNSSEC, monitoring metrics, cost estimate.

**Timer strategy**: 0:00-1:00 architecture overview and provider choice. 1:00-2:00 TTL strategy and reasoning. 2:00-3:00 capacity planning (QPS math). 3:00-4:00 failure modes and mitigation. 4:00-5:00 cost and operations.

### Drill 3: The 10-Minute Question

**Prompt**: "A major e-commerce site just migrated DNS providers. The cutover was 6 hours ago. 20% of users still see the old site. Diagnose the issue. You have 10 minutes."

**Expected structure**: 
- Phase 1 (2 min): Gather data — what is the TTL on the old NS records? What is the TTL on the A records? Check dig +trace output.
- Phase 2 (3 min): Identify likely cause — the NS record TTL at the registrar is typically 86400 seconds (24 hours). 6 hours is within the propagation window. Also check if the old DNS provider still serves the zone (they may not have deactivated it).
- Phase 3 (3 min): Verify with data — query old NS directly, compare serial numbers, check resolver cache states.
- Phase 4 (2 min): Remediation — wait for TTL expiry (18 hours remaining), or contact old provider to decommission, or add both provider NS records for overlapping service.

### Drill 4: The Trade-off Question

**Prompt**: "Compare DNS-based round-robin load balancing with an application-layer load balancer (NGINX/HAProxy). When would you use each? You have 5 minutes."

**Expected structure**: Compare along 5 dimensions: failover speed (DNS: 60-180s vs LB: sub-1s), health awareness (DNS: none vs LB: active health checks), session affinity (DNS: none vs LB: sticky sessions), cost (DNS: free with provider vs LB: extra infra cost), and complexity (DNS: simple vs LB: requires configuration). Recommendation: DNS for geographic routing at low traffic or when LB is overkill. LB for production systems that need health-based routing.

### Drill 5: The Debugging Question

**Prompt**: "Users in Southeast Asia report slow load times for your global SaaS product. Users in North America and Europe are fine. DNS query times from Southeast Asia average 800ms. Diagnose the issue. You have 5 minutes."

**Expected structure**: 
- Hypothesis 1: No anycast DNS in Southeast Asia. Queries from Singapore go to US servers. Resolution: anycast deployment in Singapore or Tokyo.
- Hypothesis 2: Local ISP resolver is misconfigured or overloaded. Resolution: test with public resolver (1.1.1.1).
- Hypothesis 3: Authoritative nameserver is in us-east-1 with no Southeast Asian edge. Resolution: deploy secondary nameserver in ap-southeast-1.
- Data collection: dig +trace from a Southeast Asian test server, measure per-hop latency, check resolver source IP.
