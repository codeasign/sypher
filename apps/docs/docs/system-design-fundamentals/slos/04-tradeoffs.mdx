## The Core Tension

The fundamental tension in SLO systems is between precision and cost. A more precise SLO — one that measures every request, tracks every latency percentile, and evaluates every minute — costs more to compute, store, and maintain. A less precise SLO — one that samples requests, aggregates into coarse buckets, and evaluates over long windows — costs less but provides weaker guarantees.

This tension manifests in every design decision. How many requests should you sample? How many latency buckets should you use? How long should the measurement window be? How many SLOs should you define? Each decision trades precision for cost, or cost for precision.

The right answer depends on the cost of failure. A payment processing SLO that measures every transaction justifies a higher cost than a photo-sharing SLO that measures a sample. The SLO system should cost less than the failures it prevents. If you spend 100,000 dollars per year on SLO infrastructure to prevent 10,000 dollars per year in failure costs, you have optimized the wrong variable.

There is also a tension between SLO completeness and SLO focus. A system that measures every endpoint, every metric, and every dimension has comprehensive coverage but generates too much noise. Engineers spend more time managing SLOs than responding to them. A system that measures only the three most critical metrics is focused and actionable but may miss subtle degradations in non-critical endpoints.

The precision-cost tension also applies to SLO targets. A 99.99% SLO costs significantly more than a 99.9% SLO because it requires:
- More redundant infrastructure to eliminate single points of failure
- More aggressive monitoring to detect the 0.01% of failures
- More conservative deployment practices to avoid consuming the tiny error budget
- More engineering time to maintain the higher reliability standard

The marginal cost of each additional 9 increases exponentially. Moving from 99.9% to 99.99% typically requires 2-3x the infrastructure investment. Moving from 99.99% to 99.999% requires 5-10x the investment. The precision-cost curve is not linear. It is exponential.

## SLO Target Cost Analysis

### Cost per Nine

The cost of achieving each additional nine of reliability follows a predictable pattern. Understanding this pattern helps teams make informed decisions about SLO targets.

The cost per nine increases exponentially because each additional nine requires eliminating increasingly rare failure modes:

**99% (two nines):** Basic single-server setup with monitoring. Error budget: 3.65 days per year. Cost premium: baseline.

**99.9% (three nines):** Redundant servers, load balancing, automated failover. Error budget: 8.7 hours per year. Cost premium: 1.5-2x baseline.

**99.99% (four nines):** Multi-AZ deployment, active-passive database, disaster recovery plan. Error budget: 52 minutes per year. Cost premium: 3-5x baseline.

**99.999% (five nines):** Multi-region active-active, real-time replication, chaos engineering. Error budget: 5.25 minutes per year. Cost premium: 10-20x baseline.

**99.9999% (six nines):** Global load balancing, multi-cloud redundancy, full automation. Error budget: 31.5 seconds per year. Cost premium: 50-100x baseline.

The decision to pursue a higher SLO should be based on the cost of the failures it prevents, not on the abstract desire for reliability.

### Infrastructure Cost Breakdown

The cost of SLO infrastructure varies by service type and scale. The following table shows estimated monthly infrastructure costs for a typical web service at different SLO targets:

|------------|---------|----------|------------|------------|-------------|-------|
| 99% | 500 dollars | 500 dollars | 200 dollars | 100 dollars | 0 dollars | 1,300 dollars |

The engineering cost dominates at higher SLO targets because maintaining high reliability requires significant human effort. Automated systems can only eliminate so many failure modes. The remaining failure modes require human judgment and intervention.

## CAP Theorem for SLOs

The CAP theorem, traditionally applied to distributed databases, applies to SLO systems as well. An SLO system must choose between consistency, availability, and partition tolerance.

**Consistency:** Every evaluator sees the same data at the same time. If one evaluator sees a burn rate of 14.4 and another sees 0.5, the system is inconsistent. This leads to inconsistent alerting — one engineer gets paged while another does not.

**Availability:** The SLO system continues to evaluate SLOs and generate alerts even when some components fail. If the evaluator crashes, a backup evaluator takes over without gaps in coverage.

**Partition tolerance:** The SLO system continues to function correctly when network partitions separate components. If the instrumentation layer cannot reach the aggregation layer, the system should still produce correct results.

An SLO system must choose partition tolerance because network partitions are inevitable. The choice is between CP (consistent during partitions) and AP (available during partitions).

**CP SLO System:** During a partition, the SLO system stops evaluating until the partition heals. This ensures consistency — the SLO values are always correct. But it means no alerts fire during a partition. A partition that lasts 30 minutes means 30 minutes of blind operation.

**AP SLO System:** During a partition, the SLO system continues evaluating with the data it has. The evaluator may miss data from the partitioned side, but it continues to produce alerts. The alerts may be incomplete — they miss the partitioned data — but some signal is better than no signal.

The correct choice is AP for SLO systems. The cost of missing an alert during a partition is higher than the cost of a slightly inaccurate burn rate. The SLO system prioritizes availability over consistency. When the partition heals, the evaluator reconciles the data and corrects any inaccuracies.

## Failure Modes

### Failure Mode 1: The Dead Metric

A service stops emitting metrics. The instrumentation library has a bug, or the buffer is full, or the aggregation layer is unreachable. The SLO system sees zero requests. The compliance percentage stays at 100%. The error budget is untouched. Everything looks green.

The service could be completely down, returning errors on every request, but the SLO system shows 100% compliance because it sees no data. The dead metric is the most dangerous failure mode because it hides the failure.

**Mitigation:** The SLO system must monitor data freshness. If a service has not emitted a metric in 5 minutes, the system generates a "data missing" alert. The alert is separate from the SLO alert — it is a meta-alert about the SLO system itself.

### Failure Mode 2: The Double Counter

A misconfiguration causes metric events to be counted twice. Two instrumentation libraries run on the same service, or the buffer flush sends duplicate events, or the aggregation layer processes the same events twice. The total request count doubles. The bad request count doubles. The compliance percentage is unchanged.

The problem is that the error budget is consumed twice as fast. An outage that should consume 5% of the budget consumes 10%. The SLO violation fires earlier than expected. The team is paged for a problem that is half as bad as the alert suggests.

**Mitigation:** The global aggregation layer must deduplicate window entries. Each entry has an instance ID and a sequence number. The deduplication layer checks for duplicate (instance_id, sequence_number) pairs and discards duplicates.

### Failure Mode 3: The Clock Skew

Service instances have slightly different clocks. One instance is 30 seconds ahead of another. The window entries from the fast instance are assigned to the wrong minute. The SLO evaluation sees entries for minute T+1 before it sees entries for minute T.

Clock skew causes the sliding window to have gaps. The compliance calculation is based on an incomplete view of the data. The error budget may be overestimated or underestimated depending on the direction of the skew.

**Mitigation:** Use NTP across all instances with a maximum skew of 100ms. In the aggregation layer, assign each event to its minute based on the event timestamp, not the processing timestamp. The aggregator should handle out-of-order events by buffering them for up to 2 minutes before assigning them to a window.

### Failure Mode 4: The Thundering Herd

A service recovers from a 5-minute outage. The 5 minutes of bad data are in the window. The service returns to normal. The SLO compliance starts recovering. After 30 days, the bad data slides out of the window. The compliance jumps from 99.91% to 99.95% in a single minute.

The jump in compliance triggers a "service recovery" notification. But the service did not recover — the bad data simply aged out. The team celebrates a non-existent improvement.

**Mitigation:** The SLO dashboard should show the trend, not just the current value. A line chart showing compliance over the last 90 days reveals the aging-out effect. The team should compare the current compliance to the compliance before the incident to understand the true trajectory.

### Failure Mode 6: The Weekend Warrior

A service has a 99.9% SLO over a 30-day window. The service is rock-solid on weekdays (99.99% availability) but degrades every weekend due to a weekly batch job (98% availability on Saturdays). The 30-day rolling window smooths out the weekend degradation. The SLO looks fine. But the weekend users — the ones who use the service on Saturdays — experience a degraded service every single week.

The problem is that the SLO window is too long. A 30-day window hides weekly patterns. The team never has to fix the Saturday degradation because the SLO is always green. The weekend users suffer silently.

**Mitigation:** Use a 7-day SLO window for services with weekly patterns. The 7-day window captures the full weekly cycle. If the Saturday degradation causes the 7-day SLO to be violated, the team must fix the root cause. Alternatively, exclude planned maintenance windows from the SLO measurement — but only if the maintenance is communicated to users.

### Failure Mode 7: The SLA Gap

A team defines an SLO for an internal service. The SLO is 99.9%. The team uses the SLO to drive operational decisions. Meanwhile, the legal department signs an SLA with a customer that promises 99.99% availability for the customer-facing service that depends on the internal service. The internal SLO (99.9%) and the external SLA (99.99%) are mismatched by 0.09%.

The internal team cannot meet the external SLA because their infrastructure is designed for 99.9%. The customer-facing service violates the SLA even though all internal SLOs are green. The mismatch is invisible to the internal team because their dashboards show 99.9% compliance.

**Mitigation:** The SLA must be decomposed into SLOs for each dependent service. If the customer-facing SLA is 99.99% and there are three dependent services in the path, each service needs a higher SLO to account for the chain probability. The SLO target should be derived from the SLA, not from the current system performance.

### Failure Mode 8: The Optimistic Projection

A new service launches with an SLO of 99.9%. The team has no production data, so they estimate the expected error rate based on load tests and similar services. The estimate is wrong. The actual error rate is 0.5%, five times the SLO allowance. The SLO is violated in the first week.

The team is in a perpetual SLO violation from day one. The SLO is never green. The team stops looking at the SLO dashboard because it is always red. The SLO becomes noise.

**Mitigation:** New services should start with a provisional SLO of 99% for the first month. After one month of production data, the SLO is adjusted based on actual performance. The provisional SLO gives the team time to tune the service without the pressure of a permanent violation.

### Failure Mode 9: The Alert Storm

A team configures 50 SLOs for a single service. Each SLO has four alerting tiers. The total alerting surface is 200 alert rules. When a deployment causes a minor latency increase, 30 SLOs are affected. Each SLO generates a page-level alert. The on-call engineer receives 30 alerts within 2 minutes.

The engineer is overwhelmed. They cannot process 30 alerts simultaneously. They miss the critical signal — the availability SLO — because it is buried in 29 other alerts. The alert storm paralyzes the response.

**Mitigation:** Limit the number of SLOs per service to 5 maximum. Use alert aggregation to group related alerts. Prioritize alerts by severity so that the most critical alert is always the first one the engineer sees. A single service should have at most one page-level alert firing at any time.

The SLO was supposed to drive reliability improvements. Instead, it encourages complacency. The team ships features without testing, deploys without canaries, and ignores slow degradation. The budget is always full, so there is never a reason to stop shipping.

**Mitigation:** SLO targets should be set aggressively enough to force improvement. If the service consistently achieves 99.95% availability, the SLO should be 99.9% — achievable but not trivial. The error budget should be small enough that the team feels the pressure of budget consumption.

## Anti-Patterns

### Anti-Pattern 1: The Kitchen Sink

Define SLOs for every metric. Latency, error rate, throughput, CPU usage, memory usage, disk I/O, network bandwidth, database query time, cache hit rate, garbage collection time, thread pool utilization, connection pool depth, DNS resolution time, TLS handshake time, and response body size all get their own SLO.

The result is 50 SLOs per service. The team spends more time tuning SLO thresholds than improving the service. The dashboards are full of red and yellow indicators. Nobody knows which SLOs matter. The signal is buried in noise.

**The fix:** Define SLOs only for user-facing metrics. If the user does not experience it, it does not need an SLO. CPU usage is not an SLO. It is a diagnostic metric. Latency, error rate, and throughput are SLOs. Everything else is a supporting metric.

### Anti-Pattern 2: The Perfect Target

Set the SLO target at 99.999% because five nines sounds impressive. The service runs on infrastructure that is designed for 99.9% availability. The SLO is violated every week. The error budget is permanently negative. The team stops looking at the SLO dashboard because it is always red.

The SLO becomes noise. The team ignores the red indicator and makes decisions based on other signals. The SLO system has failed because the target was chosen for vanity, not for operational value.

**The fix:** Set the SLO target based on actual system capability plus a stretch goal. If the system achieves 99.93%, set the SLO at 99.9%. This is achievable with some effort. The error budget is meaningful. The team can track progress against a realistic target.

### Anti-Pattern 3: The Silent SLA

Define an SLA with 99.99% availability but no monitoring infrastructure to measure it. The SLA is a legal document that promises reliability the team cannot measure. When the customer asks for the SLA compliance report, the team has no data.

The SLA creates a legal liability without a corresponding operational capability. The team is exposed to financial penalties they cannot prevent because they cannot measure their compliance.

**The fix:** Never sign an SLA without the SLO infrastructure to measure it. The SLA should reference the SLO definitions and measurement methodology. The SLO system must be in place before the SLA is signed.

### Anti-Pattern 4: The Annual Reset

Use a calendar-year SLO window. The error budget resets on January 1. A December outage causes the team to miss the annual SLO target. The team accepts the failure and starts fresh in January. The SLO has no operational impact because the team knows a reset is coming.

Annual SLOs are too long to drive operational behavior. The team cannot connect today's deploy decision to the annual SLO target. The SLO becomes a compliance checkbox rather than an operational tool.

**The fix:** Use rolling windows for operational SLOs. The 30-day rolling window is always current. Every deploy decision, every pager rotation, every capacity investment is evaluated against the current 30-day window. The annual SLO is a compliance artifact, not an operational tool.

### Anti-Pattern 5: The SLO Stack

Chain SLOs across a service dependency graph. Service A has an SLO that depends on Service B's SLO, which depends on Service C's SLO. When Service C violates its SLO, Service A is also in violation. The team cannot determine which service caused the violation.

The SLO stack creates cascading failures in the SLO system itself. The compliance signal is meaningless because it cannot be attributed to a specific team or service.

**The fix:** Each service has its own SLO based on its own SLI. The SLO measures the service's own performance, not the performance of its dependencies. If Service A depends on Service B, Service A's SLO should still measure Service A's end-to-end behavior. The dependency is a diagnostic dimension, not an SLO dimension.

### Anti-Pattern 6: The Dashboard Hero

A team builds an elaborate SLO dashboard with 50 widgets, real-time animations, and drill-down capability. The dashboard is beautiful. The team spends more time perfecting the dashboard than improving the service. The dashboard has 15 different SLO gauges, but the team does not know which gauge to look at when an incident occurs.

The dashboard becomes a work of art. The SLO system becomes a decoration. The team enjoys looking at the dashboard but does not act on the data it shows.

**The fix:** The SLO dashboard should show exactly one number prominently: the error budget remaining. Everything else is secondary. The dashboard should be designed for quick decision-making, not for exploration. A good dashboard can be understood in 5 seconds. A bad dashboard requires 5 minutes of study.

### Anti-Pattern 7: The Vendor Lock-In

A team chooses a proprietary SLO platform that integrates deeply with their monitoring stack. The SLO definitions, the alerting rules, and the dashboards are all built on proprietary technology. The team cannot export their SLO data or migrate to another platform.

Two years later, the vendor raises prices by 300%. The team is locked in. They cannot migrate without rebuilding their entire SLO system from scratch. The cost of migration is higher than the price increase.

**The fix:** Use open standards and open-source tools for SLO management. SLO definitions should be stored in a version-controlled YAML file, not in a proprietary database. The SLO data should be exportable in a standard format (JSON, CSV). The alerting rules should be compatible with multiple alert managers. The team should be able to migrate between platforms with minimal effort.

### Anti-Pattern 8: The SLO as a Target

A team treats the SLO as a target to be achieved, not as a tool for decision-making. The team optimizes the SLO compliance percentage without improving the actual service. They adjust the SLI definition to exclude slow requests. They exclude maintenance windows. They exclude traffic from certain regions. The SLO compliance goes up. The service quality stays the same.

The team is gaming the SLO. The SLO system was designed to measure reliability, but the team is measuring something else. The SLO dashboard shows green, but users are still experiencing problems.

**The fix:** The SLO should be independently audited. A separate team reviews the SLO definitions and the SLI measurements to ensure they accurately reflect the user experience. The SLO should be based on the user's perspective, not the service's perspective. If the SLO is always green but users are complaining, the SLO is wrong.

## When NOT to Use SLOs

SLOs are not universal. There are situations where SLOs do more harm than good.

**Prototypes and MVPs:** A prototype that might be thrown away does not need an SLO. The cost of setting up the SLO infrastructure exceeds the benefit. Run without SLOs until the product is validated. The time spent building SLO infrastructure for a prototype is time not spent validating the product hypothesis. Premature SLO optimization is a form of premature optimization.

**Batch processing:** A batch job that runs once per day does not benefit from a 30-day rolling window SLO. The batch either succeeds or fails. Track success/failure, not SLO compliance. The SLO concept assumes continuous operation. Batch jobs are discrete operations with a clear success/failure signal. They do not benefit from the continuous measurement paradigm.

**Developer tools:** An internal CLI tool used by 10 engineers does not need an SLO. The engineers can tolerate occasional failures. The SLO would add overhead without meaningful benefit. The cost of maintaining the SLO exceeds the cost of the occasional failure. Developer tools should be treated as best-effort services.

**Services with zero tolerance:** A life-critical medical device system cannot tolerate any failure. An SLO of 99.999% implies 0.001% allowed failure, but the system needs 100% success. SLOs are meaningless when the target is 100%. The error budget is zero, which means there is no tolerance for deployment risk. The SLO cannot drive decisions because there is no room for trade-offs.

**Systems with no measurement capability:** A legacy system that cannot emit metrics without a major rewrite. The cost of instrumentation exceeds the benefit of SLO tracking. The system should be retired or rewritten before SLOs are added. Trying to retrofit SLOs onto a system with no observability is an exercise in frustration. Focus on basic monitoring first, SLOs second.

**Single-user systems:** A system used by a single person or a small team does not benefit from SLOs. The users can directly observe the system's behavior. They do not need an SLO to tell them whether the system is working. SLOs are most valuable for systems where the operator cannot directly observe the user experience.

**Services with no users yet:** A service that has not been released to users should not have an SLO. There is no user experience to measure. The SLO would be measuring something that does not exist yet. Focus on building the service first, defining SLOs after the first users arrive.

**Services with perfect observability:** A system where every failure is immediately visible to the operator without an SLO. If the operator can tell within seconds that the system is down, the SLO adds marginal benefit. SLOs are most valuable for detecting slow degradation, not catastrophic failure.

## SLO Governance and Organizational Impact

### Who Owns the SLO?

Every SLO must have an owner. The owner is responsible for defining the SLO, monitoring compliance, responding to violations, and adjusting the target over time. The owner should be the team that operates the service, not the team that built it.

The SLO owner is not the person who gets paged when the SLO is violated. The SLO owner is the person who is accountable for the SLO's long-term health. They make the strategic decisions about the SLO target, the error budget policy, and the investment in reliability.

### SLO Review Cadence

SLOs should be reviewed on a regular cadence. The recommended cadence is:

- **Weekly:** Review the current compliance, the budget consumption, and any recent violations. Identify trends and decide on immediate actions.
- **Monthly:** Review the SLO targets and the error budget policy. Decide whether targets need adjustment based on the previous month's performance.
- **Quarterly:** Review the SLO coverage. Are there services without SLOs that need them? Are there SLOs that are no longer useful? Adjust the SLO program based on organizational changes.
- **Annually:** Review the SLO program's effectiveness. Is the program driving the right behaviors? Is the cost justified by the benefit? Make strategic adjustments to the SLO program.

### SLO Blamelessness

The most important organizational principle for SLOs is blamelessness. When a team violates its SLO, the response should be investigation and learning, not blame and punishment. The SLO is a tool for understanding the system, not for evaluating the team.

Blamelessness requires psychological safety. The team must feel safe reporting SLO violations without fear of retribution. If the team hides violations or manipulates the SLO data to avoid blame, the SLO system is broken. The organization must actively foster a culture of blamelessness around SLOs.

### SLO and Team Incentives

SLOs should be aligned with team incentives, not in conflict with them. If the team is rewarded for shipping features, and the SLO policy blocks feature deployments when the budget is low, the team has a conflict of interest. They are incentivized to ship features, but the SLO prevents them from doing so.

The solution is to align incentives: reward the team for meeting SLO targets, not just for shipping features. The team's performance review should include SLO compliance as a factor. The team should be recognized for maintaining high SLO compliance, not just for delivering new functionality.

### SLO Maturity in Organizations

Organizations go through stages of SLO maturity:

**Stage 1: No SLOs.** The organization has no formal reliability targets. Reliability is managed by intuition and incident response. The first step is to introduce SLOs for the most critical services.

**Stage 2: SLOs exist but are not used.** The organization has defined SLOs, but they are not driving decisions. The SLOs are displayed on dashboards but are not referenced in deployment decisions or incident response. The next step is to integrate SLOs into the operational workflow.

**Stage 3: SLOs drive operations.** The organization uses SLOs for deployment gating, incident response, and capacity planning. The error budget is a real constraint on engineering decisions. The SLO dashboard is the first thing the on-call engineer checks.

**Stage 4: SLOs drive strategy.** The organization uses SLOs for strategic decisions: which services to invest in, which architecture changes to prioritize, and which reliability improvements to fund. The SLO system is integrated into the product development lifecycle.

**Stage 5: SLOs drive culture.** The organization has a reliability culture centered on SLOs. Every engineer understands the SLOs for their services. The error budget is a shared resource that the entire organization manages collectively. Reliability is everyone's responsibility.

## SLO Change Management

### When to Change an SLO Target

SLO targets should change when the underlying conditions change. The common triggers for SLO target changes are:

**User expectations change:** Users demand higher reliability. The competitive landscape shifts. The SLO must be raised to match user expectations.

**System capability changes:** The infrastructure is upgraded. The architecture is redesigned. The system can now achieve higher reliability. The SLO should be raised to reflect the new capability.

**Business requirements change:** The service becomes more critical to revenue. The SLA is renegotiated. The SLO must be adjusted to align with the new business requirements.

**Historical data shows the target is wrong:** The team consistently achieves 99.99% on a 99.9% SLO. The target is too loose. The team consistently fails to achieve 99.99% on a 99.99% SLO. The target is too tight.

### How to Change an SLO Target

The SLO target change should follow a structured process:

1. **Propose the change:** The SLO owner proposes the new target with a rationale. The proposal includes the current target, the proposed target, the reason for the change, and the expected impact.

2. **Review the change:** The SLO review board (typically the SRE team and the service owner) reviews the proposal. They assess the impact on the error budget, the alerting thresholds, and the deployment pipeline.

3. **Communicate the change:** The change is communicated to all stakeholders. The affected teams are notified through the team chat, the weekly SLO review, and the SLO dashboard.

4. **Implement the change:** The SLO definition is updated in the version-controlled YAML file. The new target takes effect immediately for the next measurement window.

5. **Monitor the impact:** The team monitors the SLO compliance for the first week after the change. If the target was raised, the compliance may drop initially. If the target was lowered, the compliance may increase. The team watches for unexpected behavior.

### SLO Versioning

SLO definitions should be version-controlled. Each change to the SLO definition is a commit in the version control system. The commit message includes the rationale for the change.

The version history enables the team to understand why an SLO target was set at a specific value. It also enables rollback if the change causes unexpected problems. The SLO version history is a valuable source of organizational knowledge about reliability decisions.

### SLO Auditing

Every SLO should be audited periodically to ensure it is still relevant and accurate. The audit checks:

**Is the SLI still user-facing?** Services change over time. An SLI that was user-facing at the start may no longer represent the user experience. The audit ensures the SLI matches the current service architecture.

**Is the SLO target still appropriate?** User expectations change. The infrastructure improves. The SLO target should be reviewed to ensure it still reflects the right balance between reliability and cost.

**Is the error budget policy still effective?** The error budget policy should be reviewed to ensure it is driving the right behaviors. If the policy is too strict, it blocks innovation. If it is too loose, it does not drive reliability improvements.

**Are the alerting thresholds still correct?** The multi-window multi-burn-rate thresholds should be reviewed to ensure they are not generating false positives or missing real problems. The threshold tuning is an ongoing process.

**Is the SLO still owned by the right team?** Team structures change. The SLO owner should be the team that currently operates the service, not the team that originally defined the SLO.

### SLO Deprecation

When an SLO is no longer useful, it should be deprecated. The deprecation process:

1. The SLO owner proposes deprecation with a rationale.
2. The proposal is reviewed by the SLO review board.
3. The SLO is marked as deprecated on the dashboard.
4. After 30 days, the SLO is removed from the active SLO list.
5. The SLO history is archived for future reference.

SLO deprecation prevents SLO bloat. Old SLOs that no longer drive decisions should be removed. The SLO set should be lean and focused on current priorities.

The trade-offs, failure modes, and anti-patterns of SLOs are well understood by the companies that have implemented them at scale. The governance and organizational practices described in this section are the foundation for a successful SLO program. The key takeaway is that SLOs are a tool for decision-making, not a metric for evaluation. When they drive better decisions, they are valuable. When they are used for blame or compliance, they are harmful.

The right SLO for a given service depends on the user's needs, the system's capability, the cost of reliability, and the organization's maturity. There is no universal SLO target.
