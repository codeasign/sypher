## How to Use These Challenges

Each challenge is designed to test a specific SLO skill. The challenges are organized by tier:

**Tier 1 (Foundation):** These challenges test your understanding of SLO fundamentals. You should be able to complete each challenge in 5-10 minutes. The expected output is a specific calculation, a definition, or a configuration.

**Tier 2 (Design):** These challenges test your ability to design SLO systems. You should be able to complete each challenge in 15-20 minutes. The expected output is a system design with specific components, data flows, and trade-offs.

**Tier 3 (Expert):** These challenges test your ability to handle edge cases and novel scenarios. You should spend 20-30 minutes on each challenge. The expected output is a discussion that covers multiple approaches, trade-offs, and failure modes.

For each challenge, try to complete it without looking at the model answer. Compare your answer to the model answer and identify areas where your answer was stronger or weaker.

### Challenge 1: Calculate the Error Budget

**Hint:** Start by calculating the total requests over 30 days. Then calculate the allowed failures (0.1% of total). Calculate the affected requests by multiplying the outage duration in seconds by the requests per second. Compare the affected requests to the allowed failures.

An authentication service processes 5 million requests per day. The SLO is 99.9% availability over a 30-day rolling window. A database migration causes a 12-minute complete outage at 2:00 PM, when the service is processing 3,000 requests per second.

**Calculate:**
- The total error budget in requests over 30 days
- The number of requests affected by the outage
- The percentage of error budget consumed by the outage
- Whether the service is in SLO violation after the outage

**Model Answer:**

```
Total requests in 30 days: 5,000,000 * 30 = 150,000,000
SLO target: 99.9% = 0.999
Error budget (requests): 150,000,000 * (1 - 0.999) = 150,000 allowed failures

Outage duration: 12 minutes = 720 seconds
Requests per second during outage: 3,000
Affected requests: 720 * 3,000 = 2,160,000

Percentage of budget consumed: 2,160,000 / 150,000 = 14.4

The outage consumed 1,440% of the error budget. The service is in
clear SLO violation. The error budget consumed by this single outage
is 14.4 times the entire budget for 30 days.

The service will remain in violation for the remaining 29 days and
23 hours of the measurement window. Recovery requires either:
(1) waiting for the outage data to age out of the 30-day window, or
(2) manually correcting the SLO data if the outage is classified as
a planned maintenance event.
```

### Challenge 2: Design the SLI

**Hint:** Think about the user's perspective. The SLI should measure what the user experiences, not what the system does internally. Consider edge cases like cities with no drivers, rider cancellations, and network failures. The SLI definition should be precise enough that two different engineers would measure it the same way.

A ride-sharing platform needs an SLO for the rider matching experience. The platform operates in 500 cities across 30 countries. Traffic varies by city, time of day, and weather conditions. The user experience goal is: a rider requests a ride, and a driver is assigned within 10 seconds.

**Define the SLI specifications:**
- What exactly do you measure?
- How do you handle cities with no available drivers?
- How do you handle the rider canceling before a driver is assigned?
- What is the measurement resolution?

**Model Answer:**

The SLI measures: "the fraction of ride requests where a driver is assigned within 10 seconds of the rider pressing Request Ride."

Handling cities with no available drivers: These requests are included in the SLI denominator. The system cannot assign a driver if none is available. The SLI reflects the actual user experience — the user sees no driver assignment regardless of the reason. The SLO target should account for the fact that some cities have limited driver supply.

Handling rider cancellations: If the rider cancels before a driver is assigned, the request is excluded from the SLI. The cancellation breaks the user's intent to complete a ride, so the request is not counted as a failure. However, if the rider cancels because the assignment took too long, the request should be counted as a failure. The distinction is made by comparing the cancellation time to the 10-second threshold. If the rider cancels after 10 seconds, the request is a failure. If the rider cancels before 10 seconds, the request is excluded.

Measurement resolution: 1-minute windows. Each window records the total number of ride requests, the number of successful assignments within 10 seconds, and the number of failures. The SLO compliance is computed over a 30-day rolling window.

### Challenge 3: Configure Burn Rate Alerts

**Hint:** The burn rate formula normalizes the error rate by the SLO target. For a 99.99% SLO, the error budget is 0.01%. A 0.144% error rate produces a burn rate of 14.4. Compare this to a 99.9% SLO where the same 0.144% error rate produces a burn rate of 1.44 — which would not trigger a page alert.

A payment service has a 99.99% SLO over a 30-day window. The service processes 10,000 requests per second. The team needs alerting rules that catch problems quickly without false positives.

**Design the multi-window multi-burn-rate alerting configuration:**
- Specify the burn rate and window for each tier
- Calculate the time to exhaust the error budget at each burn rate
- Justify your choices for a 99.99% SLO versus a 99.9% SLO

**Model Answer:**

```
For a 99.99% SLO (0.01% error budget):

Tier 1 (Page): Burn rate 14.4 over 5 minutes
  - Budget exhaustion at 14.4x rate: 30 days / 14.4 = 2.08 days
  - 5-minute window prevents false positives from brief blips
  - At 10,000 req/sec, 5 minutes = 3,000,000 requests
  - 14.4 burn rate means 14.4 * 0.01% = 0.144% error rate
  - 0.144% of 3,000,000 = 4,320 bad requests in 5 minutes

Tier 2 (Ticket): Burn rate 6.0 over 30 minutes
  - Budget exhaustion at 6.0x rate: 30 days / 6.0 = 5 days
  - 30-minute window catches sustained degradation
  - 6.0 burn rate means 6.0 * 0.01% = 0.06% error rate
  - 0.06% of 18,000,000 = 10,800 bad requests in 30 minutes

Tier 3 (Notify): Burn rate 1.0 over 6 hours
  - Budget exhaustion at 1.0x rate: 30 days
  - 6-hour window ensures the degradation is sustained
  - 1.0 burn rate means 0.01% error rate (nominal budget)

Tier 4 (Trend): Burn rate 0.5 over 3 days
  - Budget exhaustion at 0.5x rate: 60 days (never exhausts in 30-day window)
  - 3-day window detects slow shifts

Key difference from 99.9% SLO: At 99.99%, the error budget is 10x smaller.
A 14.4 burn rate at 99.99% means 0.144% error rate, which would be a
1.44 burn rate at 99.9% SLO. The same error rate triggers different
alert severities at different SLO levels.
```

## Tier 2: Design

### Challenge 4: Design the SLO Pipeline for a Global E-Commerce Platform

An e-commerce platform serves 100 million users across 10 regions. The platform has 50 microservices, each with its own SLO. The platform processes 500,000 requests per second at peak. The team needs a complete SLO pipeline from instrumentation to alerting.

**Design the system covering:**
- Instrumentation approach (sidecar vs. agent vs. direct emit)
- Local aggregation strategy
- Global aggregation strategy
- Storage and retention
- Evaluation and alerting
- Multi-region considerations
- Failure mode mitigations

**Model Answer Discussion:**

The instrumentation layer uses the sidecar pattern. Each service instance runs a sidecar agent on the same host. The sidecar receives metric events via a Unix socket, batches them every 10 seconds or 10,000 events, and flushes to the local aggregation tier via gRPC streaming. The sidecar buffers up to 50,000 events in memory. If the buffer is full, the oldest events are dropped.

The local aggregation tier runs in each region. Three aggregator instances receive events from all sidecars in the region. The aggregators group events into 1-minute windows and merge them by summing counters. Each minute, the aggregator writes a window entry to the regional time-series database and publishes the entry to a cross-region Kafka topic.

The global aggregation tier runs in a single region (us-east-1). It consumes the Kafka topic from all 10 regions and merges window entries by summing. The global aggregator writes the merged entries to the global time-series database.

The storage tier uses a time-series database with 30-day retention for 1-minute window entries. Entries older than 30 days are moved to an object store for compliance reporting. Hourly snapshots of the sliding windows are written to the object store for crash recovery.

The evaluation tier runs three evaluator instances in active-active mode. Each evaluator is responsible for a shard of SLOs, sharded by SLO name hash. The evaluator reads new window entries from the database, updates the sliding window, computes burn rates, and generates alerts if thresholds are exceeded.

For multi-region, each region has its own SLO evaluation. Regional alerts are the primary alerting path. Global evaluation is used for SLA compliance reporting. The global SLO is computed with a 1-5 second delay due to cross-region replication.

Failure mode mitigations: Data freshness monitoring alerts if no data is received for 5 minutes. Deduplication by (instance_id, sequence_number) prevents double counting. NTP keeps clock skew under 100ms. Hourly snapshots ensure crash recovery in under 30 seconds.

### Challenge 5: Design the Error Budget Policy

A social media platform has 10 services, each with its own SLO. The platform ships 50 deployments per week across all services. The team needs an error budget policy that governs the deployment pipeline.

**Design the policy covering:**
- How the error budget gates deployments
- What happens when the budget is partially consumed versus fully consumed
- How the policy handles services with different SLO targets
- How the policy handles multi-service deployments
- The escalation path for manual overrides

**Model Answer Discussion:**

The error budget policy is defined at the service level. Each service has a budget consumption percentage. The policy defines three zones:

- **Green zone** (budget consumption under 50%): Deployments proceed normally. Any feature, bug fix, or reliability improvement can be deployed. The deployment pipeline performs standard checks (tests, canary, rollback).

- **Yellow zone** (budget consumption 50-80%): Only reliability improvements, performance optimizations, and bug fixes can be deployed. New features are blocked. The deployment pipeline adds a manual approval step. The team must review the deployment plan and confirm it will not degrade reliability further.

- **Red zone** (budget consumption over 80%): No deployments are allowed. The team enters incident response mode. The SLO violation is treated as a P0 incident. The team investigates the root cause, implements a fix, and validates the fix. Deployments resume only when the budget consumption drops below 50%.

For services with different SLO targets, the budget consumption is normalized. A 99.99% SLO with 60% budget consumption is the same yellow zone as a 99.9% SLO with 60% consumption. The zones are defined by budget consumption percentage, not by absolute error rate.

For multi-service deployments, the deployment is blocked if any service in the deployment is in the yellow or red zone. The deployment must wait until all services are in the green zone, or the deployment is split into per-service deployments that proceed independently.

Manual overrides require a two-person approval from the engineering manager and the SRE lead. The override is logged and reviewed in the weekly SLO review meeting. Overrides that lead to SLO violations trigger a separate incident review.

### Challenge 6: Design the SLO Dashboard

A team of 15 engineers needs a single SLO dashboard that shows the health of all 5 services they own. The dashboard must support both daily operations and weekly SLO reviews.

**Design the dashboard layout:**
- What widgets are shown?
- What is the layout hierarchy?
- What time ranges are displayed?
- What interactive features are available?
- How does the dashboard support drill-down from aggregate to per-service to per-endpoint?

**Model Answer Discussion:**

The dashboard has three sections:

**Top section: Executive Summary.** Shows the error budget consumption for all 5 services in a single bar chart. Each bar shows the service name and the budget consumption percentage. The bar is colored green (under 50%), yellow (50-80%), or red (over 80%). A summary line shows the number of services in each zone.

**Middle section: Per-Service Detail.** Five panels, one per service. Each panel shows:
- Error budget remaining (large number, colored by zone)
- Current burn rate (small number, 1-minute resolution)
- Burn rate sparkline (last 6 hours, showing the multi-window burn rates)
- Compliance trend (line chart showing compliance over the last 90 days)
- Recent alerts (last 5 alerts with timestamp, burn rate, and status)

**Bottom section: Incident Log.** A table showing the last 20 SLO-relevant incidents. Each row shows the incident timestamp, the affected service, the budget consumption, the root cause, and the resolution.

The default time range is 30 days. A dropdown allows switching to 7 days, 90 days, or a custom range. The dashboard auto-refreshes every 60 seconds.

Interactive features: Clicking a service bar opens the per-service detail. Clicking a compliance trend line opens a per-endpoint breakdown. Clicking an incident row opens the incident post-mortem. The dashboard supports filtering by alert severity and time range.

## Tier 3: Expert

### Challenge 7: Design an SLO System for a Serverless Architecture

A platform runs entirely on AWS Lambda with 200 functions. Each function is independent, but they compose into user-facing flows. The platform has no persistent servers, no fixed service instances, and no stable IP addresses. Traffic patterns are highly variable — some functions run once per day, others run 10,000 times per second.

**Design the SLO system covering:**
- How to instrument Lambda functions without sidecars
- How to handle the cold start problem in SLO measurement
- How to aggregate metrics across functions that compose into user flows
- How to handle the SLO for functions that run infrequently
- How to alert on SLO violations when the infrastructure is ephemeral

**Discussion:**

The Lambda functions are instrumented using the AWS Lambda Extensions API. Each function runs a custom extension that intercepts the function invocation, records the start and end time, and emits a metric event to CloudWatch Logs. The extension runs as a separate process in the Lambda execution environment and does not affect the function's cold start time.

Cold starts are a critical SLI component. The SLO separates cold start latency and warm start latency. The cold start SLO target is 99% of cold starts completing in under 3 seconds. The warm start SLO target is 99.9% of warm starts completing in under 500 milliseconds. The overall SLO is a weighted combination based on the cold start rate.

For composite user flows, a distributed tracing system (AWS X-Ray) correlates individual function invocations into a single user request. The trace ID is propagated through the flow. The SLO system reads the trace data and computes end-to-end latency for each user flow. The per-function SLO is a secondary metric; the flow-level SLO is the primary metric.

Functions that run infrequently (less than once per hour) use a time-based SLO instead of a request-based SLO. Each minute that the function is idle is counted as a "good" minute. Each invocation is evaluated against the latency and error rate thresholds. The SLO compliance is the fraction of minutes where the function either met the threshold or had no invocations.

The alerting system uses CloudWatch Metrics and CloudWatch Alarms. The SLO evaluation runs in a scheduled Lambda function that fires every minute. The evaluator reads the last 30 days of metrics from CloudWatch, computes the SLO compliance and burn rates, and updates CloudWatch Alarms. The alarms trigger SNS notifications for paging.

### Challenge 8: Design an SLO System for a Real-Time System

A real-time multiplayer game platform serves 10 million concurrent players. The platform requires p99 latency under 50ms for game state updates. The system uses UDP, not HTTP, for game traffic. The game state is updated 60 times per second per player.

**Design the SLO system covering:**
- How to measure latency for UDP traffic
- How to handle the 60 updates-per-second granularity
- How to define the SLI for game state correctness
- How to handle the trade-off between freshness and consistency
- How to alert on SLO violations that affect player experience

**Discussion:**

UDP latency is measured using sequence numbers. Each game state update includes a sequence number. The client sends the highest received sequence number back to the server in the next update. The server computes the round-trip latency by comparing the received sequence number to the current server sequence number. The one-way latency is estimated as half the RTT.

The 60 updates-per-second granularity means 600 million metric events per second at 10 million concurrent players. Sampling is essential. The system samples 0.1% of game sessions — 10,000 sessions — and measures their full event stream. The sampling provides statistically significant latency measurements without overwhelming the instrumentation pipeline.

The SLI for game state correctness is "the fraction of game state updates that arrive within 50ms and contain the correct state." Correctness is verified using a checksum of the game state. The client computes a checksum of each received state update and sends it back to the server. The server compares the checksum to the expected value. A mismatch indicates state corruption.

The trade-off between freshness and consistency is captured by two SLOs: a latency SLO (p99 under 50ms) and a consistency SLO (99.99% of updates have matching checksums). The two SLOs are independent — a system can have low latency and incorrect state, or correct state and high latency. The combined SLO is the product of the two: 99.9% * 99.99% = 99.89% combined compliance.

Alerting on player experience uses a combination of burn rate and player impact. A burn rate of 14.4 over 1 minute pages the on-call engineer only if at least 1,000 players are affected. This prevents paging for isolated issues that affect a single player's network connection. The player impact threshold ensures that alerts correspond to real platform-wide problems.

### Challenge 9: Design an SLO System for a Data Pipeline

A data processing platform ingests 10 TB of data per day from 5,000 sources. The data flows through a pipeline: ingestion, validation, transformation, enrichment, and storage. Each stage has different reliability characteristics. The data must be available for query within 1 hour of ingestion.

**Design the SLO system covering:**
- How to define SLIs for each stage of the pipeline
- How to aggregate per-stage SLOs into an end-to-end SLO
- How to handle data that arrives late or out of order
- How to handle partial failures (some records fail validation but most pass)
- How to alert on pipeline degradation before it affects the end-to-end SLO

**Discussion:**

The data pipeline SLO is fundamentally different from a request-response SLO because data processing is asynchronous and batched. The SLI for each stage is measured in terms of throughput and latency, not individual request success rate.

**Ingestion SLI:** "The fraction of 1-minute windows where the ingestion rate is at least 95% of the expected rate." The expected rate is calculated from historical data and adjusted for day-of-week and seasonal patterns. A drop below 95% indicates a problem.

**Validation SLI:** "The fraction of records that pass validation checks." The target is 99.9% — 0.1% of records may fail validation due to malformed input. Records that fail validation are quarantined for manual review.

**Transformation SLI:** "The fraction of 1-minute windows where the transformation latency is under 5 seconds." This measures the processing speed of the transformation stage. A sustained increase in transformation latency indicates a bottleneck.

**Enrichment SLI:** "The fraction of records that are successfully enriched with external data." The target is 99.5% — some external data sources may be unavailable temporarily. Records that are not enriched are flagged but continue through the pipeline.

**Storage SLI:** "The fraction of records that are available for query within 1 hour of ingestion." This is the ultimate end-to-end SLI. It measures the complete pipeline from ingestion to query availability.

The end-to-end SLO is the product of all per-stage SLOs. If each stage has a 99.9% SLO, the end-to-end SLO is approximately 99.5% (0.999^5). To achieve a 99.9% end-to-end SLO, each stage must have a 99.98% SLO.

Late-arriving data is handled by a "data freshness" metric. The freshness metric tracks the age of the most recent data available for query. If the freshness exceeds 1 hour, the end-to-end SLO is violated. The freshness metric provides early warning of pipeline problems.

Partial failures are handled by tracking the "good record rate" for each stage. If the validation stage processes 100,000 records and 100 fail, the good record rate is 99.9%. The SLO target for validation is applied to the good record rate, not to individual records.

Alerting uses a two-tier approach. Per-stage burn rate alerts detect problems within each stage. End-to-end pipeline lag alerts detect problems that affect the overall pipeline. The pipeline lag alert is the most critical — if data is not available for query within 1 hour, the on-call engineer is paged immediately.

### Challenge 10: Design an SLO System for a Global DNS Service

A global DNS service processes 10 trillion queries per month from users in 200 countries. The service has 500 nameservers distributed across 50 locations worldwide. The DNS protocol is connectionless and uses UDP. A single query can be answered from any nameserver.

**Design the SLO system covering:**
- How to define the SLI for DNS query resolution
- How to measure latency for UDP queries
- How to handle the distributed nature of the service (queries may be answered by any nameserver)
- How to handle regional differences in network quality
- How to set SLO targets for individual nameservers versus the global service

**Discussion:**

The DNS service SLI measures "the fraction of DNS queries that receive a valid response within 50ms from the closest nameserver." The response time includes network latency from the user to the nameserver plus the nameserver's processing time. The SLI is measured from the nameserver's perspective because the DNS protocol does not provide client-side timing information.

UDP latency is measured using the nameserver's internal timing. When a DNS query arrives, the nameserver records the arrival time. When the response is sent, the nameserver records the departure time. The difference is the processing time. The network latency is estimated based on the nameserver's location and historical RTT data to users in different regions.

The distributed nature of the service makes it impossible to track individual query success globally. Instead, the SLO is measured per-nameserver. Each nameserver tracks its own query volume, latency, and error rate. The global SLO is an aggregate of all nameserver SLOs, weighted by query volume.

Regional differences are handled by setting different SLO targets for different regions. A nameserver in North America, where network infrastructure is excellent, has a 99.99% SLO target. A nameserver in a region with less reliable infrastructure has a 99.9% SLO target. The regional SLO targets reflect the achievable reliability given the local network conditions.

The per-nameserver SLO is more aggressive than the global SLO. Each nameserver targets 99.99% availability. The global SLO is 99.999% because queries are routed to the healthiest nameserver. The global SLO benefits from the redundancy of 500 nameservers — a failure in one nameserver affects only 0.2% of queries.

Alerting uses per-nameserver burn rate alerts for fast detection and global burn rate alerts for SLA compliance. A nameserver that exceeds a 14.4 burn rate over 5 minutes is removed from the query routing pool. The global burn rate alert pages the on-call team only if the aggregate error rate exceeds the 99.999% target.

The most important lesson from the DNS service is that SLOs for globally distributed, connectionless services must account for network variability and geographic redundancy. The per-component SLO is the operational signal. The aggregate SLO is the strategic signal. Both are necessary, but they serve different purposes.

## Common Mistakes in Challenge Solutions

When solving these challenges, watch out for these common mistakes:

**Mistake 1: Confusing requests and minutes.** In Challenge 1, remember that the error budget can be expressed as either a number of requests or a number of minutes. Make sure you use the same unit consistently throughout your calculation.

**Mistake 2: Forgetting the measurement window.** In Challenge 2, the measurement window (30 days rolling) affects how the SLI is evaluated. A 1-hour outage on day 1 has a different impact than the same outage on day 29.

**Mistake 3: Ignoring bursty traffic.** In Challenge 3, a short burst of errors may not trigger a 30-minute window alert but will trigger a 5-minute window alert. Both windows are needed to catch different types of problems.

**Mistake 4: Over-engineering the solution.** In Challenge 4, start with a simple design and add complexity only where needed. A direct-to-Kafka pipeline may be sufficient for many services without the overhead of sidecar agents.

**Mistake 5: Forgetting the feedback loop.** In Challenge 5, the error budget policy is only useful if it is enforced. Design the policy with specific enforcement mechanisms, not just aspirational guidelines.

**Mistake 6: Designing for the average case.** In Challenge 8, the 60 updates per second per player creates a worst-case instrumentation load that is much higher than the average. Design for the peak, not the average.

**Mistake 7: Missing the cost dimension.** In Challenge 10, the DNS service has 500 nameservers. Instrumenting all 500 nameservers with full SLO tracking is expensive. Consider sampling or partial instrumentation to control costs.

## Beyond the Challenges

If you have completed all 10 challenges, you have demonstrated a strong understanding of SLO concepts, design, and application. The next step is to apply these concepts to real-world systems.

### Apply SLOs to Your Own Service

Take a service you work with and define its SLOs. Start with the three core SLOs: availability, latency, and error rate. Define the SLI precisely. Set the SLO target. Calculate the error budget. Configure the alerting. Implement the error budget policy.

### Review SLO Post-Mortems

Review post-mortems from SLO violations in your organization or in public SRE literature. For each post-mortem, identify: which SLO was violated, how much budget was consumed, how the violation was detected, how it was mitigated, and how the SLO system could have detected it faster.

### Compare SLO Practices Across Teams

If you work in a larger organization, compare SLO practices across teams. How do different teams set SLO targets? What SLIs do they use? How strict is their error budget policy? The comparison reveals organizational patterns and opportunities for improvement.

### The SLO Mindset

The ultimate goal of learning about SLOs is not to memorize definitions or configurations. The goal is to develop the SLO mindset: the habit of thinking about reliability in terms of measurable objectives, error budgets, and trade-offs. When you see a system, you should immediately ask: what is the SLO? What is the SLI? What is the error budget? How is the budget being consumed? This mindset separates engineers who manage reliability from engineers who react to incidents.
