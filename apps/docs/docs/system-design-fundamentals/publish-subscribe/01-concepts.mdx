## The Problem That Pub-Sub Solves

Imagine you run a news agency with 50 reporters. Each reporter writes 3 articles per day. You have 10,000 subscribers — news websites, mobile apps, radio stations, email newsletters, and AI news aggregators.

The naive approach: every time a reporter publishes an article, your system sends a copy to every subscriber. Each subscriber gets 150 articles per day. Your server opens 1,500,000 connections per day (10,000 subscribers x 150 articles). When a subscriber goes offline, their connection times out and your server retries 5 times before giving up. When a new subscriber signs up, they backfill 90 days of archives — 13,500 articles — all at once, crashing the database.

The news agency grows. Now you have 500 reporters and 1,000,000 subscribers. The naive approach sends 1,500,000,000 messages per day. Your server budget grows from 5 machines to 500. Latency spikes from 50ms to 12 seconds during peak hours. Subscribers start receiving duplicate articles because retry logic and backpressure collide. The engineering team spends 80% of their time on delivery infrastructure and 20% on actual news features.

This is the tight coupling problem. Producers and consumers are directly linked. Each side must know about the other's existence, capacity, availability, and protocol. Scaling either side breaks the other. Any failure cascades.

## The Insight: Introduction of the Broker

Pub-sub decouples publishers from subscribers through an intermediary called a broker (sometimes called a message broker or event bus). Publishers send messages to a named topic. Subscribers express interest in topics. The broker handles matching, delivery, retries, buffering, and scaling.

Think of a magazine subscription system:

- The publisher (magazine company) does not know each subscriber by name
- The publisher sends issues to a distribution hub
- Subscribers tell the hub which magazines they want
- The hub handles the logistics of printing labels, bundling, and mailing

In software terms, the broker is the distribution hub. It accepts messages from publishers, stores them temporarily (or durably), and forwards them to every subscriber that has registered interest in that topic.

## Core Vocabulary

A publish-subscribe system uses specific terminology. Each term maps to a distinct component or behavior.

### Publisher

A publisher is any component that produces messages and sends them to a topic on the broker. A publisher does not know which subscribers will receive the message. It does not know how many subscribers exist. It does not wait for subscriber acknowledgment before sending the next message.

Publishers are often microservices, backend services, IoT devices, or user-facing applications that generate events. In a typical e-commerce system, the `order-service` publishes an `OrderPlaced` event. It does not care whether the email-service, analytics-service, or inventory-service receives it.

```json
// Message published by order-service
{
  "eventType": "OrderPlaced",
  "timestamp": "2026-07-02T14:30:00Z",
  "data": {
    "orderId": "ORD-4421",
    "customerId": "CUST-8891",
    "totalAmount": 149.99,
    "currency": "USD",
    "items": [
      {"sku": "WIDGET-A", "quantity": 2, "price": 49.99},
      {"sku": "GADGET-B", "quantity": 1, "price": 50.01}
    ]
  }
}
```

### Subscriber

A subscriber is any component that registers interest in one or more topics and receives messages published to those topics. Subscribers can be services, databases, caches, analytics pipelines, or external webhook endpoints.

A subscriber declares its interest to the broker at startup (or via configuration) and receives a stream of messages. The subscriber processes messages as they arrive, often in real time or near-real time.

### Topic

A topic is a named channel to which publishers send messages and subscribers subscribe. Topics are the fundamental addressing mechanism in pub-sub. The broker uses the topic name to route messages to the correct subscribers.

Topic naming conventions vary by system. Common patterns include:

- Hierarchical: `orders.created`, `orders.shipped`, `orders.cancelled`
- Flat: `OrderPlaced`, `PaymentReceived`, `InventoryUpdated`
- Namespaced: `com.company.service.event`

### Event

An event is a discrete message that represents something that happened. Events are immutable — they describe a fact that has already occurred. The publisher cannot unsend an event. Subscribers interpret events independently.

An event typically contains:
- A type or label identifying what happened
- A timestamp of when it happened
- A payload with relevant data
- Optional metadata (correlation ID, source, version, schema)

### Broker

The broker is the central intermediary. It receives messages from publishers, stores them (in memory or on disk), matches them against subscriber subscriptions, and delivers them. The broker handles:

- Authentication and authorization
- Message validation and schema enforcement
- Subscription management
- Message routing and fan-out
- Delivery guarantees (at-most-once, at-least-once, exactly-once)
- Retry and dead-letter handling
- Load balancing across subscriber instances

Popular brokers include Apache Kafka, RabbitMQ, Google Pub/Sub, Amazon SNS/SQS, NATS, Redis Pub/Sub, and MQTT brokers like Mosquitto.

### Fan-Out

Fan-out is the ability of a single published message to be delivered to multiple subscribers. This is the defining characteristic of pub-sub. A message published to a topic is broadcast to every subscriber of that topic.

Fan-out is distinct from load balancing (where a message is delivered to one consumer of many). In pub-sub, each subscriber gets its own copy of the message.

### Subscription

A subscription is a registration of interest in a topic. Subscriptions can have configuration:

- **Filter**: only receive messages matching certain criteria
- **Push endpoint**: where to send messages (HTTP URL, queue, gRPC stream)
- **Acknowledgement deadline**: how long the subscriber has to process before the message is redelivered
- **Dead letter topic**: where to send messages that cannot be delivered after max retries

### Push vs Pull Delivery

In **push** delivery, the broker sends messages to subscribers as they arrive. The subscriber exposes an endpoint (HTTP, gRPC, or WebSocket) that the broker calls. Push is low-latency but requires the subscriber to be reachable and handle backpressure.

In **pull** delivery, subscribers request messages from the broker when they are ready to process them. The subscriber polls the broker or long-polls. Pull gives subscribers full control over processing rate and is more resilient to subscriber slowdowns.

```python
# Example: Pull-based subscriber (pseudocode)
def subscriber_loop(broker_client, topic):
    while True:
        messages = broker_client.pull(topic, max_messages=10)
        for msg in messages:
            process(msg)
            broker_client.ack(msg.id)
```

### Message Filtering

Message filtering allows subscribers to receive only a subset of messages on a topic. Two common approaches:

**Topic-based filtering**: the broker uses topic hierarchy or wildcards. A subscriber subscribes to `orders.*` to get all order events, or `orders.created` to get only creation events.

**Content-based filtering**: the broker evaluates message payload against subscriber-defined predicates. For example, subscribe to all `OrderPlaced` events where `amount > 100`.

### Wildcard Subscription

Wildcard subscriptions use pattern matching on topic names. Common wildcards:

- Single-level wildcard: `orders.*` matches `orders.created`, `orders.shipped`, but not `orders.europe.created`
- Multi-level wildcard: `orders.**` matches all descendants of `orders`
- Examples in MQTT: `sensors/+/temperature` matches `sensors/room1/temperature`

### Durable Subscription

A durable subscription persists subscription state across broker restarts and subscriber disconnections. If a subscriber disconnects, the broker holds messages for it (up to a retention limit). When the subscriber reconnects, it receives all messages it missed.

Non-durable subscriptions lose undelivered messages when the subscriber disconnects or the broker restarts.

### Shared Subscription

A shared subscription allows multiple subscriber instances to share the workload of processing messages from a single subscription. Messages are distributed among the instances — each message goes to exactly one instance. This provides horizontal scalability and load balancing within a subscriber group.

Shared subscriptions are common in MQTT (shared topic subscriptions) and Kafka consumer groups.

## Step-by-Step Walkthrough

How a message travels from publisher to subscriber.

### Step 1: Publisher Creates and Sends a Message

A publisher generates an event and publishes it to a topic. The publisher opens a connection to the broker and sends the message along with the topic name.

```python
# Pseudocode: publisher publishes a message
publisher = broker.create_publisher()
message = {
    "event": "OrderPlaced",
    "order_id": "ORD-4421",
    "amount": 149.99,
    "timestamp": "2026-07-02T14:30:00Z"
}
publisher.publish(topic="orders.created", message=message)
```

### Step 2: Broker Receives and Validates the Message

The broker receives the message over the network. It validates:

- Is the publisher authenticated and authorized to publish to this topic?
- Does the message match the expected schema?
- Is the message size within limits?

If validation fails, the broker rejects the message and returns an error to the publisher. If validation passes, the broker accepts the message.

### Step 3: Broker Stores the Message

The broker writes the message to its storage layer. In a durable system, this means writing to disk, often to a log-structured file or a database. This ensures the message survives crashes.

```text
Broker Storage (Log Segment):
[Offset 1] | Topic: orders.created | Timestamp: T1 | Payload: {order_id: "ORD-4421", ...}
[Offset 2] | Topic: orders.created | Timestamp: T2 | Payload: {order_id: "ORD-4422", ...}
[Offset 3] | Topic: orders.shipped | Timestamp: T3 | Payload: {order_id: "ORD-4420", ...}
```

### Step 4: Broker Assigns an Offset

Each message receives a unique, monotonically increasing offset (or sequence number). This offset is the message's position in the log. Subscribers track their offset to know which messages they have processed and where to resume after a disconnect.

### Step 5: Broker Acknowledges to Publisher

The broker sends an acknowledgment to the publisher, confirming the message was accepted. Depending on the durability setting, this acknowledgment may wait for:

- Write to memory only (fast, not durable)
- Write to disk on the leader (medium durability)
- Write to disk on leader and N replicas (maximum durability)

```python
# Acks returned to publisher
ack = broker.publish(topic="orders.created", message=msg)
if ack.status == "ok":
    print(f"Message accepted at offset {ack.offset}")
```

### Step 6: Broker Looks Up Subscribers

The broker queries its subscription index for all subscribers interested in the topic. This lookup must be fast — sub-millisecond — because it happens for every message. The subscription index is usually an in-memory hash map or tree structure.

```text
Subscription Index (in-memory):
┌──────────────────────────────┐
│ orders.created   -> S1, S2  │
│ orders.shipped   -> S2, S3  │
│ payments.*       -> S4      │
│ *.critical       -> S5      │
└──────────────────────────────┘
```

### Step 7: Broker Delivers to Subscribers (Fan-Out)

The broker delivers the message to each subscriber. Depending on the delivery model:

- **Push**: broker sends the message to each subscriber's endpoint
- **Pull**: the message becomes available for subscribers to pull

For push delivery, the broker may use a worker pool to parallelize delivery. Each subscriber gets its own delivery slot.

### Step 8: Subscriber Processes Message

Each subscriber receives the message and processes it. Processing could mean:

- Updating a database
- Sending an email
- Triggering a workflow
- Aggregating into an analytics pipeline
- Forwarding to another system

```python
# Pseudocode: subscriber receives and processes
def handle_order_placed(message):
    order = parse(message)
    reserved = inventory.reserve(order.items)
    if reserved:
        logger.info(f"Reserved items for order {order.order_id}")
        email.send_confirmation(order.customer_id, order.order_id)
    else:
        logger.warning(f"Insufficient stock for order {order.order_id}")
        trigger_reorder(order.items)
```

### Step 9: Subscriber Acknowledges

After successful processing, the subscriber sends an acknowledgment (ack) to the broker. This tells the broker the message was processed and does not need to be redelivered.

If the subscriber fails to ack within a timeout, the broker marks the message for redelivery. After a configurable number of redelivery attempts (typically 3-10), the broker sends the message to a dead letter queue (DLQ).

```python
# Acknowledgment flow
def pull_and_process(broker, subscription_id):
    messages = broker.pull(subscription_id, max_messages=5)
    for msg in messages:
        try:
            process(msg.payload)
            broker.ack(subscription_id, msg.id)
        except TemporaryError:
            broker.nack(subscription_id, msg.id)
            # Message will be redelivered
        except FatalError:
            broker.send_to_dlq(subscription_id, msg.id)
```

### Step 10: Broker Updates Offset

For pull-based subscribers (or tracked push subscribers), the broker updates the subscriber's offset. If the subscriber disconnects and reconnects, it resumes from the last committed offset — it does not reprocess messages it already acknowledged.

```text

│ S3 (Analyt) │  orders.created │  4418              │
│ S2 (Invtry) │  orders.shipped │  3321              │
```

## Architecture Variants

Pub-sub is not a single pattern. Different implementations make different trade-offs.

### Point-to-Point vs Pub-Sub

**Point-to-point** (sometimes called competing consumers): a message is delivered to exactly one consumer from a group. Used for workload distribution — a job queue, for example. Each message is processed once.

**Pub-sub**: a message is delivered to every subscriber. Used for event broadcasting — each subscriber gets an independent copy.

### Topic-Based vs Content-Based Filtering

**Topic-based filtering** (most common): the broker routes messages based on the topic name only. Subscribers subscribe to a topic or a pattern of topics. This is simpler and faster because the broker does not inspect message bodies.

**Content-based filtering**: the broker evaluates message content against subscriber-defined conditions. A subscriber might subscribe to `orders.created` but only receive messages where `amount > 500`. This gives subscribers finer control but adds overhead — the broker must parse and evaluate message content for every subscriber.

```python
# Content-based filter expression (pseudocode)
subscription = broker.subscribe(
    topic="orders.created",
    filter="data.currency == 'USD' AND data.amount > 100.00"
)
```

### Push vs Pull Delivery

**Push model**: broker initiates the delivery. Lower latency, but requires the broker to manage backpressure and handle subscriber unavailability. The subscriber must expose a reachable endpoint.

**Pull model**: subscriber initiates the retrieval. Higher latency (polling delay), but the subscriber controls its own processing rate. Better for high-volume systems where subscribers need to batch process.

| Aspect | Push | Pull |
|---|---|---|
| Latency | 1-10ms | 50-500ms (poll interval) |
| Backpressure | Broker must handle | Subscriber controls |
| Subscriber reachability | Required (endpoint) | Not required |
| Batch processing | Harder | Natural |
| Resource use at broker | Higher (holds connections) | Lower |

### Persistent vs Non-Persistent Delivery

**Persistent delivery**: the broker stores messages to disk. Messages survive broker restarts. This provides durability guarantees but adds latency (disk writes) and uses storage.

**Non-persistent delivery**: the broker keeps messages in memory only. Faster but messages are lost on broker restart or crash. Suitable for real-time data where loss of a few messages is acceptable (stock tickers, sensor readings, chat messages).

## Mental Model Check

Test your understanding with these questions.

### Question 1

Your team builds a notification system. When a user receives a message, the system should send an email AND a push notification AND update the unread count. Would you use pub-sub or point-to-point?

**Answer**: Pub-sub. The single event (message received) must fan out to three independent subscribers (email service, push service, counter service). Each subscriber processes the event independently. With point-to-point, only one consumer would receive the event.

### Question 2

You have 10 worker instances processing image uploads. Each upload must be processed by exactly one worker. Would you use pub-sub or a queue?

**Answer**: A queue with competing consumers (point-to-point). Each upload message should be consumed by exactly one worker. Pub-sub would send each upload to all 10 workers, wasting computing resources.

### Question 3

A subscriber to `orders.*` is down for 30 minutes. When it comes back, should it receive the orders it missed? What determines this?

**Answer**: It depends on the subscription type. With a durable subscription, the broker stored messages during the downtime and will deliver them upon reconnection. With a non-durable subscription, messages published during the downtime are lost. The retention policy also matters — if messages expire after 15 minutes, a 30-minute outage means some or all messages are gone regardless of durability.

### Question 4

Your system publishes 100,000 messages per second to a single topic with 5 subscribers. How many messages per second does the broker deliver to subscribers?

**Answer**: 500,000 messages per second. Each of the 100,000 published messages is fanned out to all 5 subscribers. The broker must handle 100,000 writes (ingress) and 500,000 deliveries (egress). This fan-out multiplication is why broker throughput is often measured separately for ingress and egress.

### Question 5

An analytics pipeline uses pull-based consumption with a poll interval of 2 seconds. A spike of 50,000 messages arrives in 1 second. The broker can store 500 MB of messages. Each message is 2 KB. What happens?

**Answer**: 50,000 messages at 2 KB each = 100 MB of data in one second. Over 2 seconds of polling, the broker accumulates up to 200 MB — well within the 500 MB limit. The analytics pipeline, when it pulls, receives all accumulated messages. If the pipeline processes slower than the publish rate, the broker continues to accumulate until it hits 500 MB (about 3-4 more seconds at this rate), after which older messages are dropped or the broker applies backpressure to publishers.
