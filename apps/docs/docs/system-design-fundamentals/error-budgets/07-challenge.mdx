# Error Budgets — Prove It

## Tier 1: Foundation

### Challenge 1: Calculate the Error Budget

**Scenario:** Your service has an SLO of 99.95% availability over a 30-day window. The 30-day window contains 2,592,000 seconds. In the first 15 days of the month, the service experienced 450 seconds of downtime. In the remaining 15 days, the service experienced 720 seconds of downtime.

**Tasks:**
1. Calculate the total error budget for the 30-day window.
2. Calculate how much budget has been consumed after 15 days.
3. Calculate how much budget remains for the second half of the month.
4. Determine if the service is on track to meet the SLO at the end of the month.

**Model Answer:**

**Step 1:** Total allowed downtime = (1 - 0.9995) * 2,592,000 = 0.0005 * 2,592,000 = 1,296 seconds. This is the total error budget.

**Step 2:** After 15 days, the budget consumed is 450 seconds. Remaining budget for the window = 1,296 - 450 = 846 seconds.

**Step 3:** The second half experiences 720 seconds of downtime. Remaining budget after full month = 846 - 720 = 126 seconds. The service ends the month with 126 seconds of budget remaining, meaning it achieved 1,296 - 126 = 1,170 seconds of actual downtime, which corresponds to 1 - (1,170 / 2,592,000) = 99.955% availability — above the SLO of 99.95%.

**Step 4:** Yes, the service is on track. The budget burn rate averaged 30 seconds per day in the first half (450 / 15) and 48 seconds per day in the second half (720 / 15). The total burn rate of 39 seconds per day stayed below the allowed burn rate of 43.2 seconds per day (1,296 / 30).

### Challenge 2: Define SLIs for Error Budget Tracking

**Scenario:** You are building an error budget system for a video streaming platform. The platform has three critical user journeys: video playback, search, and account login. Your SRE team needs SLIs for each journey.

**Tasks:**
1. Define one SLI for each of the three user journeys.
2. Explain why each SLI is appropriate.
3. Identify one pitfall for each SLI that could give a false sense of health.

**Model Answer:**

**Video Playback SLI:** Ratio of video streams that start within 3 seconds and play without rebuffering for at least 90% of the session duration, divided by total video stream attempts. This SLI captures both startup latency and streaming quality — two factors that directly determine user satisfaction.

**Pitfall:** A short session (under 10 seconds) might be a user who clicked the wrong video, not a streaming failure. You need to filter out sessions under 5 seconds to avoid counting false positives.

**Search SLI:** Ratio of search queries that return results in under 500ms with a non-empty result set, divided by total search queries. This captures both latency and correctness — an empty result set is as bad as a timeout.

**Pitfall:** A search returning results for "Netflix" when the user typed "Netflix" is a successful query but a bad experience. The SLI does not measure result relevance, only availability of the search function.

**Account Login SLI:** Ratio of login attempts that complete successfully within 5 seconds, divided by total login attempts. This captures the critical authentication path.

**Pitfall:** A login that fails due to incorrect password is a user error, not a system failure. You must distinguish between 401 Unauthorized (user error) and 500 Internal Server Error (system failure) in your SLI counting.

### Challenge 3: Multi-Service Error Budget

**Scenario:** Your checkout flow spans three services: Cart Service (SLO 99.9%), Payment Service (SLO 99.99%), and Notification Service (SLO 99.5%). Each service has its own error budget calculated over a 7-day window (604,800 seconds). A single checkout request touches all three services. If any service fails, the checkout fails.

**Tasks:**
1. Calculate the error budget for each service.
2. Calculate the composite availability of the system.
3. If the Payment Service exhausts its budget on day 3, what happens?
4. Describe how you would allocate budget across these services fairly.

**Model Answer:**

**Budget per service:**
- Cart Service: 0.001 * 604,800 = 604.8 seconds of allowed downtime
- Payment Service: 0.0001 * 604,800 = 60.48 seconds of allowed downtime
- Notification Service: 0.005 * 604,800 = 3,024 seconds of allowed downtime

**Composite availability:** 0.999 * 0.9999 * 0.995 = 0.9939 or 99.39%. The system as a whole is substantially less reliable than any individual service because failures cascade.

**Payment Service exhausts budget on day 3:** The Payment Service has the tightest budget (60.48 seconds). If it exhausts budget on day 3, the team enters a "freeze" state — no new deployments, no risky changes. The Cart and Notification services still have budget, but the Payment Service gates the entire checkout flow. This is a good argument for giving the Payment Service a higher SLO or a separate budget window.

**Fair allocation:** Allocate budget proportional to business impact. The Payment Service handles money — it should have the tightest budget (highest SLO). The Notification Service can tolerate more downtime because a delayed email is not a revenue loss. Consider independent error budgets per service with a shared "composite error budget" that the checkout team manages centrally.

## Tier 2: Design

### Challenge 4: Design an Error Budget Dashboard

**Scenario:** Your company has 12 microservices, each with its own SLO. Management wants a single dashboard that shows the health of the entire system. You have 30 minutes to plan the dashboard.

**Tasks:**
1. Design the dashboard layout (what panels, what data sources).
2. Define the key metrics and their aggregation windows.
3. Explain how you would handle alerting from the dashboard.
4. Describe how you would communicate budget status to non-technical stakeholders.

**Model Answer:**

**Dashboard Layout:**
- Top row: Four "health tiles" showing overall system error budget remaining as a percentage. Color-coded green (over 50% remaining), yellow (20-50%), red (under 20%), and flashing red (exhausted).
- Middle row: 12 sparkline charts, one per service, showing budget burn rate over the last 7 days. Each sparkline has a horizontal line at the budget exhaustion point.
- Bottom row: A heatmap showing which services are consuming budget at which hours of the day, helping identify patterns (e.g., Payment Service always burns budget during 2-3 AM deployments).

**Key Metrics and Aggregation:**
- Budget remaining: percentage of total budget not yet consumed, calculated over the SLO window (30 days)
- Burn rate: budget consumed per hour, rolling 6-hour window
- Time to exhaustion: remaining budget / current burn rate, projected as a date
- All metrics refreshed every 60 seconds with data from Prometheus

**Alerting Strategy:**
- Warning alert: when any service drops below 50% budget remaining
- Critical alert: when any service drops below 20% budget remaining
- Burn rate alert: when the 6-hour burn rate would exhaust the budget in under 3 days
- Avoid alert fatigue by not alerting on every SLI violation — only on budget exhaustion risk

**Non-Technical Communication:**
- Use a traffic light system: green = healthy, yellow = caution, red = at risk
- Weekly executive summary: "System health is at 78% budget remaining. Payment Service is burning faster than expected due to a known database issue. Estimated recovery: 5 days."
- Never use nines in executive communication. Say "we are on track to meet our reliability target" instead of "we are at 99.945%."

### Challenge 5: Error Budget Policy for a Fintech Platform

**Scenario:** You are the SRE lead for a fintech platform processing $50 million in daily transactions. The platform has a regulatory requirement of 99.999% availability for the transaction processing system. You need to design an error budget policy.

**Tasks:**
1. Define the error budget window and calculate it.
2. Design the policy for what happens when the budget is exhausted.
3. Design the policy for what happens when the budget is at 50% with 10 days remaining.
4. Describe how you would handle planned maintenance windows.

**Model Answer:**

**Error Budget Window and Calculation:**
24-hour window (86,400 seconds). Five nines means 0.001% allowed downtime: 0.00001 * 86,400 = 0.864 seconds per day. This is extremely tight — one database failover can exhaust the entire daily budget. Using a 30-day window gives 0.00001 * 2,592,000 = 25.92 seconds per month, which is more practical.

**Budget Exhausted Policy:**
- All production deployments stop immediately. Only security patches and critical bug fixes are allowed, and they require CEO sign-off.
- An incident review is triggered within 24 hours with the SRE team, engineering leadership, and compliance officers.
- A root cause analysis is published within 72 hours.
- The budget is reset only after a formal review confirms the systemic issue is fixed.
- Automated rollback capabilities are triggered for any recent changes that may have contributed to the budget depletion.

**50% Budget at 10 Days Remaining Policy:**
- This is a moderate burn rate scenario. The team has consumed half the budget halfway through the window.
- A "caution" state is declared: deployments must pass an additional review by a senior engineer.
- The team must produce a recovery plan within 48 hours showing how they will stay within budget.
- All non-critical feature flags are turned off to reduce change risk.
- Monitoring is elevated to 15-second intervals for the affected services.

**Planned Maintenance Windows:**
- For a fintech platform, zero-downtime maintenance is expected. Blue-green deployments and rolling updates are mandatory.
- If maintenance downtime is unavoidable, it must be scheduled during the lowest-traffic period (typically 3-4 AM Sunday) and communicated to the business 72 hours in advance.
- The maintenance downtime is included in the error budget — no exclusion. This incentivizes the team to minimize maintenance windows.
- Exception: if the regulator mandates a maintenance window, that downtime is excluded from the budget but must be documented.

### Challenge 6: Burn Rate Alert Design

**Scenario:** You need to design a multi-window burn rate alerting system for a service with a 99.9% SLO over a 30-day window. The error budget is 0.001 * 2,592,000 = 2,592 seconds. You want to detect budget exhaustion at different speeds.

**Tasks:**
1. Design alerting thresholds for fast, medium, and slow burn rates.
2. Calculate the time to exhaustion for each threshold.
3. Design the escalation path for each threshold.
4. Explain how you would avoid false positives during traffic spikes.

**Model Answer:**

**Alerting Thresholds:**

| Burn Rate | Multiplier | Window | Exhaustion Time | Alert Level |
|-----------|-----------|--------|-----------------|-------------|
| Fast | 10x | 1 hour | 2.4 hours | Page |
| Medium | 5x | 6 hours | 12 hours | Escalated |
| Slow | 2x | 24 hours | 30 hours | Warning |

**Time to Exhaustion Calculations:**
- Fast burn (10x budget rate): 10 * 2,592 seconds consumed per hour = 1,296 seconds per hour. To exhaust 2,592 seconds at 10x rate: 2,592 / 1,296 = 2 hours. But with 1-hour window, you detect at 1 hour when 4% of budget is already consumed.
- Medium burn (5x budget rate): 5 * 2,592 / (30 * 24) = 18 seconds per hour. To exhaust: 2,592 / 18 = 144 hours = 6 days. But the 6-hour window catches it faster.
- Slow burn (2x budget rate): 2 * 2,592 / (30 * 24) = 7.2 seconds per hour. To exhaust: 2,592 / 7.2 = 360 hours = 15 days. The 24-hour window catches this slow bleed.

**Escalation Path:**
- Fast burn alert: Pages the on-call SRE immediately. The incident commander is expected to acknowledge within 5 minutes. If the burn rate persists for 30 minutes, the engineering manager is pulled in.
- Medium burn alert: Creates a PagerDuty incident with a 15-minute acknowledgment target. The team investigates during business hours unless the burn rate accelerates.
- Slow burn alert: Creates a Jira ticket assigned to the service owner. Reviewed in the daily standup. No immediate action required unless the burn rate crosses into medium territory.

**False Positive Prevention:**
- Traffic spikes are handled by windowing: the alert fires only if the burn rate exceeds the threshold for the entire window duration, not for a single spike.
- Use a sliding window, not a resetting window. A single 10-minute spike at 20x burn rate should not trigger a fast burn alert if the rest of the hour is normal.
- Add a "hysteresis" period: after the burn rate drops below the threshold, wait 10 minutes before clearing the alert. This prevents flapping.
- Exclude known traffic patterns from the alert: if the service always sees a 3x traffic spike during daily batch processing, have a separate baseline for that period.

## Tier 3: Expert

### Challenge 7: Multi-Layered Error Budget with Dependencies

**Scenario:** A large e-commerce platform has 30 microservices organized into three tiers: Frontend (5 services), Business Logic (15 services), and Data (10 services). Each tier has its own SLO. The Frontend SLO is 99.99%, the Business Logic SLO is 99.95%, and the Data SLO is 99.9%. The composite user-facing SLO is 99.9%. You need to design an error budget system that accounts for dependencies between tiers.

**Discussion Points:**

**Budget Allocation Across Tiers:** The Data tier has the lowest individual SLO (99.9%) but it is the foundation. If the Data tier fails, all Business Logic and Frontend services fail. You might want to give the Data tier a tighter budget than its SLO suggests, because its failures have a multiplicative effect. A better approach: set the Data tier SLO to 99.99% internally, even though the external SLO is 99.9%. This creates a safety buffer.

**Dependency Budget Tracking:** When a Frontend request fails due to a Data tier outage, which service's budget is consumed? The answer matters. One approach: consume the Data tier's budget (because that is where the root cause is). Another approach: consume all three tiers' budgets (because the user experience failed at every layer). The industry standard is to consume the root cause service's budget, but also track "cascading failures" in a separate dashboard.

**Budget Sharing Between Tiers:** Consider a "shared pool" model where each tier contributes 10% of its budget to a central pool. When a dependency-induced failure occurs, the central pool absorbs the cost. This prevents unfair budget exhaustion on downstream services that have no control over upstream failures.

**Composite Budget with Weighted Allocation:** Not all services are equal. The checkout service has a higher business impact than the product recommendation service. Weight the budget allocation by business criticality: checkout gets 40% of the composite budget, product catalog gets 20%, recommendations get 10%, and the remaining 30% is distributed across the other 27 services.

**Operational Complexity:** The biggest challenge is operational complexity. Each additional layer of budget tracking adds monitoring overhead, alerting noise, and cognitive load on the on-call engineer. Start simple: track only the composite user-facing SLO. Add per-tier budgets only when the composite budget is consistently exhausted without clear attribution.

### Challenge 8: Error Budgets in a Multi-Tenant System

**Scenario:** You are designing an error budget system for a SaaS platform with 500 tenants. Each tenant has a different SLA: Enterprise tenants have 99.99% SLA, Business tenants have 99.9% SLA, and Free tenants have 99% SLA. The platform runs on shared infrastructure. A single incident affects all tenants simultaneously.

**Discussion Points:**

**Tenant Isolation:** The core problem is that shared infrastructure means shared fate. A database migration that causes 5 minutes of downtime for an Enterprise tenant also causes 5 minutes of downtime for a Free tenant, but the Enterprise tenant's SLA is 10x stricter. The Free tenant's downtime might be within budget, but the Enterprise tenant's budget could be exhausted by the same incident.

**Per-Tenant Budget Tracking:** You need to track error budgets per tenant, not just per service. This means 500 budget counters, each with a different SLO target. The data volume is manageable (Prometheus can handle 500 time series), but the alerting logic becomes complex. You need to alert when any single Enterprise tenant approaches budget exhaustion, not just when the aggregate is exhausted.

**Premium Budget Pool:** Allocate a separate "premium error budget" pool that only Enterprise tenants draw from. When shared infrastructure fails, the premium pool absorbs the cost. This ensures that Free tier incidents do not deplete the Enterprise budget. The premium pool gets a higher SLO target (99.995%) to provide additional headroom.

**Per-Tenant Features:** Segment the error budget policy by tenant tier:
- Enterprise: automatic compensation when budget drops below 50%
- Business: notification when budget drops below 25%
- Free: no notification, but the feature is rate-limited when budget is exhausted

**Blast Radius Considerations:** When a shared component fails, the blast radius impacts all tenants. Your error budget system should detect when a single component is causing disproportionate budget consumption across many tenants. This is a sign that the component needs architectural isolation.

### Additional Challenge: Budget Reconciliation for a Migration

**Scenario:** Your team is migrating from a monolithic architecture to microservices. The monolith has a single SLO of 99.9%. After the migration, you will have 5 microservices, each with its own SLO. The total error budget of the microservices must be at least as good as the monolith's budget.

**Tasks:**
1. Calculate the monolith's error budget for a 30-day window.
2. Determine the SLO for each microservice such that the composite SLO equals or exceeds the monolith's SLO.
3. Explain how you would handle the transition period where both the monolith and microservices are running.
4. Discuss the operational challenges of maintaining separate budgets during the migration.

**Model Answer:**

**Monolith budget:** 99.9% SLO over 30 days = 2,592 seconds of allowed downtime.

**Microservice SLO allocation:** The composite SLO of 5 services must be at least 99.9%. If achieved by multiplying individual SLOs: SLO_composite = SLO_1 * SLO_2 * SLO_3 * SLO_4 * SLO_5. If each has the same SLO: SLO_service = 0.999^(1/5) = 0.9998 = 99.98%. This is significantly tighter than the monolith's 99.9%. A better approach: give each service a different SLO based on its criticality. The critical "order" service gets 99.99%. The less critical "notification" service gets 99.95%. The composite: 0.9999 * 0.9995 * 0.9995 * 0.9995 * 0.9995 = 0.9979 = 99.79%. This is below the monolith's 99.9%. You need tighter SLOs.

**Transition period:** During the migration, both the monolith and the microservices are running. The budget is calculated for both. If a request goes through the monolith, it consumes the monolith's budget. If it goes through the microservices, it consumes the microservices' budgets. The total budget consumption is the sum of both. This creates a period where the team has to manage two sets of budgets simultaneously.

**Operational challenges:** The biggest challenge is attribution. If a request fails, is it because the monolith failed or because the microservice failed? You need distributed tracing to attribute failures correctly. During the transition, you should track the "migration budget" separately — the budget consumption specifically caused by the migration process (e.g., bugs in the new microservices, configuration errors). This prevents the migration from consuming the regular operational budget.

### Discussion: Budget Allocation Strategy

The key strategic question is whether to allocate budget by tenant or by service. Both approaches have merits:

**Per-tenant allocation:** Each tenant gets a fixed budget proportional to their SLA. This is fair but complex. A single incident can drain the budgets of all 500 tenants simultaneously, leaving you with 500 exhausted budgets and 500 blocked deployments. The operational overhead of managing 500 alerts is significant.

**Per-service allocation:** Each service gets a fixed budget. The service's budget is consumed regardless of which tenant triggered the error. This is simpler but unfair. A noisy free tenant could consume the budget that an enterprise tenant depends on.

**Hybrid approach:** Each service has a base budget. Each tenant has a small "overage" budget. The service budget covers infrastructure failures. The tenant budget covers tenant-specific issues (misconfiguration, unusual traffic patterns). A shared infrastructure failure consumes the service budget, not the tenant budgets. A tenant-specific issue consumes the tenant budget.

### Discussion: Budget Exhaustion Recovery

When a multi-tenant system exhausts the budget, the recovery path is more complex. You cannot simply wait for the window to roll over, because the tenants with the highest SLAs cannot afford the downtime. Consider these recovery strategies:

**Budget replenishment:** Enterprise tenants can request a one-time budget replenishment. The replenishment is approved by the SRE team and documented in the incident report. This prevents the budget from being a hard ceiling for high-value customers.

**Budget borrowing:** A tenant can borrow budget from the next month. The borrowed amount is deducted from the next month's allocation. This provides short-term relief but requires the tenant to be more conservative in the following month.

**SLA relaxation:** For free tenants, the SLO can be temporarily relaxed when the budget is exhausted. The service degrades gracefully (rate limiting, cached responses) instead of failing. This is better than a hard outage because the user still gets some functionality.

**Infrastructure investment:** If the budget is consistently exhausted, the solution is not to adjust the policy — it is to invest in infrastructure improvements. The budget exhaustion is a signal that the system needs more redundancy, better monitoring, or faster incident response.

### Discussion: The Budget as a Business Tool

Error budgets in a multi-tenant system are not just an engineering tool — they are a business tool. The budget data feeds directly into customer conversations:

- "Your tenant has consumed 80% of its error budget this month. Based on this trend, we recommend upgrading to our Enterprise plan, which provides a higher SLO and a larger budget."
- "Your tenant's budget was exhausted due to a shared infrastructure incident. We are processing a Service Credit as per your SLA."
- "Your tenant's budget is healthy. The 99.99% SLO has been met for 6 consecutive months. Here is a summary of our reliability improvements."

The error budget becomes a shared language between engineering, product, and customer success teams. It translates technical reliability into business value.

### Additional Challenge: Budget Attribution Across a Dependency Graph

**Scenario:** Your platform has this dependency chain for the checkout flow:

```
Checkout API -> Cart Service -> Inventory Service -> Pricing Service
                                                    -> Payment Gateway (external)
```

The Payment Gateway is a third-party service with its own SLO of 99.95%. When the Payment Gateway fails, the entire checkout flow fails. Your composite SLO for checkout is 99.9%.

**Tasks:**
1. Determine how to attribute budget consumption when the Payment Gateway fails.
2. Calculate the effective SLO of the checkout flow if the internal services have 99.99% SLO and the Payment Gateway has 99.95%.
3. Design a strategy to handle third-party dependency failures in your error budget.
4. Propose a service-level agreement with the Payment Gateway provider that aligns with your internal error budget.

**Model Answer:**

**Budget attribution:** When the Payment Gateway fails, the budget consumption should be attributed to the checkout flow's composite budget, not to any individual internal service. The internal services (Cart, Inventory, Pricing) should not be penalized for a dependency they cannot control. However, the checkout flow should track a "dependency failure" sub-budget that is separate from the "internal failure" sub-budget.

**Effective SLO:** Internal services composite: 0.9999 * 0.9999 * 0.9999 = 0.9997. With Payment Gateway: 0.9997 * 0.9995 = 0.9992 = 99.92%. This is above the 99.9% target, but with very little margin. Any degradation in the Payment Gateway's SLO would push the composite below 99.9%.

**Strategy for third-party dependencies:**
- Monitor the Payment Gateway's error rate independently and include it in the composite budget
- Have a separate "dependency budget" that is 30% of the total checkout budget
- If the Payment Gateway exhausts its share of the dependency budget, trigger a review of whether the provider is meeting their SLA
- Build a circuit breaker that isolates the checkout flow from Payment Gateway failures (fail fast instead of timing out)
- Cache pricing data when possible to reduce dependency on the Payment Gateway during normal operations

**SLA with Payment Gateway:**
- Require 99.95% availability measured over a 30-day window
- Require the provider to publish their error budget status
- Include a service credit clause: if the provider falls below 99.9%, they provide a 10% credit
- Align the measurement window with your internal budget window
- Build a fallback strategy: if the Payment Gateway is down for more than 5 minutes, switch to a secondary provider

### Additional Challenge: Incident Response Integration

**Scenario:** An incident occurs at 2:30 PM. The service has a 99.9% SLO over a 30-day window. The incident lasts 35 minutes and causes a complete outage. The current budget remaining before the incident is 65%.

**Tasks:**
1. Calculate the budget consumption from this incident.
2. Determine the new budget remaining after the incident.
3. Design the incident response workflow that integrates with the error budget system.
4. Propose post-incident actions based on the new budget state.

**Model Answer:**

**Budget consumption:** The incident consumed 35 minutes = 2,100 seconds of the 2,592-second budget. Since the service was completely unavailable during the incident, all 2,100 seconds count against the budget.

**New budget remaining:** 65% of 2,592 = 1,684.8 seconds remaining before incident. After the incident: 1,684.8 - 2,100 = -415.2 seconds. The budget is exhausted and overspent by 415.2 seconds.

**Incident response workflow:**
1. Monitoring detects the outage and triggers a critical alert (burn rate over 10x in the 1-hour window)
2. The on-call engineer is paged automatically
3. The incident is declared and tracked in the incident management system
4. During the incident response, the budget calculator continues to update
5. When the incident is resolved, the budget system sends a notification: "Budget consumed: 81%. Budget remaining: exhausted."
6. An incident report is automatically created, linking the budget consumption data

**Post-incident actions:**
- All deployments are blocked until the budget recovers (approximately 30 days)
- An incident review is scheduled within 24 hours
- The review focuses on: "What caused the outage and how do we prevent it from happening again?"
- The budget exhaustion is considered a data point, not a failure metric
- A formal budget exception request must be submitted for any emergency deployment
- The team monitors the burn rate daily to ensure the system is stable
- A reliability improvement project is prioritized in the next sprint

### Additional Challenge: Error Budget Policy for a Regulated Industry

**Scenario:** You are designing an error budget system for a healthcare platform that must comply with HIPAA regulations. The platform has a regulatory requirement of 99.99% availability for patient data access. Additionally, the regulator requires that all incidents affecting patient data be reported within 24 hours.

**Tasks:**
1. Design the error budget policy that meets regulatory requirements.
2. Define the SLI for patient data access.
3. Explain how you would handle the 24-hour incident reporting requirement in the budget system.
4. Design the audit trail for budget decisions.
5. Discuss how you would handle the tension between regulatory compliance and engineering velocity.

**Model Answer:**

**Error budget policy:** The SLO is set at 99.99% (regulatory minimum). The error budget is 259.2 seconds per month. Given the regulatory requirement, the policy is strict: any deployment that consumes more than 10% of the budget (25.9 seconds) triggers an automatic review. If the budget drops below 50%, all deployments are blocked until a compliance review is completed. The exception process requires sign-off from both the VP of Engineering and the Compliance Officer.

**SLI for patient data access:** The SLI measures the fraction of patient data access requests that return the correct data within 2 seconds. This includes both the API response time and the database query time. The SLI must be auditable — every request must be logged with a unique ID, timestamp, and response status for compliance purposes.

**24-hour incident reporting:** The budget system automatically generates an incident report when the budget drops by more than 5% (12.96 seconds) in a single event. The report is sent to the compliance team within 1 hour of detection. The report includes: incident duration, number of affected requests, root cause, and remediation plan. The 24-hour clock starts from the incident detection, not from the incident occurrence.

**Audit trail:** Every budget decision is logged to an immutable audit log. The log records: the budget state at the time of decision, the decision made (deploy, block, exception), the person who made the decision, and the reason. The audit log is retained for 7 years (HIPAA requirement) and is queryable by the compliance team.

**Regulatory vs. velocity tension:** The tension is managed by having a separate regulatory compliance SLO and a separate engineering velocity SLO. The regulatory SLO is 99.99% (hard limit). The engineering SLO is 99.95% (internal target). The difference of 0.04% gives engineers a buffer for experimentation. As long as the engineering SLO is met, the regulatory SLO is safe. This gives engineers room to move while maintaining compliance.

### Additional Challenge: Error Budgets for a Global Real-Time Application

**Scenario:** Your company operates a real-time multiplayer game with 5 million daily active users. The game has a latency SLO: 99.9% of game state updates are delivered in under 50ms. The game runs in 8 AWS regions. A single region having issues should not affect other regions.

**Tasks:**
1. Design per-region error budgets that are independent of each other.
2. Define a global composite SLO that represents the overall player experience.
3. Design a deployment strategy that accounts for regional budget differences.
4. Explain how you would handle a regional network partition that causes 100% packet loss for 15 minutes.

**Model Answer:**

**Per-region budgets:** Each region has its own SLO of 99.9% latency-compliance, tracked independently. The budget window is 7 days (shorter than the standard 30 days because the game industry moves fast). Each region gets 0.1% of its traffic as the error budget. If a region has 10 million game state updates per day, its budget is 0.001 * 10,000,000 = 10,000 slow updates per day.

**Global composite SLO:** The composite SLO is the weighted average of all regional SLOs, weighted by the number of active players in each region. If US-East has 2 million players (40%) and EU-West has 1.5 million players (30%), the composite is heavily influenced by these regions. The composite SLO is used for executive reporting, not for deployment gating.

**Regional deployment strategy:** Deployments are gated per region, not globally. A region with a healthy budget gets the deployment first. After 24 hours of monitoring (no budget impact), the deployment rolls out to the next region. This phased approach minimizes the blast radius of a bad deployment.

**Regional network partition:** A 15-minute partition in one region causes 100% packet loss, consuming the full 7-day budget in a single event. The response: the affected region enters maintenance mode (no deployments) immediately. Traffic from the affected region is rerouted to the nearest healthy region (e.g., US-East traffic goes to US-West). Players in the affected region experience higher latency (50-100ms instead of under 50ms), which is acceptable for a regional outage. The budget is manually replenished for the affected region (this is a known exception for infrastructure failures outside the team's control). An incident review focuses on improving regional resilience (additional network paths, cross-region fallback, local caching).

**Key lesson:** Regional error budgets give you the granularity to manage reliability at the infrastructure level while maintaining a global view of the player experience. The independence of regional budgets is critical for a global system — one region's problems should not block deployments in another region.
