# Understanding Error Budgets

## The Friday Afternoon Deployment

It is 4:52 PM on a Friday. Sarah, a senior engineer, has been working on a new checkout feature for three weeks. The feature is fully tested, reviewed, and approved. She clicks "Deploy to Production" and watches the deployment pipeline progress bar.

The deployment succeeds. Sarah breathes a sigh of relief. She starts packing up to leave.

At 5:14 PM, her phone buzzes. The on-call engineer: "The checkout latency just spiked. The 95th percentile went from 200ms to 4 seconds. Users are seeing spinning checkouts. We have had 2,300 failed transactions in the last 8 minutes."

Sarah's deployment did not cause a crash. It did not cause an error. It caused a subtle degradation: a database query that was efficient at 1,000 requests per second became slow at 10,000 requests per second. The new feature added an extra JOIN that was not covered by an index.

The system is still "up" — it returns HTTP 200 responses — but the user experience is terrible. The standard availability SLI (HTTP 200 / total requests) shows 99.9% — well within the SLO. But the latency SLI (requests completed in under 500ms) shows 72% — a disaster.

This is the problem that error budgets solve. They force you to define what "good enough" means, measure it continuously, and stop deploying when you are burning through your allowed failure budget too fast.

## The Insight

An **error budget** is the maximum amount of failure your system can experience while still meeting its Service Level Objective (SLO). It is the gap between 100% perfection and the SLO target.

The math is straightforward:

```
Error Budget = 1 - SLO Target
```

If your SLO is 99.9% availability over a 30-day window, your error budget is 0.1% of the window duration. For a 30-day window (2,592,000 seconds), that is 2,592 seconds of allowed downtime — approximately 43 minutes.

The insight is that error budgets reframe reliability from a binary "is it up or down" question into a resource management question. You have 43 minutes of downtime to spend this month. How do you want to spend it?

You can spend it on:
- A deployment that introduces a slight regression
- A database migration that causes a brief outage
- A dependency that has a transient failure
- A traffic spike that overwhelms a component

The budget is a shared resource. The SRE team wants to preserve it. The product team wants to spend it to ship features faster. The error budget makes this tension explicit and measurable.

The error budget concept was pioneered by Google's Site Reliability Engineering team. Before error budgets, the relationship between SRE teams and product teams was adversarial. The SRE team enforced strict availability targets, and product teams felt constrained. Error budgets changed the conversation from "you cannot deploy" to "you have budget remaining for two more deployments this month, choose wisely."

## Core Vocabulary

### SLI (Service Level Indicator)

An SLI is a quantitative measure of some aspect of the service's quality. It is the raw measurement — the data point. Common SLIs include:

- **Request latency:** The time it takes to respond to a request, measured at the p50, p99, or p999 percentile.
- **Error rate:** The fraction of requests that result in a failure (HTTP 5xx, unhandled exceptions, timeouts).
- **Throughput:** The number of requests processed per second.
- **Availability:** The fraction of time the service is accessible and usable.
- **Freshness:** For data pipelines, the age of the data available for querying.
- **Correctness:** The fraction of responses that produce the correct result.
- **Coverage:** The fraction of functionality or infrastructure that is monitored.

Choosing the right SLI is the most important decision in error budget design. A poorly chosen SLI gives false confidence. A well-chosen SLI captures what matters to users.

### SLO (Service Level Objective)

An SLO is a target value for an SLI over a specific time window. It is the commitment you make to yourself (or your team) about the level of service you intend to deliver.

Examples of SLOs:
- "99.9% of requests will complete in under 200ms, measured over a rolling 30-day window."
- "99.99% of requests will return a non-5xx response, measured over a rolling 7-day window."
- "99.95% of API calls to the payment service will succeed, measured over a rolling 28-day window."

The SLO must be:
- **Specific:** "Fast" is not specific. "Under 200ms at the p99 percentile" is specific.
- **Measurable:** You must be able to collect the data to calculate the SLI.
- **Time-bound:** The window over which the SLO is measured must be defined.
- **Attainable:** An SLO of 100% is not attainable. Aim for 99.9% to 99.99% for most services.

### Error Budget

The error budget is the inverse of the SLO. If the SLO is 99.9%, the error budget is 0.1%. It represents the amount of failure you can tolerate without violating the SLO.

The error budget is typically expressed in the same units as the SLI:
- For availability SLOs: seconds of allowed downtime
- For latency SLOs: number of requests that can exceed the latency threshold
- For throughput SLOs: number of requests that can be dropped

### Burn Rate

Burn rate is the speed at which you are consuming the error budget. It is measured as a multiplier of the expected consumption rate. A burn rate of 1x means you are consuming budget at exactly the rate that would exhaust it by the end of the window. A burn rate of 10x means you are consuming 10x faster.

```
Burn Rate = Actual Error Budget Consumption / Expected Consumption

Expected Consumption = Total Budget / Window Duration
  = 2,592 seconds / 30 days
  = 86.4 seconds per day
  = 3.6 seconds per hour

At 10x burn rate: consuming 36 seconds per hour
  Time to exhaust budget: 2,592 / 36 = 72 hours (3 days)
```

### Budget Window

The budget window is the time period over which the SLO is measured. Common windows are 7 days, 28 days, 30 days, and 90 days. The window choice affects how the budget behaves:

- **Rolling window:** A sliding window that always covers the last N days. The budget recovers gradually as good days push out bad days. This is the most common approach.
- **Calendar window:** A fixed window aligned to calendar months. The budget resets at the start of each month. This is simpler to explain to stakeholders but can cause end-of-month spikes in behavior.

### Deployment Gate

A deployment gate is a policy check that prevents deployments when the error budget is too low. The gate checks the current budget state before allowing a deployment to proceed. If the budget is exhausted, the deployment is blocked.

### Burn Rate Alert

A burn rate alert fires when the rate of budget consumption exceeds a threshold over a specific time window. Burn rate alerts are more useful than absolute budget alerts because they detect problems early — before the budget is exhausted.

### Composite Budget

A composite budget combines the error budgets of multiple services into a single aggregate. This is useful for user-facing features that depend on multiple backend services. The composite budget answers the question: "is the overall user experience within budget?"

### Budget Exhaustion

Budget exhaustion occurs when the error budget drops to zero. The service has consumed its entire allowed failure allocation for the window. At this point, the SLO has been violated, and the service is not meeting its target.

### Budget Carryover

Some error budget systems allow unused budget from one window to carry over to the next. This creates an incentive to be conservative with budget consumption. However, carryover can mask a gradual decline in reliability. Most teams do not use carryover.

The error budget system connects four stages in a continuous feedback loop:

**Define:** You set the SLO and calculate the budget. This is done when the service is created and reviewed quarterly.

**Measure:** You continuously collect SLI data from every request. This is the operational phase — the system runs 24/7, collecting data.

**Compare:** You compare actual performance against the SLO to calculate budget consumption. This happens every minute (or every calculation cycle).

**Act:** You enforce policy based on budget state. This is the feedback loop — the budget status influences deployment decisions, alerting, and incident response.

## Step by Step: How Error Budgets Work

### Step 1: Define the SLO

The process begins with defining the SLO. For a payment service, the SLO might be: "99.99% of payment requests complete successfully in under 2 seconds, measured over a rolling 30-day window."

This SLO has three components:
- **Availability target:** 99.99% of requests succeed
- **Latency target:** 2 seconds or less
- **Window:** 30-day rolling window

The SLI would be: "the fraction of payment requests that return a 2xx or 4xx status code within 2 seconds."

### Step 2: Calculate the Error Budget

```
SLO = 99.99% = 0.9999
Error Budget = 1 - 0.9999 = 0.0001 = 0.01%

For a 30-day window:
  Window duration = 30 days = 2,592,000 seconds
  Error budget = 2,592,000 * 0.0001 = 259.2 seconds

This is approximately 4 minutes and 19 seconds of allowed downtime per month.
```

### Step 3: Collect SLI Data

Every payment request is instrumented. The metrics agent records:
- Total requests: 1
- Successful requests: 1 (if status 2xx and latency under 2s)
- Failed requests: 0 (if status 5xx or latency over 2s)

### Step 4: Aggregate Over the Window

The minute-level data is aggregated over the 30-day window. The system maintains a running total of successful and failed requests.

```
Total requests in 30 days: 86,400,000
Failed requests: 43,200
Actual availability: 1 - (43,200 / 86,400,000) = 0.9995 = 99.95%

But the SLO is 99.99%. Calculate budget remaining:
SLO target: 99.99%
Actual: 99.95%
Budget consumed: (0.0001 - 0.0005) / 0.0001 = -4 (negative = over budget)

Wait — this is wrong. The actual availability (99.95%) is below the SLO (99.99%).
The budget of 0.01% has been consumed and exceeded.

Error budget consumed: (1 - 99.95%) / (1 - 99.99%) = 0.05% / 0.01% = 5x
The service has consumed 5x its error budget. It is not meeting its SLO.
```

### Step 5: Calculate Burn Rate

The burn rate tells you how fast you are consuming budget. For the payment service:

```
Budget window: 30 days = 720 hours
Total budget: 259.2 seconds
Expected burn rate: 259.2 / 720 = 0.36 seconds per hour

If the service has consumed 45 seconds of budget in the last 24 hours:
Actual burn rate: 45 / 24 = 1.875 seconds per hour
Burn rate multiplier: 1.875 / 0.36 = 5.2x

At this burn rate, the budget will be exhausted in:
259.2 / 1.875 = 138.24 hours = 5.76 days
```

### Step 6: Set Alert Thresholds

Based on the burn rate, you set alert thresholds:

```
Fast burn alert: 10x burn rate over 1 hour
  — At 10x, budget exhausts in 72 hours
  — Pager the on-call engineer

Medium burn alert: 2x burn rate over 6 hours
  — At 2x, budget exhausts in 15 days
  — Create a ticket, investigate during business hours

Slow burn alert: 1.5x burn rate over 24 hours
  — At 1.5x, budget exhausts in 20 days
  — Monitor the trend, no immediate action
```

### Step 7: Implement the Deployment Gate

The deployment gate checks the budget before allowing a deployment:

```
Deployment Request for Payment Service v2.3.1

Check 1: Current budget remaining: 67%
Check 2: Current burn rate: 1.1x (within normal range)
Check 3: Recent trend: stable (no accelerating burn)

Result: PASS — Proceeding with deployment.
```

If the budget was below 25%, the deployment would be blocked:

```
Deployment Request for Payment Service v2.3.1

Check 1: Current budget remaining: 12%
Check 2: Current burn rate: 3.2x (above threshold)
Check 3: Recent trend: accelerating

Result: BLOCKED — Error budget at critical level.
Reason: Only 12% budget remaining with 18 days left in window.
Action: Contact SRE team for exception approval.
```

### Step 8: Handle Budget Exhaustion

When the budget is exhausted, the system enters a maintenance-only mode:

```
ERROR BUDGET EXHAUSTED — PAYMENT SERVICE

Current state:
  Budget remaining: 0%
  SLO: 99.99% (target: 43.2 min/month, actual: 43.2 min consumed)
  Window: 30 days rolling (day 22 of window)

Immediate actions:
  1. All production deployments blocked
  2. Incident review triggered (within 24 hours)
  3. Root cause analysis required
  4. Only security patches and critical bug fixes allowed
  5. Exception process: VP of Engineering approval required

Recovery:
  Budget will recover in 8 days when the oldest incident
  (which consumed 15% of budget) falls out of the 30-day window.
```

## Variants of Error Budget Design

### Variant 1: Single-Threshold Budget

The simplest variant. There is one threshold: budget exhausted or not. If the budget is above zero, deployments are allowed. If it is at zero, deployments are blocked.

```
Single-Threshold Policy:
  Budget > 0:  Allow all deployments
  Budget = 0:  Block all deployments
```

**Cons:** No early warning. By the time you block deployments, the budget is already gone. Teams may rush to deploy on the last day of the window before the budget resets.

### Variant 2: Multi-Stage Budget

The most common variant. Multiple thresholds create a graduated response to budget consumption.

```
Multi-Stage Policy:
  Green (50-100%):  Business as usual
  Yellow (25-50%):  Deployments require second reviewer
  Orange (10-25%):  Only critical deployments
  Red (0-10%):      All deployments blocked, incident review triggered
```

**Pros:** Graduated response gives teams time to react. Early warning at yellow stage prevents surprise exhaustion.
**Cons:** More complex to implement. Teams may argue about threshold definitions. The policy can feel bureaucratic.

### Variant 3: Burn Rate Only Budget

Some teams skip the absolute budget threshold and use only burn rate to drive policy. The idea is that burn rate gives early warning regardless of the absolute budget remaining.

```
Burn Rate Only Policy:
  Burn rate under 1.5x: Normal operations
  Burn rate 1.5x to 3x: Requires review
  Burn rate over 3x: Block deployments, investigate
```

**Pros:** Detects problems early. Does not depend on window size. Works well for systems with short attention spans.
**Cons:** Does not account for cumulative budget. A service with 1% budget remaining and a 1x burn rate is in trouble, but the burn rate policy would not block it. Best used in combination with an absolute budget check.

### Variant 4: Per-Tenant Budget

For multi-tenant systems, each tenant gets its own error budget. This prevents a noisy tenant from consuming the shared budget.

```
Per-Tenant Policy:
  Enterprise tenants: 99.99% SLO (259 seconds/month budget)
  Business tenants: 99.9% SLO (2,592 seconds/month budget)
  Free tenants: 99% SLO (25,920 seconds/month budget)

  Each tenant's budget is tracked independently.
  A tenant that exhausts its budget is rate-limited.
  The shared infrastructure budget is tracked separately.
```

**Pros:** Fair allocation. Noisy tenants do not affect others. Enables tiered SLAs.
**Cons:** Significant monitoring overhead. 500 tenants means 500 budget trackers. Complex policy enforcement.

### Variant 5: Budget Banking

Budget banking allows unused budget to carry over to the next month. This incentivizes conservative budget consumption.

```
Budget Banking Policy:
  Monthly budget allocation: 2,592 seconds (99.9% SLO)
  Unused budget at month end: banked at 50% value
  Maximum banked balance: 2x monthly budget

  If a team uses 1,500 seconds in a month:
    Remaining: 2,592 - 1,500 = 1,092 seconds
    Banked: 1,092 * 0.5 = 546 seconds
    Next month's budget: 2,592 + 546 = 3,138 seconds
```

**Pros:** Incentivizes reliability investment. Teams that keep their system stable are rewarded with more deployment flexibility.
**Cons:** Complex to explain. Can mask declining reliability. Hard to implement across multiple teams.

## Mental Model Check

Test your understanding of error budgets with these five questions.

### Question 1

A service has a 99.9% availability SLO over a 30-day window. The service experienced 30 minutes of downtime this month. Is it within its error budget?

**Answer:** No. The error budget is 0.1% of 2,592,000 seconds = 2,592 seconds = 43.2 minutes. The service used 30 minutes = 1,800 seconds. This is under the 43.2-minute budget, so yes, the service is within its error budget.

Wait — the math says 30 minutes is less than 43.2 minutes. So the answer is yes, the service is within budget. The error budget is 43.2 minutes, and the service used only 30 minutes, leaving 13.2 minutes remaining.

### Question 2

What is the difference between an SLI, an SLO, and an error budget?

**Answer:** The SLI is the measurement. The SLO is the target for that measurement. The error budget is the allowed deviation from the target. Think of it as: SLI = what you measure, SLO = what you promise, error budget = how much you can miss the promise by.

### Question 3

A service has a 99.99% SLO with a 7-day window. The window is 604,800 seconds. Calculate the error budget. If the service has consumed 30 seconds of budget in 3 days, what is the burn rate?

**Answer:** Error budget = (1 - 0.9999) * 604,800 = 0.0001 * 604,800 = 60.48 seconds. Expected consumption rate = 60.48 / 7 = 8.64 seconds per day. Actual consumption rate = 30 / 3 = 10 seconds per day. Burn rate = 10 / 8.64 = 1.16x. The service is burning budget slightly faster than expected, but at only 1.16x, it is not a critical concern.

### Question 4

Your team has a 99.9% SLO over a 30-day window. You have 15 days remaining in the window and 40% of the budget remaining. A deployment caused a 5-minute outage. What is the new budget status?

**Answer:** Initial budget = 2,592 seconds. 40% remaining = 1,036.8 seconds. A 5-minute outage = 300 seconds. New remaining = 1,036.8 - 300 = 736.8 seconds = 28.4% of the original budget. The burn rate spikes temporarily. If the outage is a one-time event, the budget should recover to approximately 35% by the end of the window (assuming no further incidents). The team should consider whether the deployment risk was worth 300 seconds of budget consumption.

### Question 5

Why would a team choose a 7-day window over a 30-day window?

**Answer:** A 7-day window reacts faster to changes. If you deploy a bad change, the budget impact is visible within days instead of weeks. The budget also recovers faster — a good week of reliability can restore the budget. However, a 7-day window is more volatile. A single bad day consumes 14% of the budget instead of 3%. The choice depends on your deployment frequency and risk tolerance. Fast-moving teams prefer shorter windows. Mature systems with stable SRE practices prefer longer windows.

## Common Pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: Confusing Availability with Latency

A common mistake is to define the SLO only in terms of binary availability (up or down) while ignoring latency. A system that returns HTTP 200 responses after 5 seconds is technically "available" but provides a terrible user experience. Your SLI must capture the latency dimension.

**Fix:** Define your SLI as "requests that complete successfully in under N milliseconds," not just "requests that return a non-error status code."

### Pitfall 2: Setting the SLO Too Tight

Teams often set aggressive SLOs to impress stakeholders, only to discover that they cannot meet them. A 99.99% SLO requires significant engineering investment in redundancy, failover, and monitoring. If your team is not ready for that investment, start with 99.9% and tighten over time.

**Fix:** Start with a realistic SLO based on your current reliability. Measure your actual availability for a month, then set the SLO at half a nine tighter.

### Pitfall 3: Ignoring the Cost of Measurement

Tracking SLIs and error budgets is not free. Every metric requires instrumenting code, storing data, and maintaining dashboards. For a small service with 10,000 requests per day, the cost of a sophisticated error budget system may exceed the cost of the occasional downtime.

**Fix:** Match your error budget sophistication to your service's criticality. A payment service needs full instrumentation. An internal admin tool can use a simpler approach.

### Pitfall 4: Treating Budget Exhaustion as a Failure

Error budget exhaustion is not a failure. It is information. The budget was designed to be consumed. If you never exhaust the budget, your SLO may be too loose. The goal is not to preserve the budget at all costs — it is to use the budget as a signal for when to be more careful.

**Fix:** Celebrate budget exhaustion as a learning opportunity. Conduct a blameless postmortem. Ask: "What can we do to make our system more resilient so we do not need to exhaust the budget next month?"

## Common Questions

### How do I choose the right SLO?

Start with user expectations. Measure the latency and error rate that your users actually experience. Set the SLO slightly above your current performance (tight enough to improve, but not so tight that it is impossible to meet). Then tighten the SLO over time as your system matures.

### How do I handle multiple services with different SLOs?

Each service gets its own error budget. The composite user-facing experience is the product of the individual SLOs. If Service A has 99.9% and Service B has 99.9%, the composite is 99.8% (0.999 * 0.999 = 0.998). You need to account for this multiplication when setting individual SLOs.

### Can I have a latency SLO and an availability SLO?

Yes. Many services have multiple SLOs. The payment service might have:
- Availability SLO: 99.99% of requests return a non-5xx response
- Latency SLO: 99.9% of requests complete in under 500ms

Each SLO has its own error budget. A deployment that makes the system slower but not unavailable would consume the latency budget but not the availability budget.

### What happens to the budget when a service is down for planned maintenance?

This depends on your policy. If you exclude planned maintenance from the budget, you must clearly define what counts as "planned" and enforce the definition. If you include it, you incentivize teams to minimize maintenance windows. The industry best practice is to include planned maintenance in the budget, with the exception of emergency maintenance (security patches, critical infrastructure updates).

### How do I explain error budgets to non-technical stakeholders?

Use the bank account analogy. "We have a reliability budget of 43 minutes per month. Every outage, slow deployment, or database migration makes a withdrawal. When the budget is empty, we stop deploying new features until the budget recovers. This helps us balance reliability and speed."
