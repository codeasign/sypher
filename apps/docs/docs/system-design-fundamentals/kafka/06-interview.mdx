Kafka appears in system design interviews in two forms: as the central technology in a "design a messaging system" question, and as a building block in broader architecture questions such as "design a real-time analytics pipeline" or "design a ride-hailing platform." This page provides a structured approach to both scenarios.

## Question Types

Kafka interview questions fall into five categories.

### Category 1: Design a Messaging System

The interviewer asks: "Design a distributed messaging system similar to Kafka."

**What they are evaluating:**
- Understanding of distributed systems fundamentals (replication, partitioning, consensus).
- Ability to reason about trade-offs (ordering vs. parallelism, durability vs. throughput).
- Knowledge of Kafka's internal architecture.

**High-level approach:**

1. **Start with the core abstraction**: "The system is an append-only log. Producers write to the end. Consumers read from any position."

2. **Define the data model**: "Records are organized into topics. Topics are split into partitions for parallelism. Each record within a partition has a sequential offset."

3. **Explain partitioning**: "Partitions are the unit of parallelism. Records with the same key go to the same partition, preserving order. Partitions are distributed across brokers."

4. **Describe replication**: "Each partition has a replication factor. One replica is the leader; the rest are followers. The leader handles all reads and writes. Followers replicate from the leader."

5. **Discuss consumer groups**: "Consumers in a group share the work of reading partitions. Each partition is assigned to exactly one consumer in the group."

6. **Address durability**: "Writes go to the leader's log. With acks=all, the leader waits for ISR followers to acknowledge before responding to the producer."

7. **Cover the hardest part — exactly-once semantics**: "Combine idempotent producers (dedup by sequence number) with transactional producers (atomic commits across partitions) and read-committed consumers."

**Traps to avoid:**
- Claiming global ordering. Kafka provides ordering within a partition, not across partitions.
- Claiming exactly-once delivery without qualifying the conditions. EOS requires idempotent producers, transactions, and read-committed consumers.
- Ignoring the failure case. What happens when a broker fails? When a controller fails? The interviewer wants to hear about leader election, ISR management, and unclean leader election.

### Category 2: Design a Real-Time Analytics Pipeline

The interviewer asks: "Design a real-time analytics system that processes 1 million events per second."

**What they are evaluating:**
- System design breadth.
- Integration thinking — how different components fit together.
- Understanding of event processing architectures.

**High-level approach:**

1. **Ingest with Kafka**: "Producers write events to Kafka topics. Partition by event type (user actions, page views, errors)."

2. **Process with Kafka Streams or Flink**: "Stream processing jobs aggregate events into time windows — count per page per minute, error rates per service."

3. **Store results**: "Write aggregated results to a database optimized for time-series queries — ClickHouse, Druid, or Elasticsearch."

4. **Serve to dashboards**: "Dashboards query the time-series database directly. No dashboard queries hit Kafka."

5. **Handle the batch path**: "Raw events are also written to S3 or HDFS for batch processing via Spark or Presto."

**Key decisions to discuss:**
- Partition count: "One million events per second at 1 KB each is 1 GB/s. With 10 partitions handling 100 MB/s each, the system needs at least 3 brokers with RF=3."
- Retention: "3-7 days for stream processing. 30 days archived in S3."
- Consumer lag management: "Monitor records-lag-max. Alert if lag exceeds 10,000 records."
- Backpressure: "If the stream processor falls behind, increase partitions and consumer parallelism. If the database is the bottleneck, batch writes."

### Category 3: Design a Notification System

The interviewer asks: "Design a notification system that delivers push notifications, emails, and SMS to users."

**What they are evaluating:**
- Understanding of fan-out patterns.
- Handling of different delivery guarantees per channel.
- Retry and dead-letter handling.

**High-level approach:**

1. **Use Kafka as the backbone**: "The notification service produces events to a `notifications` topic. Downstream services consume from this topic based on channel."

2. **Fan-out via consumer groups**: "The push-notification consumer, email consumer, and SMS consumer each have their own consumer group. Each group reads all notifications independently."

3. **Handle channel-specific requirements**: "Push notifications have low latency tolerance (less than 1 second). Email can tolerate minutes. SMS has rate limits from providers."

4. **Dead-letter queue**: "Failed deliveries go to a `notifications-dead-letter` topic. A separate process retries with exponential backoff."

**Traps to avoid:**
- Not discussing idempotency: If the email consumer processes the same notification twice, the user gets two emails. The consumer must deduplicate by notification ID.
- Ignoring rate limits: SMS providers limit to 10-100 messages per second. The consumer must respect this limit.

### Category 4: Design a Ride-Hailing Platform

The interviewer asks: "Design Uber's backend."

**What they are evaluating:**
- Handling of high-frequency location updates.
- Geospatial matching at scale.
- Real-time event processing.

**Kafka's role:**

- **Driver location updates**: 10,000+ drivers send location updates every 3-5 seconds. Kafka ingests these into a `driver-locations` topic with many partitions (partition by driver ID or region).
- **Ride requests**: Produced to a `ride-requests` topic. Dispatch consumers match requests with nearby drivers.
- **Trip events**: Start, end, payment — each produces to a `trip-events` topic for downstream processing (surge pricing, driver ratings).

**Key discussion points:**
- Partition count: "Location updates for 50,000 drivers at 100 bytes per update is 5 MB/s. Partition by geographic region to keep related drivers together."
- Latency: "Dispatch requires under 100 ms. Use acks=1 for location updates (some loss is acceptable) and acks=all for trip events (no loss)."

### Category 5: Design a Log Aggregation Pipeline

The interviewer asks: "Design a system that collects logs from 10,000 servers and makes them searchable within 10 seconds."

**What they are evaluating:**
- Understanding of high-throughput ingestion.
- Integration of Kafka with search infrastructure.
- Handling of schema diversity across services.

**High-level approach:**

1. **Install log shippers**: Deploy Filebeat or Fluentd on each server. Each shipper tails log files and produces them to a Kafka topic. Shippers batch 100-500 lines or 1 MB, whichever comes first.

2. **Ingest with Kafka**: One or more Kafka topics receive all logs. Partition by `server_id` or `service_name` to preserve ordering within a log file. With 10,000 servers at 100 KB/s each, the total ingress is 1,000 MB/s. Use 30-50 partitions to distribute across 10+ brokers.

3. **Normalize and transform**: A stream processing job reads from Kafka, parses each log line into a structured record (timestamp, severity, service, message), and writes to a structured topic.

4. **Index in Elasticsearch**: A Kafka Connect Elasticsearch sink reads from the structured topic and indexes logs. Use time-based indices (`logs-2026-07-02`) for efficient retention management.

5. **Serve via Kibana**: Users search through Kibana, which queries Elasticsearch. The 10-second SLA requires Elasticsearch to index within 5 seconds and queries to complete within 5 seconds.

**Key decision points:**
- Schema management: Each service may use a different log format. The stream processor must handle multiple formats. Use a structured format like JSON in the normalized topic, with a schema field indicating the log format version.
- Retention: Kafka retention of 30 minutes for raw logs (sufficient for consumer recovery). Elasticsearch retention of 7 days for hot data, 30 days for warm data.
- Error handling: Malformed log lines go to a dead-letter topic and are indexed separately for manual inspection.

### Category 6: Kafka Migration and Upgrade

The interviewer asks: "You need to upgrade Kafka from version 2.8 to 3.5 with minimal downtime. Describe the process."

**What they are evaluating:**
- Understanding of rolling upgrades.
- Knowledge of compatibility between versions.
- Rollback planning.

**High-level approach:**

1. **Verify compatibility**: Kafka 3.5 supports upgrading from 2.8 directly (check the upgrade matrix on the Kafka website). The message format is compatible. The protocol is backward compatible but not forward compatible — old clients may not work with new brokers.

2. **Update all clients first**: Upgrade producer, consumer, and Streams libraries to 3.5 before upgrading brokers. Test clients against a staging cluster running 3.5. This ensures that once brokers are upgraded, no client compatibility issues arise.

3. **Rolling broker upgrade**: Update one broker at a time:
   - Gracefully shut down the broker.
   - Upgrade the Kafka binary.
   - Update the broker configuration for any new required settings.
   - Restart the broker.
   - Verify that the broker rejoins the cluster and partitions are replicated.
   - Wait 5 minutes before upgrading the next broker.

4. **Post-upgrade steps**: 
   - Update the message format version if required (not needed for compatible upgrades).
   - Enable new features (for example, KRaft migration if planned).
   - Update monitoring dashboards for any new metrics.

5. **Rollback plan**: If a critical issue is found:
   - Keep the previous binary on each broker.
   - Roll back one broker at a time in the same rolling manner.
   - Downgrade client libraries if the new version introduced incompatibilities.

**Common migration pitfalls:**
- Upgrading brokers before clients: old clients may not work with new protocol versions.
- Skipping intermediate versions: some versions require an incremental upgrade path.
- Ignoring ZooKeeper compatibility: ZooKeeper version must be compatible with the new Kafka version.
- Not testing with production traffic volume in staging.

### Category 7: Troubleshooting Scenarios

The interviewer describes a production problem and asks you to diagnose it.

**Common scenarios:**

| Scenario | Likely Cause | Question to Ask |
|---|---|---|
| Consumers not receiving messages | Consumer group not subscribed to topic, offset at latest, or group ID mismatch | "What is the consumer group name and what are the subscribed topics?" |
| High produce latency | acks=all with slow follower, disk I/O bottleneck, or GC pause | "What is the ISR state? What is the broker's GC pause time?" |
| Consumer lag growing | Consumer processing too slow, too few consumers, or downstream slow | "What is the processing time per record? How many consumers are in the group?" |
| Broker crashing repeatedly | Disk full, memory pressure, or configuration mismatch | "What does the broker log show about the last crash? What is the disk usage?" |
| Rebalance happening frequently | session.timeout.ms too low, consumer processing too slow, or network issues | "What are the consumer timeout settings? Are there network connectivity issues?" |

## Expectations by Level

Interviewers evaluate Kafka knowledge differently based on seniority.

### Entry Level (0-2 Years)

**Expectation**: Understand the basic concepts — producer, consumer, topic, partition, offset.

**Questions they might ask:**
- "What is Kafka and what problem does it solve?"
- "How does a consumer group work?"
- "What is a partition and why is it important?"

**Response standards:**
- Correctly define 8+ terms from the core vocabulary.
- Explain the publish-subscribe model.
- Describe a simple use case (log aggregation, metrics collection).
- Do NOT need to know about ISR, replication protocol, or performance tuning.

### Mid Level (2-5 Years)

**Expectation**: Can design with Kafka, understand configuration trade-offs, and handle common failure scenarios.

**Questions they might ask:**
- "How would you design a real-time event processing pipeline?"
- "What is the difference between acks=0, acks=1, and acks=all?"
- "How does consumer rebalancing work?"
- "What is consumer lag and how do you monitor it?"

**Response standards:**
- Choose appropriate configuration for the workload.
- Explain the rebalance protocol (eager vs. cooperative).
- Describe monitoring approach for consumer lag.
- Discuss partition count sizing.

### Senior Level (5-8 Years)

**Expectation**: Deep understanding of Kafka internals, performance tuning, production operations, and alternative technologies.

**Questions they might ask:**
- "Design a distributed messaging system — walk me through the replication protocol."
- "How would you migrate from ZooKeeper to KRaft?"
- "Compare Kafka, Pulsar, and Redpanda. When would you choose each?"
- "Design a multi-region Kafka deployment."

**Response standards:**
- Explain the ISR protocol in detail.
- Discuss consistency trade-offs (clean vs. unclean leader election).
- Provide concrete capacity planning numbers.
- Compare alternatives with specific trade-offs.

### Staff+ Level (8+ Years)

**Expectation**: Can design the system from scratch, reason about edge cases, and anticipate failure modes that the interviewer has not considered.

**Questions they might ask:**
- "Design a system that guarantees exactly-once processing at 10 million events per second."
- "How would you implement Kafka's storage layer if you were building it today?"
- "Design a multi-tenant Kafka cluster for 100 teams with different SLAs."

**Response standards:**
- Propose novel solutions to known Kafka limitations.
- Discuss trade-offs the interviewer has not considered.
- Reference relevant literature or prior art.
- Provide quantitative analysis of design decisions.

## Phrase Bank

These phrases communicate Kafka knowledge effectively during interviews.

### For Explaining Partitioning

"Partitions are the unit of parallelism in Kafka. A partition is an ordered, immutable sequence of records. Records within a partition are totally ordered — the offset guarantees this. Across partitions, there is no order guarantee. This trade-off enables horizontal scaling."

### For Explaining Replication

"Kafka uses a leader-follower replication model. Each partition has one leader and N-1 followers. The leader handles all produce and consume requests. Followers replicate from the leader asynchronously. A follower that falls behind is removed from the in-sync replica set. This provides a configurable trade-off between durability and throughput."

### For Explaining Durability

"Kafka's durability is configurable through the acks parameter and replication factor. With acks=all and a replication factor of 3, the system can survive two broker failures without data loss. The trade-off is higher latency — the leader must wait for acknowledgment from at least two nodes before responding."

### For Discussing Trade-Offs

"Kafka optimizes for high-throughput, durable, ordered delivery within partitions. The trade-off is operational complexity — you need to manage brokers, monitor consumer lag, and plan capacity. It is not a database, not a job queue, and not suitable for sub-millisecond delivery."

### For Handling Failure Questions

"The most common failure mode in Kafka is consumer lag — consumers falling behind the produce rate. Monitoring records-lag-max and alerting when lag exceeds a threshold is the first line of defense. The second line is configuring retention long enough to recover from extended consumer downtime."

### For Multi-Region Questions

"For multi-region replication, MirrorMaker 2 provides active-passive replication using Kafka Connect. Confluent's Cluster Linking provides low-latency replication with richer semantics. The fundamental challenge is cross-region latency — 50-200 ms of replication lag is unavoidable."

## Red Flags

These statements signal weak Kafka knowledge during an interview.

"Kafka guarantees exactly-once delivery." This is true only with idempotent producers, transactions, and read-committed consumers. Without these, Kafka provides at-least-once delivery.

"Kafka is ordered." Ordering is per-partition, not global. If you need global ordering, you must use a single partition, which eliminates parallelism.

"Kafka is a database." Kafka is a log. It does not support indexes, queries, or point lookups. Conflating Kafka with a database indicates lack of understanding.

"More partitions are always better." Each partition adds overhead — file descriptors, memory, rebalance time. The optimal partition count balances throughput requirements against operational overhead.

"Kafka can replace my message queue." Kafka is optimized for streaming, not for individual job processing. If each message requires complex routing, per-message acknowledgment, or delayed delivery, a message queue (RabbitMQ, SQS) is more appropriate.

## Common Trap Questions

Interviewers use trap questions to expose gaps in understanding. Here are three common traps and how to handle them.

### Trap 1: "Kafka guarantees exactly-once delivery."

**Why it is a trap**: This statement is technically incorrect without qualification. Kafka provides exactly-once semantics only when the producer is idempotent and the consumer uses read_committed isolation. Even then, exactly-once is between Kafka and the consumer — the consumer's side effects (database writes, API calls) are not covered by Kafka's guarantees.

**Correct response**: "Kafka provides exactly-once delivery between the producer and the consumer when the producer is configured with `enable.idempotence=true` and the consumer uses `isolation.level=read_committed`. However, the consumer's external side effects are not covered by this guarantee. The consumer must implement idempotent processing or use Kafka's transactional API to achieve end-to-end exactly-once semantics."

### Trap 2: "If you need ordering, use a single partition."

**Why it is a trap**: A single partition is a valid answer for ordering, but it eliminates parallelism. The interviewer wants to see if you understand the trade-off and can propose alternatives.

**Correct response**: "A single partition guarantees global ordering but limits throughput to what a single partition can handle, typically 10-50 MB/s. If you need ordering for a subset of records — for example, all records for the same user — you can use key-based partitioning to send related records to the same partition while maintaining parallelism across unrelated records. If you need true global ordering at high throughput, you need to question whether ordering is a hard requirement or a nice-to-have. Many systems that claim to need global ordering actually need per-key ordering."

### Trap 3: "Kafka is slow because it writes to disk."

**Why it is a trap**: This reveals a misunderstanding of Kafka's performance characteristics. Kafka's sequential writes are faster than random writes on any storage medium.

**Correct response**: "Kafka's sequential writes are one of its key performance advantages. Sequential writes to NVMe are 10-50x faster than random writes. Additionally, Kafka's writes go to the OS page cache, not directly to disk. The page cache provides sub-millisecond access for recently written data. The disk flush happens asynchronously. This means producers see write latency of 1-5 ms, not the 50-200 ms you would expect from a synchronous disk write."

## Advanced Response Patterns

For senior-level interviews, these response patterns demonstrate deeper understanding.

### Pattern 1: The "It Depends" Framing

When asked about a Kafka design decision, start with "It depends on the workload characteristics and requirements." Then enumerate the trade-offs:

"For example, if latency is the primary concern (under 10 ms), you should use acks=1 and a small batch size. If durability is the primary concern (financial transactions), you should use acks=all with min.insync.replicas=2. If throughput is the primary concern (log aggregation), you should use acks=0 and large batches with compression."

### Pattern 2: The Failure-Driven Design

When designing a system with Kafka, explicitly discuss failure modes:

"The system I am designing must handle the following failure modes: broker failure, consumer failure, controller failure, and network partition. For broker failure, the system relies on ISR-based leader election. For consumer failure, the system relies on consumer group rebalancing. For controller failure, the system relies on ZooKeeper or KRaft quorum. For network partitions, the system relies on the ISR mechanism to maintain consistency."

### Pattern 3: The Quantitative Anchor

Ground your answers in concrete numbers. Instead of saying "Kafka is fast," say:

"Kafka achieves 200-500 MB/s of sequential write throughput per broker on NVMe hardware. A single broker can handle 10,000-50,000 produce requests per second. End-to-end latency with acks=1 is 2-5 ms within a data center. With acks=all, latency increases to 5-15 ms depending on the number of ISR replicas and their network distance."

## Drills

Practice these drills to build fluency.

### Drill 1: Partition Math

You have a topic with 24 partitions and a consumer group with 6 consumers. Each consumer processes 1000 records per second. Each record is 2 KB.

- What is the maximum throughput of the consumer group? (6 consumers x 1000 records/s x 2 KB = 12 MB/s)
- What happens if you add 6 more consumers? (12 consumers consume 24 partitions at 2 partitions each — throughput remains 12 MB/s because total processing capacity increased but partition throughput is the bottleneck)
- What happens if a consumer fails? (The group rebalances. Each of the remaining 5 consumers now handles 4-5 partitions. The group processes at 10 MB/s until the failed consumer is replaced.)

### Drill 2: Configuration for a Critical Workload

You are designing a Kafka deployment for a financial transaction system. Requirements: zero data loss, p99 latency under 50 ms, throughput of 50,000 transactions per second at 1 KB each.

- What is the acks setting? (acks=all)
- What is the minimum replication factor? (3, with min.insync.replicas=2)
- How many partitions? (50,000 x 1 KB = 50 MB/s. At 10 MB/s per partition, 5 partitions minimum. With 5 consumers for redundancy, 10 partitions.)
- How many brokers? (RF=3 with 10 partitions equals 30 replica assignments. With 3 brokers, each broker handles 10 replicas. 3 brokers minimum, 6 for better load distribution.)
- What compression? (snappy — good balance of compression ratio and CPU usage.)
- What batch size and linger.ms? (32 KB and 10 ms to balance latency and throughput.)

### Drill 3: Explaining the Rebalance Protocol

Describe consumer group rebalancing in 5 sentences:

"Each consumer group has a group coordinator, which is one of the brokers. The coordinator monitors consumer heartbeats. When a consumer joins or leaves, the coordinator triggers a rebalance. During the rebalance, the coordinator assigns partitions to consumers. The cooperative rebalancing protocol reassigns only the affected partitions rather than stopping all consumers."

### Drill 4: Diagnose Consumer Lag

A team reports that their Kafka consumer falls behind by 1 million records every hour during peak traffic. The consumer processes events and writes to a database. Peak throughput is 10,000 records/second. Average record size is 5 KB. The consumer group has 3 consumers. The topic has 6 partitions.

**Possible causes:**

- The database write is the bottleneck. With 3 consumers writing to a single database, the database connection pool may be exhausted.
- The consumer is doing too much per record — multiple database queries, API calls, or heavy computation.
- The consumer group has 3 consumers but 6 partitions. Each consumer handles 2 partitions. This is fine unless one consumer handles a hot partition.

**Diagnosis steps:**

1. Check consumer processing time per record (from consumer metrics).
2. Check database write throughput.
3. Check if any partition has significantly more records than others.
4. Check for consumer rebalances during peak hours.

**Possible solutions:**

1. If the database is the bottleneck: batch writes to the database (collect 100 records, write once).
2. If consumer processing is slow: scale consumers to 6 (one per partition).
3. If a hot partition exists: re-partition the topic or re-key the data.

### Drill 5: End-to-End Design

Design a system that:

- Ingests 100,000 events per second from 50 services.
- Routes events to different processing pipelines based on event type.
- Supports replay — consumers can re-read events from the last 14 days.
- Provides at-least-once delivery with no duplicates.
- Handles processing backpressure without data loss.

**5-minute design:**

| Component | Decision | Rationale |
|-----------|----------|-----------|
| Kafka cluster | 3 brokers, RF=3 | 100,000 events at 2 KB = 200 MB/s. 3 brokers distribute leader load. RF=3 survives one broker failure. |
| Partitions | 20 partitions | 10 MB/s per partition target. 20 partitions supports 20 consumers for parallelism. |
| Topics | Single raw-events topic | One topic simplifies routing. Event type is a field in the record. |
| Retention | 14 days, size-capped at 5 TB per broker | 200 MB/s x 86400 x 14 = 242 TB total. At RF=3 with 3 brokers, 242 TB. With 2:1 compression, 121 TB — 40 TB per broker. 5 TB per broker is too low; adjust to 50 TB per broker or reduce retention. |
| Routing | Kafka Streams or Flink | Reads from raw-events, filters by event type, produces to pipeline-specific topics. |
| Deduplication | Idempotent producers + consumer dedup by event ID | Idempotent producers prevent broker-level duplicates. Consumer-level dedup (storing processed event IDs) prevents at-least-once duplicates. |
| Backpressure | Buffer for 5 minutes of data in Kafka | 200 MB/s x 300 = 60 GB of buffer. Retention provides the remaining buffer. Consumer lag monitoring triggers alert at 50,000 records. |

### Drill 6: Budgeting End-to-End Latency

You are designing a real-time ad-serving platform. The SLA requires that a user click is processed and the resulting action appears within 500 ms. The pipeline is:

1. User click event transmitted via HTTP to the event ingestion service.
2. Event service serializes and produces to Kafka.
3. Kafka broker appends and replicates.
4. Stream processor consumes, enriches, and produces a result.
5. Result consumer writes to a serving cache.

**Step 1: Budget the 500 ms across stages.**

| Stage | Budget | Notes |
|-------|--------|-------|
| Network (client to event service) | 10 ms | Same region, so 2-10 ms is realistic. |
| Event service serialization + produce | 20 ms | `linger.ms=20` enables batching. |
| Kafka broker append + replicate (acks=1) | 5 ms | Leader append to page cache. |
| Network (broker to stream processor) | 5 ms | Consumer poll returns immediately. |
| Stream processor deserialize + process | 200 ms | Enrichment and business logic. |
| Stream processor produce result | 5 ms | Write to result topic. |
| Network (result to serving cache) | 10 ms | Write to Redis or Memcached. |
| **Total budget used** | **255 ms** | 245 ms headroom under the 500 ms SLA. |

**Step 2: Identify where the budget would break.**

- Cross-region latency: If the user is in Europe and the Kafka cluster is in US-East, add 80 ms for each network hop. The total becomes 255 + 70 (additional cross-region hops) = 325 ms. Still under 500 ms but headroom is reduced to 175 ms.
- Consumer lag: If the stream processor is behind, it reads old data while fresh data sits in Kafka. Every second of lag adds 1 second to end-to-end latency. Lag must stay under 200 ms to meet the SLA.
- Processing spikes: If ML inference takes 400 ms during a traffic spike, the total exceeds 500 ms. The system needs a fast path (simpler fallback model) and a slow path (full model).

**Step 3: Design for budget enforcement.**

- Add a timeout in the stream processor. If processing takes longer than 450 ms, fall back to a cached result or a degraded response.
- Monitor p99 latency per stage. If any stage consistently exceeds its budget, scale that stage independently.
- Implement circuit breakers on downstream dependencies. If the ML inference service is slow, use a default prediction.

### Drill 7: Handling a Poison Pill Message

A consumer in the `order-processor` group crashes every time it encounters a specific record. The record has malformed JSON that causes deserialization to fail. Each time the consumer restarts, it reads from the last committed offset, processes the same bad record, and crashes again.

**Immediate fix (stop the bleeding):**

1. Stop the consumer from automatically restarting (disable the restart policy in the deployment system).
2. Find the problematic offset from the consumer group state: `kafka-consumer-groups.sh --describe --group order-processor`.
3. Skip the problematic offset: `kafka-consumer-groups.sh --reset-offsets --group order-processor --topic orders --to-offset <offset+1> --execute`.
4. Restart the consumer. It resumes from the offset after the bad record.
5. The bad record remains in the topic and will be deleted by retention.

**Permanent fix (prevent recurrence):**

1. Wrap deserialization in a try-catch block.
2. On deserialization failure, log the record key, offset, partition, and error details.
3. Write the failing record to a dead-letter topic: `orders-dlt`.
4. Commit the current offset and continue processing the next record.
5. Monitor the dead-letter topic for manual inspection and reprocessing.

**Example consumer code pattern:**

```java
while (true) {
    ConsumerRecords<String, String> records = consumer.poll(Duration.ofMillis(100));
    for (ConsumerRecord<String, String> record : records) {
        try {
            Order order = objectMapper.readValue(record.value(), Order.class);
            processOrder(order);
        } catch (JsonProcessingException e) {
            // Send failing record to dead-letter topic
            dltProducer.send(
                new ProducerRecord<>("orders-dlt", record.key(), record.value())
            );
            logger.warn("Malformed order record sent to DLT: partition={} offset={} key={}",
                record.partition(), record.offset(), record.key());
        }
    }
    consumer.commitSync();
}
```

### Drill 8: Explain the Flow of Data During a Broker Addition

A 6-broker Kafka cluster is scaled to 8 brokers. A topic with 120 partitions and RF=3 needs to be rebalanced to use the new brokers.

**Step-by-step explanation:**

1. **Add the new brokers to the cluster.** Install Kafka on two new servers, configure them with unique broker IDs, and start them. The controller detects the new brokers and adds them to the cluster metadata.

2. **Generate a partition reassignment plan.** Use `kafka-reassign-partitions.sh --generate` with a topics-to-move JSON file. The tool outputs a reassignment plan that distributes partitions across all 8 brokers.

3. **Review the reassignment plan.** Check that: (a) each partition has 3 replicas, (b) no broker has more than 10% more replicas than the average, and (c) replicas are on different racks if rack awareness is configured.

4. **Execute the reassignment.** Apply the plan with `kafka-reassign-partitions.sh --execute`. The controller starts moving partition replicas to the new brokers.

5. **Monitor the reassignment.** The reassignment adds replicas to the new brokers and removes replicas from the old brokers. During replication, the old replicas continue serving. The reassignment rate is limited by `num.replica.fetchers` and network bandwidth.

6. **Verify completion.** Use `kafka-reassign-partitions.sh --verify`. The command shows that all partitions are fully replicated and preferred replicas are balanced.

**Impact on production during reassignment:**

- Network bandwidth increases by 10-30% due to cross-broker replication.
- CPU usage on existing brokers increases as they serve data to the new brokers.
- No downtime — clients continue producing and consuming normally.
- Leadership may temporarily shift during reassignment, causing brief unavailability of individual partitions.

## Preparation Strategy

How to prepare for a Kafka interview based on your timeline.

### One Week Before the Interview

**Days 1-2: Core concepts.** Review the vocabulary list from this section. You should be able to define producer, consumer, consumer group, partition, offset, broker, topic, replication, ISR, and retention without hesitation. These terms are the building blocks of every Kafka answer. Practice explaining each term in one sentence.

**Days 3-4: Configuration and trade-offs.** Practice explaining the trade-offs between different `acks` settings, replication factors, and retention policies. For each workload scenario from the decision matrix, be able to recommend a configuration and justify it. Quiz yourself on what happens when you change one parameter — for example, "What happens if I change `acks` from 1 to all without changing anything else?"

**Day 5: Failure modes.** Learn the six failure modes and five anti-patterns. For each one, describe the sequence of events, the impact on the system, and the recovery steps. Practice explaining failure mode 3 (consumer lag) in under 1 minute.

**Day 6: System design.** Practice two design scenarios — one messaging-system-focused (design a system like Kafka) and one integration-focused (design a real-time analytics pipeline). Walk through each one aloud. Record yourself if possible to check for unclear explanations.

**Day 7: Drills.** Run through the drills in this section. Time yourself. Each drill should take 2-5 minutes to answer. If a drill takes longer, break it down and simplify your explanation.

### Day-of-Interview Checklist

- Bring three concrete numbers: throughput per broker (200-500 MB/s), latency range (2-15 ms), and message size tipping point (1 KB typical for benchmarking).
- Know the one diagram you would draw on the whiteboard: producers writing to partitioned topics, ISR replication, consumers in groups reading from partitions.
- Have one production story ready: "At my previous company, we used Kafka for X and encountered Y. We fixed it by Z." A real story demonstrates operational experience that no amount of theory can replace.

### What Interviewers Look For

Interviewers evaluating Kafka knowledge prioritize these signals, in order:

1. **Understanding of the core abstraction**: Can you explain why the append-only log is the foundation of Kafka's performance? Do you understand the trade-off between ordering (within a partition) and parallelism (across partitions)?

2. **Configuration awareness**: Do you know the important configuration parameters and their impact? Can you choose between `acks=0`, `acks=1`, and `acks=all` with a clear rationale for each?

3. **Failure mode knowledge**: Can you describe what happens when a broker fails? When a consumer crashes? When a disk fills? Do you know the recovery sequence without guessing?

4. **Practical experience**: Have you operated Kafka in production? Can you reference real scenarios — a rebalance storm, a consumer lag incident, a storage emergency? Do you know monitoring metrics and alert thresholds?

5. **Comparison with alternatives**: Can you explain when to choose Kafka vs. Pulsar vs. Redpanda vs. a message queue? Do you understand the trade-offs without bias toward one technology?
