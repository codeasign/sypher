# Capacity Estimation Concepts

## The Problem Story

A team at a mid-size e-commerce company deploys a new recommendation service on a Friday afternoon. They spin up two servers, each with 4 CPU cores and 16 GB of RAM. Within three minutes of going live, both servers are at 100% CPU. Within seven minutes, the service is completely unresponsive. Users see 5xx errors. The on-call engineer has to scramble, triple the server count, and restart. Post-mortem analysis reveals they never estimated how many queries per second the service would handle.

A different startup builds a video-processing pipeline. They provision 200 GPU instances "to be safe" before they have a single customer. Their cloud bill runs $87,000 per month. Three months later, they have 12 paying customers. They burned through 40% of their seed funding on idle compute.

An engineer sits in a System Design interview at Google. The interviewer asks: "Design YouTube. How many servers do you need for the upload pipeline? How much storage for one day of uploads? What's the bandwidth cost?" The engineer knows the features. They know video codecs, CDNs, and database schemas. But they freeze on the numbers. They have no framework for estimation.

These three stories share one root cause: nobody did the math on paper before building or buying infrastructure.

## The Insight

Capacity estimation is back-of-envelope math to predict how much infrastructure a system needs. You do it with rough numbers, simple arithmetic, and a few key assumptions. The goal is not perfect precision — it is a fast, credible answer within a factor of 2.

Think of it like planning a wedding. You start with the guest count. Each guest eats 3 plates of appetizers and drinks 2 glasses of wine. You multiply: 150 guests x 2 glasses = 300 glasses. One bottle pours 5 glasses. That is 60 bottles. The venue holds 200 people seated, so 150 fits. You do not need to know every guest's exact shoe size or favorite dessert. You just need to know the venue is big enough, the food is enough, and the bar will not run dry.

Cloud infrastructure works the same way. Daily active users (DAU) is your guest count. Queries per second (QPS) is the drink rate. Storage per user is the buffet portion. Bandwidth is the number of delivery trucks. You estimate each dimension with back-of-envelope math, then provision accordingly.

## Core Vocabulary

**DAU (Daily Active Users).** The number of unique users who interact with your system in a 24-hour window. Twitter reported roughly 250 million DAU in 2024. A startup launch might see 10,000 DAU on day one.

**MAU (Monthly Active Users).** The number of unique users in a 30-day window. Typically 3 to 5 times DAU for social products, 1.5 to 2 times for utility tools.

**QPS (Queries Per Second).** The rate of requests hitting your servers. A single database server handles roughly 1,000 to 10,000 simple read QPS. A web server handles 100 to 1,000 QPS per core for dynamic requests.

**Concurrent Users.** Users who have an active connection at the same instant. For real-time systems (chat, gaming), concurrent users matter more than DAU. A chat app with 1 million DAU might have 50,000 concurrent users during peak hour.

**Peak-to-Average Ratio.** Traffic does not arrive evenly. Social media apps see 2x to 5x spikes during evening hours. E-commerce sees 10x to 50x spikes during flash sales. Black Friday traffic can be 100x normal for retailers.

**Storage Per User.** How much data each user generates. A photo-sharing app allocates roughly 5 MB per uploaded photo. A messaging app stores roughly 1 KB per text message. A video platform stores roughly 500 MB per uploaded minute of HD video.

**Bandwidth.** Data transferred over the network per second. Measured in Mbps or Gbps. One 10 Gbps network link handles roughly 1 million requests per second if each request is 1 KB.

**Read-to-Write Ratio.** The number of read requests for every write request. News feeds are roughly 100:1 reads to writes. Sensor data logging is roughly 1:10 reads to writes (more writes than reads). This ratio determines whether you optimize for read throughput or write throughput.

**Cache Hit Ratio.** The percentage of requests served from cache instead of the origin server. A 95% cache hit ratio means only 5% of requests reach your database. Improving from 90% to 95% is roughly halving your database load.

**Headroom.** Extra capacity beyond your estimate to handle unexpected spikes. Standard practice is 30% to 50% headroom for production systems.

**Bottleneck.** The component that limits overall system throughput. A database that handles 5,000 QPS becomes a bottleneck when traffic reaches 6,000 QPS, even if everything else can handle 10,000 QPS.

**Power of Two Estimations.** Knowing rough magnitudes by heart: 2^10 = 1 thousand, 2^20 = 1 million, 2^30 = 1 billion. You use these to convert between units quickly.

**90/10 Rule.** 90% of traffic comes from 10% of users in many systems. A small fraction of power users drives most of the load.

## Step by Step

The capacity estimation process follows eight steps. Each step narrows the estimate and adds a concrete number.

### Step 1: Define the User Base

Start with the number of users the system serves. If the product already exists, use analytics data. If it is a new product, use market size multiplied by adoption assumptions.

```
50 million total addressable users (TAM)
x 20% adoption in year one
= 10 million registered users

10 million registered users
x 30% daily active rate
= 3 million DAU
```

### Step 2: Estimate Traffic Per User

Figure out what each user does in a session. A social media user views 20 posts, makes 1 post, sends 3 messages. A video platform user watches 5 videos, searches 2 times, uploads 0.1 videos.

### Step 3: Calculate Average QPS

Multiply DAU by actions per user per day, then divide by seconds in a day. Use 100,000 seconds for rough mental math (actually 86,400).

```
3 million DAU
x 31 actions per user per day
= 93 million actions per day

93 million / 86,400 seconds
= approximately 1,076 average QPS
```

Round to 1,000 QPS for planning.

### Step 4: Apply Peak Multiplier

Traffic is not flat. Apply a peak-to-average ratio. Social media sites typically see 2x to 3x peak. E-commerce during holidays sees 10x to 50x peak.

```
1,000 average QPS
x 3 peak multiplier
= 3,000 peak QPS
```

Design for 3,000 QPS, but have auto-scaling up to 5,000 QPS.

### Step 5: Estimate Server Count

Each server handles a certain number of requests per second. A typical application server with 4 CPU cores handles 500 to 1,000 dynamic requests per second. Divide peak QPS by per-server capacity.

```
3,000 peak QPS
/ 750 QPS per server
= 4 application servers

Add 50% headroom
= 6 application servers
```

### Step 6: Estimate Storage

Calculate storage per user, multiply by total users, and account for replication. If each user stores 50 MB of data and you have 10 million users:

```
10 million users
x 50 MB per user
= 500 million MB = 500 TB

3x replication for durability
= 1.5 PB raw storage
```

### Step 7: Estimate Bandwidth

Calculate data transferred per request, multiply by QPS. If each response averages 200 KB and you handle 5,000 peak QPS:

```
5,000 peak QPS
x 200 KB per response
= 1,000,000 KB/s = 1 GB/s = 8 Gbps
```

A 10 Gbps network link handles this with headroom.

### Step 8: Add Headroom and Growth

Never provision exactly to your estimate. Add headroom for traffic spikes and growth. Standard practice is 30% to 50% headroom plus quarterly growth allowance.

```
6 application servers (current estimate)
x 1.5 headroom factor
= 9 servers deployed now

Expected 30% quarterly growth
9 x 1.3 = 12 servers needed next quarter
```

## Variants

### Top-Down Estimation

Start with the total user population and work down to individual resources. This approach is best when you know the market size.

**Formula:**
```
TAM x adoption % = total users
Total users x DAU % = DAU
DAU x actions/user = total daily actions
Total daily actions / seconds = QPS
```

**When to use:** Investor pitches, high-level architecture reviews, interview questions about established products.

### Bottom-Up Estimation

Start with the resources required for a single use case and scale up. This approach works best for new features within an existing system.

**Formula:**
```
Cost per request = (CPU time + memory + storage I/O + network)
Total cost = cost per request x expected request volume
```

**Example:** A new image-resize feature takes 50 ms CPU per image and 10 MB memory per file handle. If you expect 100 concurrent resize operations:

```
100 concurrent operations
x 50 ms CPU each
= 5,000 ms CPU per second = 5 CPU cores

100 concurrent operations
x 10 MB memory each
= 1,000 MB = 1 GB memory
```

You need 5 CPU cores and 1 GB of memory just for this feature.

### Hybrid Estimation

Combine top-down for user traffic estimates and bottom-up for per-operation resource costs. This is the approach used by most FAANG teams.

```
Top-down:
100 million DAU → 58,000 average QPS → 174,000 peak QPS

Bottom-up:
Each read operation costs 10 ms CPU + 5 MB memory
Each write operation costs 50 ms CPU + 20 MB memory + 200 MB disk I/O

Hybrid:
58,000 QPS x 90% reads = 52,200 read QPS
52,200 x 10 ms CPU = 522 CPU seconds / sec = 522 cores

58,000 QPS x 10% writes = 5,800 write QPS
5,800 x 50 ms CPU = 290 CPU seconds / sec = 290 cores

Total: 812 cores → roughly 200 servers (4 cores each)
```

## Mental Model — The Five Questions

When you need to estimate capacity for any system, ask five questions:

**Question 1: How many users?**
Total users, DAU, growth rate. If you know DAU, you have the foundation.

**Question 2: What does each user do?**
Requests per session, session length, actions per action type. Multiply DAU by actions to get total volume.

**Question 3: What is the peak load?**
Apply peak-to-average ratio. Do not design for average. Design for peak plus headroom.

**Question 4: How much data per request?**
Request size, response size, storage per action, log volume per action. Data volume determines bandwidth and storage.

**Question 5: How many resources per request?**
CPU milliseconds, memory per connection, disk I/O per read/write. Multiply by QPS to get total compute.

## Worked Mini-Example: Photo Sharing App

Walk through a complete estimation for a photo-sharing app with 1 million DAU.

**Given:**
- 1 million DAU
- Each user uploads 0.5 photos per day
- Each user views 50 photos per day
- Average photo size: 2 MB (compressed JPEG)
- Response size for photo feed: 200 KB (thumbnails + metadata)
- Peak-to-average ratio: 5x

**Traffic calculation:**

```
Uploads (writes):
  1M DAU x 0.5 = 500,000 uploads/day
  500,000 / 86,400 = 5.8 write QPS average
  5.8 x 5 = 29 write QPS peak

Photo views (reads):
  1M DAU x 50 views = 50M views/day
  50M / 86,400 = 579 read QPS average
  579 x 5 = 2,895 read QPS peak
```

**Server calculation:**

```
Peak total QPS: 29 writes + 2,895 reads = 2,924 QPS
At 750 QPS per server: 2,924 / 750 = 4 servers
+ 30% headroom = 6 application servers
```

**Storage calculation:**

```
Photo storage per day:
  500,000 uploads x 2 MB = 1,000,000 MB = 1,000 GB = 1 TB/day
  1 TB/day x 365 days = 365 TB/year
  3x replication = 1,095 TB/year raw storage

Thumbnail storage (20% of original):
  0.5 photos/user x 0.4 MB = 0.2 MB/user/day
  200 GB/day for thumbnails
```

**Bandwidth calculation:**

```
Upload: 29 peak QPS x 2 MB = 58 MB/s = 464 Mbps inbound
Feed reads: 2,895 peak QPS x 200 KB = 579 MB/s = 4.6 Gbps outbound
Photo full-view reads: 10% of views x 2 MB = 579 MB/s = 4.6 Gbps outbound
Total peak outbound: 9.2 Gbps
```

**Result:**

```
Servers:       6 application + 2 database = 8 servers
Storage:       3+ TB/day, cold storage for older photos
Bandwidth:     10 Gbps link (outbound), 1 Gbps link (inbound)
Monthly cost:  roughly $8,000 to $12,000
```

This mini-example shows how a single estimation exercise produces actionable infrastructure decisions. No guesswork — just math.

### Common Estimation Pitfalls in the Example

The photo-sharing mini-example above contains several assumptions worth examining for sensitivity:

**Assumption 1: Compressed JPEG at 2 MB average.** If your app uses HEIC or WebP, average size drops to 1 MB. Storage halves. If you support RAW photos (10 MB+ per image), storage multiplies by 5x. The encoding decision alone changes your infrastructure cost by 5x.

**Assumption 2: 0.5 photos uploaded per user per day.** This is typical for Instagram-like apps. A document-scanning app would have 0.05 uploads/user/day. A security camera app would have 100+ uploads/user/day. Know your domain.

**Assumption 3: 50 photo views per day.** A feed-based app where users scroll through 50 photos is typical. But if users view 200 photos per day (Pinterest-style browsing), read QPS quadruples. Server count goes from 6 to roughly 18.

**Assumption 4: CDN offloads all image delivery.** If you cannot use a CDN (e.g., for privacy reasons), outbound bandwidth goes from 9.2 Gbps to over 100 Gbps. Infrastructure cost multiplies by 5x to 10x.

Every assumption in an estimate is a lever that can swing the final number by 2x to 10x.

### Estimation by Analogy

When you have no data for a new system, anchor your estimate to a known system and adjust for differences.

```
Known system: Instagram (1 billion DAU, photo sharing)
Target system: New food photo app (estimated 5 million DAU)

Instagram QPS: ~100,000 at 1B DAU
Scale factor: 5M / 1B = 0.005

Estimated QPS for food app: 100,000 x 0.005 = 500 QPS

Adjustments:
  - Food app users post 3x more photos/day (foodies)
  - Food app users share to social media 2x more (external traffic)
  Adjusted QPS: 500 x 3 x 2 = 3,000 QPS

Estimated servers: 3,000 / 750 = 4 servers
```

Analogy works well for interview settings where you reference known systems. "This is roughly Instagram for food, so expect similar QPS per DAU but higher upload frequency."

### The 80/20 Rule in Estimation

80% of the estimation value comes from roughly 20% of the calculation effort. The most critical numbers to get right are:

1. **DAU** — this is the foundation. Everything else multiplies from it.
2. **Peak multiplier** — this determines the top end of your infrastructure.
3. **Storage per user** — this compounds over time and drives the highest long-term cost.

If you only have time for three accurate numbers, pick these three. Errors in per-server QPS, exact response sizes, and connection overhead are less impactful.

## The Estimation Checklist

Before you present any capacity estimate, verify these five items:

**Check 1: Are my DAU assumptions realistic?**
Compare to known products. A new app claiming 100 million DAU in year one without a viral strategy or massive marketing budget is unlikely. Use 1 million to 10 million for a successful startup.

**Check 2: Did I account for peak?**
If you only calculated average QPS, you will be wrong by 3x to 10x. Apply a peak multiplier. Always.

**Check 3: Did I separate reads and writes?**
Read capacity and write capacity are different. Reads use cache and replicas. Writes use primary database and replication bandwidth. Combining them hides the write bottleneck.

**Check 4: Did I include replication overhead?**
Raw storage numbers are not the same as provisioned storage. Add replication factor (typically 3x) and index overhead (20% to 50%) to your disk estimates.

**Check 5: Did I add headroom for growth?**
Systems do not stay at current load. Apply a growth multiplier for the planning horizon. A 12-month horizon with 10% monthly growth means 3.1x capacity at the end of year one.
