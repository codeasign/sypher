## The Problem Story

A senior engineer at a FAANG company sits down for a system design interview. The prompt: "Design a URL shortener." Within thirty seconds, the candidate is drawing boxes. A load balancer. Application servers. A database. Redis cache. The interviewer nods. The candidate adds more boxes — a CDN, a message queue, a separate analytics database. Forty-five minutes later, the candidate has drawn an Instagram-scale architecture for a service that needs to store 6-character keys.

The interviewer asks: "How many URLs per day are you designing for?" The candidate freezes. They never asked. The design handles 10 million writes per second — the candidate assumed massive scale. The actual requirement was 10,000 URLs per day. The candidate just designed a system with 100 times more complexity than needed, and the interview is a rejection.

At a mid-sized e-commerce company, a team of six engineers spends three months building a microservices platform. Six services. Six databases. An event bus. A service mesh. Kubernetes. CI/CD pipelines for each service. Four months of infrastructure work before a single feature ships. The product fails in market after three weeks. The team never validated that customers wanted the product. The architectural complexity was irrelevant — the process failure was figuring out what to build before figuring out how to build it.

A startup hires a contractor to build their payment processing system. The contractor does not clarify requirements. They assume the system needs to handle peak Black Friday traffic of 50,000 transactions per minute. They design a multi-region Kafka pipeline with exactly-once semantics, a fraud detection service, and a reconciliation engine. Two months late and 80,000 dollars over budget, the system goes live. The startup has 200 customers. Peak traffic is 12 transactions per minute. The system works perfectly. It also costs 4,000 dollars per month in infrastructure. The startup has three months of runway left. The contractor designed for Twitter. The startup needed a lemonade stand.

These stories share a single root cause: the designers started solving before they understood the problem. They drew boxes before they asked questions. They selected technologies before they knew the constraints. They designed for a scale that no one had requested. The system design process exists to prevent exactly these failures.

## The Insight

The system design process is a structured framework for designing systems — moving from requirements through capacity estimation and high-level architecture to detailed component design, trade-off analysis, and a final wrap. It is the set of steps that ensures you build the right system at the right scale with the right trade-offs.

Think of the system design process as a chef's recipe. A chef does not start cooking by grabbing pots and pans. The chef reads the recipe, checks the ingredients, preheats the oven, prepares the mise en place, and only then starts cooking. Skip the prep step and the onions burn while you search for the knife. Skip the ingredient check and you run out of butter halfway through the sauce. Do the steps in the wrong order and the dish fails — you cannot season the steak after it has been plated, you season it before it hits the pan.

The system design process works the same way. You gather requirements before you estimate. You estimate before you design. You design the high-level architecture before the low-level components. You analyze trade-offs before you finalize. Each step feeds into the next. Each step constrains the options at the next step. Skip one, and the entire design becomes unreliable.

The structured process also serves as your defense against nervousness and scope creep. In an interview, the process gives you a roadmap when your mind goes blank. "I am on step three, high-level design. I have already clarified requirements and estimated capacity. I know what to do next." In a real project, the process ensures you have documented the requirements before the team invests weeks of engineering time. The process is not bureaucracy — it is a survival mechanism for complex systems.

## Core Vocabulary

**Requirements gathering:** The process of asking questions to determine what the system must do (functional requirements) and how well it must perform (non-functional requirements). Functional requirements answer "what": create a short URL, redirect to the original URL, track clicks. Non-functional requirements answer "how well": 99.9% availability, under 200ms p99 latency, support 10,000 writes per second. Requirements gathering is the most skipped step and the most expensive to skip.

**Capacity estimation:** The back-of-envelope math that determines how much storage, bandwidth, compute, and memory the system needs. Estimates are not precise — they are order-of-magnitude approximations that tell you whether your design fits on one server or needs a thousand. Capacity estimation uses rules of thumb: a single PostgreSQL instance handles roughly 10,000 QPS, a Redis node handles roughly 100,000 ops/sec, a server handles roughly 5,000 concurrent connections.

**Data flow:** The path that data follows through the system from entry to storage. For a URL shortener: user request arrives at load balancer, gets forwarded to application server, application checks cache, cache miss triggers database query, result flows back through the same path. Data flow diagrams reveal bottlenecks, unnecessary hops, and single points of failure.

**Component diagram:** A visual representation of the system's components and their relationships. Boxes are services, cylinders are databases, arrows are communication channels. A component diagram is the map of your system — it shows what exists, how pieces connect, and where data moves.

**API design:** The specification of how components communicate. RESTful endpoints, gRPC services, WebSocket connections, GraphQL queries. API design defines the contracts between services — the request format, the response format, the error codes, the rate limits. Good API design makes the system testable and evolvable. Bad API design makes every change a breaking change.

**Data model:** The structure of the data the system stores and the relationships between entities. Tables, columns, indexes, foreign keys, partitions, shards. The data model determines query patterns, which determines cache strategy, which determines performance. Choose the data model for the read pattern, not the write pattern — most systems read 10 times more than they write.

**Trade-off analysis:** The systematic comparison of design alternatives along multiple axes — cost, complexity, latency, availability, consistency, operational burden. Trade-off analysis is the core skill of system design. Every design choice involves giving up something to gain something else. A trade-off analysis makes those sacrifices explicit and deliberate.

**Bottleneck:** The component that limits the throughput of the entire system. In a web system, the bottleneck is often the database, then the application server, then the network. Finding and eliminating the bottleneck is the single highest-leverage activity in system design. Before you optimize anything, find the bottleneck.

**Single point of failure (SPOF):** A component whose failure causes the entire system to fail. A single database server without a replica. A single load balancer. A single network switch. A single engineer who knows how the deployment works. Eliminate SPOFs before production, add redundancy after you measure.

**Deep dive:** The phase of design where you focus on one specific component or subproblem in detail. You have the high-level architecture. Now you zoom in on the database schema, the cache invalidation strategy, the data partitioning scheme, the consistency guarantees. Deep dives reveal the implementation-level challenges that the high-level diagram hides.

**Scaling strategy:** The plan for how the system grows from its initial load to its projected peak. Vertical scaling (bigger servers) works for 2x growth. Horizontal scaling (more servers) works for 100x growth. A scaling strategy specifies which parts scale vertically, which scale horizontally, and at what thresholds you add capacity.

**Back-of-envelope calculation:** A rough, order-of-magnitude estimate done without calculators or precise data. Examples: 10 million URLs per day is roughly 115 writes per second. 1 TB of storage across 10 servers is 100 GB per server. 5 million concurrent users at 10 requests per user per minute is roughly 833,000 requests per minute. Back-of-envelope math converts abstract requirements into concrete numbers that drive architectural decisions.

**SLA / SLO:** Service Level Agreement (the contract) and Service Level Objective (the target). 99.9% availability means under 8.77 hours of downtime per year. 99.99% means under 52.6 minutes. 200ms p99 latency means 99% of requests complete within 200 milliseconds, and 1% can be slower. SLOs define the performance targets that your design must achieve.

## Step by Step — Designing a URL Shortener

The best way to learn the process is to watch it applied. Here is a complete walkthrough of the six steps applied to the prompt "Design a URL shortener like bit.ly."

### Step 1: Requirements Clarification — 5 Minutes

You do not start with boxes. You start with questions. The interviewer expects you to ask these questions. A candidate who asks zero questions is a candidate who has memorized a design and is trying to fit every problem into it.

**Functional requirements:**

"Are users creating short URLs, or only reading them? Both. Do we need custom aliases? Yes, for premium users. Do URLs expire? Optional — users can set an expiration time. Do we need click analytics? Yes — per-URL click count, referrer, timestamp, geolocation. Do we need user accounts? Not required for the core flow, but useful for managing URLs."

Functional requirements summary:
- Create short URL from long URL (with optional custom alias)
- Redirect short URL to original URL
- Optional URL expiration
- Click analytics with referrer, timestamp, country
- User accounts (premium feature)

**Non-functional requirements:**

"How many URLs per day? 100 million new URLs per day. How many redirects per day? 1 billion redirects per day. What is the latency target for redirects? Under 50ms p99 — the user is waiting for a redirect, every millisecond matters. What is the availability target? 99.99% — the service must be accessible. How long should data persist? At least 5 years. What regions? US initially, expand to EU and Asia in year 2."

Non-functional requirements summary:
- 100 million writes per day (1,200 writes/second average, 4,000 writes/second peak)
- 1 billion reads per day (11,600 reads/second average, 35,000 reads/second peak)
- Read-to-write ratio: 10:1
- p99 latency under 50ms for redirects
- 99.99% availability (under 53 minutes of downtime per year)
- 5-year data retention
- US region initially, multi-region in year 2

### Step 2: Capacity Estimation — 3 Minutes

You convert the requirements into concrete numbers. These are back-of-envelope calculations — order-of-magnitude accuracy is sufficient.

**Storage estimate:**

Each short URL record: 6-byte key + 200-byte average original URL + 8-byte timestamp + 8-byte user ID + 8-byte click counter = 230 bytes.

Daily storage growth: 100 million x 230 bytes = 23 GB.
Yearly storage: 23 GB x 365 = 8.4 TB.
Five-year storage: 8.4 TB x 5 = 42 TB.
With 3x replication for fault tolerance: 126 TB raw storage.

Click analytics storage is larger. Each click event: 8-byte ID + 6-byte short key + 8-byte timestamp + 50-byte referrer + 100-byte user agent + 16-byte IP + 2-byte country code = ~190 bytes. At 1 billion clicks per day: 190 GB per day. This is too large for a relational database — you need a time-series or columnar storage approach.

**Bandwidth estimate:**

Incoming writes: 1,200 URL creations/second x 300 bytes per request = 0.36 MB/s.
Incoming reads: 11,600 redirects/second x 100 bytes per request = 1.16 MB/s.
Outgoing redirects: 11,600 responses/second x 500 bytes per response (HTTP headers + redirect) = 5.8 MB/s.

Total bandwidth: roughly 8 MB/s — a single server can handle this.

**Compute estimate:**

A single modern application server handles roughly 5,000 concurrent connections and 10,000 RPS for simple operations. You need 2-3 application servers for the redirect traffic (11,600 RPS) and 1-2 servers for URL creation (1,200 RPS). Total: 4-5 application servers.

**API design:**

```
POST /shorten
  Request:  { "url": "https://example.com/very/long/path?with=params" }
  Response: { "short_url": "https://short.ly/abc123", "key": "abc123" }

POST /shorten (with custom alias)
  Request:  { "url": "https://example.com", "custom_alias": "myalias" }
  Response: { "short_url": "https://short.ly/myalias", "key": "myalias" }

GET /{short_key}
  Response: HTTP 302 redirect to original URL
  Headers:  Location: https://original-url.com
            Cache-Control: private, max-age=3600

GET /{short_key}/stats
  Response: { "clicks": 15000, "top_referrers": [...] }
```

**Data flow for redirect:**

1. User clicks short URL `https://short.ly/abc123`
2. Browser sends DNS lookup for `short.ly`
3. DNS returns load balancer IP
4. Browser sends GET /abc123 to load balancer
5. Load balancer forwards to an app server
6. App server checks Redis cache for key `url:abc123`
7. If cache hit (likely): return HTTP 302 with original URL (under 5ms)
8. If cache miss: query PostgreSQL read replica, populate cache, return redirect (under 50ms)
9. App server emits click event to Kafka topic (async, non-blocking)
10. Browser receives 302 redirect and follows it to original URL

### Step 4: Deep Dive — Database Schema, Cache Strategy, Key Generation — 15 Minutes

The high-level design gives you the skeleton. The deep dive adds the muscles, tendons, and blood vessels.

**Database schema:**

```sql
-- Core URL mappings table
CREATE TABLE urls (
    short_key    VARCHAR(10) PRIMARY KEY,
    original_url TEXT NOT NULL,
    created_at   TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL DEFAULT NOW(),
    expires_at   TIMESTAMPTZ,
    user_id      BIGINT,
    is_custom    BOOLEAN DEFAULT FALSE
);

-- Index for reverse lookup by user
CREATE INDEX idx_urls_user_id ON urls (user_id, created_at DESC);

-- Click events — append-only, time-series
CREATE TABLE click_events (
    id           BIGSERIAL,
    short_key    VARCHAR(10) NOT NULL,
    clicked_at   TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL DEFAULT NOW(),
    referrer     TEXT,
    user_agent   TEXT,
    ip_address   INET,
    country_code CHAR(2),
    PRIMARY KEY (short_key, clicked_at, id)
) PARTITION BY RANGE (clicked_at);

-- Monthly partitions for click_events
CREATE TABLE click_events_2026_01
    PARTITION OF click_events
    FOR VALUES FROM ('2026-01-01') TO ('2026-02-01');

CREATE TABLE click_events_2026_02
    PARTITION OF click_events
    FOR VALUES FROM ('2026-02-01') TO ('2026-03-01');
-- ... and so on, created monthly by cron job
```

**Cache strategy:**

Cache-aside pattern with Redis. Each short URL is cached on first read with a 1-hour TTL. The most popular 1% of URLs get a 24-hour TTL with background refresh.

```
Cache key format:  "url:{short_key}"
Cache value:       JSON { "original_url": "...", "created_at": "..." }
TTL:               3,600 seconds (1 hour) default
                   86,400 seconds (24 hours) for top 1% URLs

Populated on:      Cache miss (lazy population)
Invalidated on:    URL expiration or deletion

Memory requirement:
  Hot set (top 1% URLs accessed per hour):
  1% of 1B reads/day = 10M unique URLs in hot set
  10M x 500 bytes (key + value + overhead) = 5 GB
  With 3x replication: 15 GB — fits comfortably in 64 GB cluster
```

`} alt="Cache-aside strategy with Redis showing two TTL layers — standard 3,600-second TTL and 86,400-second TTL for hot URLs — along with cache miss path to PostgreSQL read replica" caption="Figure 4: Cache-aside with layered TTL optimizes both memory usage and cache hit rate." />

**Key generation:**

Short keys must be globally unique and unpredictable. Sequential keys (1, 2, 3) let users enumerate all short URLs. UUIDs are 36 characters — too long. The solution is base62 encoding of a unique numeric ID with a key-generation service.

Base62 uses A-Z, a-z, 0-9 = 62 characters. With 7 characters: 62^7 = approximately 3.5 trillion keys. With 100 million new URLs per day, this lasts 96 years.

```
Key generation — distributed range allocation:

KeyGen Service (centralized, but rarely contacted):
  Manages the counter: current_max_id
  Allocates ranges of 100,000 IDs to each app server

App Server 1: requests range → receives [0, 99,999]
  Local counter: 0
  base62encode(0) = "aaaaaaa"
  base62encode(1) = "aaaaaab"
  ...
  After 100,000 keys: request new range

App Server 2: requests range → receives [100,000, 199,999]
  Local counter: 100,000
  base62encode(100,000) = "abcaaq"

This eliminates the centralized ID generator as a bottleneck.
Each server can generate 100,000 keys locally without coordination.
```

Base62 encoding function:

```python
import string

BASE62 = string.digits + string.ascii_lowercase + string.ascii_uppercase

def base62_encode(num: int) -> str:
    """Encode a positive integer to a base62 string."""
    if num == 0:
        return BASE62[0]
    result = []
    while num > 0:
        result.append(BASE62[num % 62])
        num //= 62
    return ''.join(reversed(result))

# Example:
# base62_encode(0) = "0"
# base62_encode(123456789) = "8M0kX"
```

### Step 5: Trade-Off Analysis — 7 Minutes

You now evaluate the design decisions and acknowledge the trade-offs. This step separates experienced engineers from junior engineers. A junior engineer presents a design as correct. A senior engineer presents a design as a set of deliberate trade-offs.

**Trade-off 1: SQL vs NoSQL for primary storage.**

PostgreSQL offers transactions, joins, ACID guarantees, and 15 years of production battle-testing. It handles 10,000 writes per second with proper connection pooling and indexing. For the URL shortener's scale (1,200 writes/sec, 11,600 reads/sec), PostgreSQL is well within its operating range.

Cassandra offers linear scalability, no single point of failure, and multi-region writes. It handles 1,000,000 writes per second across clusters. But Cassandra offers no joins, no foreign keys, no transactions — and the URL shortener needs none of those. The write volume does not justify Cassandra's operational complexity.

Decision: PostgreSQL. The system does not need Cassandra's write volume or multi-region capabilities. PostgreSQL is simpler, better understood, and cheaper to operate at this scale.

**Trade-off 2: Cache-Aside vs Read-Through.**

Cache-aside: the application reads from cache, writes to cache on miss. Simple, well-understood, cache host failure degrades performance but does not break reads.

Read-through: the cache layer automatically queries the database on miss. Hides the database from the application but couples cache and database configuration. If the cache fails, read operations fail entirely.

Decision: Cache-aside. The application controls the cache policy, cache failure degrades to database reads, and the implementation is straightforward.

**Trade-off 3: Kafka vs RabbitMQ for analytics.**

Kafka offers high throughput (1M+ messages/sec), log-based storage, replay capability, and exactly-once semantics with proper configuration. RabbitMQ offers lower latency (sub-millisecond) and simpler routing. For click analytics at 11,600 events per second with downstream batch processing, Kafka's throughput and replay capabilities win.

Decision: Kafka. Click events are a high-volume stream that benefits from partitioning, replay, and batch consumption.

**Trade-off 4: Monthly partitions for click_events.**

Partitioning by month limits each partition to roughly 3 billion rows. Queries that filter by date scan fewer rows. Older partitions can be compressed or moved to cold storage. Trade-off: queries crossing month boundaries scan multiple partitions, and partition management requires a cron job.

**Trade-off 5: 7-character vs 8-character keys.**

7 characters: 3.5 trillion keys — sufficient for 96 years at current volume. 8 characters: 218 trillion keys — overkill. Shorter keys mean shorter URLs and less storage in click events (every click stores the key). Decision: 7 characters. The 7-character key saves 1 byte per click event, which at 1 billion clicks per day saves 1 GB of storage per day.

### Step 6: Wrap and Next Steps — 5 Minutes

You summarize the design and show you have been thinking about the future.

**Design summary:** URL shortener serving 1 billion redirects and 100 million new URLs per day. Six application servers behind a load balancer. PostgreSQL primary with two read replicas for URL storage. Redis cache-aside with layered TTLs. Kafka pipeline for click analytics with batch writes to a columnar analytics database. Key generation via distributed base62 encoding with pre-allocated ID ranges.

**Scaling roadmap:**

2x growth in year 2: Add more application servers horizontally, add read replicas, increase cache cluster memory. No architectural changes needed — the design scales linearly.

10x growth in year 3: Add a CDN for static assets. Implement a write buffer for URL creation (batch writes to PostgreSQL). Add a second primary database for geographical sharding (US East, EU West). Migrate click analytics to a dedicated time-series database. Evaluate Cassandra if write volume exceeds PostgreSQL's capacity.

100x growth in year 5: Multi-region active-active deployment with global load balancing. Shard URL storage by geographic region. Implement eventual consistency for URL creation (write to local region, async replicate). Move from PostgreSQL to a distributed SQL database like CockroachDB or Spanner.

## Variants and Approaches

The six-step process is not the only way to do system design. Different frameworks emphasize different aspects. Here are three common variants.

### Grokking the System Design Interview — Product-Based

The Grokking course organizes design around the product being built. The process is: understand the problem and establish design scope (functional requirements, non-functional requirements, constraints, scale) → propose high-level design and get buy-in → design deep dive (database schema, data partitioning, caching, concurrency, consistency, fault tolerance, indexing, proxy).

The strength of the Grokking approach is its emphasis on scope and getting buy-in at each step. The weakness is that it can feel like a checklist — you may rush through steps without understanding why they matter.

### Real-World Adaptive Process — Interview and Production

The real world rarely follows a script. In a 45-minute interview, you adapt the six-step process to the constraints. In a production environment, you adapt it to the organizational context.

**Interview adaptation (45 minutes):**
- Requirements: 3 minutes — ask 3-5 clarifying questions, make reasonable assumptions for the rest
- Capacity estimation: 2 minutes — storage and QPS only, skip bandwidth
- High-level design: 10 minutes — 5-6 components, focus on data flow
- Deep dive: 20 minutes — one or two components in detail
- Trade-offs: 5 minutes — three key decisions
- Wrap: 5 minutes — summary and future roadmap

**Production adaptation (multi-week):**
- Requirements: multiple meetings with stakeholders, written PRD
- Capacity estimation: spreadsheet with actual traffic data from existing systems
- High-level design: RFC document with diagrams, reviewed by 3+ senior engineers
- Deep dive: separate design documents per component, implementation tickets
- Trade-offs: written decision log with alternatives considered, data from proof-of-concept tests
- Wrap: rollout plan, monitoring setup, rollback plan, on-call runbook

**Production adaptation (startup, 1-week sprint):**
- Requirements: 30-minute conversation with the founder
- Capacity estimation: "Will this fit on a single Postgres instance?" Yes → proceed
- High-level design: draw on a Miro board, 1 hour
- Deep dive: implement the simplest version that works, add complexity when needed
- Trade-offs: decide on the call, document in a PR description
- Wrap: deploy to production, monitor error rates

## Mental Model Check

Test your understanding of the system design process before moving deeper.

**Question 1:** An engineer spends 30 minutes designing a pastebin service. They draw a load balancer, five microservices, a Redis cluster, a Cassandra database, a Kafka pipeline, and a Kubernetes deployment diagram. The interviewer asks: "How many pastes per day?" The engineer replies: "I assumed 10 million." The requirement was 1,000 per day. What step did the engineer skip, and what is the consequence?

The engineer skipped Step 1 (requirements clarification) and Step 2 (capacity estimation). The consequence is a massively over-engineered design. The engineer built a NASA command center for a lemonade stand. The design handles 10,000 times more traffic than needed, which means the operational complexity and infrastructure cost are unjustified. In the interview, the engineer appears to be cargo-culting — applying a memorized architecture without understanding the requirements.

**Question 2:** A team designs a photo-sharing app without estimating storage. They assume "cloud storage is cheap" and never calculate how much storage 10 million photos per day at 5 MB each consumes. After three months, their storage bill is 450,000 dollars per month. What capacity estimate would have prevented this?

10 million photos per day at 5 MB each = 50 TB of new storage per day. At Amazon S3 standard pricing ($0.023/GB/month), the daily storage cost alone is 50,000 GB x $0.023 = $1,150 per day. The monthly cost for new storage each month grows linearly. After three months, the cumulative storage cost exceeds 100,000 dollars per month. With compression and image resizing, the average file size could be reduced to 200 KB, cutting storage by 25x. An estimate before implementation would have revealed this.

**Question 3:** An engineer presents a design for a payment processing system. The design uses PostgreSQL with eventual consistency — writes may take up to 5 seconds to propagate. A customer sees a negative balance of $500 and immediately transfers $500 more into the account. The system shows a balance of $500 (the deposit arrived first), and the customer transfers $500 out. Which step of the process would have caught this, and what trade-off should have been made?

The trade-off analysis step (Step 5) should have caught the inconsistency. The engineer chose eventual consistency for "better performance" without analyzing the correctness implications for financial transactions. The correct trade-off is strong consistency for the balance column — using PostgreSQL's SERIALIZABLE isolation level or using an atomic compare-and-swap operation. The performance cost of strong consistency on a single row is under 2ms — negligible for the correctness guarantee.

**Question 4:** A candidate in a system design interview designs a chat system. After 10 minutes, the candidate has covered requirements, capacity, and high-level design. The interviewer asks: "How would you handle typing indicators?" The candidate has 15 minutes left and has not done any capacity estimation for the real-time messaging component. They try to estimate on the fly and get confused. Where did the candidate's process go wrong?

The candidate did not allocate deep-dive time appropriately. In the deep-dive phase, the candidate should have listed 3-4 deep-dive topics (database schema, real-time messaging protocol, typing indicators, offline message storage) and asked the interviewer which to prioritize. Instead, the candidate assumed the deep dive was "more details on what I already drew." The fix: after the high-level design, explicitly say "I would like to deep dive into three areas. Which would you like me to focus on?" This steers the interview into your prepared areas.

**Question 5:** A team spends 6 months designing and implementing a microservices architecture for their e-commerce platform. The system goes live. Two weeks later, a bug in the inventory service causes all order failures to be silently dropped. Customers see "order confirmed" but the order never reaches the warehouse. The design had no dead-letter queue for the event stream. Which process step was missed, and what should the team have done differently?

The deep-dive step missed failure mode analysis. The team designed the happy path (order created → event published → inventory service receives event → order fulfilled) but never traced the failure paths. The process should have included a "failure mode analysis" sub-step within the deep dive: what happens when the inventory service is down? What happens when the event is published but never consumed? What happens when the event is malformed? The fix: a dead-letter queue for unprocessable events, a retry mechanism with exponential backoff, and an alert when the DLQ accumulates more than 10 messages.
