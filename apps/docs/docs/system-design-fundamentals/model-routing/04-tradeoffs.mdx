## The Core Tension

Every model routing system faces one unavoidable trade-off: **routing accuracy versus routing latency**. You can classify requests quickly using simple heuristics, but you risk misclassifying complex requests. Or you can classify requests thoroughly using LLM-based analysis, but you add hundreds of milliseconds of overhead before the model even starts generating a response.

The trade-off is shaped like an L-curve. On one axis is classification accuracy (percentage of requests routed to the optimal model). On the other axis is routing decision latency (milliseconds added by the router). The curve shows diminishing returns: the first 80 percent of accuracy costs under 5ms (keyword matching). The next 10 percent costs 5-20ms (embedding classification). The final 10 percent costs 200-800ms (prompt-based LLM classification).

Where you position on this curve depends on your application. A real-time chatbot with a 500ms latency budget cannot afford a 600ms classifier -- you must accept lower accuracy from a faster approach. A batch document analysis pipeline with a 30-second budget can use a prompt-based classifier to maximize accuracy. The key is knowing where the inflection point is for your use case and setting your routing latency budget accordingly.

The deeper tension is that routing latency is **always additive** to the total response time. If the model takes 2 seconds to respond and the router takes 200ms, the user waits 2.2 seconds. The router consumed 10 percent of the latency budget before the model even started. For fast models like GPT-4o-mini (300ms response), a 200ms router adds 67 percent overhead. The router must be proportionally faster for cheaper models, which is ironic because cheap models attract routing in the first place.

### The Numerical Breakeven

The breakeven between accuracy and speed depends on three numbers: the cost of a misclassification, the value of the request, and the penalty for extra latency.

```python
# Breakeven point for routing accuracy vs speed
request_volume = 10_000_000  # requests per month
embedding_accuracy = 0.85    # 85% accurate
prompt_accuracy = 0.97       # 97% accurate
misclassification_cost = 0.02  # $0.02 per bad route
latency_penalty_per_ms = 0.0001  # $0.0001 per ms of latency

embedding_mis = request_volume * (1 - embedding_accuracy)
prompt_mis = request_volume * (1 - prompt_accuracy)
savings = (embedding_mis - prompt_mis) * misclassification_cost

prompt_latency_ms = 350  # extra ms over embedding
latency_penalty = request_volume * prompt_latency_ms * latency_penalty_per_ms

net_benefit = savings - latency_penalty
```

At 10 million requests per month, switching from an 85 percent accurate embedding classifier to a 97 percent accurate prompt-based classifier saves $24,000 in misclassification cost but adds $10,000 in latency penalty and $3,500 in compute cost. The net benefit is $10,500 per month. But the latency penalty calculation depends on your business -- if every 100ms of extra latency reduces conversion by 0.5 percent at $1.00 per conversion, the penalty is $50,000, and the prompt-based classifier is a net loss of $39,500.

The breakeven depends on your specific latency-to-revenue relationship. Do not assume higher accuracy always wins.

## Decision Matrix

Eight decision points define every model routing system.

| Decision Point | Trade-off | Choose A When | Choose B When |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Rule-based vs. ML-based classification** | Simplicity vs. Accuracy | You have under 5 well-defined, non-overlapping request categories. Your team needs deterministic, auditable routing. | You have 10 or more categories that overlap semantically. Requests vary widely in phrasing. You need to handle novel request types. |
| **Embedding vs. Prompt-based classification** | Speed vs. Accuracy | Latency budget is under 50ms. You expect over 1,000 QPS. A 5 percent misclassification rate is acceptable. | Latency budget is over 200ms. Volume is under 100 QPS. Accuracy is critical and misclassification costs are high. |
| **In-memory cache vs. Distributed cache** | Simplicity vs. Consistency | Single-region deployment. Routing table is small (under 100 entries). Cache miss is cheap (under 5ms). | Multi-region with cache coherence needed. Large routing table (over 1,000 entries). Cache miss is expensive (over 50ms). |
| **Centralized vs. Decentralized router** | Coordination vs. Scalability | Global routing policy must be consistent. Small team of 3-5 people manages routing. | Each region or team has different model availability. High traffic (over 10K QPS) requires horizontal scaling. |
| **Static vs. Dynamic routing policies** | Stability vs. Optimality | Routing requirements are stable. Model pricing and availability change monthly at most. | Model pricing changes weekly. Traffic patterns shift daily. You need real-time optimization. |
| **Fail-open vs. Fail-closed routing** | Availability vs. Correctness | Application can degrade gracefully. Users prefer a response from any model over no response. | Regulatory or safety requirements demand specific model use. A wrong answer is worse than no answer. |
| **Cost-first vs. Quality-first routing** | Savings vs. Output quality | Cost is the primary constraint. Team has budget pressure. Quality degradation is acceptable for most requests. | Quality is non-negotiable. Application handles sensitive or high-stakes requests. Cost is secondary. |
| **Stateless vs. Stateful router** | Simplicity vs. Session awareness | Each request is independent. No continuation or conversation context needed. | Multi-turn conversations where model consistency matters. User has tier-based routing preferences. |

### How to Use the Matrix

Map your application against each row. Score each row as 0 (A applies) or 1 (B applies). Sum the scores. If the total is 4 or above, your system has complex routing requirements and should use the more sophisticated option for each decision. If the total is under 4, start simple and add complexity only when you hit a specific bottleneck.

For example, a customer support chatbot with 1,000 QPS, 50ms latency budget, 5 request categories, and a 3-person team scores: ML-based (1), embedding (0), in-memory cache (0), centralized (0), static (0), fail-open (0), cost-first (1), stateless (0). Total: 2 out of 8. Start simple: rule-based classification, centralized router, static policies. Only add ML-based classification when the category count grows past 10.

## CAP Theorem Positioning

The model registry -- the source of truth for routing decisions -- faces a classic CAP theorem choice. The registry must decide whether to prioritize **availability** (always accept routing table reads) or **consistency** (always return the latest routing configuration).

<AsciiDiagram id="model-routing/tradeoffs-cap" title="CAP Theorem Positioning for Model Registry" alt="CAP triangle showing model registry positioned closer to AP than CP with consistency achieved through async replication" caption="The model registry prioritizes availability over consistency, using async replication for eventual consistency" content={
`
                  Consistency (C)
                       │
                       │
                       │
                       │   CP Systems
                       │   (Block on sync)
                       │
                       │
                       │        AP Systems
                       │        (Serve stale)
                       │
                       │
        ───────────────┼─────────────────── Availability (A)
                       │
                       │
                       │
                       │
                       │
                       │
                       │
                 Partition Tolerance (P)
                       │
                       │
   Model Registry Position: AP (Availability + Partition Tolerance)
   ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   Rationale: The router must never block on the registry.
   A stale routing table that serves every request is better
   than a consistent one that drops requests during a partition.

   Consistency is achieved through async replication:
   Registry updates propagate with a 10-60 second delay,
   which is acceptable for routing configuration changes.
`} />

**Why AP over CP for model routing:** The router is in the hot path of every request. If the router cannot make a decision because the registry is unavailable or requires a synchronous read, every request fails. An AP approach means the router caches the routing table locally and serves from cache even when the registry is unavailable. The consistency trade-off is that routing decisions may be based on stale data for up to 60 seconds.

In practice, stale routing data causes minor issues: a new model is not used for a few minutes after deployment, or a deprecated model continues to receive traffic. These issues are far less damaging than a routing outage that blocks all requests.

The one exception is safety-critical routing. If a model is deprecated because of a safety issue (harms, biases, security vulnerabilities), stale routing data could route requests to a dangerous model. For safety-critical updates, the system supports a "force invalidation" mechanism that pushes an urgent cache invalidation to all routers within 1 second, crossing into CP territory briefly.

### The Recommended Architecture Split

The practical solution separates two concerns:

1. **Authoritative registry: CP.** Use etcd or ZooKeeper for the model registry. During a partition, the registry blocks writes until consistency is restored. No routing configuration is accepted on a minority partition. This prevents conflicting routing policies from being applied across split network segments.

2. **Routing service: AP.** The routing service caches the routing table locally with a configurable TTL of 60 seconds. It reads from the authoritative registry asynchronously. If the registry is unreachable, the routing service serves the cached configuration. This keeps the router available even when the registry is partitioned.

Combined behavior during a partition:

```
Partition scenario: Registry (etcd) split between two network segments

  Registry (CP):
    Majority partition: accepts writes. New model "claude-opus-4" is registered.
    Minority partition: rejects writes. Returns "cannot accept write: no quorum."

  Router (AP):
    Majority side: receives new config via cache refresh within 1 second.
    Minority side: serves stale config with last known model list.
      - Cannot route to "claude-opus-4" until partition heals.
      - But continues routing to all previously known models.
      - Zero downtime during partition.
```

The key insight: the cost of serving a stale routing table during a partition is much lower than the cost of an unavailable router. A stale table means you might route a request to a model that is no longer the best choice, but the request still gets processed. An unavailable router means the request fails entirely.

## Failure Modes

### Failure 1: Misclassification Cascade

A user asks a complex legal question: "Does the non-compete clause in section 4.2 of our contract apply if the employee is terminated without cause?" The embedding-based classifier scores it as a "general contract question" (confidence 0.72) and routes it to GPT-4o-mini. The small model gives a generic answer that misses jurisdiction-specific nuances and recent case law changes. The user retries, gets the same result, and escalates to support.

**Detection.** Track per-category retry rate. A misclassified request type shows a distinct pattern: high retry rate for a specific category, with all retries following the same route. Also track quality feedback scores if available. Alert when retry rate exceeds 15 percent for any category.

**Recovery.** Implement a retry escalation policy. If a user retries within 30 seconds, route the retry to the next more capable model, not the same one. Log the confidence score for every routing decision. For low-confidence requests (score under 0.8), default to a more capable model instead of the cheap one. Re-train the classifier on misclassified examples.

**Prevention.** Add a confidence threshold. If the classifier confidence falls below the threshold (0.8 by default), use a prompt-based recheck before routing. The recheck adds latency but prevents the cascade. Periodically review a sample of misclassified requests and update the classifier training data.

### Failure 2: Model Timeout

The primary model (Claude Sonnet) is experiencing a latency spike due to provider-side load. The model is still accepting requests but taking 12 seconds instead of the expected 850ms. The router's timeout is set to 30 seconds, so requests do not fail -- they just wait. Response times degrade from 1 second to 12 seconds for every request.

**Detection.** The latency predictor histogram shows p50 latency for Claude Sonnet jumping from 850ms to 8,200ms over a 2-minute window. The alerting system triggers a "latency anomaly" notification because the p50 exceeds 3 standard deviations from the rolling baseline. Also monitor the "slow request" count: requests taking longer than 2x the expected latency.

**Recovery.** The latency predictor automatically marks Claude Sonnet as "degraded" when the p50 exceeds 3x the model's latency budget for the request type. New requests are routed to the fallback (GPT-4o) instead. The degraded model is probed every 30 seconds with a simple test prompt. When latency returns to normal levels for 10 consecutive probes, the model is restored to the active routing table. The timeout for the affected model is also reduced from 30 seconds to 5 seconds to fail fast if degradation continues.

### Failure 3: Cascading Fallback Failure

The primary model (Claude Sonnet) fails. The router falls back to GPT-4o. GPT-4o is also under heavy load because it is absorbing the fallback traffic from Claude Sonnet plus its own primary traffic. GPT-4o starts timing out. The router falls back to Claude Opus, which is the most expensive model and handles both its own traffic and all overflow traffic from the two cheaper models. Cost spikes 10x in 5 minutes.

**Detection.** The fallback rate metric jumps from 2 percent to 85 percent in 3 minutes. The cost-per-request metric spikes from $0.002 to $0.02. The alert triggers on both: "fallback rate exceeds 20 percent" and "cost per request exceeds 3x baseline."

**Recovery.** The cascading fallback indicates that all models in the fallback chain share a common dependency (same provider, same region, same network path). The fix is to design fallback chains that use independent models -- ideally from different providers or different regions. For immediate recovery, the router implements "fallback depth limiting": if two consecutive fallbacks fail, return a cached response or a degraded-mode response instead of trying a third. This caps the cost and latency impact of cascading failures at 2 models instead of N.

**Prevention.** Keep 20 percent headroom in the primary model. If Claude Sonnet is consistently above 80 percent capacity, shift some of its traffic to other models before it hits the limit. Use a dedicated fallback pool: models that serve as fallbacks should not be the same models serving primary traffic for other categories.

### Failure 4: Routing Loop

Two routers are configured to share fallback responsibilities. Router A routes requests to Model X. When Model X fails, the fallback chain sends the request to Router B. Router B routes to Model Y. When Model Y fails, its fallback chain sends the request back to Router A. The request bounces between routers indefinitely, accumulating latency but never producing a response.

**Prevention.** Version-stamp routing decisions. When the router makes a decision, it records its own router ID and the config version used. A router that receives a request should check if its own router ID appears in the hop history. If it does, the fallback chain is circular and should be broken immediately.

Each router instance subscribes to the bus and refreshes its cache within 100ms of the event. Version-stamp every routing decision: the config version is included in the routing audit log. If two router instances use different config versions for the same request category, alert immediately.

### Failure 6: Cost Explosion

A configuration error causes the routing table to route all traffic intended for GPT-4o-mini ($0.15/M tokens) to Claude Opus ($15.00/M tokens). The monthly bill would increase from $5,000 to $115,000 if undetected for a full billing cycle.

**Detection.** Monitor the average cost per request in a 1-minute sliding window. Alert when the windowed average exceeds 2x the expected average. Also monitor the per-model cost share: if Claude Opus (normally 2 percent of requests) suddenly receives 50 percent of requests, cost explodes. Set a hard daily cost budget at the model provider level.

**Recovery.** Enforce a per-request cost cap. If a routing decision would send a request to a model that exceeds the per-request cost budget, override the decision and route to the cheapest model that meets the minimum capability threshold.

```python
# Cost explosion prevention with per-request cap
MAX_COST_PER_REQUEST = {
    "translation": 0.0005,   # $0.0005 max per request
    "summarization": 0.001,  # $0.001 max per request
    "analysis": 0.01,        # $0.01 max per request
    "legal_review": 0.05,    # $0.05 max per request
}

def route_with_cost_cap(category: str, request):
    selected = select_best_model(category, request)
    cost = estimate_cost(selected, request)
    max_cost = MAX_COST_PER_REQUEST.get(category, 0.01)

    if cost > max_cost:
        fallback = cheapest_capable_model(category, request)
        log_override(request.id, selected, fallback, cost, max_cost)
        return fallback
    return selected
```

**Prevention.** Use a weekly budget burn rate alert. Track total model spend per week and compare to the budgeted amount. If the burn rate exceeds the budgeted rate by 20 percent, notify the team. Run a daily cost regression test: replay the previous day's request distribution through the current routing configuration and calculate the expected cost.

## Anti-Patterns Hall of Shame

### Anti-Pattern 1: Hardcoding Model Names

Developers embed model names directly in the routing logic instead of reading from a routing table.

```python
# Anti-pattern: hardcoded model names
def route_request(request):
    if "billing" in request.lower():
        return "gpt-4o-mini"  # Hardcoded!
    elif "code" in request.lower():
        return "gpt-4"  # Hardcoded!
```

**Why it is harmful.** Model names change. Providers rename models (GPT-4 becomes GPT-4o), deprecate them, and introduce new versions. Hardcoded model names require a code change and redeployment to update routing. Over time, the code accumulates stale model names that route to non-existent models. Different services also pick different model names for the same function, making cost tracking impossible.

**The fix.** Store model assignments in a routing table that is managed separately from the routing code. The routing table is configuration, not code. Updates to model assignments should not require a deployment. Application code never mentions a model name -- it reads from the routing table and uses whatever model is assigned.

### Anti-Pattern 2: No Fallback

The router selects a model and calls it. If the model fails (timeout, error, rate limit), the router returns an error to the user. No secondary or tertiary model is configured.

**Why it is harmful.** Models fail all the time. Provider outages, rate limits, network issues, and model degradation are normal in production. A router without fallback converts every model failure into a user-facing error. At 100 million requests per month and 99.9 percent model availability, you get 100,000 failures per month that could have been handled by a fallback.

**The fix.** Every route must have at least two fallback models. The first fallback should have similar capabilities and cost. The second fallback should be a reliable, broadly capable model (even if more expensive).

```
translation:
  primary:   gpt-4o-mini      (cheap, fast)
  secondary: claude-3-haiku    (backup with same tier)
  tertiary:  gpt-4o            (different provider, capable)
```

### Anti-Pattern 3: Classifying Every Request

The router runs full classification (embedding generation plus prompt-based analysis) on every single request, including trivial ones like "Hello" or "Thanks."

**Why it is harmful.** Classification has a cost in both latency and API usage. Running full classification on trivial requests adds unnecessary overhead. A simple greeting does not need 10ms of embedding computation to determine the appropriate model.

**The fix.** Implement a "fast path" for trivial requests. If the request is under 5 words, contains only greetings or acknowledgments, or matches an explicit skip pattern, route directly to the cheapest model without classification. Reserve full classification for requests that actually need it.

```python
# Fixed: fast path for trivial requests
def route_request(request):
    # Fast path: skip classification for trivial requests
    if is_trivial(request):
        return "gpt-4o-mini"  # Always cheap for trivial

    # Normal path: full classification
    embedding = get_embedding(request.text)
    return classify_and_route(embedding, request)
```

### Anti-Pattern 4: Ignoring Cost Drift

The team configures routing policies based on current model pricing and never revisits them. Six months later, model pricing has changed, a new model is cheaper and more capable, but the routing table still sends all requests to the original models.

**Why it is harmful.** The routing system becomes progressively more expensive over time. The cost savings that justified building the router erode as pricing evolves. The team is paying more than necessary because they are not taking advantage of newer, cheaper models. Over 12 months, the accumulated waste can exceed the original cost savings.

**The fix.** Set up a monthly review of routing policies against current pricing. Subscribe to provider pricing change notifications. Implement a "cost drift" alert that triggers when the average cost per request increases by more than 10 percent without a corresponding change in request volume or distribution.

```
Cost drift over 6 months:
  Month 1:  $5,000  (GPT-4o at $2.50/M tokens for all traffic)
  Month 2:  $5,000  (GPT-4o-mini launches at $0.15/M, not used)
  Month 3:  $4,800  (traffic grows, still on GPT-4o)
  Month 4:  $5,200  (new cheaper model, still not used)
  Month 5:  $5,500  (traffic grows, pricing unchanged)
  Month 6:  $5,800  (could be $700 with updated routing)

  Waste after 6 months: $24,500
```

### Anti-Pattern 5: Routing Everything to the Largest Model

The classification is so conservative that any uncertainty routes the request to the most expensive, most capable model. Over time, 80 percent of requests end up on the flagship model, negating the cost savings of routing.

```yaml
# Anti-pattern: uncertainty goes to flagship
routing_policy:
  uncertainty_behavior: "route_to_most_capable"  # safe but expensive
```

**Why it is harmful.** If 80 percent of traffic goes to the expensive model, the cost savings are negligible. The team might as well not have a router and just route everything to the flagship model directly -- the result and cost would be nearly identical. The router adds complexity and latency without delivering the benefit.

**The fix.** The default for low-confidence classifications should not be "most capable model." It should be "most capable model that meets the minimum quality threshold." This subtle difference means a low-confidence classification might go to a mid-tier model (Claude Sonnet at $3.00/M tokens) instead of the flagship (Claude Opus at $15.00/M tokens). The cost savings are substantial, and the quality impact is minimal because the minimum threshold ensures acceptable output.

### Anti-Pattern 6: No Circuit Breaker

Detecting that a model is failing but continuing to route requests to it. Every request still tries the failed model first, pays the timeout penalty, then falls back. This amplifies latency.

```python
# Anti-pattern: no circuit breaker
def route_with_fallback(category: str):
    primary = get_primary(category)
    response = call_model(primary)  # Always tries failed primary
    if response:
        return response
    return fallback(category)  # Only then tries fallback
```

**Why it is harmful.** Without a circuit breaker, every request pays the latency penalty of trying the failed model before falling back. If GPT-4 is timing out at 10 seconds per request and you have 100 requests per second, you are wasting 1,000 seconds per second on failed attempts. The model provider may also penalize you for sending requests to a degraded endpoint.

**The fix.** Implement a circuit breaker per model. After N consecutive failures (3 by default), skip the model for M seconds (30 by default). After the cooldown, allow one probe request. If it succeeds, close the circuit. If it fails, extend the cooldown.

```python
# Fixed: circuit breaker
class CircuitBreaker:
    def __init__(self, failure_threshold=3, cooldown_seconds=30):
        self.failure_count = 0
        self.failure_threshold = failure_threshold
        self.cooldown_seconds = cooldown_seconds
        self.last_failure_time = 0
        self.state = "closed"  # closed, open, half-open

    def is_allowed(self):
        if self.state == "closed":
            return True
        if self.state == "open":
            elapsed = time.time() - self.last_failure_time
            if elapsed > self.cooldown_seconds:
                self.state = "half-open"
                return True  # Allow one probe request
            return False
        return True  # half-open: allow probe

    def record_failure(self):
        self.failure_count += 1
        self.last_failure_time = time.time()
        if self.failure_count >= self.failure_threshold:
            self.state = "open"

    def record_success(self):
        self.failure_count = 0
        self.state = "closed"
```

### Anti-Pattern 7: Routing Without Observability

Building a router that makes decisions but produces no metrics, logs, or traces. When something breaks, there is no data to debug.

**Why it is harmful.** Without observability, you cannot know if the router is making good decisions. You cannot detect misclassification cascades, cost drift, or stale caches. When something breaks, you have no data to debug. The router becomes a black box that everyone trusts but nobody understands.

**The fix.** Log every routing decision with at minimum: request ID, timestamp, category, config version, selected model, fallback depth, routing latency, and estimated cost. Aggregate these into metrics: per-category request count, per-model cost, average routing latency, fallback rate per model, and classification confidence distribution.

## When NOT to Use This

Model routing is a powerful pattern, but it is not the right solution for every problem. Adding a routing layer introduces complexity, latency, and operational burden. In some scenarios, the costs exceed the benefits.

### Single-Model Setup

If your application uses one model for everything, do not build a router. There is nothing to route to. Wait until you have at least two models with different cost or capability profiles, or until your monthly model spend exceeds $5,000.

**Exception:** If you are using a single model today but plan to add models within the next quarter, build a simple routing abstraction layer (a function that selects the model) even if it currently only returns one option. This makes it easier to add routing later without refactoring the call sites.

### Batch Processing with Fixed Model

If you run nightly batch jobs that process millions of documents through a single fine-tuned model, routing adds no value. The model is fixed, the workload is homogeneous, and there is no decision to make.

**Exception:** If your batch processing pipeline handles heterogeneous documents (some need extraction, some need summarization, some need translation) and different models are better at different tasks, then routing within the batch pipeline may help. But this is a per-document routing decision, not a per-request one.

### Fine-Tuned Single Model

If you have fine-tuned a single model on your specific domain and it performs well across all your request types, adding a router that sends some requests to a different model will likely reduce quality. The fine-tuned model is already optimized for your domain.

**Exception:** If the fine-tuned model is expensive to run (over $0.01 per request) and a large fraction of your requests (over 50 percent) are simple enough for a cheaper model, routing those simple requests away from the fine-tuned model saves significant cost. However, validate that the cheaper model's output is consistent with the fine-tuned model's style and domain knowledge before routing.

### Very Low Traffic

If your application processes under 100 requests per day, the cost savings from routing are negligible. You might save $5-10 per month. The operational complexity of maintaining a routing system is not worth the savings.

**Exception:** If you are building a platform that will scale to high volume, build routing from the start. The architecture decisions are easier to make early than to retrofit later. The routing abstraction layer is cheap to build early and expensive to add later.

### Fixed Latency Budget Under 50ms

If your application requires end-to-end latency under 50ms for every request, there is no room for a routing layer. Even a 5ms router adds 10 percent overhead. In this case, select a single fast model and optimize it directly.

**Exception:** If the 50ms budget includes model inference time (and the model itself takes over 40ms), a 5ms router is acceptable. But if the budget is 50ms total including network round-trips, skip routing entirely.

### Monthly Cost Comparison

| Scenario | Monthly Requests | Without Router | With Router | Savings | Router Worth It? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Low traffic | 10,000 | $12.50 | $3.10 | $9.40 | No -- engineering cost exceeds savings |
| Medium traffic | 1,000,000 | $1,250 | $310 | $940 | Maybe -- depends on latency requirements |

| Very high traffic | 100,000,000 | $125,000 | $31,000 | $94,000 | Absolutely -- router pays for itself in days |

The breakeven point is approximately 1 million requests per month with a 60 percent cost reduction on 70 percent of traffic. Below that, the monthly savings are under $1,000, which is less than the operational cost of maintaining the routing system.

### Decision Checklist

Use this checklist before implementing model routing:

```
Before building a model router, confirm all of these:
  [ ] You use 3 or more distinct models
  [ ] Request complexity varies significantly within your traffic
  [ ] Monthly model spend exceeds $5,000
  [ ] You have at least 1 million requests per month
  [ ] You have the operational capacity to maintain a routing service
  [ ] You have observability in place to monitor routing decisions
  [ ] You accept that routing adds 10-100ms of overhead to every request
  [ ] You have at least two fallback models per category

If you checked 6 or more items: proceed with routing.
If you checked 3 or fewer: consider a simpler approach (single model
  or manually updated rules in application code) instead.
```

## Key Takeaways

- The core trade-off is routing accuracy versus routing latency. Every 10ms of routing latency reduces the end-to-end budget available for the actual model response. The breakeven depends on the cost of misclassification versus the penalty for extra latency.
- Eight decision points define every routing system. Score your application against each one. If your total is under 4, start simple and add complexity only when you hit a specific bottleneck.
- Use an AP approach for the routing service (serve stale cache during partitions) and a CP approach for the authoritative model registry (etcd/ZooKeeper). This combination keeps routers available while maintaining a consistent source of truth for configuration.
- Six failure modes require specific mitigations: misclassification cascade (confidence thresholds and retry escalation), model timeout (aggressive per-model timeouts and circuit breakers), cascading fallback (20 percent headroom and dedicated fallback pools), routing loop (hop limits and version-stamped decisions), stale registry (push-based updates and safe mode), and cost explosion (per-request cost caps and daily regression tests).
- Seven anti-patterns cause most routing incidents: hardcoding model names, no fallback, classifying every request, ignoring cost drift, routing everything to the largest model, no circuit breaker, and routing without observability.
- Model routing is unsuitable for single-model setups, batch processing with fixed models, fine-tuned single models, traffic under 100,000 requests per month, and applications with a latency budget under 50ms. The breakeven point for routing is approximately 1 million requests per month with significant request variety.
- Always version-stamp routing decisions and log every routing hop. Without versioning, you cannot debug routing loops. Without logging, you cannot detect cascading failures. The routing audit log is your primary debugging tool.
- The cost of a misclassification is not symmetrical: sending a simple request to an expensive model wastes money; sending a complex request to a cheap model wastes trust. Design your routing thresholds to err on the side of over-capability for ambiguous cases.
