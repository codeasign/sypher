Two engineers sit in a cramped San Francisco coffee shop, staring at a cloud cost dashboard. Their startup, SwiftSupport, has been live for six weeks with an AI customer service chatbot. The dashboard shows a painful number: they burned over $6,000 on GPT-4 API calls last month. Every customer query, from "where is my order" to a complex multi-part refund dispute, goes through the same expensive model. The founders know cheaper models exist, but they cannot decide which request to send where without breaking their customer experience.

The first attempt at a solution was simple: switch to a smaller, cheaper model for everything. Costs dropped to under $30 a day. But customer satisfaction tanked. The cheaper model could not handle nuanced policy questions. It invented return windows that did not exist. It confused electronics warranties with furniture warranties. Customers escalated to human agents at triple the rate. Within a week, the founders switched back to GPT-4 for everything, accepting the $200-a-day cost as the price of doing business.

The problem escalated. Users complained about latency on simple requests -- a "yes or no" answer to a tracking question took 8 seconds because GPT-4 spent as much cognitive effort on it as on a contract negotiation. The CTO, Maya, pulled a week of raw logs and analyzed 50,000 requests by hand. She found that 65 percent of all queries were simple: order status checks, store hours, return window lookups, shipping address changes. These required almost no reasoning. The remaining 35 percent needed deep policy knowledge, multi-step reasoning, or careful tone management.

That was the breakthrough. Maya realized the system needed a decision layer -- something that looked at each incoming request, determined what kind of task it was, and sent it to the right model for that task. Not a static choice between two models, but a dynamic routing system that adapts per request. The solution was not about picking the perfect model. It was about building an intelligent dispatcher. This dispatcher would need to examine every request, understand what it needed, and match it to the model that could handle it best at the lowest cost. The concept was simple; the execution was everything.

## The Insight

Model Routing is a decision layer that sits between your users and your language models. It examines each incoming request -- the text, the intent, the complexity, the domain -- and sends it to the most appropriate model based on predefined criteria: difficulty, cost tolerance, latency requirements, and capability needs. Instead of every request going to a single model, the router decides dynamically per request.

Think of a shipping dock. Packages arrive on a conveyor belt at a major distribution center. A small padded envelope containing a single document goes to the courier bike -- fast, cheap, and perfectly adequate. A large wooden crate full of electronics goes to the freight truck -- slower, more expensive, but capable of handling the weight and fragility. A refrigerated box of pharmaceuticals goes to the climate-controlled van. An envelope marked "URGENT -- LEGAL" goes to the express courier who drives directly to the destination.

Every package has different requirements: size, fragility, urgency, destination. The dock router examines each package and assigns it to the correct carrier. Without this routing, every package would go via freight truck -- reliable but slow and expensive for small items. The dock does not put every package on the largest truck just to be safe, and it does not put every package on the small van either -- some would not fit or would arrive too late.

Model routing works exactly the same way:

- **Simple queries** -- order status, store hours, basic Q&A -- route to a fast, cheap model like a lightweight instruct-tuned 8B parameter model. These handle 65 percent of your traffic at under 5 percent of your total cost.

- **Medium-complexity queries** -- policy explanations, troubleshooting, product comparisons -- route to a mid-tier model that balances capability and cost. A 70B parameter model handles these at moderate latency with good quality.

- **High-complexity queries** -- multi-step reasoning, dispute resolution, escalation decisions -- route to a frontier model like GPT-4o or Claude Opus. These are the 10 percent of requests that drive 50 percent of your value.

- **Specialized requests** -- image analysis, code generation, structured data extraction -- route to models with specific capabilities. A vision model for screenshots, a code model for debugging, a JSON-tuned model for data extraction.

The routing layer does not just reduce cost. It improves latency (simple queries complete 6x faster), increases reliability through fallback paths (if the primary model fails, the router tries the next best model), and gives you a single control point for managing model behavior across your entire application. The routing layer becomes the central nervous system of your AI infrastructure.

## Core Vocabulary

**Model Router.** The decision engine that inspects each incoming request and determines which model should handle it. The router receives the request payload, evaluates it against routing policies, and returns a model identifier. Production routers operate at under 10 milliseconds decision time and handle 50,000 requests per second across multiple concurrent deployments.

**Routing Policy.** A set of rules or criteria that guide routing decisions. Policies can be simple -- "route intent=tracking to model=fast-tiny" -- or complex -- "if complexity score greater than 0.7 AND domain equals financial, route to frontier model, else route to standard model." Policies are versioned, tested against historical traffic, and deployed through a controlled rollout process.

**Fallback Strategy.** The behavior when a routing decision fails or the selected model underperforms. Common strategies include cascading (try model A, fall back to model B, then C), circuit breaking (stop routing to a failing model for 30 seconds), and degradation (route all traffic to the safest model when uncertainty is high). A good fallback strategy prevents model failures from becoming application failures.

**Latency Budget.** The maximum acceptable response time for a given request type, measured from ingestion to first token. Typical budgets: 500 milliseconds for simple queries, 2 seconds for standard queries, 10 seconds for complex reasoning. The router uses latency budgets to filter out models that cannot meet the deadline. The budget must include classification time, routing table lookup, network round trip, and model inference combined.

**Capability Matching.** The process of aligning a request's requirements with a model's known strengths. Some models excel at code generation. Others produce better creative writing. Some handle structured output more reliably. The model registry stores capability metadata so the router can match requests to models that are actually good at the task rather than assuming all frontier models are equally capable.

**Model Registry.** A catalog of all available models with their metadata: provider, model name, cost per token, average latency, context window size, supported modalities (text, image, audio), capability scores, and current health status. The registry must be highly available and support dynamic updates as models are added, deprecated, or experience degraded performance. Without a registry, the router has no reliable source of truth about what models exist and what they can do.

**Prompt Classifier.** A lightweight classification model that analyzes incoming prompts to determine intent, domain, complexity, and language. Prompt classifiers run in under 50 milliseconds and typically achieve 90 to 95 percent accuracy. They are the most common mechanism for feature extraction in routing systems. The classifier output is the primary input to the routing policy.

**Semantic Router.** A routing variant that uses text embeddings and vector similarity to classify requests. Instead of keyword matching or intent labels, the semantic router compares the incoming embedding against a set of reference embeddings for each route using cosine similarity. This handles ambiguous or novel phrasing that rule-based systems miss, because it captures meaning rather than exact word patterns.

**Cost-Optimized Routing.** A routing strategy that minimizes total cost while meeting quality constraints. The router maintains a cost model for each model (including both token cost and any fixed per-request overhead) and selects the lowest-cost model that can satisfy the request's quality requirements. Typical savings range from 50 to 80 percent compared to using a single frontier model for all traffic.

**Model Gateway.** A unified API layer that abstracts away differences between model providers. The gateway handles authentication, request formatting, retry logic, rate limiting, and response parsing. The router selects a model, and the gateway executes the call against that model's specific API without the application needing to know the provider. The gateway also handles streaming, timeouts, and response normalization.

**Observability.** The practice of collecting metrics, traces, and logs for every routing decision. Key observability data points: which model was selected, why it was selected, how long the decision took, how long the model took, the cost of the request, whether a fallback was triggered, and the final quality score. Without observability, you cannot improve your routing decisions or diagnose routing failures.

**A/B Testing.** The practice of running two routing policies or model configurations side by side on live traffic to compare outcomes. A typical test splits 5 percent of traffic: 2.5 percent goes to policy A and 2.5 percent goes to policy B, while 95 percent runs on the current production policy. Results are compared on quality, cost, latency, and error rate over a 24-hour window before a decision is made.

## How It Actually Works -- Step by Step

A model routing system processes tens of thousands of requests per minute. Here is exactly how one customer query flows through the system from arrival to response.

### Step 1: Request Arrival

A customer types "What is your return policy for electronics purchased 45 days ago?" into the chat widget and presses enter. The application sends an HTTP POST request to the model gateway at `POST /v1/chat/completions` with the message payload. The gateway parses the request, extracts the message text, and attaches a unique request ID: `req_a1b2c3d4e5`. This ID traces the request through every stage of the pipeline. The gateway records the arrival timestamp at nanosecond precision and forwards the request to the model router.

### Step 2: Feature Extraction

The router extracts features from the request that will be used for classification. It computes: message length (12 words total), presence of urgency keywords (none detected -- the query is factual, not urgent), language detection (en -- English), question type (how-to or policy inquiry), and domain keywords identified ("return policy," "electronics," "purchased," "45 days"). The feature extraction process is a simple string analysis pass that runs in under 2 milliseconds. These raw features are fed into the classifier engine for deeper analysis.

Feature extraction is intentionally lightweight. The goal is not to classify the request at this stage but to gather the surface-level signals that the classifier needs. The most important extracted features are the raw text, token count, and any explicit user-tier metadata (the user is on the "premium" plan, which affects cost caps). The extracted features are passed to the classifier as a structured dictionary.

### Step 3: Intent and Complexity Classification

The classifier -- a lightweight BERT-based model with 110 million parameters running on a single GPU instance -- evaluates three dimensions in a single forward pass. Intent classification determines the user goal: "return_policy_inquiry" with 94 percent confidence. Complexity scoring rates the request on a scale of 1 to 10: this query scores 6 because it involves a time condition ("45 days ago") and a category condition ("electronics" versus "furniture" -- the policy differs by product category). Domain classification identifies the subject area: "electronics_returns" with 91 percent confidence. The entire classification takes 38 milliseconds from input to output.

### Step 4: Policy Evaluation

The classifier output -- intent=return_policy_inquiry, complexity=6, domain=electronics_returns -- enters the policy engine. The engine evaluates this feature vector against the current routing policy, which contains 24 rules organized in priority order. Each rule has a condition (the features it matches) and an action (the model to route to). The engine iterates through rules from highest to lowest priority until it finds a match. The most specific rules have the highest priority, and the default catch-all rule has the lowest priority.

Rule 12 (priority 400): "if complexity greater than 5 AND domain in [electronics_returns, financial, legal] then route to frontier_model" -- this rule matches. Complexity of 6 is greater than 5, and electronics_returns is in the target domain list. The policy engine selects model ID `frontier-v1` (GPT-4o, the current frontier model in the registry). If no rule had matched, the default rule (priority 0) would route to `standard-v2` with a "low_confidence" flag attached to the request for observability.

### Step 5: Model Registry Validation

The policy engine passes the chosen model ID (`frontier-v1`) to the model registry for validation. The registry checks three critical conditions. First, the model is active -- not deprecated, not in maintenance mode, the API provider has not announced a sunset date. Second, the model has capacity -- current load is at 63 percent of its rate limit of 3,000 requests per minute, well within the safe threshold of 80 percent. Third, the model is healthy -- the last health check 15 seconds ago returned HTTP 200 with under 2 seconds latency. All three checks pass. The registry returns the model endpoint URL, authentication credentials, and cost metadata. If any check had failed, the registry would return the next best model from the fallback chain defined in the routing policy.

The router constructs a routing decision record containing: request ID `req_a1b2c3d4e5`, selected model `frontier-v1`, routing reason "rule 12 matched at priority 400 -- complexity 6 domain electronics_returns", classifier confidence 94 percent, classification time 38 milliseconds, policy evaluation time 12 milliseconds, registry validation time 5 milliseconds. The router appends this metadata to the request and forwards it to the gateway's model dispatch layer.

### Step 7: Model Execution

The gateway formats the request into the provider's expected schema and sends it. GPT-4o receives the full prompt including the system instructions, retrieved context, and user message. The model generates a response in 4.2 seconds. It explains the 30-day return policy for electronics, notes that a purchase made 45 days ago falls outside the standard window, and offers store credit as a possible exception. The response contains 287 tokens -- 47 input tokens and 240 output tokens. The gateway records the token count, timing data (4,210 milliseconds total latency, 680 milliseconds to first token), and the cost ($0.00287 based on GPT-4o pricing at $10 per million input tokens and $30 per million output tokens). The gateway also tracks whether the model streamed tokens correctly or had any interruptions.

### Step 8: Quality Verification

Before the response is delivered to the user, it passes through a quality verification step. A lightweight evaluator model checks three signals. Refusal detection confirms the model did not refuse to answer something it should have answered -- passed. Hallucination risk checks whether the response contains specific claims that cannot be verified -- low risk at 0.15 out of 1.0, the model cited the standard return window. Policy adherence checks whether the response aligned with the expected tone and format -- passed. The verification takes 210 milliseconds and returns a quality score of 0.91 out of 1.0. If the score had fallen below the threshold of 0.7, the request would have been sent to the fallback model for a second attempt.

### Step 9: Response Delivery

The verified response is packaged with metadata: the selected model name ("GPT-4o"), total latency (4,510 milliseconds including classification and verification), total cost ($0.00287), and quality score (0.91). The gateway sends the HTTP 200 response back to the client. The user sees the answer in their chat window. Total elapsed time from user pressing enter to seeing the response: 4.6 seconds. The gateway also sets caching headers so identical questions from other users in the same session can reuse the response without a new model call. The response headers include the route decision metadata for debugging purposes.

### Step 10: Observability and Feedback Loop

The observability pipeline receives the complete trace for this request: the trace ID, every span (gateway ingress, classification, policy evaluation, registry lookup, gateway dispatch, model execution, quality verification, response egress), each span's duration and metadata, the final cost, the quality score, the routing decision, and whether a fallback was triggered. This trace is indexed and becomes available in the observability dashboard within 2 seconds. The routing team can query for all requests that matched rule 12 today, see their average quality score, and determine whether the rule threshold should be adjusted.

If the user rates the response with a thumbs down, that feedback flows back into the routing system. The system logs the request with a negative quality signal. Over time, a pattern emerges: requests about "electronics returns after 30 days" classified as complexity 6 and routed to the frontier model have a 95 percent satisfaction rate. The routing team can review these patterns weekly and adjust rules -- for example, lowering the complexity threshold for electronics returns from 6 to 4 to route more of these requests to the standard model, saving cost without reducing quality.

## Variants and Flavors

Model routing is not a single pattern. The right approach depends on your traffic patterns, latency requirements, cost constraints, and the predictability of your inputs. Here are the four main variants, each with its own trade-offs.

### Rule-Based Routing

Rule-based routing uses explicit if-then-else rules written by developers or operations teams. Each rule specifies a condition (matching intent, domain, complexity, or other features) and an action (route to a specific model). Rules are evaluated in priority order, and the first match wins. Rules can be expressed in a configuration file, a database table, or a DSL designed for routing.

**When to use rule-based routing:** Your traffic patterns are well understood and stable. You have clear boundaries between request types that do not overlap. Your team includes domain experts who can write and maintain rules. You need auditable, explainable routing decisions for compliance reasons.

**When to avoid rule-based routing:** Your inputs are highly varied or ambiguous. Requests do not fit cleanly into predefined categories. You see frequent novel request types that require new rules. Your rule set has grown beyond 50 rules and becomes difficult to maintain without introducing conflicting conditions.

### Semantic / ML-Based Routing

Semantic routing uses a trained classifier or embedding similarity to route requests. Instead of hand-written rules, the system learns to categorize requests based on examples. Each request is converted to a vector embedding, which is compared against the centroid vectors of each routing category using cosine similarity. The closest matching category determines the route.

**When to use semantic routing:** Your input phrasing is diverse and you cannot enumerate every possible formulation. You have a large dataset of labeled examples to train the classifier or compute centroid embeddings. You want the routing to improve over time as more data arrives.

**When to avoid semantic routing:** You need provably deterministic routing for regulatory compliance. You cannot tolerate the 5 to 10 percent misclassification rate that ML models typically exhibit. Your traffic volume is too low to generate meaningful training data (under 10,000 requests per day).

### Queue-Based Routing with Fallbacks

Queue-based routing places incoming requests into a priority queue and uses separate worker pools assigned to different model tiers. Each worker pulls from the queue and processes requests against its assigned model. If a worker fails or the model becomes unavailable, the request is automatically retried on a different worker with a fallback model.

**When to use queue-based routing:** Reliability is your top priority. You operate in a production environment where model APIs experience intermittent failures, rate limits, or latency spikes. You have multiple models available with overlapping capabilities.

**When to avoid queue-based routing:** Your application requires real-time responses under 200 milliseconds total. The queuing and retry overhead adds 100 to 500 milliseconds per request. You only have a single model available and no fallback options.

### Multi-Stage Routing

Multi-stage routing breaks the routing decision into multiple sequential stages. Stage one does a coarse classification -- is this request simple or complex? Stage two does detailed classification within the coarse bucket. Stage three selects the specific model based on current system conditions. This separation of concerns makes each stage simpler and more testable.

**When to use multi-stage routing:** You have a large and diverse set of requests that require different levels of classification effort. Most requests are simple and can be routed with a cheap classifier, while a small fraction need deeper analysis.

**When to avoid multi-stage routing:** Your request volume is under 1,000 requests per day and the complexity of a multi-stage system is not justified. Your latency budget is under 200 milliseconds total. You have a small number of models (two or three) and simple routing needs.

## Classification Approaches in Depth

Each classification approach has different performance characteristics and failure modes.

### Rule-Based Classification

Rule-based classification uses explicit conditions to determine the request category. Each condition tests a feature of the request and assigns a category based on the result. Rules are typically expressed as a decision tree or a set of if-else statements. A decision tree is more maintainable because it is easier to visualize and modify.

**Accuracy characteristics:** Rule-based classification achieves 70 to 85 percent accuracy for well-defined categories with distinctive keywords. Accuracy drops significantly when categories share vocabulary or when requests use synonyms not covered by rules.

**Common failure modes:** Missing keywords -- a user asks "How do I get my money back?" which contains no billing keywords but is clearly a billing question. Overlapping rules -- a request contains both "code" and "legal," two categories match, and priority determines the winner. Brittle maintenance -- every new request pattern requires a new rule.

**Maintenance burden:** Medium. Rules are easy to add but hard to reason about at scale.

### Embedding-Based Classification

Embedding-based classification converts each request into a vector using a pre-trained embedding model. The vector captures semantic meaning -- requests with similar meaning produce similar vectors. The classifier finds the nearest category centroid in vector space.

**Accuracy characteristics:** 85 to 95 percent accuracy. Handles synonyms naturally. Works across languages with multilingual embedding models. Accuracy degrades for highly domain-specific requests.

**Common failure modes:** Out-of-distribution requests produce low-confidence classifications. Centroids become outdated as request patterns evolve. Embedding model bias can affect certain request types.

**Maintenance burden:** Low to medium. Centroids need periodic recomputation (monthly). No rules to update.

### Prompt-Based Classification

Prompt-based classification sends each request to a small, cheap LLM with a prompt asking it to classify the request. The LLM analyzes the request and returns a structured classification result.

```python
classification_prompt = """System: You are a request classifier.
Classify each request into one of these categories: billing,
technical_support, account, general_qa, legal.

Output JSON: {"category": "...", "complexity": 0-1,
"confidence": 0-1}

User: {request_text}"""
```

**Accuracy characteristics:** 92 to 98 percent accuracy -- the highest of all approaches. The LLM understands nuance and implied intent. However, it adds 200 to 800 milliseconds of latency and costs $0.001 to $0.005 per classification.

**Common failure modes:** The LLM may hallucinate non-existent categories or format output incorrectly. Prompt injection attempts can skew classification. Latency varies based on provider load.

**Maintenance burden:** Low. Adding a new category means updating the system prompt.

### The Hybrid Approach

Most production systems use a hybrid approach:

1. **Fast path:** Rule-based or embedding-based classification for most requests. Runs in under 10 milliseconds.
2. **Slow path:** Prompt-based classification for low-confidence cases. Adds 200 to 800 milliseconds but fires for only 10 to 20 percent of requests.
3. **Safety net:** If both paths disagree, route to the most capable model.

This achieves 96 to 98 percent effective accuracy with average classification latency under 20 milliseconds.

## Cost Analysis and Optimization

Understanding the cost dynamics of model routing is essential for making informed decisions.

### Classification Cost

Each approach has a different cost per request:
- Rule-based: $0 (zero marginal cost)
- Embedding-based: $0.00001 to $0.00005 per request
- Prompt-based: $0.001 to $0.005 per request

For a system processing 1 million requests per day, monthly classification costs:
- Rule-based: $0
- Embedding-based: $300 to $1,500
- Prompt-based: $30,000 to $150,000

Prompt-based classification is only cost-effective for low-volume systems or high-stakes requests.

### Model Inference Cost

Cost savings depend on your traffic distribution and model pricing.

Example with 70 percent simple and 30 percent complex requests:
- Without routing (all GPT-4o): $2.50 per 1M tokens
- With routing (simple to min at $0.15, complex to GPT-4o at $2.50): blended cost of $0.855 per 1M tokens
- Monthly savings at 10M requests: $16,450

At 1M requests per month, routing saves approximately $1,600 per month. At 100M requests per month, savings exceed $160,000.

### Fallback Cost Impact

A 2 percent fallback rate with a 3x cost multiplier adds 6 percent to total inference cost. If the fallback rate exceeds 5 percent, the primary model selection is likely incorrect.

## Mental Model Check

Test your understanding of model routing with these five questions.

**Question 1.** A customer support platform routes 100,000 requests per day to GPT-4o at $15 per million tokens. Average request size is 500 tokens. The team estimates 60 percent of requests could be handled by a small model at $0.30 per million tokens. What is the daily cost savings of implementing model routing?

**Answer.** Current daily cost: 50 million tokens at $15 per million equals $750. With routing: 30 million tokens to the small model at $0.30 equals $9, plus 20 million tokens to GPT-4o at $15 equals $300. Total: $309. Daily savings: $441 or 59 percent.

**Question 2.** A routing system uses a prompt classifier with 92 percent accuracy. One percent of requests truly require a frontier model, but the classifier has a 15 percent false positive rate for the "complex" label. How many requests per 1,000 are incorrectly routed to the expensive model?

**Answer.** For 1,000 requests, 10 truly need the frontier model. The classifier correctly identifies 9. The false positive rate of 15 percent on the remaining 990 means 149 are incorrectly classified as complex. Total routed to frontier: 158 instead of 10. The 92 percent accurate classifier increases costs by 15x on the expensive tier due to the false positive rate.

**Question 3.** Your system uses a cascading fallback with three models: fast-tiny (200ms), standard-v2 (1.2s), and frontier-v1 (4s). Fast-tiny fails 10 percent of the time and standard-v2 fails 2 percent of the time. What is the expected average latency per request?

**Answer.** 90 percent succeed at 200ms. 9.8 percent succeed at 1.2s. 0.2 percent go to frontier at 4s. Expected latency: (0.90 x 0.2s) + (0.098 x 1.2s) + (0.002 x 4.0s) = 0.306 seconds, or approximately 306ms. Cascading fallbacks add only 106ms of average latency over the base model because fallbacks are infrequent.

**Question 4.** A multi-stage router uses classification (40ms), category selection (10ms), instance selection (20ms), observability (15ms), and gateway dispatch (50ms). What is the total routing overhead? If the latency budget is 500ms and the model takes 300ms, does the budget fit?

**Answer.** Total overhead: 135ms. End-to-end: 135ms plus 300ms equals 435ms. The budget of 500ms fits with 65ms of headroom. However, the 135ms overhead represents 31 percent of total response time.

**Question 5.** A routing policy routes "complexity greater than 7" to the frontier model. After three months, 40 percent of requests route to frontier, but only 12 percent need it. What is the likely problem?

**Answer.** The complexity scorer is over-classifying. Possible causes: biased training data, a threshold that is too low, or spurious correlations. Recommended fixes: recalibrate on production traffic, raise the threshold to 8 or 9, add second-opinion checks for borderline scores, and implement monitoring that alerts when the frontier rate deviates from the expected 10 to 15 percent by more than 5 percentage points.
