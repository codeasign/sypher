## Tier 1 — Foundation

### Challenge 1: Explain Retrieval vs. Generation

**Prompt:** In two sentences or fewer, explain the difference between the
retrieval and generation components of a RAG system. Then describe a scenario
where optimizing the retrieval component would help more than optimizing the
generation component.

**Model Answer:**
Retrieval finds the relevant source documents from the knowledge base given a
user query. Generation produces the natural language answer from the LLM using
those retrieved documents as context. Optimizing retrieval matters more when the
documents are large, diverse, or contain precise information that the LLM is
likely to get wrong from memory — for example, asking about the specific
formulation of a legal contract clause. A perfect LLM prompt cannot compensate
for retrieving the wrong contract.

### Challenge 2: Chunking Strategy Choices

**Prompt:** You have two document collections. Collection A contains 10,000
medical research papers averaging 15 pages each. Collection B contains 500
product API documentation pages averaging 2 pages each. Propose a chunking
strategy for each collection, including chunk size, overlap, and splitting
method. Justify your choices.

**Model Answer:**

For Collection A (medical research papers):
- **Chunk size:** 1024 tokens. Medical papers contain dense information with
  precise terminology. Larger chunks preserve the context needed to understand
  technical terminology. A smaller chunk might split a treatment protocol across
  two chunks, losing the complete picture.
- **Overlap:** 200 tokens (20%). Medical research uses specialized vocabulary
  where a term at the boundary of one chunk needs to be accessible in the next
  chunk for continuity.
- **Splitting method:** Recursive splitting by section (abstract, methods,
  results, discussion). Medical papers have well-defined sections, and queries
  often target a specific section. A query about "methodology" should retrieve
  from the methods section, not the discussion.

For Collection B (API documentation):
- **Chunk size:** 256 tokens. API documentation is structured as small,
  self-contained pieces (function signatures, parameter descriptions, return
  values). Small chunks let the retriever find the exact function or parameter
  the user is asking about.
- **Overlap:** 25 tokens (10%). Minimal overlap because API documentation is
  typically not prose-heavy. The overlap handles edge cases where a parameter
  description spans two chunks.
- **Splitting method:** Semantic splitting at function boundaries. Each function
  or method should be a complete chunk. This ensures that a query about a
  specific function retrieves its full signature, parameters, and return type.

### Challenge 3: Embedding Model Selection

**Prompt:** You are building a RAG system for a multilingual European company
with documents in English, German, French, and Spanish. You have narrowed the
choice to three embedding models: text-embedding-3-small (OpenAI, 1536
dimensions), BGE-multilingual-gemma2 (open-source, 768 dimensions), and
Cohere embed-multilingual-v3 (768 dimensions). Recommend one and justify your
choice.

**Model Answer:**

I recommend Cohere embed-multilingual-v3 for this use case.

**Rationale:**
- Cohere embed-multilingual-v3 is specifically trained for multilingual retrieval
  and supports 100+ languages. In cross-lingual retrieval benchmarks (where the
  query is in one language and relevant documents are in another), it
  consistently outperforms the alternatives.
- BGE-multilingual-gemma2 is a strong open-source option but requires self-hosting
  the model, adding operational complexity. The 768-dimension vectors are more
  storage-efficient than the 1536-dimension vectors from text-embedding-3-small.
- text-embedding-3-small is excellent for English but its multilingual
  performance, while good, is not as strong as Cohere's dedicated multilingual
  model. In a European company, cross-lingual queries are common — a German
  employee might ask a question in German and expect to retrieve an English
  document.

**Trade-off:** Cohere is a paid API, while BGE is free. If the budget is tight
and the team has ML infrastructure, BGE-multilingual-gemma2 is a viable
alternative. But for a production system with cross-lingual requirements, the
quality improvement from Cohere justifies the cost.

## Tier 2 — Design

### Challenge 4: Legal Document Q&A System

**Prompt:** Design a RAG system for a law firm that needs to answer questions
about a 500,000-document contract repository. The documents are a mix of
executed contracts, amendments, and correspondence. The system must show which
specific contract clause supports each answer. The firm has 500 lawyers who will
ask approximately 50,000 questions per day.

**Scale Estimates:**
- 500,000 documents, averaging 20 pages each = 10 million pages
- At 500 tokens per page = 5 billion tokens total
- With 512-token chunks and 50-token overlap = approximately 10 million chunks
- Embedding dimension 1536 = 10 million x 1536 x 4 bytes = 61 GB for vectors
- 50,000 queries per day = approximately 580 QPS at peak
- 500 concurrent users = 1000-2000 concurrent connections

**Architecture Design:**

**Key Design Decisions:**
- **Semantic chunking at clause boundaries.** Legal documents have explicit
  clause numbering. Chunking at clause boundaries ensures each chunk is a
  self-contained legal provision. This is critical for citation accuracy.
- **Hybrid search.** Legal language uses precise terminology (frustration of
  purpose, force majeure, indemnification). BM25 keyword search is essential for
  finding exact legal terms. Combined with vector search for semantic similarity
  of concepts.
- **Metadata filtering.** Every chunk is tagged with jurisdiction, contract type,
  effective date, and parties. Queries can filter by jurisdiction to find only
  relevant contracts. This is important because different jurisdictions have
  different legal standards.
- **Clause-level citation.** The LLM is instructed to cite specific clause
  numbers (e.g., "Section 3.2(b)"). The system verifies that the cited clause
  actually exists in the retrieved document.

**Failure Mode:** A lawyer asks about a contract that was amended last week. The
vector index contains the old version. The answer cites the old clause. The
system must detect version conflicts and surface the most recent version.

### Challenge 5: Codebase Assistant

**Prompt:** Design a RAG system for a development team that needs to answer
questions about a 5-million-line Python and JavaScript codebase. The system
should retrieve relevant code, explain it, and generate code snippets. It must
handle function-level, class-level, and file-level queries.

**Scale Estimates:**
- 5 million lines of code across 20,000 files
- Average file: 250 lines, 20 functions or classes
- Total code objects: approximately 400,000 functions and classes
- Embedding model: code-aware (CodeBERT, or fine-tuned OpenAI)
- Chunk size: function/class level (average 50-200 lines, 200-800 tokens)
- 1000 developers, 10,000 queries per day = 115 QPS peak

**Key Design Decisions:**
- **AST-aware chunking.** Parse the code into an abstract syntax tree and chunk
  at function and class boundaries. Each chunk includes the function signature,
  docstring, and body. This preserves syntactic structure.
- **Dependency-aware retrieval.** When a function is retrieved, also retrieve
  its dependencies (imports, called functions, parent classes). This provides
  the LLM with the full context needed to understand the code.
- **Multi-level indexing.** Maintain separate indices for functions, classes,
  files, and documentation. The query router determines which index to search
  based on the query intent.

**Model Answer:**
"I would design a two-tier retrieval system. The first tier identifies which
files or modules are relevant using a coarse-grained index at the file level.
The second tier retrieves specific functions or classes from those files using
a fine-grained index. This two-tier approach reduces the search space and
improves retrieval accuracy. Each function chunk includes its dependency graph
up to two levels deep. The LLM receives the function signature, its direct
dependencies, and the relevant test cases. The system also includes a code
generation safety check: if the LLM generates code that references a function
not in the retrieved context, the system flags it."

### Challenge 6: Customer Support Bot

**Prompt:** Design a RAG system for a customer support bot for a SaaS company
with 50,000 support articles. The bot must handle 1 million conversations per
day. Each conversation averages 3 back-and-forth exchanges. The bot must
escalate to a human agent if it cannot find the answer.

**Scale Estimates:**
- 1 million conversations per day, 3 exchanges each = 3 million queries per day
- Peak QPS: 3 million / 86400 * 2x peak factor = 70 QPS sustained, 140 QPS peak
- 50,000 support articles, average 300 tokens each = 15 million tokens total
- At 512-token chunks = approximately 30,000 chunks (small corpus)
- LLM cost: 100 tokens input (query + system) + 300 tokens output = 400 tokens
  per query. At 3 million queries per day = 1.2 billion tokens per day.
  At $3/M input tokens and $15/M output tokens (GPT-4o) = $3,600 + $18,000 = $21,600/day

**Key Design Decisions:**
- **Aggressive caching.** With 3 million queries per day, even a 30% cache hit
  rate saves $6,500/day. Cache the most common support questions.
- **Conversation context.** The bot must remember the previous turns in the
  conversation. Use a sliding window of the last 3 exchanges to form the query
  context.
- **Escalation with context.** When the bot cannot answer, the entire
  conversation history and the retrieved context are passed to the human agent,
  so the agent does not need to repeat the investigation.

**Model Answer:**
"I would build the system with a tiered retrieval approach. The first tier uses
a fast keyword index to check for FAQ matches. If the query matches a known FAQ
(cosine similarity above 0.95), return the answer immediately without LLM
generation — this handles 15-20% of queries with zero LLM cost. The second tier
uses vector search for non-FAQ queries. The third tier involves a re-ranker for
ambiguous queries. For escalation, I would set a confidence threshold: if the
top-1 relevance score is below 0.6, the bot offers to connect to a human agent
and pre-fills the agent's screen with the conversation context and the
candidate documents that were retrieved."

## Tier 3 — Expert

### Challenge 7: Multi-Modal RAG

**Prompt:** Design a RAG system that handles documents containing both images
and text. The system should answer questions about the content of images (charts,
diagrams, screenshots) as well as the surrounding text. The knowledge base
contains 100,000 technical reports, each with 10-20 embedded images.

**Multiple Valid Approaches:**

**Approach 1: Caption-Only Retrieval**
The simplest approach. Extract image captions and alt text from the documents.
Index the captions as text chunks alongside the document text. When a relevant
caption is retrieved, include the image in the LLM prompt using a multi-modal
model (GPT-4V, Claude Vision, Gemini).

**Trade-offs:** Simple to implement and low latency. But if the image caption is
uninformative ("Figure 1"), the system will not retrieve the image for relevant
queries. Works well for documents with descriptive captions.

**Approach 2: Image Embedding Retrieval**
Generate embeddings for each image using a vision model (CLIP, SigLIP). Store
the image embeddings in a separate vector index. At query time, use the text
query embedding to search both the text index and the image index. Return the
most relevant images and text chunks.

**Trade-offs:** Higher retrieval quality than caption-only. Requires a separate
embedding model for images. Latency increases because you need to search two
indices. Storage doubles (text vectors + image vectors).

**Approach 3: Unified Embedding Space**
Use a model that maps both images and text into the same embedding space (CLIP).
Index both image patches and text chunks using the same model. A single query
vector can retrieve both images and text from the same index.

**Trade-offs:** Elegant architecture with a single index. But CLIP embeddings
(512 dimensions) are lower resolution than dedicated text embedding models (1536
dimensions), so text-only retrieval quality may be lower than a dedicated
text-only index.

### Challenge 8: Real-Time Streaming News RAG

**Prompt:** Design a RAG system that ingests a real-time news feed (1000 articles
per minute) and answers questions about breaking news. The system must have a
latency of under 30 seconds between an article being published and it being
available for retrieval. Users ask questions about recently published events.

**Multiple Valid Approaches:**

**Approach 1: Incremental Indexing with Streaming Pipeline**
Use a streaming ingestion pipeline. Each article is processed as it arrives:
parse, chunk, embed, and insert into the vector index. The index supports
real-time insertion without locking. The query pipeline reads from the same
index.

**Implementation challenges:**
- Vector index insertion must not block queries. HNSW supports batched
  insertions with a lock-free mechanism, but insertions during high query load
  can cause latency spikes.
- The 30-second SLA requires the entire pipeline (fetch, parse, chunk, embed,
  index) to complete in under 30 seconds per article. This is achievable with
  a GPU-backed embedder, but the parser and chunker must also be fast.

**Approach 2: Two-Tier Freshness Architecture**
Maintain two indices: a "hot" index for recent articles (last 24 hours) and a
"cold" index for older articles. New articles are inserted into the hot index
immediately. The hot index is optimized for fast writes (smaller, in-memory).
The cold index is optimized for reads (larger, disk-backed). Queries search both
indices and fuse results.

**Advantages:**
- The hot index can be optimized for write throughput without affecting read
  performance on the larger cold index.
- The hot index is small (1M articles per day at 1000/min), so it can be
  in-memory for fast access.
- Articles are moved from hot to cold in a background batch process.

**Trade-offs:**
- Query latency increases because you must search two indices and fuse results.
- The hot index must be rebuilt or cleared periodically to prevent unbounded
  growth.

**Approach 3: Event-Driven Architecture with Pre-Indexing**
Pre-compute embeddings for common article templates. When a breaking news article
arrives, the system identifies the template and only needs to embed the
differences (new facts, quotes, statistics). This reduces the embedding latency
from 200ms per article to 20ms per article.

**When to use:** This approach is valuable when news articles follow predictable
patterns (earnings reports, sports scores, election results). It is less useful
for general news where each article is substantially different.

**Summary of all approaches:**
- Approach 1 (incremental indexing) is the simplest and most general. It works
  for any news type but requires careful index management to avoid write
  contention.
- Approach 2 (two-tier freshness) is the most robust for high query volume.
  It separates the write-optimized and read-optimized workloads.
- Approach 3 (event-driven pre-indexing) is the most optimized for latency but
  only works for structured news types.
