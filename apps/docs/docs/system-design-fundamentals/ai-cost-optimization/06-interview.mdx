## Why This Topic Appears in Interviews

AI cost optimization is a relatively new interview topic. It appears in three contexts: specialized AI infrastructure roles at companies like OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google; platform engineering roles at AI-first startups where cost management is everyone's concern; and system design rounds where the interviewer wants to see if you consider cost as a first-class constraint alongside latency and throughput.

Companies ask about cost optimization to test whether you understand the economics of AI systems. They want to know if you can design systems that are not just technically sound but also financially sustainable. At scale, a poorly optimized AI system can burn millions of dollars per month. The interviewer wants to see that you factor this into your design decisions.

## Question Types

### Type 1: Design a Cost-Efficient LLM Serving Pipeline

**Question:** You are building an AI writing assistant. Users generate 10 million requests per day. Each request averages 500 input tokens and 200 output tokens. The model costs $3 per million input tokens and $15 per million output tokens. Design a system to minimize costs while keeping p95 latency under 2 seconds.

**What the interviewer tests:** Whether you understand the cost breakdown of LLM inference, caching strategies, model selection, and batching. They want to hear concrete numbers and tradeoffs.

### Type 2: Cost Attribution and Chargeback

**Question:** Your company has five product teams sharing one LLM API account. The monthly bill is $500,000. Design a system to attribute costs to each team and enforce per-team budgets.

**What the interviewer tests:** Cost allocation, metering, budget enforcement, and the organizational challenges of shared AI infrastructure. This question assesses your understanding of multi-tenant systems.

### Type 3: Model Selection Strategy

**Question:** You need to deploy a customer-facing chatbot. You have access to GPT-4 ($30/M input tokens, $60/M output tokens) and GPT-3.5 ($1/M input, $2/M output). 80% of queries are simple FAQs. 20% require complex reasoning. How do you design the routing strategy?

**What the interviewer tests:** Cost-aware routing, tiered model architectures, and the ability to classify queries by complexity. They want to see if you waste expensive models on trivial tasks.

### Type 4: Prompt Compression System Design

**Question:** Your application sends 4,000-token context windows with every request. The context includes retrieved documents that average 60% irrelevant content. Design a system to reduce token usage without losing answer quality.

**What the interviewer tests:** Understanding of prompt compression techniques, retrieval quality, and the cost-quality tradeoff. They want to see if you can reduce costs without degrading the user experience.

### Type 5: Batch Processing Cost Optimization

**Question:** You process 500,000 documents per day through an LLM for summarization. Each document is 3,000 tokens. Throughput matters more than latency -- documents can take up to 5 minutes each. How do you minimize cost?

**What the interviewer tests:** Batch processing economics, the difference between real-time and async pipelines, and how to leverage batch discounts and provider pricing tiers.

### Type 6: Cache Design for LLM Costs

**Question:** Your AI customer support system receives 100,000 queries per day. 30% of queries are exact duplicates, 20% are near-duplicates (asking the same thing in different words), and 50% are unique. Design a caching strategy to minimize API costs.

**What the interviewer tests:** Multi-level caching, semantic caching with embeddings, cache invalidation, and the cost-benefit analysis of different cache layers.

### Type 7: Cost Anomaly Detection System

**Question:** Design a system that detects unexpected spikes in LLM API costs and alerts the engineering team within 5 minutes. The system should handle 10,000 requests per second across 20 different model endpoints.

**What the interviewer tests:** Real-time monitoring, anomaly detection algorithms, cost aggregation at scale, and incident response for cost overruns.

### Type 8: Multi-Cloud Cost Optimization

**Question:** Your AI platform uses three cloud providers (AWS Bedrock, GCP Vertex AI, Azure OpenAI) for LLM inference. Each provider has different pricing, latency profiles, and available models. Design a system that minimizes total cost across all providers.

**What the interviewer tests:** Multi-cloud routing, provider pricing knowledge, data transfer costs, and the complexity of managing heterogeneous infrastructure.

### Type 9: Cost-Quality Tradeoff Decision

**Question:** Your team is building a medical diagnosis assistant. Using GPT-4 costs $0.50 per diagnosis with 99.5% accuracy. Using GPT-3.5 costs $0.05 per diagnosis with 97% accuracy. The cost of a misdiagnosis is approximately $10,000. How do you make the decision?

**What the interviewer tests:** Business judgment, risk assessment, and the ability to factor the cost of errors into the cost optimization equation. This is not a purely technical decision -- it requires understanding the business impact.

### Type 10: Cost Optimization ROI Analysis

**Question:** Your current AI infrastructure costs $1 million per month. You propose building a cost optimization system that will take 3 months to build and cost $200,000 in engineering time. It is projected to save 40% of costs. Should you build it? What factors would change your decision?

**What the interviewer tests:** Financial thinking, understanding of opportunity cost, and ability to calculate ROI of optimization itself. The answer is not a simple yes or no -- it depends on the timeline, growth rate, and alternatives.

## Interviewer Testing Goals

When an interviewer asks about AI cost optimization, they are typically assessing:

- **Cost awareness**: Do you naturally factor cost into design decisions, or do you treat it as an afterthought? The best candidates start discussing cost implications during the requirements gathering phase.

- **Quantitative reasoning**: Can you do back-of-envelope calculations for cost per request, break-even points for caching, and ROI of optimization strategies? Vague answers ("we will cache things") without numbers signal weak understanding.

- **Tradeoff articulation**: Do you understand that every cost optimization comes with a tradeoff? Caching saves money but adds stale-data risk. Model routing adds complexity and latency. Prompt compression can reduce quality.

- **System thinking**: Can you trace cost implications through the entire system, from user request to billing report? Strong candidates discuss cost tracking, allocation, and feedback loops.

- **Production realism**: Do you know what actually works in production versus what sounds good in theory? Candidates who mention specific tools (LiteLLM, Portkey, OpenRouter) and strategies that survived real-world pressure score higher.

## Level Expectations

### L3 (Entry Level)

At this level, you should demonstrate basic cost awareness. Know the difference between model tiers (GPT-4 vs GPT-3.5 pricing). Understand that caching reduces costs. Be able to estimate cost per request given token counts and pricing.

**Sample answer to Type 1:** "With 10 million requests per day, each using 500 input and 200 output tokens, the daily cost would be about $45,000 using GPT-4 pricing. I would look into caching common prompts, using a smaller model for simple tasks, and batching requests where possible."

**What they expect:** You understand the problem and can do basic math. You know the major optimization categories. You may not have deep implementation knowledge.

### L4 (Mid Level)

At L4, you should demonstrate practical knowledge of optimization techniques. You should be able to design a multi-level caching system, explain model routing with concrete examples, and discuss batching strategies. You should know at least two tools or frameworks.

**Sample answer to Type 1:** "I would start by analyzing the request distribution. If 40% of requests are common templates, a semantic cache with embedding similarity can serve them from cache at near-zero cost. I would route the remaining 60% through a model router: simple queries to a fine-tuned Llama 3 8B model hosted on our own GPUs for $0.30 per hour, and complex queries to GPT-4. I would also implement prompt compression to trim irrelevant context from the 4,000-token windows. Estimated savings: 65% reduction from $45,000 to $15,750 per day."

**What they expect:** You can design a multi-layered optimization system with concrete numbers. You understand the tradeoffs of each approach.

### L5 (Senior Level)

At L5, you should demonstrate architectural thinking around cost optimization. You should discuss the cost tracking infrastructure, feedback loops, and organizational processes needed to maintain cost efficiency over time. You should understand how cost optimization interacts with other system properties like latency, availability, and correctness.

**Sample answer to Type 1:** "I would design a three-layer optimization system. Layer one is the model router, which classifies each request by complexity using a lightweight classifier (10ms, $0.00001 per classification) and routes to the appropriate model. Layer two is a multi-level cache: an exact-match Redis cache for identical requests, a semantic cache using a vector database for near-duplicates, and a prefix cache for common conversation starters. Layer three is a batch processor for non-urgent requests that can wait 5 seconds -- these are batched into groups of 8 for processing at half the per-token cost."

**What they expect:** You design systems, not just features. You consider the full lifecycle from implementation to ongoing operations. You provide specific numbers and tradeoffs.

### L6 (Staff Level)

At L6, you should demonstrate organization-wide thinking. You should discuss cost governance frameworks, chargeback models, and how to build a cost-conscious engineering culture. You should understand the business implications of cost optimization -- when to spend more for better quality and when to cut costs aggressively.

**Sample answer to Type 1:** "I would implement a cost governance framework with three components. First, a cost observability platform that tracks cost per feature, per team, and per user cohort in real time -- this allows us to identify which features are burning money. Second, a cost-aware routing system with dynamic model selection that adjusts based on current load, budget remaining, and quality requirements. Third, a cost optimization feedback loop where the system automatically tests cheaper models against production traffic, measures quality degradation using automated eval metrics, and proposes routing changes. I would also implement a chargeback system so each product team sees their AI costs in their own dashboard."

**What they expect:** You think at the organizational level. You understand that cost optimization is not just a technical problem but a cultural and process problem. You can design systems that make cost efficiency sustainable.

### L7 (Principal)

At L7, you should demonstrate industry-level thinking. You should discuss how cost optimization strategies change at different scales (startup vs enterprise vs hyperscaler). You should understand the economics of model training vs inference, the impact of hardware advances on cost, and how to influence industry standards.

**Sample answer to Type 1:** "At this scale, the optimization strategy depends on the growth trajectory. If we are growing 20% month over month, the cost will double every 4 months. I would build a cost capacity planning model that forecasts costs under different optimization scenarios and different growth rates. I would evaluate building our own inference infrastructure using open-weight models -- at 10 million requests per day, the break-even point for GPU ownership is approximately 8 months. I would also establish partnerships with model providers for committed-use discounts. Finally, I would contribute to open-source cost optimization tools to shape the ecosystem in our favor."

**What they expect:** You think in terms of industry trends, economics, and long-term strategy. You understand that cost optimization at this level involves partnerships, infrastructure investments, and ecosystem influence.

### L8 (Distinguished/Fellow)

At L8, you should demonstrate that you shape the industry. You should discuss how your cost optimization strategies influenced company-wide architecture, product decisions, and even the pricing models of your vendors. You should have opinions on where the industry is heading and how to prepare.

**Sample answer to Type 1:** "I would establish AI cost optimization as a core architectural principle, not a reactive measure. This means designing every new feature with a cost budget in the same way we design with latency and availability budgets. I would push for a heterogeneous model architecture where different product surfaces use different models based on willingness to pay -- premium features get the best models while free tier uses quantized, distilled models. I would also work with our model providers to negotiate custom pricing based on our volume and commit to specific usage patterns. The end goal is to reduce cost per token by 90% over 18 months through a combination of infrastructure investment, model optimization, and strategic partnerships."

**What they expect:** You operate at the intersection of technology, business, and industry trends. Your answers shape how the company thinks about cost, not just how the system works.

## FAANG Framework for Cost Optimization Questions

### F -- Frame the Problem

Start by clarifying the scope. Ask: "Are we optimizing for lowest possible cost, or are we optimizing cost subject to quality constraints? What is the maximum acceptable latency? How many users or requests per day are we serving?" Establish the constraints before proposing solutions.

### A -- Analyze the Cost Breakdown

Break down where the money goes. Calculate cost per request: (input_tokens x input_price + output_tokens x output_price) / 1,000,000. Multiply by daily request volume. Identify the biggest cost drivers. Is it input tokens (long context windows), output tokens (long generations), or request volume (many small requests)?

### A -- Assess Optimization Opportunities

Walk through each optimization layer and estimate savings for each. Use a decision matrix to compare options:

| Optimization | Est. Savings | Complexity | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Exact-match cache | 30% | Low | None |
| Model routing | 40% | Medium | Quality risk |
| Prompt compression | 20% | Medium | Accuracy risk |
| Batch processing | 15% | Low | Latency increase |

### N -- Navigate Tradeoffs

For each optimization, explain the tradeoff explicitly. "Model routing saves 40% but requires a classifier that adds 50ms of latency and can misclassify complex queries to a cheap model, degrading quality. We mitigate this by routing ambiguous queries to the expensive model."

### G -- Go Deeper on One Area

Pick the optimization with the highest ROI and design it in detail. Draw the architecture. Describe the data flow. Discuss failure modes. The interviewer wants to see that you can go from concept to implementation.

## Phrase Bank

Use these phrases to communicate cost optimization concepts effectively in interviews:

### Setting Context
- "Let me start by calculating the baseline cost per request."
- "Before optimizing, I want to understand the cost breakdown by component."
- "The cost structure tells us which optimization will have the biggest impact."
- "I always treat cost as a first-class constraint alongside latency and throughput."

### Discussing Tradeoffs
- "Every optimization introduces a tradeoff I need to manage."
- "The cost-quality Pareto frontier shifts as we add more optimization layers."
- "The break-even point for this optimization is approximately X months."
- "There is a diminishing returns curve here -- the first 60% is easy, the next 20% requires significant complexity."

### Explaining Architecture
- "The model router sits between the API gateway and the inference endpoints."
- "The semantic cache indexes embeddings in a vector database for similarity matching."
- "The cost tracker emits metrics on every inference call for real-time monitoring."
- "The budget enforcer acts as a circuit breaker when spending exceeds thresholds."

### Discussing Operations
- "Cost observability is essential -- you cannot optimize what you cannot measure."
- "I would set up automated alerts for cost anomalies with pager duty escalation."
- "Regular cost review cadences with each product team keep everyone accountable."
- "A chargeback model makes cost optimization everyone's responsibility, not just the infrastructure team's."

### Handling Pushback
- "I agree that this adds latency, but the cost savings of 50% justify the 100ms overhead for non-real-time use cases."
- "You are right that prompt compression can reduce quality. I would A/B test it against the uncompressed version before rolling out."
- "The caching invalidation risk is real. I would use a TTL-based approach with automatic purging for time-sensitive content."

## Red Flags

Avoid these mistakes in your answers:

- **Ignoring costs entirely**: If you design a system without once mentioning cost, the interviewer will assume you do not think about it. Even if the question does not explicitly ask about cost, mention it as a constraint.

- **Assuming all models are interchangeable**: If you treat GPT-4 and a fine-tuned Llama 3 8B as equivalent, you miss the entire point of cost optimization. Discuss the quality differences and when each is appropriate.

- **Suggesting caching without discussing cache misses**: Caching is obvious. What distinguishes great answers is discussing cache hit rates, miss handling, stale data, invalidation strategies, and the cost of the cache itself.

- **Proposing complex routing without discussing classification errors**: A model router is only as good as its classifier. If you do not discuss what happens when a complex query gets routed to a cheap model, you miss a critical failure mode.

- **Forgetting about operational costs**: The cost to run the optimization infrastructure itself -- vector databases, caching servers, monitoring systems -- eats into savings. A caching system that costs $5,000 per month to run is only worthwhile if it saves more than that.

- **Treating cost optimization as a one-time project**: Cost optimization is an ongoing process. Model prices change. Usage patterns shift. New models emerge. If you design a static system without feedback loops, it will become suboptimal within months.

- **Proposing a single optimization as a silver bullet**: No single optimization reduces costs by 90%. The best systems combine multiple approaches. If you propose only caching or only model routing, you suggest shallow thinking.

- **Failing to quantify**: "We will use caching to reduce costs" is weak. "We will use a semantic cache with estimated 35% hit rate, saving approximately $15,750 per day" shows you have thought it through.

## Practice Drills

### Drill 1: Cost Calculation Under Pressure

Set a timer for 5 minutes. Given: GPT-4 costs $30/M input tokens, $60/M output tokens. Your application uses 2,000 input tokens and 500 output tokens per request. You process 500,000 requests per day. Calculate daily cost, monthly cost, and identify the biggest cost driver. This should take less than 2 minutes once you are practiced.

### Drill 2: Optimization Stack Design

Set a timer for 15 minutes. Design a three-layer cost optimization stack for an AI chatbot. Draw the architecture (mental or on paper). For each layer, describe: what it does, how much it saves, what tradeoff it introduces, and how you mitigate the risk. Practice this until you can do it without hesitation.

### Drill 3: Tradeoff Walkthrough

Pick any optimization technique and spend 10 minutes listing every tradeoff it introduces. For example, for prompt compression: reduced accuracy, increased latency for decompression, engineering cost to implement, maintenance burden as prompts change, potential for information loss that produces incorrect answers, user experience degradation if truncation removes important context.

### Drill 4: Real-World Scenario Response

Read a case study (Notion AI's model routing, GitHub Copilot's batching, or Jasper's caching strategy -- all covered in the Real World chapter). Spend 10 minutes explaining: what problem they solved, how they solved it, what tradeoffs they accepted, and what you would do differently.

### Drill 5: Budget Enforcement Design

Set a timer for 20 minutes. Design a budget enforcement system that: tracks cost per team in real time, alerts when a team exceeds 80% of monthly budget, throttles requests when budget is exceeded, and generates a chargeback report. Include the API design, database schema, and alerting strategy.

### Drill 6: Cost Anomaly Detection

Set a timer for 15 minutes. Describe how you would detect a cost anomaly caused by a misconfigured retry loop that is re-sending failed requests 50 times each. Cover: detection method, alert threshold, response procedure, and how to prevent recurrence.

### Drill 7: Multi-Provider Strategy

Set a timer for 20 minutes. You have access to three LLM providers with different pricing and latency profiles. Provider A: cheapest, highest latency, limited to small models. Provider B: medium cost, medium latency, good model selection. Provider C: most expensive, lowest latency, best models. Design a routing strategy that minimizes cost while keeping p95 latency under 3 seconds.

### Drill 8: Cost Optimization Review

Take an existing system design you have worked on or studied. Spend 30 minutes applying cost optimization thinking to it. Identify the cost drivers, propose optimization strategies, calculate estimated savings, and document tradeoffs. This builds the habit of cost-aware design.

## Quick Reference: Cost Calculation Formulas

Memorize these formulas for fast calculation during interviews:

**Cost per request:**
```
cost = (input_tokens x input_price_per_million + output_tokens x output_price_per_million) / 1,000,000
```

**Daily cost:**
```
daily_cost = cost_per_request x requests_per_day
```

**Monthly cost:**
```
monthly_cost = daily_cost x 30
```

**Savings from caching:**
```
savings = monthly_cost x cache_hit_rate
```

**Savings from model routing:**
```
savings = monthly_cost x (1 - (cheap_pct x cheap_cost_ratio + expensive_pct x 1.0))
```

**Break-even for self-hosting:**
```
months = setup_cost / (api_cost_per_month - hosting_cost_per_month)
```

**Break-even for reserved capacity:**
```
months = commitment_amount / (payg_cost - reserved_cost)
```

Practice these formulas until you can calculate them in under 30 seconds. The ability to produce concrete numbers on the fly is what separates strong candidates from weak ones.

## Sample Answers Deep Dive

### Type 3 Detailed Answer: Model Selection Strategy

**Question:** You need to deploy a customer-facing chatbot. You have access to GPT-4 ($30/M input, $60/M output) and GPT-3.5 ($1/M input, $2/M output). 80% of queries are simple FAQs. 20% require complex reasoning. How do you design the routing strategy?

**Full answer:**

"First, let me calculate the baseline cost if we use GPT-4 for everything. Assume each simple query uses 500 input tokens and 100 output tokens. Each complex query uses 1,000 input tokens and 300 output tokens. For 1 million queries per day:

Simple queries (800,000): (500 x $30 + 100 x $60) / 1,000,000 = $0.021 per query. Daily: $16,800.
Complex queries (200,000): (1,000 x $30 + 300 x $60) / 1,000,000 = $0.048 per query. Daily: $9,600.
Total daily: $26,400. Total monthly: $792,000.

Now, if I route simple queries to GPT-3.5: (500 x $1 + 100 x $2) / 1,000,000 = $0.0007 per query. Daily: $560.
Complex queries still go to GPT-4: $9,600 per day.
Total daily with routing: $10,160. Total monthly: $304,800.

Savings: $792,000 - $304,800 = $487,200 per month. That is a 61% reduction.

The key design decisions are:
1. How to classify queries as simple vs complex. I would use a lightweight classifier (DistilBERT) trained on a sample of our queries with labels. The classifier runs in under 10ms and costs essentially nothing.
2. What to do when the classifier is unsure. I would set a confidence threshold of 0.8. Below that, route to GPT-4 as a safety net.
3. How to monitor quality. I would sample 1% of queries that were routed to GPT-3.5 and re-run them through GPT-4, comparing the outputs. If the GPT-3.5 quality drops below an acceptable threshold (say 95% of GPT-4 quality), I would adjust the classifier or routing rules.

The risk is misclassification: routing a complex query to GPT-3.5 produces a poor answer. I mitigate this with the confidence threshold and fallback mechanism. I also implement A/B testing: route a random 10% of queries through the new system first, measure quality and cost, and roll out gradually."

### Type 6 Detailed Answer: Cache Design for LLM Costs

**Question:** Your AI customer support system receives 100,000 queries per day. 30% are exact duplicates, 20% are near-duplicates, and 50% are unique. Design a caching strategy to minimize API costs.

**Full answer:**

"I would design a three-tier caching strategy:

**Tier 1: Exact-match cache.** Store the full prompt string as a Redis key with the response as the value. TTL depends on the content: short TTL (1 hour) for time-sensitive information (order status) and long TTL (24 hours) for evergreen content (policies, FAQs). Estimated hit rate: 30% of queries. Infrastructure cost: minimal (Redis runs on existing infrastructure).

**Tier 2: Semantic cache.** For the 20% near-duplicate queries, use a vector database (Pinecone or pgvector). Embed each query and store the embedding with the response. On each query, compute the embedding (using a small, cheap embedding model), and search for similar queries with cosine similarity above 0.92. If found, return the cached response. The 0.92 threshold balances hit rate against accuracy. Estimated additional hit rate: 15% (75% of near-duplicates match). Infrastructure cost: moderate (embedding compute, vector storage).

**Tier 3: Prefix cache.** For the 50% unique queries, many share the same prefix (system prompt, conversation history). Cache the KV cache prefix so the model does not recompute attention over common prefixes. This saves compute time rather than API cost, but it improves throughput and reduces latency.

Total estimated hit rate: 30% + 15% = 45%. Daily savings: 45,000 queries served from cache.

The key challenge is cache invalidation. If a policy changes, all cached responses about that policy must be invalidated. I would use a two-pronged approach: TTL-based expiry for routine freshness, and a purge API for manual invalidation when content changes. I would also implement probabilistic validation: for every 100th cache hit, re-run the query through the model to verify the cached response is still accurate."

## Follow-Up Questions to Expect

Interviewers often drill deeper with follow-ups after your initial answer. Be prepared for:

**Q: "What happens when your classifier fails?"**
A: "I would design a two-tier fallback. First, if the classifier confidence is low, route to the expensive model by default. Second, if the cheap model produces a low-confidence response (measured by token probability), re-route to the expensive model. This catches most failure cases."

**Q: "How do you update the pricing registry when provider prices change?"**
A: "I would automate it. Most providers publish pricing as structured data (JSON or API). I would scrape this daily and validate changes with a manual approval step for significant changes above 10%."

**Q: "How do you know if your optimization is working?"**
A: "I track two metrics: cost per request (primary) and user satisfaction (guardrail). If cost per request drops but user satisfaction also drops, the optimization is too aggressive. I compare the rolling 7-day average of both metrics to the baseline period before the optimization was deployed."

**Q: "What if the cache service goes down?"**
A: "Cache failures are handled gracefully. If Redis is unreachable, the system falls through to the API directly. If the semantic cache service is down, the exact-match cache still works. The failure is logged and the operations team is alerted. The system still serves requests, but at higher cost temporarily."

**Q: "How do you decide between building vs buying cost optimization tools?"**
A: "I evaluate based on timeline and complexity. If we need optimization within a week, I buy (Portkey, OpenRouter, LiteLLM). If we have 3+ months and complex requirements (multi-cloud, custom routing logic), I consider building. Build costs 3x more in engineering time but gives full control."

## Interview Strategy Summary

1. **Always lead with numbers.** Start your answer with a cost calculation. This demonstrates that you understand the economics, not just the technology.

2. **Identify the dominant cost driver.** Is it input tokens, output tokens, or request volume? Your optimization strategy should target the biggest cost first.

3. **Propose a layered approach.** Never suggest a single optimization. Present 3-4 optimizations that work together. Explain how they interact.

4. **Discuss tradeoffs proactively.** For each optimization you propose, mention the downside before the interviewer asks. This shows depth of understanding.

5. **Mention a specific tool or framework.** LiteLLM, Portkey, OpenRouter, or LangSmith. This shows you have real-world experience, not just theoretical knowledge.

6. **Close with monitoring and iteration.** End your answer by explaining how you would measure the impact and adjust over time. This shows operational maturity.

7. **Address the business context.** If the interviewer gives a scenario, connect your technical answer to the business outcome. How much money does this save? How does it affect the user experience?
