Designing a system that satisfies all requirements perfectly is impossible. Requirements conflict. Stakeholders disagree. Budgets are finite. This section covers the core tensions in requirements engineering, the failure modes that derail projects, and the anti-patterns that even experienced teams fall into.

## The Core Tension: Completeness vs Speed

The fundamental tension in requirements engineering is between completeness and delivery speed.

The sweet spot depends on your context:

- **MVP (minimum viable product):** Specify only the Must-Have functional requirements. For NFRs, specify only the ones that could kill the product if wrong. Accept that you will refactor later.
- **Regulated system:** Specify everything. Full requirements traceability. The cost of missing a requirement (regulatory fine, patient harm) vastly exceeds the cost of specification.
- **Internal tool:** light specification. Most requirements emerge during development. What matters is whether it works, not whether it was spec'd upfront.
- **System design interview:** Specify the critical FRs and the top 3-5 NFRs. Show you know they exist and can prioritize.

## Decision Matrix: Choosing What to Specify

Not every requirement deserves the same level of detail. Use this decision matrix:

| Factor | Specify Thoroughly | Specify Lightly |
|---|---|---|
| Business impact | Core revenue flow | Nice-to-have feature |
| Safety/regulatory | Patient data, payments | Internal dashboard |
| Technical risk | New architecture, unfamiliar tech | Well-known pattern |
| Change likelihood | Stable regulation, known domain | Volatile market, early product |
| Integration count | High (many services depend on it) | Low (standalone feature) |
| User count | Millions of users | Dozens of internal users |

### Decision Scenarios

**Scenario 1: Payment processing for an e-commerce app**

- Business impact: Critical (revenue)
- Safety/regulatory: PCI DSS
- Technical risk: High (fraud detection, idempotency)
- Change likelihood: Low (payment flows are standard)
- Integration count: High (order service, inventory service, accounting)
- User count: High
- **Verdict: Specify fully. Every FR and every NFR.**

**Scenario 2: User profile photo upload**

- Business impact: Low (no direct revenue)
- Safety/regulatory: None
- Technical risk: Low (S3 upload, CDN serving)
- Change likelihood: Medium (might switch to avatar generation)
- Integration count: Low (user service only)
- User count: High
- **Verdict: Specify FR, lightly specify NFRs (max file size, allowed formats). The NFR for latency is "good enough" — do not over-specify.**

**Scenario 3: Internal reporting dashboard**

- Business impact: Medium (used for quarterly decisions)
- Safety/regulatory: None
- Technical risk: Low (read-only queries on existing DB)
- Change likelihood: High (metrics change quarterly)
- Integration count: Low
- User count: Low (10 internal users)
- **Verdict: Specify FR as user stories. NFRs are informal: "loads in under 5 seconds."**

## CAP Theorem and Requirements

The CAP theorem is a formal expression of conflicting NFRs in distributed systems. No distributed system can simultaneously provide all three of:

- **Consistency:** Every read receives the most recent write or an error.
- **Availability:** Every request receives a non-error response (not necessarily the latest write).
- **Partition tolerance:** The system continues operating despite network failures between nodes.

Since partitions are inevitable in any distributed system, you must choose between Consistency and Availability during a partition.

### How CAP Maps to NFRs

- **Consistency is an NFR:** "All reads return the most recent write." This maps to strong consistency requirements (banking, inventory).
- **Availability is an NFR:** "The system remains operational even if some nodes fail." This maps to uptime requirements (e-commerce, social media).
- **Partition tolerance is an infrastructure reality:** Networks fail. You must design for partitions.

When the network partitions (and it will), you must choose:

**CP (Consistency + Partition Tolerance):** The system refuses to serve reads or writes on the minority side of a partition. This protects data integrity at the cost of availability. Example: a banking system that returns "service unavailable" rather than showing a stale balance.

**AP (Availability + Partition Tolerance):** The system serves reads and writes on both sides of a partition. When the partition heals, the system reconciles the divergent data. Example: a social media feed that shows slightly stale posts but remains accessible.

### Practical Guidance

Your application's NFRs should drive your CAP choice:

| If your NFR says ... | Your CAP choice ... | Database examples |
|---|---|---|
| "Account balance must always be accurate" | CP | PostgreSQL (synchronous replication), CockroachDB |
| "Website must always load, even during issues" | AP | DynamoDB, Cassandra, Couchbase |
| "Orders must never be duplicated" | CP | MySQL with strong consistency |
| "User comments should appear everywhere" | AP | Cassandra with eventual consistency |
| "Payment transactions are ACID" | CP | Any SQL DB with 2PC |

## Failure Modes

Here are the most common ways requirements go wrong and how to prevent them.

### Failure Mode 1: Requirements Creep

**Description:** The scope of requirements grows continuously without corresponding increases in timeline or budget. A feature that was "search by name" becomes "search by name, category, price range, rating, and location" — each addition reasonable in isolation, collectively catastrophic.

**Early symptoms:** "While we are at it, we should also ..." in every requirements review. Stories marked "M" becoming "L" and then "XL" without acknowledgment.

**Systemic cause:** Lack of a clear scope boundary. No one wants to say no.

**Prevention:**
- Freeze requirements at release boundaries. Anything new goes into the next release.
- Use MoSCoW rigorously. If everything is Must Have, nothing is Must Have.
- Require a cost estimate for new requirements. Show the trade-off: "Adding this feature means we push this other feature to v2."

**Cost of failure:** Delay, budget overrun, team burnout, shipping something worse because too many features were crammed in.

### Failure Mode 2: Silent NFR Assumptions

**Description:** Stakeholders assume NFRs are implied and never state them. The team builds for the NFRs they are used to, which may not match what the stakeholder expects.

**Example:** A team builds a data pipeline that processes 10 GB per day. The stakeholder assumes it can also handle 100 GB with no changes. When the data volume grows, the pipeline crashes. The requirement was never stated.

**Early symptoms:** No NFRs in the requirements document. "Just build it, you know what I mean."

**Systemic cause:** NFRs are invisible to non-technical stakeholders. They do not think about latency, availability, or scalability as requirements.

**Prevention:**
- Ask specific NFR questions during elicitation: "How many users?", "How fast does it need to be?", "What happens if the system goes down for an hour?", "How long can we afford to lose data?"
- Quantify everything. Do not accept "fast enough" — ask "under 500ms?"
- Create NFR checklist templates so nothing is missed.

**Cost of failure:** System collapses under real-world load. Rewrite costs 5x to 50x.

### Failure Mode 3: Impossible NFRs

**Description:** Stakeholders demand NFR targets that are physically or economically impossible. "Zero latency, infinite scalability, and it must run on a Raspberry Pi."

**Example:** A startup with $10K seed funding requires "99.999% availability" and "instant global replication." The infrastructure alone would cost $50K per month.

**Early symptoms:** NFRs that would require technology that does not exist. NFRs contradicted by known physics (speed of light, disk I/O limits).

**Systemic cause:** Stakeholders do not understand the cost of NFRs. They pick targets from industry benchmarks without understanding context.

**Prevention:**
- Map NFRs to costs: "99.99% availability requires multi-region deployment, which will cost approximately $X/month."
- Present trade-off options: "We can achieve 99.9% availability on $2K/month or 99.99% on $15K/month."
- Use the "five whys" technique for extreme NFRs: "Why do you need 99.999%?" "Because we cannot afford downtime." "How much does an hour of downtime cost?" "$5K." "Then 99.9% is fine — that is 8 hours of allowed downtime per year for $40K of cost, versus $180K for 99.99%."

**Cost of failure:** Wasted resources on unnecessary infrastructure, or paralyzed team that cannot ship because they cannot meet impossible targets.

### Failure Mode 4: Conflicting NFRs

**Description:** Two or more NFRs cannot be simultaneously satisfied. Example: "Lowest possible cost" and "highest possible availability."

**Example:** A team must deliver a system with "P99 latency under 50ms" and "runs on consumer-grade hardware with no redundancy."

**Early symptoms:** Requirements approved without cross-reference checking. Each requirement sounds reasonable in isolation.

**Systemic cause:** Requirements are gathered in silos (one person writes performance, another writes cost) and never reviewed together.

**Prevention:**
- Cross-reference check: for every NFR, ask "does this conflict with any other requirement?"
- Use a conflict matrix. For each pair of NFRs, mark them as aligned, neutral, or conflicting.
- When conflicts are found, negotiate the priority: "Which matters more — cost or latency?"

**Cost of failure:** Team wastes weeks trying to meet both impossible targets, or makes an arbitrary trade-off that stakeholders reject.

### Failure Mode 5: Vague Requirements

**Description:** Requirements that cannot be tested because they lack specificity. "The system should be user-friendly." "The system should be fast."

**Example:** "The system must handle high traffic." (What is high? 100 users? 1 million? Over what time period?)

**Early symptoms:** Stakeholders nod along during requirements review. No one objects because the requirement sounds right. No one can write a test for it.

**Systemic cause:** The person writing the requirement does not know the exact target or does not want to be held to a specific number.

**Prevention:**
- Apply the "testability test": if you cannot write a pass/fail test for it, the requirement is not specific enough.
- Convert adjectives to numbers: "fast" becomes "P99 under 200ms," "user-friendly" becomes "new user completes core task in under 3 minutes without help."
- For every vague requirement, ask "how will we know when this is done?"

**Cost of failure:** The requirement is interpreted differently by each stakeholder. Developer builds one thing, QA tests another, customer expects a third. Rework at every stage.

## Anti-Patterns

Beyond failure modes, there are recurring patterns of behavior that systematically produce poor requirements.

### Anti-Pattern 1: Requirements by Anecdote

**Description:** A single customer complaint or executive whim becomes a hard requirement. "The CEO's friend said the font is too small, so make the font size configurable — P0."

**Why it is harmful:** One data point replaces systematic thinking. Resources are diverted from higher-impact work. The team learns that loud voices trump data.

**Prevention:** Require evidence for requirements. "How many users does this affect?" "What is the expected impact on conversion/revenue?" A single anecdote should generate a "Could Have" at most.

### Anti-Pattern 2: Everything Is P0

**Description:** Every requirement is labeled "Critical" or "Must Have." The prioritization process becomes meaningless.

**Why it is harmful:** Without prioritization, the team has no guidance when trade-offs must be made. Everything gets partial attention. The most critical items slip because there is too much to do.

**Prevention:** Set hard limits per release: "10 Must Haves maximum. If a new Must Have is added, an existing one must be demoted." Use the MoSCoW percentages as a forcing function.

### Anti-Pattern 3: Ignoring NFRs Until Production

**Description:** The team focuses entirely on functional requirements during design and development. NFRs are addressed only when the system fails in production.

**Why it is harmful:** Retrofitting NFRs is expensive. A system not designed for horizontal scaling requires architecture changes that touch every component. Security retrofits require breaking changes to data models and APIs.

**Prevention:** Make NFRs explicit in the first requirements document. Even if the NFRs are loose ("availability: 99% for MVP"), writing them down ensures they are tracked.

### Anti-Pattern 4: Requirements as a Checklist

**Description:** The team treats the requirements document as a checklist of items to mark done, rather than as a guide to system behavior. They implement each requirement in isolation without considering how they interact.

**Why it is harmful:** Requirements interact in complex ways. Implementing "login with Google OAuth" separately from "remember me for 30 days" leads to token refresh issues. Implementing "search products" separately from "search results must load in under 200ms" leads to a search that works but is too slow.

**Prevention:** Review requirements as a system, not as individual items. For each requirement, ask "how does this interact with each other requirement?" Write integration tests that verify multiple requirements simultaneously.

### Anti-Pattern 5: The Frozen Specification

**Description:** Requirements are frozen the day after sign-off and never revisited. The team builds to the specification while the market moves.

**Why it is harmful:** By the time the system ships, the requirements may be outdated. Competitors have filled gaps. Customer needs have shifted.

**Prevention:** Build in review cycles. Revisit NFRs every quarter. Update the requirements document as the team learns more. In agile environments, use a backlog that evolves.

## When the Full Requirements Process is NOT Worth It

There are situations where the formal requirements process described in this topic is overkill. Recognize them to avoid process for its own sake.

### When to Go Light

**Side projects and prototypes.** If you are building something for yourself or a small group, write the requirements on a whiteboard or index card. The cost of formalizing is higher than the cost of rework.

**Internal tools with fewer than 20 users.** The users are also the developers or sit in the same room. Requirements emerge from conversation. Write a one-pager at most.

**Temporary systems.** If the system has a planned lifespan of under 6 months, do not over-specify. Ship it, use it, retire it.

**Well-understood domains with established patterns.** Building the 100th e-commerce checkout is not novel. Use your previous spec as a template. Focus on the differences.

**Hackathons and experiments.** Speed is everything. Skip formal requirements and test your hypothesis directly.

### When to Go Heavy

**Safety-critical systems** (medical devices, aircraft control, autonomous vehicles). Lives depend on getting requirements right. Full traceability is mandatory.

**Financial systems** (payments, trading, banking). Regulatory compliance requires documented requirements and audit trails.

**Government contracts.** The contract itself is the requirements document. Miss a requirement and you may not get paid.

**Systems at scale.** When you have millions of users, every NFR violation causes visible damage. Invest in getting them right.

## Practical Checklist for Every Project

Before you move from requirements to design, run this quick checklist:

**Functional requirements:**
- [ ] Can you list the top 5 user-facing features?
- [ ] Can you describe the primary workflow in 5 steps or fewer?
- [ ] Are there any regulatory or compliance constraints?
- [ ] Do you know which FRs are Must Have vs Nice to Have?
- [ ] Is each FR testable with a pass/fail test?

**Non-functional requirements:**
- [ ] Do you have a target for latency (P50, P95, P99)?
- [ ] Do you have a target for availability (percentage or minutes of downtime)?
- [ ] Do you have a target for throughput (requests per second)?
- [ ] Do you have a target for data durability (acceptable data loss window)?
- [ ] Do you have a security compliance requirement (SOC 2, PCI DSS, HIPAA)?
- [ ] Do you have a cost budget (infrastructure cost per month or per transaction)?
- [ ] Do you know the expected traffic growth rate over the next 12 months?
- [ ] Have you checked for conflicts between any of the above?

---

**Key takeaway:** Requirements engineering is about managing trade-offs and avoiding known failure modes. The goal is not a perfect document — it is a shared understanding that lets the team make good decisions under uncertainty. Use the tools (MoSCoW, Kano, CAP, conflict matrices), watch for anti-patterns, and calibrate your process rigor to the stakes of the project.
