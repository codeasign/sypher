The best way to understand FR vs NFR is to see how the industry's most respected engineering organizations handle the distinction. This section examines practices at Google, Amazon, Netflix, and Uber, along with open-source tools and cloud frameworks that formalize the FR/NFR boundary.

## Google: SRE and the SLI/SLO Framework

Google's Site Reliability Engineering (SRE) book, written by Google engineers Betsy Beyer, Chris Jones, Jennifer Petoff, and Niall Richard Murphy, transformed how the industry thinks about non-functional requirements. The key insight: NFRs should be treated with engineering rigor, not as aspirational goals.

### SLIs, SLOs, and Error Budgets

Google's framework defines three levels:

- **SLI (Service Level Indicator):** The raw measurement. "Latency at the 99th percentile for search requests over a 1-minute window."
- **SLO (Service Level Objective):** The target derived from the NFR. "99.9% of requests complete in under 100ms over a 30-day rolling window."
- **Error Budget:** The allowed headroom. "0.1% of requests can exceed 100ms. That gives us 43 minutes of 'bad' requests per month."

### How This Connects to FR vs NFR

Google's framework separates concerns cleanly:

- **Functional requirements** become service features and API contracts. "Search returns results matching the query."
- **Non-functional requirements** become SLIs, SLOs, and error budgets. "Search returns results in under 100ms at P99."

The error budget creates a direct business trade-off. When the error budget is exhausted, teams stop shipping features (functional changes) and focus on reliability (non-functional improvements). This forces explicit prioritization between FR and NFR work.

### Lesson 1: Quantify Everything

Google does not accept "fast" or "reliable" as requirements. Every NFR must have:

- A specific measurement (latency at which percentile, measured over what window)
- A specific target (99.9% within threshold)
- A time window (30-day rolling)
- An error budget (the allowed violation rate)

**Your takeaway:** When someone says "the system should be fast," ask "at which percentile, under what window, for which operation, and what is the exact threshold?"

## Amazon: Working Backwards from the Press Release

Amazon's "Working Backwards" product development process, documented by former Amazon executives Colin Bryar and Bill Carr, starts with the customer and works backwards to requirements. The process produces both FRs and NFRs as a byproduct.

### The Press Release

Every new feature at Amazon starts with a mock press release that announces the feature as if it were already launched. The press release covers:

- **The headline:** What is the feature, in one line?
- **The customer problem:** What does this solve?
- **The solution:** How does it work? (Functional requirements)
- **The customer benefit:** Why will customers care?
- **The quantitative impact:** What measurable result? (Non-functional requirements)

### Example: Amazon Prime Video

**Press release excerpt:**

"Today, Amazon announced that Prime Video now loads 40% faster than last year. Customers in 200+ countries can start watching their favorite shows in under 3 seconds, even on 5 Mbps connections. The service maintains 99.99% uptime during peak hours, with no buffering interruptions for 99.5% of playback sessions."

**Functional requirements extracted:**
- Customer can browse catalog by genre, actor, or title.
- Customer can start playback on any supported device.
- System remembers playback position across devices.

**Non-functional requirements extracted:**
- Video starts in under 3 seconds on 5 Mbps connections.
- 99.99% availability for streaming during peak hours.
- 99.5% of playback sessions experience zero buffering.
- Available in 200+ countries.

### Lesson 2: NFRs Are Customer-Facing

Amazon treats NFRs as customer-facing commitments, not internal technical details. The press release and FAQ contain NFR targets because customers care about speed, reliability, and availability.

**Your takeaway:** When writing requirements, ask "would a customer care about this NFR?" If the answer is no, consider whether the NFR matters. If the answer is yes, write it in customer-facing language with specific numbers.

## Netflix: Chaos Engineering for NFR Validation

Netflix pioneered chaos engineering as a systematic approach to verifying non-functional requirements in production. The core insight: you cannot verify NFRs like availability and resilience in a test environment — you must test them in production.

### The Chaos Engineering Approach

Netflix built a suite of tools to continuously test NFRs:

- **Chaos Monkey:** Randomly terminates production instances to verify the system survives instance failures.
- **Latency Monkey:** Introduces artificial delays to verify the system handles slow dependencies.
- **Conformity Monkey:** Checks every instance is running the expected configuration and terminates non-conforming instances.
- **Chaos Gorilla:** Simulates an entire AWS availability zone failure.

### Lesson 3: Test NFRs Continuously

Netflix does not wait for a crisis to verify NFRs. Every production system continuously validates its own non-functional requirements. If an NFR violation is detected, the incident response process triggers automatically.

**Your takeaway:** Add chaos engineering experiments to your NFR validation plan. At minimum, test instance failure, dependency latency, and traffic spikes. Scale up to zone failure and region failure as your system matures.

## Uber: Golden Signals for NFR Monitoring

Uber's engineering blog popularized the concept of "golden signals" — the four metrics that every system should monitor to understand its non-functional health.

### The Four Golden Signals

Uber defines these four measurement categories, based on Google SRE practices:

1. **Latency:** The time it takes to service a request. Measures the NFR for response speed.
2. **Traffic:** The demand on the system. Measures whether throughput targets are being met.
3. **Errors:** The rate of failed requests. Measures reliability NFR compliance.
4. **Saturation:** How full the system is. Measures capacity NFR headroom.

### Lesson 4: Monitor NFRs Continuously

Uber's golden signals approach makes NFR monitoring continuous and actionable. Every team can see at a glance whether their NFR targets are being met.

**Your takeaway:** For each NFR, define which golden signal category it belongs to, set up a dashboard with the current value and target, and alert when the NFR is at risk.

## Open Source: OpenAPI/Swagger for FRs

The OpenAPI Specification (formerly Swagger) is the industry standard for documenting functional requirements of APIs. It provides a machine-readable format for describing:

- What endpoints exist (FR: "the service provides a /search endpoint")
- What parameters each endpoint accepts (FR: "accepts query string q, page, per_page")
- What responses each endpoint returns (FR: "returns a JSON array of results")
- What authentication is required (FR: "requires Bearer token in Authorization header")
- What status codes are returned (FR: "200 on success, 401 on auth failure")

OpenAPI is purely about functional requirements. It does not and cannot express:

- How fast the endpoint must be
- How available the endpoint must be
- How many concurrent requests it must handle
- What happens under load

### The Missing Piece

Every OpenAPI spec needs a companion document or system that captures the NFRs. Some teams embed NFRs in OpenAPI extensions (x-nfr-latency, x-nfr-availability). Others maintain a separate NFR registry.

**Your takeaway:** Use OpenAPI to document functional requirements precisely and automatically generate API tests. But recognize that OpenAPI covers only FRs — you need a separate mechanism (or extensions) for NFRs.

## Open Source: Prometheus for NFR Monitoring

Prometheus is the industry standard open-source monitoring system for non-functional requirement tracking. Its architecture reflects the Google SRE philosophy of measuring SLIs and tracking SLOs.

### How Prometheus Maps to NFRs

| NFR | Prometheus Metric | PromQL Query |
|---|---|---|
| P99 latency under 200ms | http_request_duration_seconds | histogram_quantile(0.99, rate(http_request_duration_seconds_bucket[5m])) |
| 99.9% availability | http_requests_total (status) | sum(rate(http_requests_total&#123;status!~"5.."&#125;[5m])) / sum(rate(http_requests_total[5m])) |
| Error rate under 1% | http_requests_total (status) | sum(rate(http_requests_total&#123;status=~"5.."&#125;[5m])) / sum(rate(http_requests_total[5m])) |
| Throughput at least 100 RPS | http_requests_total | sum(rate(http_requests_total[1m])) |
| Queue depth under 500 | queue_depth | queue_depth |

### Lesson 5: Automate NFR Verification

Prometheus plus Grafana enables automated NFR dashboards that show in real time whether each NFR target is met. When paired with Alertmanager, NFR violations trigger immediate notification.

**Your takeaway:** Set up Prometheus as your NFR monitoring backbone from day one. Define recording rules for each SLI, create Grafana dashboards for each SLO, and configure alerts that fire when error budgets approach exhaustion.

## Cloud Framework: AWS Well-Architected

AWS's Well-Architected Framework provides a structured approach to evaluating non-functional requirements across six pillars:

1. **Operational Excellence:** Run and monitor systems. NFRs: deployment automation, observability, incident response time.
2. **Security:** Protect data and systems. NFRs: encryption, access control, audit logging, compliance scope.
3. **Reliability:** Recover from failures. NFRs: MTBF, MTTR, backup RPO/RTO, failover automation.
4. **Performance Efficiency:** Use resources effectively. NFRs: latency, throughput, cost-per-request.
5. **Cost Optimization:** Avoid unnecessary cost. NFRs: infrastructure cost, cost-per-transaction, waste elimination.
6. **Sustainability:** Minimize environmental impact. NFRs: energy consumption, carbon footprint per request.

### Lesson 6: Use a Structured NFR Framework

AWS Well-Architected provides a comprehensive checklist that ensures you do not forget any NFR category. Review your system against all six pillars during design. Even if you are not on AWS, the categories are universal.

**Your takeaway:** Use the Well-Architected Framework (or an equivalent like Google's Architecture Framework or Azure Well-Architected) as your NFR checklist. Review against it during every major design phase.

## Summary: Industry Lessons for FR vs NFR

| Company / Tool | Key Lesson | Your Action |
|---|---|---|
| Google SRE | Define SLIs, SLOs, and error budgets for every NFR. | Convert every NFR to a measurable SLI with a target SLO. |
| Amazon | Start from the customer press release to extract both FRs and NFRs. | Write a one-page press release for your next feature. Extract FRs and NFRs from it. |
| Netflix | Validate NFRs in production using chaos engineering. | Define 3 chaos experiments: instance failure, dependency latency, traffic spike. |
| Uber | Monitor NFRs via golden signals: latency, traffic, errors, saturation. | Set up a golden signals dashboard for every service. |
| OpenAPI/Swagger | Document FRs precisely in machine-readable format. | Use OpenAPI for all APIs. Extend with NFR annotations. |
| Prometheus | Automate NFR monitoring with metrics and alerts. | Instrument every service with Prometheus metrics from day one. |
| AWS Well-Architected | Use a structured framework to cover all NFR categories. | Run a Well-Architected review against your system design. |

---

**Key takeaway:** The industry's best engineering organizations treat non-functional requirements with the same rigor as functional requirements. They measure NFRs continuously, validate them in production, and tie them to business outcomes. Adopting these practices — even a simplified version — will dramatically improve your system designs.
