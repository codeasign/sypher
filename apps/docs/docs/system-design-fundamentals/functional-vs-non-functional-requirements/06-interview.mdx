In system design interviews, how you handle requirements — especially the distinction between functional and non-functional — often determines whether you pass. This section covers exactly what interviewers test, how to frame your answers, and the specific language that signals senior-level thinking.

## Why Interviewers Care About This

Interviewers ask about requirements for three reasons, and understanding which reason is active helps you tailor your answer:

### Reason 1: They want to see if you know the difference

This is the easiest version. Interviewers ask "what are the functional and non-functional requirements?" to check that you can categorize correctly. Your answer should be crisp and confident:

- Functional: "The system must allow users to create, read, update, and delete posts."
- Non-functional: "The system must serve feed requests with P99 latency under 500ms, be available 99.9% of the time, and handle 1 million daily active users."

### Reason 2: They want to see if you can prioritize

Harder. The interviewer gives you a vague prompt and watches to see which requirements you extract first and which you deprioritize. They want to see you identify the 3-5 most important requirements that shape the architecture, not list 30 requirements of varying importance.

- Weak: "The user needs to log in, post, comment, like, follow, search, message, upload photos, change settings, reset password..."
- Strong: "The core functional requirements are creating posts and viewing a feed. The critical non-functional requirements are feed latency under 200ms and 99.9% availability. Everything else — comments, likes, messaging — is secondary and can be addressed after the core architecture is established."

### Reason 3: They want to see if you can drive requirements gathering

Hardest. The interviewer gives you a one-sentence prompt and expects you to ask clarifying questions that surface the real requirements. They are testing your ability to do requirements elicitation live.

- Weak: Accept the prompt at face value and jump to architecture.
- Strong: Ask 3-5 clarifying questions, then summarize: "From our discussion, the FRs are X, Y, Z. The NFRs are: P99 latency under 200ms for reads, 99.9% availability, and 10 million daily active users. Does this match your expectations?"

## Question Phrasings and How to Handle Them

### Phrasing 1: "Design Twitter."

**What they are testing:** Can you break down a well-known product into requirements before discussing architecture? Do you know which requirements drive the architecture?

**Your approach:**
1. Start with clarification: "I will assume approximately 100 million daily active users, with 10x traffic spikes during major events. Does that match your expectation?"
2. Extract FRs: "The core functional requirements are: post tweets (text only for this scope), view timeline, follow users, and search tweets."
3. Extract NFRs: "The key non-functional requirements are: timeline load under 200ms P99, 99.9% availability, write-heavy for tweets but read-heavy for timelines."
4. Validate: "These are my top requirements. Are there any other critical ones I should prioritize?"

### Phrasing 2: "We need to build a URL shortener."

**What they are testing:** Can you extract requirements from a simple prompt? Do you ask about scale, durability, and analytics?

**Your approach:**
1. Ask clarifying questions: "How many URLs per month? How long should shortened URLs live? Do we need analytics on click counts? Should the short URLs be guessable?"
2. Extract FRs: "A user submits a long URL and gets a short code. Visiting the short URL redirects to the original. The system tracks total clicks per URL."
3. Extract NFRs: "Redirect must complete in under 50ms (every millisecond of redirect delay costs conversion). The system must support 10,000 writes/second at peak. Short codes must be unique for 10+ years. 99.99% availability for the redirect service."

### Phrasing 3: "Design the backend for a ride-sharing app."

**What they are testing:** Can you identify real-time matching, geospatial queries, and availability requirements? Do you recognize that the core FR (matching riders with drivers) has NFRs that dominate the architecture?

**Your approach:**
1. Extract FRs: "Rider requests a ride. System matches rider with nearest available driver. Rider sees driver location and ETA. Driver accepts or declines. System processes payment after trip ends."
2. Extract NFRs: "Match must complete in under 3 seconds. Driver location must update every 5 seconds. 99.99% availability during peak hours (Friday and Saturday nights). Geospatial queries must return nearest drivers in under 100ms. System handles 100K concurrent users at peak."

### Phrasing 4: "Design a distributed rate limiter."

**What they are testing:** This is a component-level question. The FR is simple (limit requests per user). The NFRs are critical (low latency, no single point of failure, accurate across nodes).

**Your approach:**
1. Extract FRs: "Given a user ID and API endpoint, the rate limiter must allow or reject the request based on configurable limits (e.g., 100 requests per minute). Returns 200 for allowed, 429 for rejected."
2. Extract NFRs: "Rate limit check must complete in under 5ms to avoid adding latency. 99.999% availability — rate limiter failure takes down the entire API. Accurate within 5% across distributed nodes. Supports 1 million checks per second."

### Phrasing 5: "What are the requirements for this system?"

**What they are testing:** Open-ended. Can you drive the conversation? Do you know what to ask?

**Your approach:**
1. Ask about users: "How many monthly active users? What is the growth rate? What regions?"
2. Ask about usage patterns: "Read-heavy or write-heavy? Are there traffic spikes? What is the peak-to-average ratio?"
3. Ask about tolerance: "What is the acceptable latency? What happens if the system goes down for 5 minutes, for 1 hour?"
4. Summarize and validate: "Based on your answers, I see three critical FRs and four NFRs that drive the architecture. Let me list them..."

## What Interviewers Are Looking For at Each Level

### Entry-Level / New Grad

- Can you define functional and non-functional requirements correctly?
- Can you list 2-3 requirements for each category from a given prompt?
- Do you understand why NFRs matter?

**Good:** "Functional requirements are what the system does. For Twitter, users can post tweets and view a timeline. Non-functional requirements are how well it does it — like loading the timeline in under 500ms."

**Great:** Same, plus you connect NFRs to architecture: "The 500ms latency requirement means I need a cache layer and a CDN."

### Mid-Level (3-5 years)

- Can you prioritize requirements and identify which ones drive architecture?
- Do you quantify NFRs with specific numbers, not vague terms?
- Can you handle conflicting requirements?

**Good:** "The most important NFRs are timeline latency under 200ms and 99.9% availability. These will drive caching, read replicas, and multi-AZ deployment."

**Great:** "I identify a potential conflict between the latency requirement (under 200ms) and the consistency requirement (immediate timeline updates for new tweets). I would prioritize latency for the timeline view and use eventual consistency, with a separate mechanism for the poster to see their own tweet immediately."

### Senior (6-10 years)

- Can you drive the requirements gathering conversation?
- Do you ask about cost, business impact, and error budgets?
- Do you account for growth and changing requirements over time?

**Good:** "Before I design, I need to understand the scale: how many users, what regions, what are the latency and availability targets?"

**Great:** "I also need to understand your cost constraints — a startup with 100 users has different NFR priorities than a mature platform with 100 million users. Is this a greenfield build or are you replacing an existing system? That affects whether we need backward compatibility, which is itself a constraint that shapes the architecture."

### Staff+ (10+ years)

- Can you discuss requirements uncertainty and how to handle it?
- Do you propose iterative approaches where requirements are refined over time?
- Can you recognize when a stakeholder's stated requirement is not the real requirement?

**Good:** "I have designed similar systems before. The typical NFRs for this domain are X, Y, Z. Let me validate these with you."

**Great:** "The requirement you stated — P99 under 50ms — is extremely aggressive for this type of system. Let me walk through what that costs. To achieve 50ms P99, we need dedicated hardware, edge caching, and a global CDN. The monthly bill would be approximately $50K. If you relax to 200ms P99, that drops to $8K and still provides a good user experience. Can we start with 200ms and optimize to 50ms if the data shows we need it?"

## Phrase Bank: What to Say and What to Avoid

### Instead of saying "the system should be fast" say:

- "The API should respond with P99 latency under 200ms."
- "The user should see the first paint within 1 second."
- "The data pipeline should process 10,000 events per second."

### Instead of saying "the system should be reliable" say:

- "The system should maintain 99.9% availability."
- "Data durability should be 99.999999% (eight nines)."
- "The maximum acceptable data loss is 1 minute of writes."

### Instead of saying "the system should handle growth" say:

- "The system should scale horizontally to handle 10x traffic spikes."
- "Adding capacity should require no architecture changes."
- "The system should maintain P99 latency under 200ms at up to 50,000 requests per second."

### Instead of saying "that depends on the requirements" say:

- "Let me list what I assume for now. Correct me if I am wrong."
- "I will start with these core requirements. As we discuss, I can refine them."
- "Given your description, the critical requirements are X, Y, Z. Are there any others I should prioritize?"

## Practice Drills

### Drill 1: Requirements Classification

Take any product you use daily. Write down 5 functional requirements and 5 non-functional requirements. Be specific with NFRs — use numbers. Do this for:

- Google Search
- YouTube
- Uber
- Slack

**Time limit:** 5 minutes per product.

### Drill 2: Vague to Specific

Take these vague requirements and rewrite them with specific, testable NFRs:

1. "The platform must be user-friendly."
2. "The API must perform well under load."
3. "The system must be secure."
4. "Data must never be lost."
5. "The mobile app should feel instant."

**Sample answer for #1:** "A new user must complete the onboarding flow (register, create first project, invite a teammate) in under 4 minutes without documentation. The onboarding completion rate must be above 70%."

### Drill 3: Conflict Detection

Identify the conflict in each requirement pair and propose a resolution:

1. "P99 latency under 50ms" AND "Must use a shared PostgreSQL database across 3 regions."
2. "99.999% availability" AND "Monthly infrastructure budget under $5,000."
3. "All data encrypted at rest and in transit" AND "P99 latency under 100ms."
4. "Strong consistency across all reads" AND "Global deployment with 200ms cross-region latency."
5. "Handle 10x traffic spikes automatically" AND "Must use pre-provisioned reserved instances."

**Sample answer for #1:** "Conflict: Network latency between regions is 50-200ms, so cross-region database queries alone use the entire latency budget. Resolution: Use a single-region primary with async read replicas in other regions. Write operations go to the primary region. Read operations use local replicas with cache warming."

### Drill 4: The 60-Second Requirements Summary

Practice summarizing the requirements for a system in 60 seconds. Frame it exactly as:

"This system needs three functional requirements: [FR1], [FR2], [FR3]. And three critical non-functional requirements: [NFR1 with number], [NFR2 with number], [NFR3 with number]. The key constraint is [constraint]. Does this match what you are looking for?"

Practice with: WhatsApp, Netflix, Airbnb, Spotify, Zoom.

### Drill 5: NFR Cost-Benefit

For each NFR, estimate the cost adder over a basic implementation:

| NFR | Basic | Premium | Cost Ratio |
|---|---|---|---|
| Latency | P99 under 1s | P99 under 50ms | 3-10x |
| Availability | 99.9% | 99.99% | 3-5x |
| Durability | Daily backups | Synchronous replication | 3x |
| Consistency | Eventual | Strong | 2x write latency |
| Throughput | 100 RPS | 10,000 RPS | 5-20x |

Practice explaining: "For your scale, the cost-effective approach is [X]. You can upgrade to [Y] when the business metrics justify it."

---

**Key takeaway:** In system design interviews, how you handle requirements — especially requirements you elicit yourself — is a strong signal of your seniority. Use the Requirements-First 5-Step framework, quantify everything, check for conflicts, and always validate with the interviewer. Avoid the 8 red flags and you will stand out from candidates who jump straight to boxes and arrows.
