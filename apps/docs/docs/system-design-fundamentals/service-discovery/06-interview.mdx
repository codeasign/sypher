## Question Types

Service discovery appears in system design interviews in three forms:

### Dedicated Service Discovery Questions

The interviewer asks you to design a service discovery system from scratch. Example: "Design a service registry for a microservices platform with 10,000 services." These questions test your distributed systems fundamentals and your ability to balance consistency, availability, and performance.

### Service Discovery as a Sub-Component

The interviewer asks you to design a larger system (ride-sharing service, video streaming platform, e-commerce site) and expects you to address service discovery as part of the architecture. Example: "How do services in your video transcoding pipeline find each other?"

### Troubleshooting and Debugging

The interviewer describes a failure scenario and asks you to diagnose the root cause. Example: "During a deployment, 20% of requests fail with connection refused errors. What went wrong?"

## Testing Signals

Interviewers evaluate your response on these dimensions:

**Completeness**: Do you cover registration, health checking, discovery, and failure handling? Mentioning only "we use Consul" without explaining how it integrates with the rest of the system is insufficient.

**Depth of understanding**: Can you explain the tradeoff between client-side and server-side discovery? Do you know when to choose CP over AP?

**Practicality**: Does your design account for real-world issues like DNS caching, network partitions, and cascading failures?

**Operational awareness**: Do you consider deployment, monitoring, and recovery? A design that works perfectly in theory but cannot be operated in practice is not a good design.

**Communication**: Can you explain complex concepts clearly without jargon? Can you adapt your explanation based on the interviewer's follow-ups?

## Level Expectations

| Level | What They Expect |
|---|---|
| Entry-level / Intern | Understand the basic concept. Can explain why hardcoded IPs do not scale. Knows the terms: registry, heartbeat, TTL. |
| Mid-level (SDE 2) | Can compare client-side vs server-side discovery. Knows DNS-based discovery tradeoffs. Has opinions on when to use each. |
| Senior (SDE 3) | Can design a service registry with internal architecture. Understands Raft, gossip, consistent hashing. Considers failure modes and operational concerns. |
| Staff / Principal | Can design a multi-region, multi-cluster discovery system. Understands the business implications of consistency choices. Can articulate the tradeoff surface comprehensively. |

## Framework: The RADD Flow

Use the RADD framework to structure your service discovery interview answers.

**R — Requirements**: Clarify scale, consistency needs, and operational constraints.

```
"Before designing, I need to understand a few things about the system:
  - How many services? (estimate: 100, 1,000, 10,000?)
  - How many instances per service?
  - Is the system stateless or stateful?
  - Are we operating in one region or multiple?
  - What is the acceptable latency for discovery? (sub-5ms or sub-50ms?)
  - Can consumers tolerate stale data?"
```

**A — Architecture**: Present the high-level design showing components and data flow.
**D — Deep Dive**: Go into specific subsystems: storage, health checking, API layer, replication.
**D — Decisions and Tradeoffs**: Explain why you made each design choice and what you sacrificed.

## Phrase Bank

Use these phrases in your interview responses. Each one demonstrates specific knowledge.

### Requirements Clarification

1. "Can you clarify the scale of the system? Knowing the number of services and instances helps me pick the right discovery pattern."
2. "What consistency model does the system need? For stateless services, eventual consistency is usually fine. For leader election, I need strong consistency."
3. "Is this a greenfield system, or am I integrating with existing infrastructure like Kubernetes?"
4. "What is the deployment frequency? High deployment frequency favors registries with fast registration and eviction."
5. "What is the network topology? Single region, multi-region, or multi-cloud?"

### Architecture Explanation

6. "I will use a client-side discovery pattern because it removes the extra network hop of a load balancer and keeps latency under 1 millisecond."
7. "The service registry will be a Raft-based cluster of 5 nodes spread across 3 availability zones for fault tolerance."
8. "Every instance will send heartbeats every 15 seconds with a TTL of 45 seconds. If 3 consecutive heartbeats are missed, the instance is evicted."
9. "I will add a watch mechanism so consumers receive push notifications when instance lists change, instead of polling every few seconds."
10. "The consumer will cache the instance list locally for 10 seconds. If the registry is unreachable, the cache is used for up to 5 minutes as a fallback."

### Tradeoff Discussion

11. "Server-side discovery simplifies the consumer but adds latency and operational complexity from the load balancer. I would choose it for environments where consumers use multiple languages and frameworks."
12. "Strong consistency from Raft means that during a leader election (100-500ms), write operations are unavailable. If the system requires 99.99% write availability, I would choose an AP model instead."
13. "DNS-based discovery is simple but has a critical weakness: DNS caching. A 60-second TTL means changes take at least a minute to propagate. I only use DNS for services that rarely change."
14. "Active health checks are more thorough but increase load on both the registry and the service. For high-throughput services, passive heartbeats with connection-level circuit breakers are more efficient."
15. "The tradeoff of self-registration is that every service must embed the registry client. Third-party registration works better for legacy services that cannot be modified."

### Failure Handling

16. "If the registry cluster loses quorum, the system enters a read-only mode. Existing services continue running because consumers have cached instance lists, but new deployments cannot register."
17. "A thundering herd on registry restart is mitigated by jittered registration delays. Each instance waits a random interval between 0 and 30 seconds before registering."
18. "To handle the zombie instance problem, I use application-level health checks that verify the application process is alive, not just the network port."
19. "Circuit breakers on the consumer side prevent cascading failures. If a service instance fails 5 consecutive connections, the consumer stops routing to it for 30 seconds."

### Operations

20. "I will monitor the registry leader stability, heartbeat success rate, and eviction rate. A sudden spike in evictions usually indicates a network partition."
21. "Snapshot the registry state every 6 hours. If the cluster is completely lost, restore from the latest snapshot and have all services re-register."
22. "For disaster recovery, I maintain a warm standby registry cluster in a different region and use a global DNS name that can be switched manually if the primary region fails."

## Red Flags

Watch for these mistakes. They signal to the interviewer that your understanding is shallow.

**Red flag 1: Ignoring failure modes.** You describe the happy path (registration, query, response) but never discuss what happens when instances crash, networks partition, or the registry itself fails.

**Fix**: After describing the happy path, always add: "Now let me walk through what happens when things go wrong."

**Red flag 2: One-size-fits-all solution.** You propose the same discovery mechanism for every service regardless of its requirements. You do not distinguish between stateless web services, stateful databases, and batch processing jobs.

**Fix**: Show that you understand different services have different needs. "For stateless services, I use client-side discovery with eventual consistency. For the database cluster, I use DNS-based discovery with manual health management."

**Red flag 3: No caching strategy.** Your design has every request go through the registry. You do not consider the load or latency impact of doing a registry lookup on every single inter-service call.

**Fix**: Always include client-side caching, mention the cache TTL, and explain the cache-fallback behavior.

**Red flag 4: Ignoring DNS caching.** You propose DNS-based discovery without discussing TTL, negative caching, or how DNS resolvers behave when records change.

**Fix**: If you mention DNS, immediately follow with: "The main challenge with DNS is caching. DNS resolvers and clients cache records aggressively. I set TTL to 5-10 seconds for frequently-changing services, and I use SRV records to include port information."

**Red flag 5: Single point of failure.** Your registry runs as a single instance. Or you run three instances but they are all in the same availability zone.

**Fix**: "I deploy the registry cluster across three availability zones with at least 3 nodes. This tolerates a full AZ failure."

**Red flag 6: No health checks.** You assume every registered instance is healthy. You do not distinguish between a registered instance and a healthy instance.

**Fix**: "Registration is just the first step. I separate the registration from the health status. An instance is only returned in query results if its health check is passing."

**Red flag 7: Over-engineering for small scale.** You design a 5-node Raft cluster with gossip protocols and multi-region federation for a system with 5 services and 15 instances.

**Fix**: "At this scale, DNS-based discovery with a simple script to update records would be more appropriate than a full Consul deployment. Let me start simple and add complexity only when the requirements demand it."

**Red flag 8: Ignoring operational cost.** You propose a complex system without discussing the operational burden of maintaining it.

**Fix**: "The registry cluster requires dedicated maintenance: OS updates, certificate rotation, snapshot management, and performance tuning. I would factor this into the design. Is there an operations team that will support this, or should I minimize operational complexity?"

## Practice Drills

### Drill 1: Design a Service Registry (15 minutes)

Set a timer for 15 minutes. Design a service registry for a ride-sharing platform. Cover:
- Storage layout (data structures)
- Registration and heartbeat protocol
- Query protocol with caching
- Health checking strategy
- Failure handling (registry crash, instance crash, network partition)
- Deployment topology

### Drill 2: Compare and Contrast (5 minutes)

Set a timer for 5 minutes. Compare:
- Client-side vs server-side discovery: When does each make sense?
- DNS-based vs registry-based discovery: What are the latency, consistency, and operational tradeoffs?
- Active vs passive health checks: When is each appropriate?

### Drill 3: Debug the Failure (10 minutes)

Scenario: Your team deployed the registry as a 3-node cluster. During a network incident that split the cluster (Node A isolated, Nodes B and C together), 30% of services reported they could not register. Diagnose the problem and propose a fix.

**Solution**: The split created a minority side (Node A) that cannot form quorum. Services connected to Node A cannot write. Fix: Add a Consul or Eureka proxy that forwards registration requests from the minority side to the majority side. Alternatively, switch to an AP system like Eureka that does not require quorum.

### Drill 4: Multi-Region Design (20 minutes)

Design a service discovery system that spans 3 regions (us-east-1, eu-west-1, ap-southeast-1). Each region has 500 services with 5 instances each. Requirements:
- Cross-region calls must be minimized (each region should prefer local instances)
- When a region fails, traffic should automatically redirect to the nearest healthy region
- The registry must tolerate the loss of one full region

### Drill 5: Explain to a Non-Engineer (3 minutes)

Explain service discovery to a product manager in 3 minutes or less. Use the phone book analogy. Be ready for the follow-up: "Why can't we just keep a spreadsheet of which service runs where?"

## Scoring Rubric for Interviewers

If you are preparing for a mock interview, use this rubric to evaluate your performance.

### Score 1-2 (Weak)
- Does not define basic terms (registry, heartbeat, TTL)
- Proposes a single-node registry without discussing failure modes
- Cannot explain the difference between client-side and server-side discovery
- Does not mention caching or health checks

### Score 3-4 (Average)
- Defines core terms correctly
- Names client-side and server-side discovery
- Mentions health checks and heartbeats
- Has a basic understanding of failure modes (registry crash, instance crash)
- References at least one real-world example (Netflix Eureka)

### Score 5-6 (Good)
- Designs a multi-node registry cluster with replication
- Discusses consistency tradeoffs (CP vs AP) and gives a reasoned recommendation
- Explains watch mechanisms and caching strategy
- Covers at least 3 failure modes with mitigations
- Discusses operational concerns (monitoring, deployment, DR)
- References multiple real-world examples

### Score 7-8 (Excellent)
- All of the above, plus:
- Designs a tiered discovery system with different mechanisms for different use cases
- Discusses cross-region discovery and federation
- Considers security (mTLS, ACLs, rate limiting)
- Analyzes the cost-benefit tradeoff of each decision
- References specific failure incidents from real companies
- Has a clear, structured communication style

## Additional Practice Questions

### Question 1: Service Mesh vs. Traditional Discovery

"You are designing a service discovery system for a company migrating from a monolith to microservices. The CTO wants to use a service mesh (Istio). The VP of Engineering wants to use Consul. Compare the two approaches and make a recommendation."

**Considerations**: Service mesh adds sidecar overhead (5-10% CPU) but provides transparent discovery, mTLS, and observability. Consul is simpler and has lower overhead but requires application-level integration. Recommendation: Use Consul for the first 50 services, migrate to Istio when the system exceeds 100 services and the operational overhead of managing discovery in application code becomes significant.

### Question 2: Regional Failover

"Your primary region (us-east-1) has a complete failure. Your services are registered in a Consul cluster in us-east-1. You have a standby Consul cluster in us-west-2. How do you fail over?"

**Answer**: The failover should be automated:
1. A health checker detects that us-east-1 Consul cluster is unreachable.
2. Global DNS record for `service.discovery.internal` is updated to point to us-west-2 Consul.
3. Services in us-west-2 are already registered with the local Consul cluster.
4. Services in us-east-1 cannot reach their local Consul, so they fall back to cached instance lists.
5. If the outage lasts more than 5 minutes, a script triggers re-registration of dr-replicated services in us-west-2.
6. Traffic is routed to us-west-2 via DNS or load balancer changes.

### Question 3: Watching vs. Polling

"What is the tradeoff between watch-based discovery and polling-based discovery?"

**Answer**: Watches provide near-instant notification of changes (under 50ms) but maintain persistent connections that consume server resources. Polling is stateless and simpler but introduces latency proportional to the polling interval. For a system with 10,000 consumers, watches require 10,000 persistent connections, consuming significant memory on the registry. Polling at 10-second intervals would generate 1,000 queries per second but consume no persistent connection state. The choice depends on whether notification speed or server resource utilization is more important.
