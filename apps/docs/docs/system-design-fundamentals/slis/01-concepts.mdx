You are on call at 3 AM. Your phone buzzes with an alert from the monitoring system. The dashboard shows 15 red graphs, each measuring something different — CPU, memory, request latency, error count, disk I/O, queue depth, garbage collection pause time, database connection pool usage, network throughput, cache hit ratio, thread pool utilization, file descriptor count, swap usage, context switch rate, and load average. Every single one is spiking or dropping. You have no idea which metric matters. You have 30 seconds to decide whether to page the full incident response team.

This is the problem that Service Level Indicators exist to solve. Without SLIs, you drown in signals. With them, you know exactly which measurements tell you whether your system is actually working for your users.

## The Insight

An SLI — Service Level Indicator — is a carefully chosen measurement that tells you whether your system is meeting its reliability goals from the user's perspective. Not from the server's perspective. Not from the infrastructure's perspective. From the user's perspective.

The core insight is brutally simple: most metrics are noise. Out of the hundreds of data points your monitoring system collects every second, only a handful actually tell you if users are having a good experience. SLIs are those handful.

A latency graph showing p99 response time of 230 milliseconds tells you something about user experience. A CPU graph showing 87 percent utilization tells you something about the server — but it does not tell you whether users are happy. A server can be at 100 percent CPU and still serve requests within acceptable latency, or it can be at 30 percent CPU and have a deadlocked thread pool that makes every request hang.

SLIs bridge the gap between raw infrastructure metrics and user-facing reliability. They are the translation layer between "is the server healthy" and "are users getting value."

## Core Vocabulary

These terms form the foundation of everything you will do with SLIs. Internalize them.

**Service Level Indicator (SLI)** — A quantitative measure of some aspect of the service's performance or reliability. Examples: request latency at the 99th percentile, proportion of requests that return HTTP 200, throughput in requests per second. An SLI is always a measurement, not a target.

**Service Level Objective (SLO)** — A target value or range for an SLI over a time window. Example: "99.9 percent of requests will complete in under 200 milliseconds over a 30-day rolling window." The SLO is the promise you make to your users.

**Service Level Agreement (SLA)** — A contractual commitment to a customer that includes consequences if the SLO is not met. Example: "If uptime drops below 99.99 percent, the customer receives a 10 percent credit on their next invoice." Not all SLOs become SLAs, but all SLAs are backed by SLOs.

**Error Budget** — The amount of time or proportion of requests that can fail before you violate the SLO. If your SLO is 99.9 percent over 30 days, your error budget is 0.1 percent — roughly 43 minutes of allowable downtime per month. Error budgets let you balance reliability against velocity.

**Burn Rate** — The rate at which you are consuming your error budget. A burn rate of 1 means you are exhausting error budget exactly at the rate that would end the period at zero. A burn rate of 2 means you are consuming budget twice as fast as sustainable, and you will run out in half the time window.

**Burn Rate Alert** — An alert triggered when the burn rate exceeds a threshold over a specific time window. For example, alert if burn rate exceeds 2 over the last 1 hour, or exceeds 6 over the last 5 minutes. These are the gold standard for SLO-based alerting because they factor in remaining budget.

**SLI Type** — The category of measurement an SLI belongs to. The three common types are availability (can users access the service), latency (how fast is the response), and quality (is the response correct). Some frameworks add throughput and durability as additional types.

**Availability SLI** — The proportion of valid requests that receive a successful response. Typically calculated as successful requests divided by total requests. A request that times out is a failure. A request that returns HTTP 500 is a failure. A request that returns HTTP 200 with a corrupted payload is a failure under quality, but counted as success under availability.

**Latency SLI** — The time taken to process a request, measured at specific percentiles. The p50 (median) tells you what the typical user experiences. The p99 (99th percentile) tells you what the slowest 1 percent of users experience. The p999 (99.9th percentile) catches extreme outliers.

**Quality SLI** — The proportion of responses that are correct, complete, and fresh. This is harder to measure than availability or latency because it requires knowing what the correct answer should be. Example: a search service measures the proportion of results that contain relevant content. A video service measures the proportion of streams that start within 2 seconds with no buffering.

**Rolling Window** — The time period over which the SLI is measured and the SLO is evaluated. Common windows are 28 days, 30 days, or 7 days. The window is sliding, meaning it always covers the most recent period. Older data falls out of the window continuously.

**Good Event** — A request or operation that satisfies the success criteria for the SLI. For a latency SLI with a threshold of 200 milliseconds, a request completing in 150 milliseconds is a good event. For an availability SLI, a request returning HTTP 2xx is a good event.

**Valid Event** — All requests or operations that should be counted in the denominator. Health checks, internal monitoring pings, and known bad actors are typically excluded. The distinction between valid and invalid events prevents SLIs from being diluted by non-user traffic.

**Throughput SLI** — The rate at which the service processes requests, typically measured in requests per second, operations per second, or bytes per second. A sudden drop in throughput might indicate a routing failure, a database connection pool exhaustion, or a downstream service outage. A sudden spike might indicate a DDoS attack or a flash crowd. Throughput SLIs are most useful when combined with latency SLIs — a drop in throughput with stable latency suggests a different root cause than a drop in throughput with rising latency.

**Durability SLI** — The probability that data written to the system persists and remains retrievable over time. This is critical for storage systems, databases, and message queues. A durability SLI of 99.999999 percent (eight nines) means that, on average, one out of every 100 million written objects is lost. Durability is hard to measure directly because you must verify that every write is still readable — it requires a reconciliation process that compares written data against stored data.

**Freshness SLI** — The age of the data presented to the user, measured as the time between a write occurring and that write being visible in reads. This is critical for eventually consistent systems, caches with TTLs, and batch processing pipelines. A freshness SLI of "p99 under 5 seconds" means that 99 percent of reads see data that is at most 5 seconds old. Violations of freshness SLIs often go unnoticed because the system returns correct data — just stale data.

**Coverage SLI** — The proportion of user-facing requests that are actually instrumented for SLI measurement. If you are only measuring 80 percent of your traffic, your SLI may not represent the experience of the other 20 percent. Coverage SLIs are meta-SLIs — they measure the quality of the SLI system itself. A coverage SLI target of 99.9 percent means that at least 99.9 percent of valid user requests produce SLI data.

**Cardinality** — The number of unique label combinations in your metric dimensions. A latency SLI segmented by service, endpoint, region, and deployment version has four dimensions. If you have 50 services, 200 endpoints, 5 regions, and 10 deployment versions, the total cardinality is 50 x 200 x 5 x 10 = 500,000 unique time series. High cardinality increases storage costs and query latency. The SLI platform must be designed to handle the cardinality your system produces.

**Multi-Window Multi-Burn-Rate Alerting** — The standard approach to SLO-based alerting, endorsed by Google SRE. It uses multiple time windows (typically 5 minutes, 30 minutes, and 6 hours) with different burn rate thresholds for each window. A catastrophic failure triggers the short window; a slow degradation triggers the long window. This approach catches both fast and slow failures without requiring the engineer to predict the failure pattern in advance.

**SLI Budget** — An informal term for the amount of degradation a service can tolerate over a period before user experience is negatively affected. Unlike the error budget (which is exactly defined as 1 minus the SLO), the SLI budget is a planning concept used during capacity reviews. If your latency SLI has been running at p99 of 80 milliseconds for the last 3 months, you have a 120-millisecond budget before you hit the 200-millisecond SLO. This budget can be consumed by adding new features that increase latency, deploying to new regions, or absorbing traffic growth.

## Step by Step: How SLIs Work in Practice

Let us walk through how you design, implement, and act on SLIs in a real system. We will use a simple web API service as our running example.

### Step 1: Identify the User Journey

Before you can choose an SLI, you must know what user journey matters. For a web API, the primary user journey is: a client sends a request, the server processes it, and returns a response. Secondary journeys might include authentication, data retrieval, or file upload.

For our example, the primary journey is an HTTP request-response cycle.

### Step 2: Define What "Good" Looks Like

A request is "good" if three conditions are met:
- It completes (the server returns a response)
- It completes quickly (under 200 milliseconds at the p99)
- It returns the correct data (HTTP 2xx with a valid payload)

Everything else is a failure from the user's perspective — even if the server considers it a success.

### Step 3: Choose Your SLI Type

For a web API, you typically need three SLIs:
- **Availability**: What fraction of requests get a valid response
- **Latency**: How fast are the responses
- **Quality**: Are the responses correct

### Step 4: Define the Measurement

For availability, the SLI is:

```
availability = count of HTTP 2xx responses / count of all valid requests
```

This is measured over a rolling window, typically 1 minute buckets aggregated over 30 days.

You must also decide what counts as "valid." Health checks from your load balancer should be excluded. Internal pings from your monitoring system should be excluded. Known crawlers and scrapers should be excluded. The mark of a well-designed SLI is that the denominator contains only user-facing traffic.

For latency, the SLI is the p99 response time over the same window. You measure the response time of every request, sort them, and take the value at the 99th percentile. The p99 means that 99 percent of requests are faster than this value, and 1 percent are slower.

Some teams measure the p50 (median) alongside the p99. The p50 tells you what the typical user experiences. If the p50 is 30 milliseconds but the p99 is 2 seconds, the typical experience is great but the worst experience is terrible. Both metrics together give a complete picture.

### Step 5: Set the SLO

Based on what your users need and what your system can deliver, you set targets:

```
SLO for availability: 99.95% over 30 days
SLO for latency: p99 < 200ms over 30 days
```

These numbers are not arbitrary. They come from understanding what your users expect and what your competitors deliver. If your competitor offers 99.99 percent availability, setting your SLO at 99.9 percent tells your users that you value reliability less than your competitor does. If your users are internal teams who use your API as part of a batch job that runs overnight, they can tolerate more latency than a real-time user-facing application.

The SLO should be ambitious enough that it sometimes requires effort to meet, but achievable enough that it does not demoralize the team. A good heuristic: the SLO should be met approximately 95 percent of months. If you are meeting it 100 percent of months, it is too loose. If you are meeting it less than 50 percent of months, it is too tight.

### Step 6: Calculate the Error Budget

For a 99.95 percent availability SLO over 30 days:

```
error budget = (1 - 0.9995) × 30 days
error budget = 0.0005 × 2,592,000 seconds
error budget = 1,296 seconds ≈ 21.6 minutes
```

You have 21.6 minutes of allowable downtime per month. Every minute of downtime consumes this budget.

In terms of requests, if your service processes 200,000 requests per day, the total requests over 30 days is 6,000,000. The SLO allows 0.05 percent failures, which is 3,000 failures over 30 days, or 100 failures per day on average.

The error budget is your team's operational currency. At the start of the month, you have 21.6 minutes in the bank. Every incident, every degraded response, every timeout withdraws from this account. If the account runs out, you have violated the SLO — even if you recover quickly.

The error budget resets at the beginning of each SLO window. This means a team that has a bad month gets a fresh start. The reset prevents a single catastrophic month from demoralizing the team for the rest of the year. It also means that a team that has a good month should not get complacent — the budget resets to zero, and the next month starts fresh.

### Step 7: Set Up Burn Rate Alerts

You do not wait until the budget is exhausted. You alert when the burn rate suggests you are on track to exhaust it.

A common setup has two tiers:
- **Page if burn rate > 6 over 5 minutes**: Catches catastrophic failures immediately
- **Page if burn rate > 2 over 1 hour**: Catches slow degradation before it exhausts the budget

### Step 8: Act on the Signal

When a burn rate alert fires, the on-call engineer investigates and mitigates. When the error budget is not exhausted, the team can deploy new features with confidence. When the error budget is running low, the team shifts focus from velocity to reliability — freezing deployments, running load tests, and fixing bugs.

### Step 9: Measure the Burn Rate Continuously

Burn rate is not a one-time calculation. You track it continuously across multiple windows. Most monitoring platforms compute burn rate every 30 to 60 seconds and update the dashboard. This gives the team a constantly refreshing view of how fast they are consuming error budget.

The burn rate dashboard answers three questions:
- How fast are we consuming error budget right now? (last 5 minutes)
- Are we on track to exhaust the budget this month? (last 1 hour projection)
- Is there a slowly growing problem we might be missing? (last 6 hours)

### Step 10: Conduct the SLO Review

At the end of each SLO window (typically every month), the team reviews the SLI data and SLO performance. The review answers these questions:
- Did we meet all our SLOs? If not, which ones did we miss and why?
- Was the SLO set at the right level? Should we tighten it or relax it for the next period?
- Did our burn rate alerts fire appropriately? Were there false positives or missed detections?
- What changes to the system or operations could improve SLI performance?

The SLO review is a blameless postmortem for reliability targets. The goal is not to assign blame for missing an SLO — the goal is to learn and adjust.

### Step 11: Iterate the SLI Set

SLIs are not static. As the system evolves — new features are added, traffic patterns shift, user expectations change — the SLI set should evolve too. A quarterly SLI review ensures that each service's SLIs still reflect the current user experience.

During the quarterly review, the team considers:
- Has a new critical user journey been added that needs SLI coverage?
- Has an existing user journey been deprecated or changed?
- Are any SLIs no longer providing useful signal (always green or always red)?
- Have user expectations changed since the SLIs were defined?

### Step 12: Link SLIs to Business Outcomes

The final step closes the loop. SLI data should inform not just engineering decisions but also business decisions. When a product manager asks "can we add this new feature this quarter?", the engineering team answers with SLI data: "Our latency SLI has a 50-millisecond buffer before the SLO. This feature is expected to add 30 milliseconds to the p99. That fits within our budget — we can ship it."

When the business team negotiates SLAs with customers, the engineering team provides SLI data: "Our availability SLI has been 99.95 percent for the last 6 months. Promising a 99.99 percent SLA would require a 5x improvement in reliability. Here is what that would cost in infrastructure."

## Variants of SLI Approaches

SLIs are not one-size-fits-all. Different systems and different reliability requirements call for different approaches.

### Variant 1: Request-Granularity SLIs

This is the most common approach. Every request is measured individually, and the SLI is computed as a proportion of good events over total events.

**When to use**: Web APIs, microservices, databases, and any system where each request is distinct and measurable.

**Strengths**: Precise, easy to understand, works well with percentile-based latency targets.

**Weaknesses**: Can be expensive to measure every request at high throughput. Requires careful filtering of non-user traffic.

### Variant 2: Time-Window SLIs

Instead of measuring each request, you measure the proportion of time windows in which the system is considered healthy. If the system serves requests correctly for 99.9 percent of 1-minute windows, it is meeting the SLO.

**When to use**: Batch processing systems, data pipelines, and systems where individual request granularity is too expensive or not meaningful.

**Strengths**: Simpler to implement, lower data volume, natural fit for systems that process jobs rather than requests.

**Weaknesses**: Less precise. A window with 1 failure and a window with 1,000 failures count the same — as one bad window.

### Variant 3: Composite SLIs

A composite SLI combines multiple individual SLIs into a single score. The system is considered healthy only if all component SLIs are healthy.

**When to use**: Complex user journeys that span multiple services. For example, a search page that requires the search service, the recommendation service, and the user profile service to all be working.

**Strengths**: Captures the true user experience when a single request touches multiple services. Prevents a false sense of security when one service is degraded but others are healthy.

**Weaknesses**: A single failing service can make the composite SLI look catastrophic even if the failure is isolated and minor. Can be harder to debug because you must check all components.

### Variant 4: Streaming-Window SLIs

Instead of fixed time windows, you use a sliding window that updates continuously. Every new request adds to the window and the oldest request drops out. This gives you a real-time view of your SLI without waiting for the window to close.

**When to use**: High-traffic systems where you need near-real-time visibility into SLI compliance. Streaming platforms, real-time APIs, and critical infrastructure.

**Strengths**: Provides the most current view of system health. No lag between a failure and its impact on the SLI.

**Weaknesses**: Computationally expensive. Requires maintaining a sliding window data structure in memory or a fast key-value store. Every request triggers an update to the window.

### Variant 5: Forecast-Based SLIs

Instead of measuring past performance, forecast-based SLIs predict future SLI values using time-series forecasting models. The SLI is the predicted probability that the SLO will be violated in the next N hours, given current trends.

**When to use**: Systems where early warning is critical. If you can predict a 90 percent probability of SLO violation 30 minutes before it happens, you can take proactive action — scaling up capacity, rerouting traffic, or restarting degraded instances.

**Strengths**: Provides proactive rather than reactive alerting. Can detect slow degradation trends that burn rate alerts might miss because the degradation is too gradual to trigger the multi-window thresholds.

**Weaknesses**: Forecasting models are imperfect. False positives erode trust in the system. Requires historical data to train the model and ongoing maintenance as traffic patterns change. A forecast-based SLI should never replace a burn rate alert — it should complement it.

### Variant 6: SLI Tiers

Instead of a single SLI for a service, define multiple tiers of SLIs with different SLOs and different response expectations. This is the approach used by Discord and other large-scale platforms.

**Tier 1 — User Experience SLI**: Measures the critical user journey. Has the tightest SLO (99.99 percent) and the most aggressive alerting (page on-call within 2 minutes). Example: "ride request success rate" for Uber.

**Tier 2 — Feature SLI**: Measures a specific feature that is important but not business-critical. Has a moderate SLO (99.9 percent) and standard alerting (create a ticket, investigate within 4 hours). Example: "file upload success rate" for Discord.

**Tier 3 — Infrastructure SLI**: Measures the health of underlying infrastructure. Has a relaxed SLO (99.5 percent) and no alerting — the data is used for capacity planning and trend analysis. Example: "database connection pool utilization" for any service.

The tier system prevents alert fatigue by ensuring that only the most critical SLIs trigger immediate response. Lower-tier SLIs are tracked and reviewed but do not page engineers.

## Mental Model Check

The best way to internalize SLI concepts is to apply them. These questions test your ability to reason about SLIs in realistic scenarios. For each question, write down your answer before reading the explanation. The goal is not to get the right answer on the first try — the goal is to identify gaps in your understanding.

### Question 1

You have a service with 100,000 requests per day. Your SLO is 99.9 percent availability over 30 days. How many failures can you tolerate per day, on average?

**Answer**: Over 30 days, you have 3,000,000 total requests. The SLO allows 0.1 percent failures, which is 3,000 failures over 30 days, or 100 failures per day on average. This is your daily error budget.

### Question 2

Your latency SLI is p99 under 200 milliseconds. You measure the p99 in one-minute buckets. For the first 59 minutes of an hour, the p99 is 150 milliseconds. In the 60th minute, a spike pushes the p99 to 800 milliseconds. Is the service meeting its SLO?

**Answer**: It depends on the SLO window. If the SLO is measured over an hourly window, the p99 for the hour is computed from all requests in that hour. If 59 out of 60 minutes were under 200 milliseconds, the raw p99 for the hour is likely still under 200 milliseconds because the 60th minute's 800 milliseconds is diluted by the 59 good minutes. But if the SLO uses a minutely window and requires 99.9 percent of minutes to be under 200 milliseconds, then 1 out of 60 minutes failing means 98.3 percent compliance — below the target. Always understand the windowing.

### Question 3

You are designing SLIs for a file upload service. Users upload files that can take 30 seconds to 5 minutes to process. What SLI types make sense?

**Answer**: Three SLI types apply. First, availability: can users initiate an upload successfully. Second, latency: how long does it take for the upload to complete. For a long-running operation, you might measure the 95th percentile of completion time instead of p99 because the tail is inherently long. Third, quality: is the uploaded file processed correctly and completely. A quality SLI here might be the proportion of uploads that result in a valid file — not corrupted, not truncated.

### Question 4

Your team wants to use a single SLI for the entire system: "99.9 percent of requests complete in under 200 milliseconds." What is wrong with this approach?

**Answer**: A single SLI hides problems. The latency SLI and the availability SLI are conflated. A request that times out after 30 seconds does not "complete in under 200 milliseconds" — it does not complete at all. But the SLI as stated does not distinguish between a slow request and a failed request. Separating availability and latency into two SLIs gives you clearer signal. Additionally, a single SLI cannot capture quality — a request that returns a fast but wrong answer is counted as success.

### Question 5

Your burn rate alert fires at 10:00 AM. The burn rate over the last 5 minutes is 8. Your error budget is 43 minutes per month. What does this mean?

**Answer**: A burn rate of 8 means you are consuming error budget 8 times faster than sustainable. At this rate, you will exhaust the remaining budget in approximately (remaining budget / 8) of the time window. If you have consumed no budget before 10:00 AM, you have 43 minutes of budget remaining. At burn rate 8, you will exhaust it in 43 / 8 = 5.4 minutes of sustained burning. This is a critical alert — you need to investigate immediately. The fact that it fired over just 5 minutes suggests a sudden, severe failure rather than gradual degradation.
