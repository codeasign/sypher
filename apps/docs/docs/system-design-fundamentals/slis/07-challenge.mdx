These challenges test your understanding of SLIs across three tiers of difficulty. Foundation challenges verify your grasp of core concepts. Design challenges require you to apply SLI principles to realistic scenarios. Expert challenges explore advanced edge cases and open-ended problems.

## Tier 1: Foundation

These challenges confirm you have the basics right. Each should take 5 to 10 minutes.

### Challenge 1: SLI Calculation

You are given the following data for a web API service over a 1-hour window:

| Time Bucket | Total Requests | 2xx Responses | Non-2xx Responses | p50 Latency | p99 Latency |
|-------------|---------------|---------------|-------------------|-------------|-------------|
| 09:00       | 1,200         | 1,195         | 5                 | 42ms        | 185ms       |
| 09:01       | 1,150         | 1,140         | 10                | 38ms        | 210ms       |
| 09:02       | 1,300         | 1,280         | 20                | 45ms        | 320ms       |
| 09:03       | 1,180         | 1,175         | 5                 | 41ms        | 178ms       |
| 09:04       | 1,250         | 1,245         | 5                 | 40ms        | 195ms       |

**Part A**: Compute the availability SLI for the full hour. Show your work.

**Part B**: The latency SLO is p99 under 200 milliseconds. Did the service meet this SLO during this hour?

**Part C**: If the 30-day SLO for availability is 99.9 percent, and the service has been meeting it perfectly until this hour, what is the new 30-day SLI value? Assume 30 days x 24 hours x 1,200 requests per hour average.

**Part D**: How much error budget was consumed by this hour's failures?

#### Model Answer

**Part A**:
Total requests = 1,200 + 1,150 + 1,300 + 1,180 + 1,250 = 6,080
Total good (2xx) = 1,195 + 1,140 + 1,280 + 1,175 + 1,245 = 6,035
Availability SLI = 6,035 / 6,080 = 99.26 percent

**Part B**:
The p99 exceeded 200 milliseconds in two buckets: 09:01 (210ms) and 09:02 (320ms). But you cannot compute the aggregate p99 over the hour by averaging the per-bucket p99 values. To determine the true p99 over the hour, you would need the raw latency histogram across all 6,080 requests. Without the histogram, the correct answer is: insufficient data to determine the aggregate p99. A good engineer would flag that the 09:02 bucket had a p99 of 320ms, which is a strong indicator of a latency spike that merits investigation.

**Part C**:
Total requests over 30 days at average = 30 x 24 x 1,200 = 864,000
Total failures allowed by SLO = 864,000 x (1 - 0.999) = 864 failures
Failures in the current hour = 6,080 - 6,035 = 45 failures
Running total of failures over 30 days before this hour = 0 (service was perfect)
Running total after this hour = 45 failures
SLI after this hour = (864,000 - 45) / 864,000 = 99.9948 percent

Still above the SLO of 99.9 percent. The hour consumed 45 of the 864 budgeted failures, or 5.2 percent of the total error budget.

**Part D**:
Error budget consumed = 45 / 864 = 5.2 percent of the monthly error budget
In time terms: the monthly error budget is 0.1 percent of 30 days = 0.03 days = 43.2 minutes. This hour consumed 5.2 percent of 43.2 minutes = 2.25 minutes of the error budget.

### Challenge 2: Design SLIs for a URL Shortener

A URL shortener service (like bit.ly) has the following characteristics:
- 10 million requests per day
- 2,000 redirects per second at peak
- 500 URL creation requests per second at peak
- Data stored in a distributed key-value store
- Global user base across 3 regions

Define the SLI set. For each SLI, specify:
- The exact formula (good events / total events)
- The SLO target and window
- Which traffic to include and exclude
- Any special considerations

#### Model Answer

**SLI 1: Redirect Availability**
Formula: count of HTTP 301/302 responses / count of all redirect requests
Target: 99.99 percent over 30 days
Exclude: health check pings, internal preview tools
Special: A redirect request that returns 404 because the short URL does not exist is still counted — the service correctly handled the request even though the URL is not valid. Only 5xx responses count as failures.

**SLI 2: Redirect Latency**
Formula: p99 of response time for all redirect requests
Target: p99 under 50 milliseconds over 30 days
Special: This is the most critical SLI because users expect instant redirects. The p50 should be under 10 milliseconds. Cache hit ratio directly affects this SLI — a cache miss adds 5 to 10 milliseconds for the database lookup.

**SLI 3: URL Creation Availability**
Formula: count of HTTP 201 responses / count of all create requests
Target: 99.9 percent over 30 days
Special: URL creation is less latency-sensitive than redirects. The creation SLI can be more lenient because users accept a brief delay when creating a link.

**SLI 4: Redirect Correctness (Quality)**
Formula: count of redirects to the correct destination / count of all redirect requests
Target: 99.99 percent over 30 days
Special: This requires a canary check. Periodically create known URLs and verify the redirect goes to the correct destination. A mismatch indicates data corruption or a caching bug.

### Challenge 3: Error Budget Math

Your team runs a service with an SLO of 99.95 percent availability over a 30-day window. On the 15th day of the month, you experience a 10-minute complete outage.

**Part A**: What percentage of the monthly error budget did this outage consume?

**Part B**: After the outage, how many minutes of additional downtime can you tolerate for the rest of the month?

**Part C**: If the same outage happens again on day 20, do you still meet the SLO?

**Part D**: What burn rate during the 10-minute outage (assume 100 percent failure rate)?

#### Model Answer

**Part A**:
Monthly error budget = 30 days x 1,440 minutes/day x (1 - 0.9995)
Monthly error budget = 30 x 1,440 x 0.0005 = 21.6 minutes
The outage was 10 minutes.
Percentage consumed = 10 / 21.6 = 46.3 percent

**Part B**:
Remaining error budget = 21.6 - 10 = 11.6 minutes
Remaining time in the month = 15 days = 21,600 minutes
Allowable additional downtime = 11.6 minutes
This means the service can be down for a total of 11.6 more minutes over the remaining 15 days.

**Part C**:
After two 10-minute outages, total downtime = 20 minutes.
Monthly error budget = 21.6 minutes.
20 minutes is less than 21.6 minutes, so you still meet the SLO — but barely. You have only 1.6 minutes of budget remaining for the last 10 days of the month. This is effectively a "condition green but caution" situation. The team should freeze deployments and focus on reliability.

**Part D**:
A 10-minute outage with 100 percent failure rate means:

Actual failure rate during outage = 100 percent
Allowed failure rate (for SLO of 99.95%) = 0.05 percent
Burn rate = actual / allowed = 100 / 0.05 = 2,000

A burn rate of 2,000 during the outage is extremely high. The burn rate alert would fire within the first minute. This confirms that burn rate alerts detect catastrophic failures almost instantly.

## Tier 2: Design

These challenges require you to design SLI systems for realistic scenarios. Each should take 20 to 30 minutes.

### Challenge 4: SLI System for a Payment Processing Platform

Design the SLI system for a payment processing platform that handles credit card transactions. The platform processes 500,000 transactions per day across 5 regions. Each transaction involves: authorization - capture - settlement. The platform has a 99.99 percent SLA with its merchants.

**Requirements**:
- Each transaction must complete within 5 seconds total
- Authorization must complete within 2 seconds
- Settlement must complete within 24 hours
- Merchants report failures if the settlement does not complete
- The platform needs to detect region-specific degradation

**Your design must include**:
- The SLI types and formulas
- SLO targets for each SLI
- The pipeline architecture (instrumentation, collection, aggregation, evaluation)
- How you handle cross-region variation
- How you prevent denominator pollution
- Error budget allocation across the 5 regions

#### Model Answer

**SLIs and SLOs**:

| SLI | Formula | SLO | Window |
|-----|---------|-----|--------|
| Authorization Availability | successful auths / total auth attempts | 99.99% | 30d |
| Authorization Latency | p99 of auth response time | under 2s | 30d |
| Capture Availability | successful captures / total auths | 99.995% | 30d |
| Settlement Completion | settled txns / total captured txns | 99.99% | 30d |
| End-to-End Duration | p95 of total txn time (auth to settlement) | under 5s | 30d |

**Exclusions**: Internal retry attempts, reconciliation requests from the accounting system, and health check pings are excluded from all SLI denominators.

**Pipeline Architecture**:

Each region runs an independent SLI pipeline. Aggregated summaries are shipped to a central global aggregator every 60 seconds.

The global aggregator computes:
- Global SLI (weighted average across regions by transaction volume)
- Worst-performing region
- Regional delta from global average

**Error Budget Allocation**:

The SLA is 99.99 percent. The internal SLOs are set to 99.995 percent — 5x tighter than the SLA — to create a safety margin.

Error budget per region is proportional to its transaction volume. Region A handles 40 percent of transactions, so it gets 40 percent of the error budget. If Region A exhausts its budget, the global SLO is still met as long as other regions stay within theirs. But the regional team must address the issue before the next month.

**Alerting Strategy**:

The payment platform uses a three-tier alerting system:
- **Critical alerts (page within 1 minute)**: Authorization availability SLO violation detected — this is the most business-critical metric. Authorization failures directly impact merchant revenue.
- **High alerts (page within 5 minutes)**: Authorization latency exceeds 2 seconds at p99. Latency degradation reduces conversion rates but does not block transactions.
- **Warning alerts (ticket within 1 hour)**: Settlement completion rate drops below 99.99 percent. Settlement failures are problematic but have a 24-hour window to resolve before they become critical.

**Regional Rollback Procedure**:

If a region's SLIs degrade below the SLO, the regional traffic can be rerouted to other regions. The rerouting is automated: if the regional availability SLI drops below 99.9 percent for 2 consecutive minutes, the region's traffic is distributed to the nearest healthy region. The rerouting is transparent to users — the API gateway handles the traffic shift without requiring any client-side changes.

### Challenge 5: SLI for a Real-Time Chat Application

Design the SLI system for a real-time chat application serving 100 million monthly active users. The application supports:
- One-to-one messaging (text, images, files)
- Group messaging (up to 200,000 members)
- Voice and video calls (up to 50 participants)
- Message search and history

The application runs on a Kubernetes cluster spanning 4 regions. Each region has its own database and cache layer. Users are assigned to the nearest region.

**Your design should address**:
- How to define SLIs for real-time vs. asynchronous operations
- How to handle the different modalities (text, voice, video)
- How to measure quality SLIs for calls
- How to handle cross-region messaging
- What SLO targets are realistic for each modality

#### Model Answer

**SLIs per Modality**:

**Text Messaging**:
- Message Send Availability: 99.99 percent — messages sent that reach the server with HTTP 2xx
- Message Delivery Latency: p99 under 500 milliseconds — time from send to acknowledgment that the recipient's client received it
- Message Durability: 99.9999 percent — messages that are stored and retrievable 30 days later

**Group Messaging**:
- Group Message Fan-Out Latency: p95 under 2 seconds for groups under 1,000 members, p95 under 10 seconds for groups over 1,000 members — fan-out to all members
- Group Message Delivery Completeness: 99.9 percent of members in a group receive the message within the latency target

**Voice and Video**:
- Call Connection Success: 99.5 percent — calls where both parties establish a peer-to-peer or relay connection within 5 seconds
- Call Quality: 95 percent of calls maintain audio quality rated "good" or better (packet loss under 1 percent, jitter under 30 milliseconds, round-trip time under 200 milliseconds)
- Call Duration Stability: 99 percent of calls do not drop unexpectedly before one party intentionally ends the call

**Cross-Region Considerations**:

Cross-region messaging should have a separate SLI with looser targets. Message delivery from US to APAC might take 1 to 2 seconds. The cross-region delivery latency SLO should be p99 under 3 seconds — 6x the in-region target.

Users are not told which region their message is routed to. The SLO should be measured from the user's perspective: if a user in APAC sends a message to a user in US, the delivery time is measured from the APAC user's send to the US user's receipt, regardless of which regions the servers are in.

**Voice Call Quality Measurement**:

Voice call quality is measured using a metric called Mean Opinion Score (MOS), which rates call quality on a scale of 1 (unusable) to 5 (perfect). The MOS is computed from three network metrics:

- **Packet loss**: The percentage of audio packets that do not reach the recipient. Packet loss above 1 percent causes noticeable audio degradation.
- **Jitter**: The variation in packet arrival time. Jitter above 30 milliseconds causes stuttering audio.
- **Round-trip time**: The time for a packet to travel from sender to recipient and back. RTT above 300 milliseconds causes noticeable delay in conversation.

The voice quality SLI is computed as: the proportion of 30-second windows within a call where the MOS rating is above 3.5 (good or better). A call is considered "good" if at least 95 percent of its 30-second windows have MOS above 3.5.

The voice quality SLO is set at 95 percent of calls being "good" over a 30-day window. This accounts for the fact that some calls will have poor quality due to the user's internet connection, which is outside Discord's control. The SLI is segmented by the user's ISP and region to distinguish between infrastructure problems (Discord can fix) and last-mile problems (ISP must fix).

**Media Gateway SLI**:

Voice and video calls are routed through media gateways that handle the real-time audio/video stream. Each media gateway has its own SLI: the proportion of calls routed through that gateway that maintain good quality.

If a media gateway's SLI drops below 95 percent, the gateway is taken out of rotation and new calls are routed to healthy gateways. This automated failover happens within 30 seconds of the SLI dropping below the threshold. The failed gateway is investigated and fixed before being returned to rotation.

### Challenge 6: SLI Migration

Your company currently uses threshold-based alerting with no SLOs. The on-call team receives 200 alerts per day. Most alerts are false positives. Real incidents are often detected by customer complaints before the monitoring system catches them.

You have been asked to lead the migration to SLI-based alerting. The company has 50 services, each with different traffic patterns, user expectations, and team maturity levels.

**Your plan must include**:
- The phased approach (what happens in month 1, month 2, month 3)
- How you determine initial SLO targets
- How you handle services with very low traffic (fewer than 100 requests per day)
- How you measure success of the migration
- How you handle resistance from teams that do not want SLOs

#### Model Answer

**Phase 1 (Month 1): Pilot with 3 services**
- Select 3 high-traffic, user-facing services for the pilot
- Define 3 SLIs per service: availability, latency, and one service-specific quality metric
- Set initial SLOs based on the observed p99 of the last 90 days of data — the SLO should be achievable but not trivial
- Run the new SLI system in shadow mode alongside the existing alerting system
- Track: false positive alerts eliminated, true positive detection time, engineer satisfaction

**Phase 2 (Month 2): Expand to 15 services**
- All user-facing services with over 10,000 requests per day must have SLIs
- Provide templates and automated SLI generation based on service type (API, batch, database)
- Decommission threshold-based alerts for services that have SLIs with burn rate alerts
- Train each team on the SLI dashboard and error budget concept
- Establish the SLO review board: any SLO change below 99 percent or above 99.999 percent requires review

**Phase 3 (Month 3): Company-wide rollout**
- All 50 services must have SLIs and SLOs
- Low-traffic services (under 100 requests per day) use time-window SLIs with 7-day windows instead of percentile-based SLIs
- Establish monthly SLO review meetings where each team presents their SLI dashboard and error budget consumption
- Link error budgets to deployment decisions — services with consumed error budget freeze deployments

**Handling Low-Traffic Services**: For services with fewer than 100 requests per day, percentile-based SLIs are noisy. Use time-window SLIs: measure whether each 1-hour window is healthy or unhealthy. The SLO is the percentage of healthy windows over 7 days. A window is healthy if all requests succeed and latency is under the threshold.

**Measuring Success**:
- Reduce on-call alert volume from 200 per day to under 20 per day
- Reduce mean time to detection from complaint-based (hours) to alert-based (minutes)
- Eliminate false positive alerts for pilot services
- 100 percent of services have defined SLIs with SLOs
- Each team can state their SLO targets and current error budget consumption without looking at a dashboard

**Handling Resistance**:
- Do not mandate SLOs for teams that resist. Instead, send your data: "your service received 12 complaints last month. Here is what the SLIs would have shown." Let the data speak.
- Offer a graduated approach: start with a 90-day window SLO at 99 percent, then tighten it by 0.05 percent each quarter.
- Get executive sponsorship. Once the VP of engineering says "all services must have SLOs," resistance drops.

**Rollback Plan**:

If the SLI migration causes problems — for example, if the burn rate alerts fire too frequently or miss real incidents — the team must have a rollback plan. The rollback plan has three stages:

1. **Stage 1 (First week)**: Keep the old threshold-based alerting system running in parallel. The SLI system alerts are advisory only — they do not page. The team compares SLI alerts against threshold alerts to calibrate the burn rate thresholds.
2. **Stage 2 (Second week)**: SLI alerts become primary for the pilot services. The old threshold alerts are still active but lower priority. If the SLI alerts miss a real incident, the old threshold alerts catch it.
3. **Stage 3 (Third week)**: Old threshold alerts are decommissioned for the pilot services. If the SLI system performs well for 2 weeks, the rollback is considered unnecessary. If it fails, the team reverts to threshold alerts and investigates the root cause.

The rollback to threshold alerts is a configuration change, not a code deployment. The threshold alert rules are kept in the monitoring system but disabled. Re-enabling them takes 5 minutes.

### Challenge 7: SLI Design for a Multi-Tenant Data Warehouse

A multi-tenant data warehouse service serves 500 enterprise customers. Each customer has their own data and queries. The service runs on a shared infrastructure with resource isolation guarantees.

The key challenge is that different customers have very different usage patterns:
- Customer A runs 10,000 small queries per day (each under 1 second)
- Customer B runs 5 large batch queries per day (each taking 30 minutes to 2 hours)
- Customer C runs 500 medium queries per day (each taking 10 to 60 seconds)
- Customer D runs a mix of 100 queries and 1,000 streaming inserts per day

Design the SLI system. Address these specific challenges:

1. How do you define a single set of SLIs that works for all 4 customer types? Or do you need different SLIs per customer type?

2. How do you set SLOs when different customers have different expectations? Customer A is a fintech company that requires sub-second query latency. Customer D is a marketing analytics company that can tolerate multi-minute query times.

3. How do you handle resource contention in a shared environment? If Customer B's large query consumes resources that degrade Customer A's queries, whose SLI failure is it?

4. How do you compute SLIs fairly when queries take very different amounts of time? A 2-hour query that fails is one event. A 1-second query that fails is also one event. Does each failure count equally?

5. How do you handle the quality SLI? A query that returns wrong results is a failure, but verifying query correctness is itself a compute-intensive operation.

#### Expert Discussion

This problem highlights the limits of standard SLI approaches and requires creative adaptation.

**On different SLIs per customer type**: Yes, you need different SLIs. Customer A and Customer B cannot share the same latency SLI because their queries are fundamentally different. The solution is to define SLIs per query type, not per customer. Small queries (under 10,000 rows, under 10 seconds) have one SLI set. Large queries (over 1 million rows, over 10 minutes) have a different set. Each customer's SLI is a weighted combination based on their query mix.

**On different customer expectations**: The fintech customer (A) gets SLOs of 99.99 percent availability and p99 under 1 second for small queries. The marketing analytics customer (D) gets SLOs of 99.5 percent availability and p95 under 5 minutes for large queries. These different SLOs are reflected in different pricing tiers. The SLIs are the same — availability and latency — but the SLO targets differ.

**On resource contention**: Resource contention is a first-class SLI dimension. The system must measure "noisy neighbor" events — instances where one customer's workload degrades another customer's experience. The SLI system tracks per-tenant resource consumption (CPU, memory, I/O) alongside the query performance SLIs. If Customer A's queries degrade and the root cause is Customer B's large query consuming resources, Customer B's SLI should include a "resource contention penalty" — a marker that their query caused a neighbor violation. This creates accountability for resource-intensive workloads.

**On fairness of counting**: Long queries and short queries should not count equally toward the availability SLI. A short query that fails affects one user for one second. A long query that fails affects one user for two hours. Weight the failure count by the query duration. A 2-hour query failure counts as 7,200 failure-seconds toward the availability denominator. This ensures that long-running failures receive proportional weight in the SLO.

**On quality SLI**: Use result-set sampling. For a subset of queries (0.1 percent of all queries, selected at random), run a verification query that checks the result against a known-good dataset. This detects data corruption, schema drift, and incorrect query results. The quality SLI is the proportion of sampled queries that return correct results. Use statistical methods to bound the error in the quality estimate.

**SLO targets by customer tier**:

| Tier | Availability SLO | Latency SLO (small) | Latency SLO (large) | Price Premium |
|------|-----------------|---------------------|---------------------|---------------|
| Enterprise | 99.99% | p99 under 500ms | p99 under 30min | 2x |
| Standard | 99.9% | p99 under 2s | p99 under 60min | 1x |
| Basic | 99.5% | p95 under 5s | p95 under 120min | 0.5x |

### Challenge 8: Theoretical Limits of SLI-Based Reliability

This is an open-ended, brainstorm-style challenge with no single correct answer. The goal is to think critically about the fundamental assumptions underlying SLI-based reliability.

**Consider these questions**:

1. Can an SLI system ever prove that a system is reliable? Or does SLI data only provide evidence that a system has been reliable in the past?

2. The standard SLO window is 30 days. Is there a theoretical basis for this number, or is it arbitrary? What would happen if you used a 1-day window? A 365-day window?

3. Error budgets treat all failures equally. A 1-minute outage at 3 AM affects fewer users than a 1-minute outage at 3 PM. Should error budgets be weighted by user traffic?

4. SLI-based decision making assumes that past behavior predicts future behavior. Is this assumption valid for systems that undergo rapid change? Can a system's reliability trajectory change faster than the SLI window can detect?

5. Burn rate alerts require choosing thresholds (burn rate > 6 over 5 minutes). Is there a theoretically optimal threshold, or is it always an engineering judgment call?

6. If you meet your SLO every month for 3 years, does that mean your system is reliable enough? Or do the SLOs need to be reevaluated as user expectations evolve?

#### Expert Discussion

This is a discussion problem, not a computation problem. There are no wrong answers, only well-reasoned and poorly-reasoned positions. Here are perspectives to consider.

**On proving reliability**: SLI data is inherently backward-looking. It tells you how the system performed over the last 30 days, not how it will perform tomorrow. This is the same limitation as all statistical inference — you cannot prove a negative (that the system will never fail). SLIs provide evidence that the system has been reliable, which is useful but not a guarantee. The more data you have, the more confidence you have in the system's reliability — but you never reach 100 percent confidence.

**On the 30-day window**: The 30-day window is not theoretically optimal. It is a convention that balances several competing concerns. A 1-day window is too noisy — a single brief outage can make the SLO look catastrophic. A 365-day window is too slow to reflect improvements. The 30-day window is long enough to absorb normal variability (weekends, holiday traffic, monthly billing cycles) but short enough to provide actionable feedback. Google popularized it in the SRE book, and it became the de facto standard. For systems with weekly cycles (batch processing, reporting), a 7-day window may be more appropriate.

**On traffic-weighted error budgets**: Yes, error budgets should be traffic-weighted. A failure that affects 1 million users should consume more budget than a failure that affects 1,000 users. The standard approach is to weight each failure by the proportion of total traffic it affected. If the 3 AM outage affected 5 percent of daily traffic, it consumes 5 percent of the day's budget allocation. This is more complex to implement but gives a more accurate picture of user impact.

**On the prediction assumption**: The assumption that past behavior predicts future behavior is weakest for systems undergoing rapid change. A service that is being actively rewritten, migrated to a new infrastructure, or experiencing rapid traffic growth has a non-stationary reliability profile. The SLI from 30 days ago may have no bearing on today's reliability. For these systems, use a shorter window (7 to 14 days) or use a weighted window where recent data counts for more than old data.

**On optimal burn rate thresholds**: There is no mathematically optimal threshold. The choice of burn rate threshold involves a tradeoff between detection speed and false positive rate. A threshold of 2 over 1 hour catches slow degradation but may trigger false positives from normal traffic variability. A threshold of 6 over 5 minutes catches fast catastrophic failures but may miss gradual degradation. The standard thresholds come from empirical testing at Google. For your system, the optimal thresholds depend on traffic volume, natural variability, and the cost of false positives vs. the cost of missed detections.

**On reevaluating SLOs**: SLOs must be reevaluated periodically. User expectations evolve. Competitors raise the bar. New features change the reliability landscape. A service that met user expectations in 2024 with 99.9 percent availability may need 99.99 percent in 2026 because users expect more from cloud services. The standard practice is an annual SLO review where each service's SLO targets are reassessed against current user expectations and industry benchmarks.

**On the ethical dimension of SLI design**: There is an often-overlooked ethical dimension to SLI design. The choice of which metrics to track and which users to prioritize reflects value judgments. If you track SLIs for premium users but not for free-tier users, you are making a statement about who matters. If you track SLIs for the checkout flow but not for the accessibility flow, you are making a statement about what matters. The best SLI systems include coverage for all user segments and all critical user journeys, including those that serve underserved populations.

**On the limits of quantification**: Not everything that matters can be measured. User satisfaction, trust, and brand perception are influenced by reliability but are not captured by SLIs. A system that meets its SLOs perfectly for 3 years can still lose users if a competitor offers a fundamentally better product. SLIs measure reliability, not value. The two are correlated, but they are not the same. The best engineers understand that SLIs are a tool, not a goal. The goal is to deliver value to users. SLIs are one of many inputs to that goal.

**On the relationship between SLIs and incident response**: SLIs change the incident response process in subtle ways. Before SLIs, the question during an incident was "is the system down?" After SLIs, the question is "how fast is the error budget being consumed?" This shift changes the conversation from binary (up/down) to continuous (how bad is it, and how fast is it getting worse). The continuous framing allows for better prioritization. A service consuming its error budget at burn rate 20 gets more urgent response than a service at burn rate 2, even if neither has violated the SLO yet.
