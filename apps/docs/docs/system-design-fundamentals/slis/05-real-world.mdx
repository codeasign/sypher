The theory of SLIs is well established. The practice — how companies actually implement, tune, and operate SLI systems at scale — is where the real learning happens. This section covers how four major technology companies use SLIs, the open source and cloud provider tools available, and the lessons distilled from years of production experience.

## Netflix: The Chaos Engineering Approach

Netflix operates one of the largest streaming services in the world, serving over 260 million subscribers across 190 countries. Their SLI philosophy is shaped by their cloud-native architecture and their culture of chaos engineering.

### Netflix's SLI Stack

Netflix uses a custom SLI platform called **Atlas**, which is a telemetry system designed for high-dimensional, high-cardinality metrics. Atlas collects metrics from over 1 million instrumented instances across AWS.

**Key SLI design decisions at Netflix:**

- **Start with the streaming experience, not the backend.** Netflix's primary SLI is "streaming start success" — the proportion of play requests that result in a successful video stream within 2 seconds. This is a composite SLI that combines availability, latency, and quality.
- **Use canary analysis for SLI validation.** Before rolling out a new SLI, Netflix runs it in shadow mode alongside existing SLIs for 30 days. They compare the new SLI's behavior against the old one to detect false positives and false negatives.
- **Chaos engineering validates SLI coverage.** Netflix's Chaos Monkey randomly terminates instances. The team monitors SLI values during chaos experiments to verify that the SLIs capture the degradation. If the SLIs do not move during a Chaos Monkey experiment, the SLIs are not measuring the right thing.

### The Streaming Start SLI

Netflix's most important SLI measures the proportion of play requests that transition to a successful streaming state within 2 seconds. This SLI encompasses:

- **Availability**: The play endpoint returns a 200 response
- **Latency**: The stream starts within 2 seconds
- **Quality**: The video plays without buffering for the first 30 seconds
- **CDN readiness**: The CDN edge has the content cached

The SLI revealed a surprising failure mode: a slow CDN origin could cause the CDN to fetch content from the origin on every request, bypassing the cache. The availability SLI showed green, but the streaming start SLI showed degradation because the startup latency increased from 500 milliseconds to 4 seconds.

## Uber: The Multi-Service Orchestration Challenge

Uber operates a microservice architecture with over 4,000 services. Each ride involves dozens of service calls — from the rider app request, to the dispatch engine, to the driver matching service, to the pricing engine, to the navigation service, to the payment processing service.

### Uber's SLI Approach

Uber developed a system called **SLI Factory** that automatically generates SLIs from service interface definitions. When a new service is created with its API specification, SLI Factory generates the default SLIs, dashboards, and alerting rules.

**Key design decisions:**

- **SLIs are generated from API specifications, not manually configured.** When a developer defines a gRPC endpoint, the SLI system automatically creates availability and latency SLIs for that endpoint. This ensures that every endpoint has baseline SLI coverage.
- **The user journey SLI is the primary metric.** Uber uses a "critical user journey" approach. The most important SLI is "ride request to driver acceptance" — the time from when a rider requests a ride to when a driver accepts it. This journey spans 15 microservices.
- **Uber uses percentile-based SLIs with a twist.** Instead of p99, Uber uses p95 for most SLIs because the p99 at their scale is dominated by edge cases and network outliers. The p95 gives a more stable signal.

### The Dispatch SLI

Uber's dispatch SLI measures the end-to-end time from ride request to driver assignment. This SLI revealed a critical insight: the dispatch engine was meeting its p99 latency target of 500 milliseconds, but the overall user experience was much worse because the dispatch engine's p99 was measured from the time the request reached the dispatch engine — not from the time the user tapped "request ride."

The gap was caused by the mobile app's network stack. The request spent 200 to 300 milliseconds traveling through the mobile carrier network before reaching Uber's servers. The user's perceived latency was 700 to 800 milliseconds at the p99, even though the dispatch engine reported 500 milliseconds.

Uber added a "mobile-to-server" latency SLI that measures the round-trip time from the mobile device to the server and back. This gave them a complete picture of the user experience.

### The SLI Factory in Practice

SLI Factory works by parsing the gRPC service definitions stored in Uber's central service registry. Each service definition specifies the endpoints, request types, and response types. SLI Factory uses this information to generate:

- An availability SLI for each endpoint: is the endpoint returning a successful gRPC status?
- A latency SLI for each endpoint: what is the p95 and p99 response time?
- A throughput SLI for each endpoint: how many requests per second is this endpoint handling?
- A quality SLI for data-critical endpoints: does the response contain the expected fields?

The SLI Factory system also generates the SLO proposal. It looks at the last 30 days of data for each endpoint and proposes an SLO at the observed p99 plus 10 percent buffer. If the endpoint has no historical data, it proposes a default SLO of 99.9 percent.

This automated approach scaled Uber's SLI coverage from 200 services to 4,000 services in 18 months. The key was that teams did not need to manually configure SLIs — they were created automatically and refined over time.

### Uber's Lessons

Uber learned that automated SLI generation is necessary at scale but not sufficient. The automatically generated SLIs provide baseline coverage, but they miss the critical user journey SLI — the end-to-end measurement that captures the user's actual experience. The dispatch SLI was not automatically generated; it was manually defined by the SRE team after a year of analyzing user complaints.

The lesson: automated SLI generation for component-level metrics, combined with manually defined end-to-end SLIs for critical user journeys.

## Discord: Real-Time Communication SLIs

Discord serves over 200 million monthly active users with real-time voice, video, and text communication. Their SLI requirements are uniquely demanding because real-time communication has hard latency requirements — voice chat is unusable if latency exceeds 200 milliseconds.

### Discord's SLI Philosophy

Discord uses a **three-tier SLI system**:

- **Tier 1: Core user experience.** Voice connection quality, message send latency, and notification delivery time. These have the tightest SLOs and the most aggressive alerting.
- **Tier 2: Feature availability.** File uploads, search, thread creation, and emoji reactions. These have moderate SLOs and standard alerting.
- **Tier 3: Infrastructure health.** Gateway connection rate, cache hit ratio, and database replication lag. These are operational metrics without explicit SLOs.

### The Voice Quality SLI

Discord's voice quality SLI measures the proportion of voice sessions that maintain a "good" quality rating throughout the call. A voice session is rated good if:

- Latency stays under 50 milliseconds (one-way)
- Packet loss stays under 1 percent
- Jitter stays under 30 milliseconds
- The session is not interrupted by a disconnect

The SLI is computed per-region, per-voice-server, and per-carrier. This granularity allows Discord to identify which ISPs and which regions have quality problems. They found that certain ISPs had consistently worse voice quality due to asymmetric routing, and they worked with those ISPs to improve peering.

A key insight from Discord's SLI data: voice quality degrades during peak hours (7 PM to 11 PM local time) not because of Discord's servers, but because of ISP congestion. The SLI data helped Discord distinguish between infrastructure problems they could fix and infrastructure problems that required external partnerships.

### Message Send Latency SLI

Discord's message send latency SLI measures the time from when a user presses "send" to when the message appears in the recipient's chat window. This involves:

1. The mobile app sends the message to the Discord gateway
2. The gateway routes the message to the appropriate voice/text server
3. The server stores the message in the database
4. The server pushes the message to all connected clients in the channel
5. Each client receives the message and renders it in the chat UI

The SLI is measured end-to-end, including the client's network round trip. Discord found that the p99 for message send latency was 450 milliseconds, but the p50 was 80 milliseconds. The tail was dominated by users on slow mobile networks and users in large servers (over 10,000 members) where the fan-out to all connected clients took longer.

Discord's response was to introduce a "fast path" for small servers: messages in servers with fewer than 1,000 members are pushed directly to clients without waiting for the database write to confirm. This reduced the p99 for small servers from 450 milliseconds to 120 milliseconds. The database write happens asynchronously in the background. If the async write fails, the message is recovered from the client's local cache.

### Discord's SLI Tooling

Discord built an internal tool called **SLI Dashboard** that shows every service's SLI values, SLO compliance, and error budget consumption in a single unified view. The dashboard has a traffic-light color scheme:

- **Green**: SLI is above the SLO with more than 50 percent of the error budget remaining
- **Yellow**: SLI is above the SLO but less than 50 percent of the error budget remains
- **Red**: SLI is below the SLO (SLO violation)
- **Gray**: Insufficient data to compute the SLI

The dashboard is displayed on a large monitor in the Discord office, giving every engineer a real-time view of the platform's reliability. The dashboard is the first thing the on-call engineer checks when they are paged.

## Cloudflare: Global Edge SLI Collection

Cloudflare operates a global network spanning 330 cities. Their SLI collection challenge is unique: they must collect metrics from thousands of edge servers across the world, aggregate them, and compute global SLI values — all while the edge servers themselves are the ones collecting the metrics.

### Cloudflare's Distributed SLI Approach

Cloudflare does not ship raw metrics from every edge server to a central location. Instead, each edge server computes its own local SLI values and sends only the aggregated summaries every 10 seconds.

Local aggregation at each edge server:

```
Per 10-second window:
  - Total requests: 45,000
  - Good requests (HTTP 2xx with latency < 200ms): 44,910
  - Local SLI: 99.8%
  - p50 latency: 12ms, p99 latency: 187ms
```

The central aggregator receives approximately 900 KB per second from 330 cities — a manageable data rate. Without local aggregation, the raw data would be 15 GB per second.

### The DDoS Detection SLI

Cloudflare uses SLIs for DDoS detection. When a customer's traffic deviates from its normal SLI baseline, the system triggers a mitigation workflow. The SLI baseline is computed per-customer over a 7-day rolling window.

A DDoS attack is detected when:
- The availability SLI drops below 90 percent of the baseline
- The latency SLI exceeds 200 percent of the baseline
- The throughput SLI exceeds 500 percent of the baseline

This approach catches attacks that volumetric detection alone might miss. A slow, low-and-slow attack that gradually degrades latency without overwhelming the network is caught by the latency SLI deviation.

## Open Source Implementations

If you are building your own SLI system, you do not need to start from scratch. Several open source projects provide SLI computation and SLO evaluation capabilities.

### Prometheus and SLO Tooling

Prometheus is the most widely used open source monitoring system. It supports SLI computation through recording rules and alerting rules.

**Prometheus recording rule for SLI:**

```yaml
groups:
  - name: sli.api.availability
    rules:
      - record: sli:api:good_requests:rate_1m
        expr: |
          sum(rate(http_requests_total{service="api", status=~"2.."}[1m]))
          /
          sum(rate(http_requests_total{service="api"}[1m]))
```

**Prometheus alerting rule for burn rate:**

```yaml
groups:
  - name: burn_rate_alerts
    rules:
      - alert: HighBurnRate
        expr: |
          (1 - sli:api:good_requests:rate_1m) /
          (1 - 0.999) > 6
        for: 5m
        labels:
          severity: critical
        annotations:
          summary: "API burn rate exceeds 6x over 5 minutes"
```

Prometheus has limitations for SLI use. It stores data at a fixed resolution (typically 15 seconds), which means you cannot compute precise percentiles over long windows. For percentile-based SLIs, you need a system that maintains histograms.

### Sloth

Sloth is an open source SLO framework that integrates with Prometheus. It generates Prometheus recording rules and alerting rules from a declarative SLO specification.

**Sloth SLO specification:**

```yaml
version: "v1"
service: "api"
slos:
  - name: "availability"
    objective: 99.9
    sli:
      events:
        total: "http_requests_total{service='api'}"
        good: "http_requests_total{service='api', status=~'2..'}"
    alerting:
      page:
        - window: 5m
          burnRate: 6
```

Sloth handles the error budget calculation, burn rate computation, and alerting rule generation automatically. It is the recommended starting point for teams using Prometheus.

**Sloth's key features:**
- Generates Prometheus recording rules for SLI computation
- Generates Prometheus alerting rules for burn rate alerts (multi-window, multi-burn-rate)
- Supports both availability SLIs (good/total events) and latency SLIs (histogram-based)
- Generates Grafana dashboard JSON for SLO visualization
- Supports custom SLI plugins for non-standard metrics
- Has a CLI tool for validating SLO specifications before deployment
- Integrates with OpenSLO format for cross-platform compatibility

Sloth is maintained by the SRE community and has been adopted by companies including BlaBlaCar, HelloFresh, and Adyen. It is licensed under Apache 2.0.

### OpenSLO

OpenSLO is a specification format for defining SLOs in a vendor-neutral way. It defines a YAML schema for SLOs, SLIs, and error budgets that can be used with any monitoring backend.

**OpenSLO example:**

```yaml
apiVersion: openslo/v1
kind: SLO
metadata:
  name: api-availability
  displayName: API Availability
spec:
  service: api
  description: Availability of the public API
  budgetingMethod: Occurrences
  objectives:
    - target: 0.999
      displayName: 99.9%
  timeWindows:
    - duration: 30d
      isRolling: true
  indicator:
    metadata:
      name: api-availability-sli
    spec:
      ratioMetric:
        counter: true
        good:
          source: prometheus
          query: >
            sum(rate(http_requests_total{status=~"2.."}[1m]))
        total:
          source: prometheus
          query: >
            sum(rate(http_requests_total[1m]))
```

OpenSLO is supported by multiple tools including Sloth, Keptn, and Nobl9. Adopting OpenSLO prevents vendor lock-in — you can switch monitoring backends without rewriting your SLO definitions.

### Keptn

Keptn is an open source platform for SLO-driven deployment and operations. It evaluates SLIs during deployments to decide whether to promote or roll back a release.

In a Keptn workflow, a deployment is promoted to production only if the new version's SLIs meet the SLO targets. If the latency SLI degrades by more than 10 percent, the deployment is automatically rolled back.

**Keptn's SLI evaluation workflow:**

1. A new version of the service is deployed to a canary environment
2. Keptn routes a percentage of production traffic to the canary (1 percent, 5 percent, 20 percent, 100 percent)
3. At each traffic level, Keptn evaluates the SLIs against the SLO targets
4. If the SLIs meet the SLOs, the traffic percentage increases
5. If the SLIs fail the SLOs, the deployment is rolled back and the canary traffic is routed back to the stable version
6. If all traffic levels pass, the canary is promoted to production

This automated approach prevents deployments that degrade reliability. Keptn integration with Prometheus, Datadog, and Dynatrace makes it compatible with most monitoring infrastructure.

### Pyroscope / Continuous Profiling SLIs

An emerging area is using continuous profiling data as a source for SLIs. Memory usage, CPU consumption, and garbage collection pause times can be correlated with user-facing SLIs to identify the root cause of degradation.

For example, if the latency SLI degrades from p99 of 200 milliseconds to 400 milliseconds, the continuous profiler can show that the degradation coincided with a spike in garbage collection pause times. The team can then correlate the GC spike with a specific code change or traffic pattern.

This approach is not yet widely adopted but represents the next frontier in SLI-based operations. The open source project Pyroscope (now part of Grafana) provides continuous profiling that can be used for this purpose.

## Cloud Provider Implementations

All major cloud providers offer managed SLI/SLO solutions.

### Google Cloud Monitoring (Cloud Operations)

Google Cloud's monitoring service includes built-in SLO support. You define SLIs from metric types (latency, availability, or custom metrics), set SLO targets, and the system automatically computes error budgets and burn rate alerts.

**Key features:**

- Integrated with Google Cloud's request-based metrics (HTTP, gRPC, and Cloud Endpoints)
- Burn rate alerting with configurable windows and thresholds
- SLO dashboard with error budget consumption visualization
- Integration with Cloud Operations incident management
- Multi-window multi-burn-rate alerting (endorsed by Google SRE)
- SLO import/export via API for automated SLO management
- Custom SLI support using Cloud Monitoring's metric query language

**GCP's strength**: Built-in burn rate alerting is the most mature among cloud providers. Google's SRE team contributed to the implementation, so it follows the canonical SRE practices. The integration with Google Cloud's load balancers means you can set up SLIs for external HTTPS load balancers with zero instrumentation — the load balancer emits the metrics automatically.

**GCP's weakness**: Limited to Google Cloud infrastructure. If you use a multi-cloud or hybrid architecture, you cannot use GCP's SLO features for non-GCP services. The custom SLI query language has a learning curve and is not portable to other monitoring systems.

### AWS CloudWatch ServiceLens

AWS CloudWatch offers ServiceLens, which provides SLI tracking with service maps. You define SLIs from CloudWatch metrics or X-Ray traces, and the system maps them to service endpoints.

**Key features:**

- Trace-based latency SLIs from X-Ray
- Automatic SLI generation from API Gateway endpoints
- Integration with AWS Systems Manager for incident response
- SLA tracking for business reporting
- Custom SLI support using CloudWatch Metrics Math
- ServiceLens service maps with health annotations

**AWS's strength**: Deep integration with the AWS ecosystem. If you use API Gateway, Lambda, ECS, and X-Ray, the SLI data flows automatically. The service maps provide a visual representation of how SLIs relate to service dependencies.

**AWS's weakness**: No built-in burn rate alerting. You must manually configure burn rate calculations using CloudWatch metrics math. Error budget tracking is not automated — you need to implement it yourself or use a third-party tool. CloudWatch pricing can be expensive at high metric volumes.

### Azure Monitor Application Insights

Azure Monitor provides SLO tracking through Application Insights. You define SLIs from application telemetry, and the system computes availability and latency SLOs.

**Key features:**

- Synthetic transaction SLIs for availability
- Browser-side latency SLIs for frontend performance
- Integration with Azure DevOps for deployment gates
- Dashboard templates for common SLO patterns
- Smart detection using machine learning for anomaly-based SLI deviation alerts

**Azure's strength**: Strong DevOps integration. The ability to gate deployments based on SLI performance is built into Azure DevOps pipelines. The smart detection feature uses machine learning to establish baseline SLI behavior and alert on deviations without manual threshold configuration.

**Azure's weakness**: Less mature SLO features compared to GCP. Error budget tracking requires manual setup. Burn rate alerting is not built-in and must be implemented using Azure Monitor alerts with custom log queries. The documentation for SLO features is sparse compared to GCP and AWS.

### Lesson 1: Start with Availability, Add Latency, Then Quality

### Lesson 1: Start with Availability, Add Latency, Then Quality

The most common SLI adoption path is: start with a simple availability SLI, then add latency, then add quality. Each step adds complexity. Availability is the easiest SLI to implement because it requires only a status code check. Latency requires a histogram data structure. Quality requires content validation, which is the most complex.

Do not try to implement all three types at once. Start with availability, get the pipeline working, and then iteratively add the other types.

### Lesson 2: SLIs Without SLOs Are Just Metrics

Many teams collect SLI data but never set SLO targets. The result is a dashboard full of metrics with no actionable signal. Without SLOs, you cannot compute error budgets, set burn rate alerts, or make data-driven decisions about reliability vs. velocity.

Set an initial SLO even if you are not sure it is the right target. You can adjust it later. The important thing is to have a target that the team can rally around.

### Lesson 3: Burn Rate Alerts Are Better Than Threshold Alerts

Traditional threshold alerts — "alert if error rate exceeds 5 percent" — create two problems. First, you must choose the right threshold, which requires knowing what error rate is abnormal — a question that is hard to answer without historical data. Second, the threshold does not account for the SLO window; a 5 percent error rate for 30 seconds is different from a 5 percent error rate for 3 hours.

Burn rate alerts solve both problems. They are automatically calibrated to the SLO, and they account for the time window. A service with a 99.9 percent SLO has a well-defined burn rate threshold regardless of the service's traffic volume.

### Lesson 4: Instrument Everything, SLI a Subset

The engineering team should instrument every request — every service should emit latency, status, and count data. But only a subset of these metrics should become SLIs with SLOs. The remaining metrics are useful for debugging and operational visibility but do not need error budgets.

This lesson is often violated. Teams set SLOs on every metric they collect, creating 50 SLOs per service. The SLOs become meaningless because no one can track 50 error budgets.

### Lesson 5: SLI Data Is a Business Asset

SLI data is not just for engineering. It is a business asset that informs product decisions, capacity planning, and customer communication.

**Product decisions**: If the latency SLI shows that the 99th percentile is slow, the product team might decide to optimize the most common user journeys rather than adding new features.

**Capacity planning**: If the throughput SLI shows sustained growth at 15 percent month-over-month, the infrastructure team can plan capacity upgrades before the system becomes overloaded.

**Customer communication**: If a customer reports a reliability issue, the SLI data provides an objective basis for the conversation. "Our availability SLI for the region you are in was 99.95 percent over the last 30 days, which is within our SLO of 99.9 percent. Let me look at the specific time window you mentioned."
