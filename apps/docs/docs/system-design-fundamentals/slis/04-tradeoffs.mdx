SLIs are powerful tools, but they come with hidden costs and failure modes. This section covers the tensions you must navigate, the ways SLI systems can fail, and the anti-patterns that turn SLIs from a reliability tool into a source of confusion.

## The Core Tension

The fundamental tension in SLI design is between **precision** and **simplicity**. A highly precise SLI captures every nuance of user experience but is expensive to compute, hard to understand, and fragile. A simple SLI is cheap, robust, and easy to explain, but may miss important signals.

This tension is inherent in the nature of measurement. Every measurement system has a precision limit. You can increase precision by spending more resources — more memory, more CPU, more storage, more engineering time — but the returns are diminishing. The first 90 percent of precision costs 10 percent of the resources. The last 10 percent of precision costs 90 percent of the resources.

The art of SLI design is knowing where the diminishing returns set in for your specific system. A search engine that loses 1 percent of relevance for 10x lower cost is a good tradeoff. A payment system that loses 0.01 percent of accuracy for 2x lower cost is a bad tradeoff.

The tension manifests in four specific decisions:

**How many SLIs should you have?** Too few and you miss failure modes. Too many and you spend more time managing SLIs than running the service. The recommended range is 3 to 5 SLIs per service.

**What percentile should you target?** The p99 is the standard choice, but it is 10x more expensive to compute than p50. The p999 is 100x more expensive. Each additional nine of precision adds significant cost.

**What window size should you use?** A 7-day window gives fast feedback but is noisy. A 90-day window is stable but slow to react. The choice affects how quickly you detect degradation and how quickly you consume error budget.

**Should you include throughput in the SLI?** Throughput SLIs detect capacity problems but are harder to set SLOs for because traffic varies naturally. A 50 percent drop in traffic might be a failure or might be a holiday weekend.

**How to use the decision matrix**: Start by identifying your service type in the left column. Then look at the recommended SLI types, percentiles, windows, and thresholds for that service type. These are starting points, not absolute rules. If your service has unique characteristics — for example, a user-facing API that serves financial transactions — adjust the thresholds accordingly.

**Why user-facing APIs use p99**: User-facing APIs have a wide distribution of latency values. Most requests are fast (p50 under 50 milliseconds), but a small percentage can be slow due to network conditions, database contention, or garbage collection. The p99 captures the experience of the slowest 1 percent of users, who are the most likely to complain or churn. Using p95 would miss the worst-case experiences. Using p999 would be too noisy at most traffic volumes.

**Why batch processors use p95**: Batch processors process jobs that take minutes or hours. The latency distribution is naturally wider than for real-time APIs. Using p99 for a batch processor would be too sensitive to outliers — a single job that takes 3 hours because of an abnormally large input would trigger the SLO, even though the system is working correctly. The p95 provides a more stable signal by excluding the top 5 percent of outliers.

**Why internal services use 7-day windows**: Internal services change more frequently than user-facing services. A 7-day window provides faster feedback on the impact of changes. If an internal service's latency degrades after a deployment, the team sees the impact within 7 days rather than waiting 30 days. The shorter window is acceptable because internal services have fewer users and lower reliability requirements.

**Why databases use p99 under 10ms**: Databases are the foundation of most applications. A database that takes 100 milliseconds to respond to a simple query causes cascading latency throughout the dependent services. The 10-millisecond threshold ensures that the database responds quickly enough that it does not become the bottleneck for higher-level services. The p99 captures the tail latency that is most likely to cause user-facing degradation.

## CAP Theorem Applied to SLI Systems

The CAP theorem — consistency, availability, partition tolerance — applies to SLI systems in a specific way.

**Consistency**: Every SLO evaluation must see the same data. If two evaluators query the same aggregator at the same time, they should get the same SLI value.

**Availability**: The SLI system must always be able to answer queries, even if some nodes are down.

**Partition Tolerance**: The system must continue operating when the network between nodes is broken.

In practice, SLI systems choose **availability over consistency** during network partitions. When a partition occurs, each side of the partition continues computing SLI values independently. The two sides may disagree on the SLI value, but both sides continue to produce alerts. A consistent SLI that is not available is worse than a slightly inconsistent SLI that is always available.

The tradeoff is acceptable because SLI data is ultimately soft — it measures reliability rather than representing a source of truth like a financial transaction. If the SLI value is off by 0.01 percent during a 30-second partition, the impact on the 30-day SLO is negligible.

**Practical implications of the CAP tradeoff:**

The decision to favor availability over consistency in SLI systems has several practical implications:

- **Dashboard values may diverge during a partition**. Two engineers looking at the SLI dashboard from different locations may see different values. This is acceptable because the divergence is temporary and small (typically less than 0.1 percent).

- **Burn rate alerts may fire on only one side of the partition**. If the partition splits the aggregation nodes, one side may see a burn rate of 2 while the other side sees a burn rate of 0.5. The side that sees the higher burn rate fires the alert, which is the correct behavior — it is better to have a false positive alert during a partition than to miss a real failure.

- **SLO compliance reporting should use the union of data from both sides**. When the partition heals, the compliance report should merge the data from both sides. This ensures that the official SLO compliance numbers are based on the complete dataset, even if the real-time dashboard showed conflicting values during the partition.

- **Alert routing must account for partition awareness**. If the partition separates the aggregation node from the alert router, alerts may not be delivered. The solution is to run the alert router on the same side of the partition as the aggregation node. If the aggregation node is in US-East, the alert router should also be in US-East.

## Failure Modes

SLI systems can fail in several ways. Understanding these failure modes helps you design against them.

### Failure Mode 1: SLI Blindness

The SLI system reports all green, but users are experiencing a problem. The SLI is not measuring the right thing.

**Example**: Your API returns HTTP 200 with a null response body because a downstream service returned null data. The availability SLI counts this as a success because the status code is 200. Your quality SLI is not set up to detect null payloads. Users see blank pages. Your dashboard shows 100 percent healthy.

**Mitigation**: Quality SLIs must validate response content, not just status codes. Set up a quality SLI that checks for null or empty responses alongside the availability SLI.

### Failure Mode 2: Denominator Pollution

Invalid traffic dilutes the SLI, masking real failures. Health checks, synthetic monitoring probes, and known scrapers are counted in the denominator, making the SLI look better than the user experience.

**Example**: Your service gets 5,000 requests per second during peak hours. Your SLO is 99.9 percent. Unknown to you, a monitoring system sends 50,000 health check requests per second — 10 times the real traffic. The health checks always succeed. Your SLI shows 99.99 percent, but actual user availability is 99.5 percent.

**Mitigation**: Filter out all non-user traffic from SLI computation. Tag each request with a source identifier and exclude known monitoring sources. Review the filter list quarterly because new monitoring systems are added over time.

### Failure Mode 3: Laddering

A team that misses its SLO renegotiates the SLO downward rather than fixing the underlying reliability issue. Over time, SLIs become meaningless because the SLO targets are set to match whatever the system naturally delivers.

**Example**: Service A has a latency SLO of p99 under 200 milliseconds. The team misses it for three months straight. Instead of improving latency, the team renegotiates the SLO to p99 under 500 milliseconds. The next quarter, they miss that too, and renegotiate to p99 under 1 second. The SLO is now useless as a reliability target.

**Mitigation**: SLO changes must be reviewed by an independent team — typically a central SRE or platform engineering team. SLOs can only be relaxed if accompanied by a documented plan to restore them within a defined time period.

### Failure Mode 4: Alert Fatigue from Burn Rate Alerts

Burn rate alerts are configured with thresholds that are too sensitive, causing the on-call team to receive pages for every minor degradation. The team starts ignoring alerts, and real failures go unnoticed.

**Example**: A team configures a burn rate alert at 1.5 over 5 minutes. At their traffic volume, normal traffic variability causes the burn rate to fluctuate between 0.5 and 3.0 during every lunch hour. The on-call engineer receives 10 pages per day, all of which self-resolve within 2 minutes. After two weeks, the engineer stops responding to all pages.

**Mitigation**: Burn rate alerts need a threshold that is high enough for the team's traffic volume. For a service with low traffic, a 5-minute window is too short — the burn rate will be noisy. Use a longer window (30 minutes or 1 hour) for low-traffic services.

### Failure Mode 5: SLI Feedback Loop (continued)

The SLI system itself causes the failure it is trying to measure. When the SLI system goes down, it generates alerts that page the on-call team, which then investigates the SLI system instead of the actual service. The real service failure goes unaddressed.

**Example**: The SLI aggregation node crashes due to a memory leak. The SLO evaluator stops receiving data and reports "insufficient data" for all services. The burn rate alerts fire for every service simultaneously, paging all on-call engineers. The engineers spend 45 minutes investigating whether the services are actually down before realizing it is the SLI system itself.

**Mitigation**: The SLI system must be monitored independently. The SLO evaluator should have a health check that detects data loss and suppresses alerts rather than creating a cascade of false pages. Separate the SLI monitoring infrastructure from the service monitoring infrastructure.

### Failure Mode 6: Window Contamination

The sliding window is contaminated by data from a previous period that should have been excluded. This happens when the circular buffer overwrites old data, but the old data was not properly written to the database before being overwritten.

**Example**: An aggregation node crashes and restarts. It reads the last 30 days of data from the database, but the database write for the last 5 minutes before the crash was lost. The in-memory sliding window now has a 5-minute gap. The SLI calculation over the next 5 minutes uses 29 days, 23 hours, and 55 minutes of data instead of 30 days, making the SLI value slightly inaccurate.

**Mitigation**: The aggregation node stores a "checkpoint" every 5 minutes in the database. On recovery, it reads the latest checkpoint and replays the Kafka queue from that point. This ensures that no data is lost and no data is double-counted. The checkpoint includes the exact offset in the Kafka queue, so the node knows exactly where to resume.

### Failure Mode 7: SLO Target Drift

The SLO target gradually drifts away from the actual user experience. The team meets the SLO every month, but user satisfaction is declining. The SLI is measuring the wrong thing, or the users' expectations have changed.

**Example**: A video streaming service has an SLO of "p99 startup latency under 5 seconds." When this SLO was set, users watched on desktop computers with fast internet connections. Two years later, most users watch on mobile devices with slower connections. The p99 latency is still under 5 seconds, but users are unhappy because 5 seconds feels slow on mobile. The SLO needs to be tightened to 2 seconds, but the team has no data on mobile-specific latency.

**Mitigation**: Segment SLIs by platform and user segment. Set separate SLOs for desktop and mobile. Track user satisfaction metrics (NPS, churn rate, support tickets) alongside SLI data. If satisfaction declines while SLIs are green, the SLIs are measuring the wrong thing. Run an annual SLO review where the targets are validated against current user expectations.

### Failure Mode 8: SLI Overshoot

The team over-optimizes for the SLI, making the metric look great while the actual user experience degrades in other dimensions. This is a form of Goodhart's Law: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.

**Example**: A team has a latency SLI of p99 under 200 milliseconds. To meet this target, they add aggressive caching. The cache serves 80 percent of requests from memory, keeping latency under 10 milliseconds. But the cache serves stale data. The quality SLI is not set up to detect staleness. Users see outdated information, but the latency dashboard shows green.

**Mitigation**: Always have SLIs for the outcomes you want to avoid, not just the outcomes you want to achieve. If you optimize for latency, add a freshness SLI. If you optimize for availability, add a quality SLI. The combination of SLIs prevents the team from optimizing one metric at the expense of another.

The SLI system itself causes the failure it is trying to measure. When the SLI system goes down, it generates alerts that page the on-call team, which then investigates the SLI system instead of the actual service. The real service failure goes unaddressed.

**Example**: The SLI aggregation node crashes due to a memory leak. The SLO evaluator stops receiving data and reports "insufficient data" for all services. The burn rate alerts fire for every service simultaneously, paging all on-call engineers. The engineers spend 45 minutes investigating whether the services are actually down before realizing it is the SLI system itself.

**Mitigation**: The SLI system must be monitored independently. The SLO evaluator should have a health check that detects data loss and suppresses alerts rather than creating a cascade of false pages. Separate the SLI monitoring infrastructure from the service monitoring infrastructure.

## Anti-Patterns

Beyond outright failures, certain patterns cause chronic problems with SLIs. These anti-patterns are common enough to have names.

### Anti-Pattern 1: The Kitchen Sink

You define 20 SLIs for a single service because you want to measure everything. The result is a dashboard with 20 graphs, none of which gets attention. When all 20 are green, you do not know which ones matter. When one turns red, you do not know whether to act.

**Fix**: Limit each service to 3 to 5 SLIs. If you want to track additional metrics, keep them as secondary metrics that do not have SLOs or error budgets.

### Anti-Pattern 2: The SLA Trap

You set every SLO to match the contractual SLA. The SLA is 99.9 percent, so the SLO is 99.9 percent. The problem is that the SLA includes a 10 percent service credit for violations. You have no buffer between the SLO and the contractual obligation. A single bad day triggers both an SLO violation and an SLA penalty.

**Fix**: Set the SLO tighter than the SLA. If the SLA is 99.9 percent, set the SLO to 99.95 percent. The 0.05 percent gap is your safety margin. Violations of the internal SLO trigger incident response without triggering financial penalties.

### Anti-Pattern 3: The Golden Metric

You define a single composite SLI that combines latency, availability, quality, and throughput into one number. The composite SLI is always between 0.97 and 0.99, but no one knows what it means. When it drops, no one knows which component caused the drop.

**Fix**: Keep SLIs separate. A composite dashboard is fine for a high-level view, but each SLI must be independently measurable and actionable. If you cannot tell which component caused a change, the composite SLI is not useful.

### Anti-Pattern 4: The False Floor

You set the SLO to 100 percent. This is impossible to achieve — there is always some amount of failure in any real system. The team spends all their time chasing the last 0.01 percent of reliability, which costs 100x more than the first 99.9 percent.

**Fix**: No service should have a 100 percent SLO. The highest realistic SLO is 99.999 percent (five nines), which allows approximately 5 minutes of downtime per year. Even this is expensive and only justified for critical infrastructure.

### Anti-Pattern 5: The Snapshot

You compute the SLI at a single point in time and use that value to make decisions. A 1-minute snapshot might show 95 percent availability, but the 30-day window shows 99.9 percent. The team panics over a transient blip.

**Fix**: Always evaluate SLIs over the full SLO window. Short-term windows are for burn rate alerts, not for SLO evaluation. A 5-minute blip of high error rate does not violate the SLO — it is absorbed by the 30-day window.

### Anti-Pattern 6: The All-or-Nothing SLO

You define the SLO as a single hard threshold. If the SLI is 99.89 percent and the SLO is 99.9 percent, the team treats it as a catastrophic failure. If the SLI is 99.99 percent, the team treats it as success. The binary pass/fail mindset ignores the gradient of reliability.

**Fix**: Use a "warning zone" concept. Define a secondary SLO at 99.5 percent for the same SLI. If the primary SLO (99.9 percent) is met, celebrate. If the primary SLO is missed but the warning SLO is met, investigate. If the warning SLO is also missed, treat it as a critical incident. This gives the team a gradient of response without binary pass/fail panic.

### Anti-Pattern 7: The SLO Only for New Services

You only define SLIs for new services. Legacy services are exempt because "they are stable and we know how they behave." The result is that your most critical systems — the ones that have been running for years and have the most users — have no SLI coverage.

**Fix**: SLIs are most important for legacy services because those services have the most users and the most risk. Define SLIs for existing services first, then apply the same standards to new services. The oldest database in your infrastructure should have the most mature SLI coverage, not the least.

### Anti-Pattern 8: The Metric Martyr

You define SLIs that are so expensive to measure that the cost of the SLI system exceeds the cost of the failures it prevents. The team spends more time maintaining the SLI system than running the service.

**Example**: A team decides to measure the p99.999 latency for every request on a service that handles 10 million requests per second. The histogram storage requires 10 TB of RAM across the aggregation cluster. The cluster costs $50,000 per month. The failures the p99.999 SLI detects are rare — perhaps one incident per year that causes $10,000 in lost revenue. The SLI system costs 60x more than the failures it prevents.

**Fix**: Match the precision of the SLI to the value of the reliability it protects. For most services, p99 is sufficient. Only use p999 or higher for systems where each millisecond of latency costs thousands of dollars in revenue. A cost-benefit analysis of the SLI system itself is a healthy exercise.

## When NOT to Use SLIs

SLIs are not the right tool for every situation. Here are cases where you should avoid or de-emphasize SLIs.

### Experimental Services

A service that is still in active development, has fewer than 1,000 users, or is being rewritten frequently does not benefit from SLIs. The service changes too fast for a 30-day SLO to be meaningful. By the time you have enough data to evaluate the SLO, the service has changed.

**Alternative**: Use simple dashboard metrics without SLOs. Track latency and error rate as raw numbers. Add SLIs when the service stabilizes and has meaningful user traffic.

### Internal Tools with Few Users

An internal tool used by 10 people in the company does not need formal SLIs. The cost of setting up the SLI pipeline, configuring alerts, and maintaining the SLO exceeds the benefit. The 10 users can tell you directly when the tool is slow or broken.

**Alternative**: Use a simple uptime monitor. If the tool is down, the users tell you. No SLIs needed.

### One-Shot Operations

A service that runs once per day, processes a batch job, and then goes idle does not benefit from percentile-based latency SLIs. The latency is dominated by the batch size, which varies day to day. A p99 latency over 30 days is meaningless when the service runs 30 times.

**Alternative**: Use duration-based SLIs: "the job completes within 4 hours." Track the completion rate: "the job completes successfully 99 percent of the time."

### Systems Where Users Have No Alternative

A platform service that no user can leave — an internal database, a core authentication service, or a single-source-of-truth system — does not benefit from competitive SLIs. Users cannot switch to a competitor if the service is slow. The SLI still matters for reliability, but the SLO should be set based on business requirements rather than competitive benchmarks.

**Alternative**: Set SLOs based on the downstream impact. If each millisecond of database latency costs the company $10,000 in lost revenue, the SLO should reflect that cost. The SLO is a business decision, not a technical one.

### Prototype Services

A service that is still in the prototype phase — fewer than 100 users, less than 1,000 requests per day, and changing daily — does not benefit from SLIs. The service will likely be rewritten or decommissioned before a 30-day SLO window completes. The cost of setting up the SLI pipeline exceeds the value of the reliability data.

**Alternative**: Use ad-hoc monitoring. Check the service manually once per day. If it is working, great. If it is not, fix it. Add SLIs when the service has stable usage patterns and a committed team.

### Services with No Defined Success Criteria

Some services have no clear definition of "success." A data exploration tool that runs arbitrary user queries cannot define a quality SLI because it does not know what result the user expects. A machine learning model service cannot define a latency SLI because the inference time depends on the input size, which varies unpredictably.

**Alternative**: For services without clear success criteria, focus on availability SLIs (the service is running and accepting requests) and throughput SLIs (the service is processing requests at an expected rate). Leave quality and latency SLIs until the success criteria are defined.

### Overlapping SLIs Across Services

In a deeply interdependent architecture, defining SLIs for every service independently can create conflicting incentives. If Service A has a latency SLI of p99 under 100 milliseconds and Service B depends on Service A, Service A may optimize its latency at the expense of correctness — returning cached data that is stale but fast. The SLIs of the two services are in conflict.

**Alternative**: In deeply interdependent architectures, define SLIs at the product level rather than the service level. Measure the end-to-end user experience that spans multiple services. The individual services can use the product-level SLI as a guide for their internal optimization, without having their own SLIs that create perverse incentives.
