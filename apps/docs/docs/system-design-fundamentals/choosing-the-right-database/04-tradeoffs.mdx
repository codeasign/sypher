# Choosing the Right Database — Trade-offs & Failure Modes

Every database choice is a bet placed with incomplete information. You do not know your future access patterns, your traffic volume in six months, or the queries your product team will invent next quarter. The best you can do is understand the trade-off axes, know which failure modes await on each path, and build enough margin into your choice that you survive being wrong.

This page maps the decision landscape: the core tension at the heart of every database choice, a decision matrix for comparing options, how CAP theorem positions databases during failures, the specific failure modes that follow from bad selection decisions, anti-patterns that lure engineering teams into preventable pain, and — most importantly — when to defer the choice entirely.

## Core Tension: Speed vs. Correctness vs. Scale

The fundamental tension in database selection is that you cannot maximize speed, correctness, and scale simultaneously. Every database optimizes for two of these three at the expense of the third. Understanding which axis your workload prizes most determines the shape of your database choice.

**Speed** means low latency for individual operations — a single key-value lookup returning in under a millisecond. Speed-optimized databases (Redis, Memcached, in-memory tables) sacrifice durability and query flexibility. They keep everything in RAM, use simple data models, and offer no joins or complex queries.

**Correctness** means strong consistency, ACID transactions, referential integrity, and the guarantee that every query returns a result that reflects all completed writes. Correctness-optimized databases (PostgreSQL with full durability, CockroachDB, Spanner) sacrifice write throughput and partition availability. They coordinate across nodes, write WAL entries to disk on every commit, and reject writes during network partitions.

**Scale** means the ability to handle growing data volume and request throughput by adding more nodes. Scale-optimized databases (Cassandra, DynamoDB, S3) sacrifice consistency and query richness. They distribute data across many nodes with no single point of coordination, accept writes on any node during a partition, and offer only simple query patterns.

The tension is not theoretical — it manifests in every dimension of database behavior.

When you choose a database, you place a bet on which of these three tensions will matter most to your application. The bet is harder because your answer changes over time — your prototype needs speed (iterate fast), your growth phase needs correctness (don't lose orders), and your mature phase needs scale (handle millions of users).

The practical approach is to optimize for the current bottleneck while designing for the future constraint. A startup that chooses Spanner "for future scale" adds months of operational complexity before needing any of Spanner's distributed consistency guarantees. A startup that chooses SQLite "for simplicity" hits a write concurrency wall at 100 concurrent writers and must migrate databases while growing.

### The Hidden Cost of Wrong Positioning

Choosing wrong on the speed-correctness-scale triangle manifests in measurable operational pain:

- **Choosing scale when you need correctness.** An e-commerce platform uses Cassandra for its order database because "we need to scale to millions of orders." Cassandra offers eventual consistency by default. During a network partition, two customers buy the last item in inventory. Both orders are accepted. The platform oversells inventory, processes refunds, and loses customer trust. The cost of the wrong choice is not just the refund — it is the engineering time to add read-repair logic, the complexity of compensating transactions, and the migration to a correct database a year later.

- **Choosing speed when you need scale.** A social media app uses Redis as its primary database for session data. Sessions grow to 500 GB — beyond a single node's RAM capacity. The team implements Redis Cluster, then discovers that cross-slot operations are limited, failover is manual, and persistence during failover is fragile. The cost is months of operational work to make a speed-optimized database behave like a scale-optimized one, followed by a migration to a database designed for the data size from day one.

- **Choosing correctness when you need speed.** A real-time bidding system uses Spanner for its ad auction database. Each bid requires a global-consistency read across continents, adding 200 milliseconds of latency. The auction loses to competitors using DynamoDB with local reads. The cost is lost revenue — millions of dollars in missed bids — because the database was chosen for correctness guarantees that the business did not actually need for bid data (a lost bid is not a data-integrity failure; it is a business outcome).

The discipline of positioning on the triangle: be honest about your actual constraints at each stage, and design your architecture so that repositioning (adding a cache layer, adding a secondary database for a specific workload) is cheap enough to attempt.

### Interpreting the Matrix: When Each Database Wins

**PostgreSQL wins when:** You need joins, transactions, schema enforcement, and a rich query layer. It is the default for any system where data relationships are complex and correctness is important. PostgreSQL handles 90 percent of production workloads without any horizontal scaling. The far more common failure is choosing a distributed NoSQL database for a workload that a single PostgreSQL instance could have handled trivially.

**MongoDB wins when:** You have semi-structured data with varying fields per document, you need to iterate on the schema quickly, and your query patterns are document-centric (look up a document by ID, filter by fields within the document). MongoDB's document model maps naturally to JSON APIs — the data your application sends and receives is already in document shape. The cost is paid when you need joins across collections or transactions across documents, both of which MongoDB supports but with performance and complexity trade-offs.

**DynamoDB wins when:** You have a known, bounded access pattern (primary key lookups, secondary index queries on well-defined attributes), you need single-digit-millisecond latency at any scale, and you want zero operational overhead. DynamoDB's simplicity is its killer feature — no servers to manage, no replication to configure, no indexes to tune (beyond choosing the right ones). The cost is paid when your access patterns change and you need new query types that the original key design does not support.

**Cassandra wins when:** You need very high write throughput (tens of thousands of writes per second per node), you can design your data model around query patterns (Cassandra requires you to model tables for specific queries, not for normalized data), and you need multi-datacenter replication with no single point of failure. Cassandra is the database for time-series event data, IoT sensor data, and application logs. The cost is paid in operational complexity — Cassandra clusters require careful tuning, monitoring, and repair.

**Redis wins when:** You need sub-millisecond access to small pieces of data — user sessions, rate limiter counters, cache entries, leaderboards. Redis is not a primary database (with few exceptions — it can be the primary for short-lived session data). It is a specialist tool for operations that need to be fast at any cost. The cost is paid when Redis is treated as a general-purpose database: data that exceeds RAM, persistence that must survive node failures, and queries that go beyond simple key-value or sorted-set operations.

## CAP Positioning Across Database Types

The CAP theorem remains the most practical mental model for understanding how databases behave during network partitions. The theorem states that a distributed database can guarantee at most two of Consistency, Availability, and Partition tolerance at any given moment. Since partitions (network failures that split a cluster into groups that cannot communicate) are a fact of distributed systems, the real choice is between CP (consistency + partition tolerance) and AP (availability + partition tolerance).

### CP Databases: Consistency Over Availability

CP databases (Spanner, CockroachDB, ETCD, ZooKeeper, Consul, MongoDB with majority write concern, PostgreSQL with synchronous replication) prioritize consistency over availability during a partition. They use consensus protocols (Paxos, Raft) to ensure that a majority of nodes agree on every write before acknowledging it. During a partition, the minority side — the group of nodes that cannot communicate with the majority — stops accepting writes. Clients connected to the minority side see errors or timeouts.

**When CP is the right choice:** Any system where data correctness is non-negotiable and stale data causes real-world harm. Payment processing, inventory management (overselling is more expensive than a temporary outage), account balances, identity systems, configuration stores (ETCD, ZooKeeper). In these systems, rejecting a write during a partition is acceptable because accepting an incorrect write is worse.

**The practical CP cost:** Reduced availability during partitions. If a partition is rare (most well-run clusters see partitions in single-digit percent of operating time), the availability reduction is negligible. If partitions are frequent (poor network, multi-region deployment with unreliable WAN links), the CP choice means your system is frequently unavailable for a subset of users.

### AP Databases: Availability Over Consistency

AP databases (DynamoDB, Cassandra, CosmosDB, Redis Cluster, MongoDB with w=1 write concern, ScyllaDB) prioritize availability over consistency during a partition. They accept writes on any available node during a partition, then reconcile conflicts when the partition heals using last-writer-wins (LWW), CRDTs, or application-level conflict resolution.

**When AP is the right choice:** Any system where availability is the priority and a moment of staleness is acceptable. User sessions (a stale session is better than a rejected login), social feeds (showing a slightly stale feed is better than showing an error), analytics events (lost events are acceptable within tolerance), content delivery (serving slightly stale content is fine).

**The practical AP cost:** Application complexity. Your code must handle:
- Stale reads — a read returns data that does not include the most recent write
- Conflicting writes — two concurrent writes to the same key produce different values on different nodes, and the database must reconcile them
- Eventually consistent behavior — a user writes data, reads it back immediately, and sees the old value

Most AP databases provide configurable consistency levels so you can opt into stronger consistency for critical operations. DynamoDB offers `ConsistentRead` as a parameter. Cassandra offers `QUORUM`, `LOCAL_QUORUM`, and `ALL` consistency levels. The common pattern is: default to eventual consistency for most operations, use strong consistency for the specific operations that need it.

### The Single-Node Escape Hatch (CA)

Single-node databases (SQLite, PostgreSQL on one server, MySQL on one server) achieve both consistency and availability because there is no partition to tolerate. A single-node database has no network to split. It is not distributed.

The single-node approach is dramatically simpler than any distributed database. There is no consensus protocol, no replication lag, no conflict resolution, no quorum configuration. You read, you write, and what you read is what you wrote.

**The single-node catch:** Availability depends on one server. If the server crashes, the database is unavailable until it restarts or you fail over to a replica. Read capacity is capped at what one server can handle. Write capacity is capped at what one server can handle. Storage is capped at what one server can attach.

The practical guidance: start with a single-node database (PostgreSQL on a single RDS instance) and only move to a distributed database when you have measured a real bottleneck. The majority of applications never outgrow a well-provisioned single-node database with read replicas.

### The CAP Positioning Timeline

A revealing pattern in production database choices: most teams migrate rightward on the CAP triangle over time.

- **Stage 1 (prototype, 0-100 users):** Single-node PostgreSQL (CA). No partitions, full consistency, trivial operations.
- **Stage 2 (growth, 100-10K users):** PostgreSQL with read replicas (still effectively CA for writes, AP-style reads from replicas with replication lag). The database is still not distributed for writes.
- **Stage 3 (scale, 10K+ users):** Sharded PostgreSQL or CockroachDB (CP), or DynamoDB/Cassandra (AP). The choice depends on whether consistency or availability matters more for the specific workload.

The mistake is jumping from Stage 1 directly to a distributed database from Stage 3 without ever measuring whether Stage 2 would suffice. Most teams that follow this pattern would have been better served by optimizing their Stage 2 database (better indexes, connection pooling, query optimization, read replicas) before adopting distributed complexity.

## Failure Modes — Bad Selection Decisions

Choosing the wrong database in a selection decision creates failure modes that emerge months later, when the system is in production and the cost of changing is highest. These failure modes are not software bugs — they are structural mismatches between the database's design and the workload's requirements.

### Failure Mode 1: The Wrong Shard Key

A DynamoDB table sharded on a monotonically increasing key (timestamp, auto-increment ID) concentrates all writes on a single partition, defeating the purpose of distributed storage. The hot partition sustains 100 percent of write throughput while other partitions sit idle. This failure mode emerges because the shard key decision is made at table creation time — before you know your write patterns — and cannot be changed without creating a new table and migrating data.

**Detection:** Monitor per-partition metrics in DynamoDB's CloudWatch (ConsumedWriteCapacityPerPartition). If the distribution is heavily skewed (one partition consuming 80 percent+ of writes), you have a hot partition.

**Prevention:** Before choosing the shard key, model your expected write distribution. A good shard key has high cardinality (many unique values) and uniform access frequency (no single value dominates writes). User IDs are typically good shard keys because they are many and each user writes roughly the same amount. Timestamps are bad shard keys because all users write at the current time, and the current time hashes to a single partition.

### Failure Mode 2: Unbounded Query Scanning

A query without a restrictive WHERE clause or with a filter on an unindexed column triggers a full table or index scan. On a 500 GB table, a full scan reads millions of pages from disk, consumes CPU for minutes, and blocks other queries through buffer pool contention and lock escalation.

This failure mode follows from choosing a database without understanding your application's query patterns. If you chose MongoDB because "schemas are flexible" but your application needs to query across relationships that would be trivial joins in PostgreSQL, you end up implementing joins in application code — each of which scans many documents.

**The root cause:** The database choice was made based on the data model (documents feel natural for JSON APIs) without analyzing the query patterns. The query patterns, not the data shape, determine which database is optimal.

**Prevention:** Before choosing a database, write down the top 10 queries your application will execute and verify that the database can execute each one efficiently — meaning it can use an index and return results without scanning more than 100 rows or documents.

### Failure Mode 3: Replication Lag Cascade

Read replicas are configured with asynchronous replication. Under normal load, replication lag is 10-50 milliseconds. Under a traffic spike, lag grows to seconds or minutes. When lag reaches the replica's replication timeout, the replica disconnects and must replay from the last checkpoint — potentially taking minutes to hours.

This failure mode follows from choosing a database with read replicas as a scaling strategy without understanding the replication mechanism. The application reads from replicas for lower latency but does not distinguish between stale-tolerant and fresh-required reads. During the lag window, users see outdated data — a user whose profile update should appear immediately sees the old profile for minutes.

**Mitigations:**
- Use session-level read-your-writes consistency (route a user's reads to the primary for N seconds after their last write)
- Monitor replication lag and alert when it exceeds 1 second (for synchronous replication targets) or 5 seconds (for asynchronous)
- Consider synchronous replication for critical data (at the cost of write latency and availability)

### Failure Mode 4: Compaction Storm in LSM-Tree Databases

LSM-tree databases (Cassandra, RocksDB, MongoDB with WiredTiger, ScyllaDB) compact data in the background by merging SSTable files, discarding deleted records, and reorganizing data for efficient reads. During compaction, disk I/O spikes to 500 MB/s or more, starving read and write operations.

This failure mode follows from choosing an LSM-tree database for write-optimized workloads without provisioning enough I/O capacity for compaction overhead. The database is chosen for high write throughput, but the operational team configures it with default compaction settings that were not designed for the actual write volume.

**The compaction storm sequence:**
1. A burst of writes creates SSTable files across many node-local shards
2. Each shard independently decides it needs compaction
3. All nodes in the cluster enter compaction simultaneously
4. Disk I/O is saturated by compaction reads and writes
5. Application read and write latency spikes 10x-100x
6. The cluster becomes effectively unavailable for the compaction duration

**Mitigations:** Tune `compaction_throughput_mb_per_sec` to cap compaction I/O. Schedule compactions during low-traffic windows. Provision I/O capacity at peak write throughput plus compaction overhead (typically 1.5x-2x the peak write throughput).

### Failure Mode 5: Connection Exhaustion

Each database connection consumes server resources — memory for session state, a file descriptor, and a thread or process. When the connection limit is reached, new connections are rejected. Application servers that do not handle connection failures gracefully cascade: retries pile up, each retry opens a new connection until the pool is saturated, and the application becomes unavailable.

This failure mode follows from choosing a database with connection limits that are too low for the application's concurrency pattern, or from deploying the application with connection pool configuration that does not match the database's capacity.

**Prevention:** Configure connection pools with `max_connections` set to 10-20 per application server, not hundreds. Monitor connection counts and alert at 80 percent of the database's `max_connections` setting. Use a connection pooler (PgBouncer for PostgreSQL, ProxySQL for MySQL) that multiplexes many application connections into a smaller number of database connections.

### Failure Mode 6: Unbounded Data Growth

A table with no data retention policy grows without bound. As the table grows past the buffer pool size, the cache hit ratio drops from 99 percent to 50 percent. Read latency increases 100x as every read requires a disk seek. Index depth increases, adding another B-tree level and doubling index size. Eventually disk space runs out and the database crashes.

This failure mode follows from choosing a database without planning for the data lifecycle. The database choice was made for query performance at day one data volume, but data growth over months makes the initial assumptions invalid.

**Prevention:** Before the table is created, define a data retention policy. For time-series data, use table partitioning (PostgreSQL declarative partitioning or TimescaleDB hypertables) with automatic partition dropping. For operational data, archive old records to cheaper storage (S3, GCS) and delete them from the primary database. The retention policy belongs in the schema design document, not the post-mortem.

### Failure Mode 7: Schema Drift in Schema-Less Databases

In a document database with no schema enforcement, different application versions write documents with different field sets to the same collection. Over months, the collection accumulates documents with 10 different field combinations for the same entity type. Application code must handle every variant with defensive checks. Queries that assume a field exists fail silently or throw errors.

This failure mode follows from choosing a schema-less database without implementing the schema enforcement that the application actually needs. The "flexible schema" feature that made the database attractive for fast iteration becomes a liability once the application stabilizes and multiple teams write to the same collection.

**Prevention:** Enforce a schema at the application layer or use schema validation. MongoDB supports JSON Schema validation (added in version 3.6). Application CI/CD pipelines should validate that all document writes conform to the expected schema. Without enforcement, a "schema-less database" becomes a "schema-in-your-head" database — and nobody remembers the schema after the original author leaves the team.

### Failure Mode 8: Cost Explosion from Query Pattern Mismatch

A DynamoDB team designs a table with a GSI that supports the "find orders by customer" query with a single lookup. The table works perfectly for 10 customers. When the application grows to 100,000 customers, the monthly DynamoDB bill grows to $50,000 because each order lookup requires reading many items via a GSI that was designed for the small-scale access pattern.

This failure mode follows from choosing DynamoDB based on the "zero ops, pay per use" value proposition without modeling the actual cost per query at scale. DynamoDB's pricing model (read capacity units, write capacity units, storage, GSI storage and throughput) means that a poorly designed access pattern can cost 100x more than a well-designed one.

**Prevention:** Before choosing a database with metered pricing (DynamoDB, CosmosDB, Bigtable), model the cost at expected scale. Calculate the RCU and WCU per operation. Multiply by expected requests per second. Compare to the cost of a fixed-size PostgreSQL or MySQL instance that can handle the same workload at a predictable monthly price.

### Failure Mode 9: Split-Brain in Distributed Consensus

In a distributed database using Raft or Paxos for consensus, a split-brain occurs when two groups of nodes each believe they are the majority. This can happen if the network recovers in a way that isolates the old leader but the isolated nodes elect a new leader while the old leader still thinks it is in charge.

Modern consensus protocols prevent split-brain by requiring a leader to maintain a heartbeat with a majority. If the leader cannot reach the majority, it abdicates. Before accepting writes, a node verifies that the known leader is still the actual leader (term number check in Raft). Split-brain prevention is a key guarantee that makes distributed ACID databases like CockroachDB and Spanner reliable.

**When split-brain risk is highest:** Choose an AP database that uses a weak consensus mechanism (or no consensus at all for conflict resolution). Cassandra's gossip protocol does not guarantee a single view of cluster membership — during a partial partition, both sides may continue accepting writes independently, creating divergent data that must be repaired later. This is by design (AP trade-off), but teams that expect the behavior of a strongly consistent database are surprised by the repair burden.

### Failure Mode 10: Backup Oversight

A daily backup runs successfully for months. The production database crashes. When restoring from the backup, the team discovers the backup was writing to a full disk for three days and silently failing, or the backup format changed during the last version upgrade and the restore tool cannot read it, or the backup captures only data files without WAL, making point-in-time recovery impossible.

This failure mode follows from choosing a database with a backup mechanism you do not understand and have never tested end-to-end. The backup configuration is set up during the initial deployment and never verified until it is needed — which is, by definition, when you are already in crisis.

**Prevention:** Test the restore procedure quarterly. Automate the restore to a staging environment and run validation queries. Document the expected RTO and measure it. If the restore takes longer than the RTO, either the backup strategy or the restore procedure needs to change.

## Anti-Patterns — Shiny Object Syndrome

Beyond specific failure modes, patterns of mistake recur across teams and projects. These anti-patterns are driven less by technical analysis and more by organizational dynamics — the lure of the new thing, the fear of being behind, the desire for a resume-building technology.

### Anti-Pattern 1: The Shiny Object Database

A team chooses the newest database because it is designed by a prestigious company, or because a conference talk showed impressive benchmarks, or because the CTO read about it on Hacker News. The database solves a problem the team does not have, comes with operational complexity the team has not budgeted for, and forces the team to learn a new query language and operational model.

**The fix:** Database choices should be reactive, not proactive. Choose a new database only when your current database causes measurable pain that the new database specifically solves. "New and cool" is not a selection criterion.

### Anti-Pattern 2: NoSQL for Everything

A team chooses MongoDB or DynamoDB as the single database because "schemas are slow" or "we need to be agile." Document databases excel at semi-structured data and high-throughput key-value access. They are poor at joins, aggregations, and enforcing referential integrity. As the application grows and data relationships become more complex, the development velocity advantage of NoSQL is replaced by the productivity cost of implementing relational features in application code.

**The fix:** Start with PostgreSQL. It is relational, transactional, has excellent JSON support, and handles 90 percent of workloads without any scaling. Add a NoSQL database only when you have a measured need for a specific NoSQL feature (flexible schema, high write throughput, geo-distribution with local reads).

### Anti-Pattern 3: One Database to Rule Them All

A team uses the same database for OLTP transactions, OLAP analytics, full-text search, session storage, logging, and caching. Each workload has different optimal data structures, access patterns, and consistency requirements. Squeezing them all into one database forces painful compromises for every workload.

**The fix:** Use the right database for each workload. Start with PostgreSQL for OLTP. Add a dedicated analytics warehouse (BigQuery, ClickHouse) when OLAP queries become slow. Add a search index (Elasticsearch, Meilisearch) when full-text queries need to be fast. Use Redis for caching and session storage. Each addition should be justified by measured need — not added preemptively.

### Anti-Pattern 4: Database as Message Queue

A team uses a database table as a work queue. Multiple workers poll the same table for new rows, update status fields, and compete for rows. This pattern creates contention: worker updates fight for row locks, polling queries create repetitive index scans, and the queue becomes a bottleneck long before the actual workload would stress the database.

**The fix:** Use a dedicated message queue (RabbitMQ, Kafka, SQS, Redis Streams). A queue is optimized for the producer-consumer pattern. A database table is not. The queue provides exactly-once delivery semantics, consumer groups, and automatic retry — features you would have to build yourself on top of a database table.

### Anti-Pattern 5: Over-Sharding Prematurely

A team shards a database before there is evidence of a write bottleneck. Sharding is the most operationally complex database pattern — it requires a shard key, cross-shard queries are slow, and adding or removing shards requires data redistribution. A single PostgreSQL instance with read replicas handles tens of thousands of reads per second.

**The fix:** Only add sharding when you have measured a real write bottleneck. A single PostgreSQL instance on moderate hardware (8 vCPU, 32 GB RAM) handles 5000-10000 writes per second. Most applications never reach this threshold. Add read replicas first. Optimize query performance with indexes and connection pooling. Shard only when the primary write node is the bottleneck.

### Anti-Pattern 6: Ignoring the Migration Path

A team chooses a database with no realistic migration path. Proprietary databases (MongoDB's document model, DynamoDB's access patterns) are hard to migrate away from because they use fundamentally different data models. Open-source relational databases (PostgreSQL, MySQL) have rich migration tooling and export data in standard formats.

**The fix:** Before choosing any database, build and test the export pipeline. If you cannot get your data out in a standard format within a week of starting the migration effort, choose a different database. The rule protects against vendor lock-in — not by avoiding proprietary features, but by knowing the exit cost before you commit.

### Anti-Pattern 7: Read-Modify-Write Without Atomicity

A team uses a read-modify-write pattern (read a value, compute a new value, write the new value) without handling concurrent modifications. Two application servers simultaneously read a counter as 10, compute 11, and write 11. The counter should be 12 but ends up as 11.

**The fix depends on the database:**
- PostgreSQL: use `UPDATE ... RETURNING` with atomic operations or `SELECT ... FOR UPDATE`
- DynamoDB: use conditional updates with condition expressions
- Redis: use `INCR` which is atomic — no read-modify-write needed
- Cassandra: use lightweight transactions (LWT) for linearizable compare-and-set

The principle: do not read and write as two separate operations unless you know the read is isolated. Every database offers a mechanism for atomic operations. Use it.

### Anti-Pattern 8: Treating Indexes as Free

Each index accelerates reads by up to 1000x but imposes costs on writes. An INSERT that touches a table with 5 indexes writes to 6 B-trees (one for the table, five for indexes) instead of one. An UPDATE that modifies an indexed column writes to the table B-tree and 1-2 index B-trees per changed key.

The write amplification from indexes can be the difference between a database that handles 10,000 writes per second and one that handles 2,000 writes per second.

**The fix:** Before adding an index, measure whether the query it accelerates is actually slow. Verify the index is used by checking `EXPLAIN` output after creation. Drop unused indexes — they waste space and slow writes for no benefit. Set up monitoring that reports index usage statistics: `pg_stat_user_indexes` in PostgreSQL, `indexStats()` in MongoDB.

### Anti-Pattern 9: Long-Held Transactions

A database transaction that spans seconds, minutes, or hours holds locks and prevents other transactions from modifying locked rows. In PostgreSQL, an open transaction blocks autovacuum from cleaning up dead tuples, causing table bloat. In MySQL InnoDB, a long transaction holds undo log entries that other long-running queries may need for consistent reads.

**The fix:** Transactions should cover the minimum work necessary: read values, compute, write results. Never hold a transaction open while waiting for user input, calling an external API, or sending an email. The "transaction timeout" at most databases defaults to 30-60 seconds for good reason.

### Anti-Pattern 10: Hyperspecialization

A team uses a specialized database (time-series DB, graph DB, geospatial DB) for a workload that a general-purpose database handles well. The team justifies the choice by citing the specialized database's benchmark advantages for the specific workload type, without accounting for the operational cost of running an additional database, the cognitive load on the team, or the integration cost with the existing data pipeline.

**The fix:** Use a specialized database only when a general-purpose database has been measured to fail at the workload. The default should be PostgreSQL — it handles time-series (with TimescaleDB extension), geospatial (with PostGIS), and full-text search (with built-in FTS and extensions like pg_trgm) without adding another database to the stack.

## When to Defer the Decision

Deferring a database decision is often the best decision. The cost of choosing wrong is higher than the cost of choosing later. The following situations call for deferral:

### Defer When You Have One Person and a Prototype

Use whatever you already know. The database choice matters far less than building something real and getting feedback. PostgreSQL is a safe default — it works for 90 percent of prototypes, scales to production, and has the richest ecosystem of tools, libraries, and hosted options. If you already know PostgreSQL, use it. If you already know SQLite, use it. If you already know MongoDB, use it.

The prototype's database choice is not the production database choice. Assume you will migrate — not because the prototype's database is wrong, but because the prototype's workload is not the production workload, and your requirements will change as you learn what users actually need.

### Defer When the Current Database Is Not Causing Pain

If your existing database works — queries complete in acceptable time, writes are not backed up, operational costs are within budget, developers are productive — do not migrate for theoretical benefits. Every migration carries risk: data loss during the cutover, application downtime, performance regressions from the new database's different query optimizer, and months of team effort spent on infrastructure instead of product features.

Set a pain threshold before considering a migration. "When P99 query latency exceeds 500ms for 3 consecutive days" or "When monthly database cost exceeds $10,000" are measurable thresholds. Migrate only when the threshold is crossed.

### Defer When You Do Not Know Your Access Patterns Yet

The most expensive database mistake is choosing a specialized database (sharded DynamoDB, graph Neo4j, time-series InfluxDB) before you know what queries your application will actually execute. The access pattern determines the optimal data model and index strategy. If you do not know the access patterns, you cannot design the database.

**The deferral strategy:** Use PostgreSQL (or your default) for the first six months of production. Instrument every query with EXPLAIN ANALYZE and slow query logging. After six months, you will know:
- Which queries run most frequently
- Which queries are slow and why
- Which access patterns would benefit from a specialized database
- Whether the workload can stay on PostgreSQL indefinitely

At that point, the database choice is data-driven instead of speculative.

### Defer When the Budget Is Near Zero

Use free tiers and open-source databases. PostgreSQL, SQLite, and Redis are free. Amazon RDS has a free tier for 12 months. Google Firestore has a generous free tier. Supabase (PostgreSQL as a service) has a free tier. Fly.io offers free PostgreSQL with reasonable limits.

Paying for a database before you have revenue is premature optimization. The database should not be the most expensive part of your stack at the prototype or early growth stage. Most applications never outgrow the free tier of services like Supabase or Planetscale.

### Defer When Scale Is Under 100 Concurrent Users

Any database works at this scale. SQLite files are the simplest option for small web applications — no server to manage, no connection pool to configure, no monitoring dashboard to set up. You can upgrade to PostgreSQL when SQLite's write concurrency limit becomes a problem, typically around 100 concurrent writers.

The counterintuitive truth: for a small-scale application, the database with the most features (PostgreSQL) is usually the best choice because it gives you the most room to grow without migrating. The database with the simplest operations (SQLite) is the best choice for a minimal-overhead deployment. The database with the most hype (new NoSQL database of the month) is the worst choice because it adds learning curve and operational risk without solving any problem you actually have.

### The Golden Deferral Rule

If you cannot articulate, in one paragraph, why your workload needs a different database than PostgreSQL (for relational workloads) or SQLite (for embedded/single-server workloads), you should defer the decision.

PostgreSQL handles:
- Relational data with complex joins and subqueries
- JSON document storage with indexes on JSON fields
- Full-text search with ranking and stemming
- Geospatial queries (with PostGIS)
- Time-series data (with TimescaleDB extension)
- Key-value access with UNIQUE constraints and covering indexes
- Queue-like workloads (with SKIP LOCKED)
- Analytics queries with window functions and CTEs

If your workload fits any of these patterns — and most workloads do — start with PostgreSQL. Defer the specialized database decision until you have measured that PostgreSQL cannot handle the workload. That day may never come.

### The Unspoken Rule: Your Database Choice Is Reversible

Most database choices are far more reversible than engineers assume. If you choose PostgreSQL for a prototype and need to migrate to a distributed database in production, the migration is a well-understood process with standard tooling (pg_dump, foreign data wrappers, logical replication to change data capture systems). The total cost is engineering time — not architectural impossibility.

The databases that are hard to migrate away from are those that use fundamentally different data models (graph databases, document databases with deeply nested schemas, event stores with custom query languages) or proprietary features with no equivalent in other systems. These databases provide genuine value for specific workloads, but they also represent a migration path cost that should be factored into the decision.

The practical heuristic: if you can pg_dump your data and load it into another database within a week, your choice is reversible. If you cannot, the migration cost should be part of the evaluation criteria — not an afterthought discovered during the migration.
