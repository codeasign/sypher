## Tier 1: Foundation

These challenges test your grasp of Raft's basic concepts and terminology. You should be able to answer each in 3-5 minutes.

### Challenge 1.1: The Split Vote

A 5-node Raft cluster's leader fails. Three followers experience election timeouts at nearly the same instant (within 5ms of each other). Each starts an election for term 7. Each candidate receives 1 vote (its own) plus 1 vote from another node. No candidate reaches a majority of 3.

**Question:** What happens next? How does Raft resolve this situation? How long is the cluster unavailable for writes?

<details>
<summary>Model Answer</summary>

Each candidate detects that its election has not completed within its election timeout (150-300ms randomized). All three candidates time out, increment their term to 8, and start new elections. This time, the randomized timeouts create enough spread that one candidate's timer expires first. That candidate sends RequestVote and collects votes from the other two nodes before they become candidates.

The cluster is unavailable for writes during the election storm. In the worst case: initial timeout (200ms average) + first election (50ms) + new timeout (200ms average) + second election (50ms) = approximately 500ms of unavailability.

Mitigation with pre-vote: the candidates could check their electability before incrementing the term, reducing the disruption.

</details>

### Challenge 1.2: The Uncommitted Write

A client sends PUT x=10 to a Raft cluster. The leader appends the entry (index 15, term 4) to its log and replicates it to 1 out of 3 followers. Before the second follower acknowledges, the leader crashes.

**Question:** Is the entry committed? What happens when a new leader is elected? Does the client need to retry?

<details>
<summary>Model Answer</summary>

The entry is not committed because it was replicated to only 2 nodes (leader + 1 follower), which is not a majority of 3 (quorum = 2, but the leader needs 2 followers to acknowledge = 3 total).

When a new leader is elected, one of two scenarios occurs:
- **Scenario A:** The new leader is the follower that received the entry. The entry is in its log at index 15. However, the entry is from term 4, and the new leader's term is 5. The new leader cannot automatically commit entries from previous terms. It needs to commit an entry from its own term first. If a client sends a new write, entry 16 of term 5 will be committed, and entry 15 of term 4 becomes implicitly committed.
- **Scenario B:** The new leader is the follower that did NOT receive the entry. The new leader does not have entry 15. Through log matching, the follower that had entry 15 will detect a mismatch when the new leader sends AppendEntries. The leader will overwrite entry 15 on that follower, discarding it.

In both cases, the client has not received an acknowledgment and must retry.

</details>

### Challenge 1.3: The Stale Follower

A follower node was offline for 30 minutes due to a network issue. During this time, the cluster processed 500,000 writes and took 2 snapshots (one at entry 200,000 and another at entry 450,000). The follower reconnects to the cluster.

**Question:** Describe how the leader brings this follower up to date. What RPCs are used? What data is transferred?

<details>
<summary>Model Answer</summary>

When the follower reconnects, the leader checks nextIndex for this follower. Since the follower has missed 500,000 entries and the log has been compacted via snapshots past the follower's last index, the leader cannot use AppendEntries (the required log entries no longer exist).

The leader uses the InstallSnapshot RPC. It sends the most recent snapshot (taken at entry 450,000) to the follower. The RPC includes:
1. The leader's current term
2. The last included index (450,000) and term from the snapshot
3. The snapshot data (serialized state machine state)
4. The chunk index (for large snapshots sent in pieces)

The follower receives the snapshot, replaces its entire state machine with the snapshot's state, and truncates its log up to the last included index. It then sets its commit index and last applied to 450,000.

After the snapshot is installed, the leader resumes normal AppendEntries replication for any entries after index 450,000 (approximately 50,000 entries). The follower catches up within seconds to minutes depending on network speed and entry size.

</details>

## Tier 2: Design

These challenges require applying Raft concepts to design scenarios. Allow 10-15 minutes each.

### Challenge 2.1: Minimum Viable Raft Cluster

**Question:** You need to deploy a Raft-based configuration store for a small team's microservices. The cluster must tolerate the failure of 1 node. All services are in the same AWS region. Describe the cluster topology, configuration parameters, and operational monitoring.

<details>
<summary>Model Answer</summary>

**Topology:** 3-node Raft cluster deployed across 3 AWS availability zones (us-east-1a, us-east-1b, us-east-1c). One node per AZ. This tolerates one node failure, one AZ failure, and one partial network partition.

**Configuration:**
- Election timeout: 300-500ms (conservative for single-region, avoids spurious elections from AZ latency)
- Heartbeat interval: 50ms
- Snapshot threshold: 10,000 entries or 64 MB
- Batch size: 64 entries per AppendEntries RPC
- Pre-vote: enabled
- Read optimization: read-index with follower reads

**Monitoring:**
- Alert on: term changes (any), election rate > 0.01/sec, commit latency p99 > 50ms, log lag > 1,000 entries for any follower
- Dashboard: current leader, term number, commit index per node, log size, snapshot duration

**Operational procedures:**
- Automated node replacement if a node is down for more than 5 minutes
- Manual defragmentation every 7 days (for etcd-style backend)
- Capacity planning at 60% disk usage

</details>

### Challenge 2.2: Multi-Region Coordination

**Question:** Your company is expanding to Europe. The existing Raft cluster is in us-east-1 (3 nodes). You need to extend the cluster to support a new data center in eu-west-1 while maintaining strong consistency for configuration data. Writes come from both regions but are infrequent (under 100/sec). Reads are 10,000/sec per region. Latency between regions is 80ms round trip.

**Question:** How do you modify the deployment? What performance characteristics do you expect?

<details>
<summary>Model Answer</summary>

**Approach 1:** Expand to 5 nodes: 3 in us-east-1, 2 in eu-west-1. Quorum is 3 of 5. This tolerates 1 node failure in any region or a full region failure of eu-west-1. However, if us-east-1 fails completely, only 2 nodes survive, which is not a majority — the cluster is unavailable until us-east-1 recovers.

Write latency: approximately 80ms (one cross-region RTT to replicate to eu-west-1 nodes and achieve quorum of 3).
Read latency: us-east-1 reads are 1-2ms (local leader). eu-west-1 reads are either forwarded to the leader (80ms) or served locally with stale data (0.2ms).

**Approach 2:** Keep 3 nodes in us-east-1, add witness nodes in eu-west-1. Witnesses receive the log asynchronously and can serve stale reads locally. Writes are always committed within us-east-1 (fast, 2-5ms), but eu-west-1 reads may be 80ms stale for strong reads (forwarded) or 2ms for eventual reads (local).

**Recommendation:** Use Approach 1. At 100 writes/sec, the 80ms write latency is acceptable for configuration data. Add read-only followers in eu-west-1 to serve local reads with read-index optimization (5-10ms each, including the round trip to get the commit index).

</details>

### Challenge 2.3: High-Throughput Metadata Store

**Question:** You are building a metadata store for a large-scale file system. The metadata includes file names, permissions, and locations. The workload is 50,000 writes/sec and 200,000 reads/sec. All operations must be linearizable.

**Question:** Can a single Raft group handle this workload? If not, how do you scale?

<details>
<summary>Model Answer</summary>

A single Raft group cannot handle 50,000 writes/sec. Even with batching (64 entries per RPC), the leader performs approximately 800 rounds/sec (50,000 / 64), each requiring at least one fsync. Practical fsync limits are 10,000-20,000 per second on modern SSDs. Additionally, AppendEntries RPCs to followers create network overhead.

**Solution: Multi-Raft with key space partitioning.**

Partition the metadata key space into 16 ranges (e.g., by file name hash prefix). Each range is an independent Raft group with its own leader. Write throughput scales linearly with the number of ranges: 16 ranges x 3,500 writes/sec per range = 56,000 writes/sec.

Design:
- 8 storage nodes, each hosting 6 Raft groups (16 groups x 3 replicas / 8 nodes = 6 groups per node)
- Each range is a Raft group with 3 replicas spread across different nodes
- Each node serves as leader for 2 of its 6 ranges (approximately)
- Client request routing: hash the file name to determine the range, then route to that range's leader
- Cross-range atomicity: not required for independent file metadata; if needed, use a distributed transaction coordinator

**Read optimization:** Read-index with follower reads distributes read load across all 8 nodes. Each node serves approximately 12,500 reads/sec from its local state machines.

</details>

## Tier 3: Expert

These challenges explore advanced Raft concepts and edge cases. Allow 20-30 minutes each.

### Challenge 3.1: Design a Fault-Tolerant Distributed Lock Service

**Question:** Design a distributed lock service using Raft. The service must support:
- Lock acquisition with timeout (auto-expiring locks)
- Lock release (only by the lock holder)
- Lock monitoring (list for lock events)
- Fault tolerance (survive node failures)
- Fairness (prevent starvation)

Describe the data model, the Raft integration, the lock acquisition protocol, and the timeout mechanism. How do you handle the case where the Raft leader holding lock state crashes?

<details>
<summary>Model Answer</summary>

**Data model:** Each lock is a key-value pair stored in the Raft state machine:
```
lock:key_name -> {holder: client_id, lease_expiry: timestamp, version: N}
lock:key_name:owner -> client_id (secondary index for release verification)
```

**Raft integration:** The Raft state machine is a deterministic key-value store. Every lock operation (acquire, release, renew) is a state machine command applied through Raft consensus. This ensures all nodes agree on lock ownership.

**Lock acquisition protocol:**
1. Client sends acquire(LOCK_NAME, TIMEOUT) to the Raft leader.
2. Leader creates a log entry: &#123;op: "acquire", key: LOCK_NAME, holder: CLIENT_ID, timeout: TIMEOUT_MS&#125;.
3. Entry is replicated to followers and committed via Raft.
4. State machine checks: is the lock free (no entry, or entry expired)?
   - If yes: set holder to CLIENT_ID, set lease_expiry to now + TIMEOUT_MS.
   - If no and held by a different client: return LOCK_HELD.
   - If no and held by same client: extend the lease.
5. Result is returned to the client through the Raft response path.

**Timeout mechanism (heartbeat-based lease):**
The client must periodically send a renew request to extend the lease. If the client crashes, the lease expires naturally after the timeout, and another client can acquire the lock.

The lease duration is set in the state machine command itself, so all replicas agree on the expiration time. The leader checks expiration when processing acquire requests (not proactively — expiration only matters when another client tries to acquire).

**Edge case — leader crash holding lock state:**
The lock state is in the Raft replicated log, not in the leader's memory. If the leader crashes, a new leader is elected. The new leader's state machine already contains all committed lock entries. Lock leases are recomputed from the state machine's clock. No lock state is lost. The lock lease timer does not reset on leader change — the original lease duration is still in effect.

**Monitoring:** Clients can subscribe to lock state changes through a watch mechanism. The Raft state machine notifies a local watcher when a lock entry changes, and the watcher streams the event to subscribed clients.

</details>

### Challenge 3.2: Raft with Dynamic Cluster Reconfiguration Under Degraded Conditions

**Question:** A 5-node Raft cluster has 2 nodes that are experiencing intermittent hardware failures (sporadic disk timeouts causing 5-second pauses every few minutes). These nodes are disrupting the cluster — they occasionally miss heartbeats and trigger spurious elections. Design a reconfiguration procedure to remove these 2 nodes and replace them with 2 new healthy nodes, without losing availability.

What are the risks? How does joint consensus help? What if the intermittent nodes fail during the reconfiguration?

<details>
<summary>Model Answer</summary>

**Procedure using joint consensus:**

Step 1 — Propose a joint consensus configuration:
C_old = [Node A (healthy), Node B (healthy), Node C (healthy), Node D (failing), Node E (failing)]
C_new = [Node A, Node B, Node C, Node F (new), Node G (new)]
C_old_new = [A, B, C, D, E, F, G] (all 7 nodes)

Step 2 — Commit joint consensus:
The leader creates a log entry with C_old_new. It must be committed by both majorities:
- Old majority: 3 of `{A, B, C, D, E}` = 3 nodes
- New majority: 4 of `{A, B, C, F, G}` = 4 nodes
Once both majorities acknowledge, the joint config is committed.

Step 3 — Switch to C_new:
The leader creates a log entry with only C_new = [A, B, C, F, G]. This entry must be committed by a majority of C_new (3 of 5). Once committed, C_old is discarded.

**Risks and mitigations:**

- **Risk 1: Intermittent nodes (D, E) cannot acknowledge joint config.**
  If D or E are unresponsive during the joint consensus commit, the leader still needs only 3 of `{A, B, C, D, E}` for the old majority. The healthy nodes A, B, C can form this majority on their own. D and E are not required.

- **Risk 2: Nodes D or E trigger spurious election during reconfiguration.**
  A spurious election during joint consensus is dangerous because the new leader must handle both configurations. Pre-vote mitigates this. Additionally, if D or E become candidates, they cannot win because healthy nodes will not vote for their stale logs.

- **Risk 3: The leader (one of A, B, C) fails during the process.**
  If the leader fails after joint consensus is committed but before C_new is committed, the new leader will be elected from the joint configuration. Any node that has the joint consensus entry can become leader. The new leader can continue with Step 3.

- **Risk 4: Two new nodes (F, G) are slow to synchronize.**
  F and G need the full log history before they can be useful. If they are slow, they simply do not count in the quorum until they catch up (they are not in the new majority if they have not yet applied entries). This is safe — they will gradually catch up through InstallSnapshot or AppendEntries.

**Safety analysis:** Joint consensus ensures that during the transition, only configurations that are safe from split-brain are used. Both old and new configurations must agree for any decision. If the cluster splits during reconfiguration, neither side can unilaterally make progress without the other's majority.

</details>
