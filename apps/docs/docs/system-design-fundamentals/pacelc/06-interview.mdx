# PACELC — Interview Playbook

PACELC appears in system design interviews at the FAANG level as a refinement question: the interviewer asks about CAP Theorem, the candidate mentions it, and the follow-up is "so do you always choose AP or CP?" The correct answer acknowledges that CAP only covers partitions and that PACELC addresses the other 97% of operating time. This playbook covers how to weave PACELC into your interview responses naturally, at every experience level.

## Phrase Bank

### Opening the PACELC Topic

- "CAP Theorem tells us what happens during a partition, but it says nothing about normal operation. That is where PACELC comes in."
- "In this system, 95% of operating time the network is healthy. I want a framework that covers that 95%, not just the 5% of failure cases."
- "PACELC reveals that even under perfect network conditions, there is a trade-off between latency and consistency. CAP only covers the partition case."

### Describing PA/EL

- "PA/EL is the right choice for user-facing operations where response time directly impacts revenue. The cost is eventual consistency."
- "DynamoDB defaults to PA/EL: writes return immediately after one replica acknowledges, and the rest catch up asynchronously."
- "In PA/EL mode, a read may return data that is 50 to 200 milliseconds stale. For shopping carts and session data, that is perfectly acceptable."

### Describing PC/EC

- "PC/EC is required when correctness is non-negotiable. Financial transactions, inventory counts, and distributed locks all need PC/EC."
- "Spanner implements PC/EC using TrueTime and two-phase commit. Every write is linearizable across any number of regions."
- "The cost of PC/EC is latency: writes take 10 to 50 milliseconds instead of 1 to 5. That is the price of global consistency."

### Handling Follow-Up Questions

- *"What about multi-region?"* "Between regions, I would use PA/EL with async replication for most data, and only use synchronous replication for critical data like payment metadata."
- *"How do you handle partitions?"* "In PA/EL, each side continues serving and we reconcile with LWW when the partition heals. In PC/EC, the minority side stops accepting writes."
- *"What about hybrid?"* "Cassandra's tunable consistency lets me use PA/EL for 90% of operations and PC/EC for the critical 10%. The same cluster handles both."

## Red Flags and How to Avoid Them

**Red Flag 1: Mentioning CAP but not PACELC.** An interviewer who asks about distributed consistency and hears only CAP will follow up with "and what happens when there is no partition?" The candidate who has never heard of PACELC will not have a good answer.

Avoid by: Always mention PACELC immediately after explaining CAP. Say "CAP covers partitions, but PACELC adds the normal-operation trade-off."

**Red Flag 2: Saying PA/EL is always wrong.** Some candidates, especially those from relational database backgrounds, argue that consistency must always be strong. In an interview for a system serving 10M users, this position is indefensible.

Avoid by: Acknowledge that strong consistency has a cost. Say "PA/EL is appropriate for some data and PC/EC for other data. The key is choosing per workload."

**Red Flag 3: Ignoring the Else regime.** A candidate who designs a distributed system and only discusses what happens during partitions is missing the bigger picture.

Avoid by: Explicitly state both regimes. Say "During normal operation, I would use CL=ONE for fast reads. During a partition, I would choose availability over consistency because the system must keep serving users."

**Red Flag 4: Confusing PACELC with CAP.** Saying "PACELC replaces CAP" or "PACELC proves CAP is wrong" shows a misunderstanding. PACELC extends CAP.

Avoid by: Say "PACELC refines CAP by adding the Else regime. CAP remains correct for partitions."

**Red Flag 5: No numbers.** Saying "PA/EL is faster than PC/EC" without concrete values shows shallow understanding.

Avoid by: Use concrete numbers. "PA/EL writes take 1-5ms. PC/EC writes take 10-50ms. The difference is coordination overhead."

## Practice Drills

### Drill 1: PACELC Classification

For each system, state its PACELC quadrant and explain why:

1. **Redis with AOF persistence, single leader** — Not a PACELC system (single-node).
2. **DynamoDB with default settings** — PA/EL. AP during partition, EL during normal operation.
3. **DynamoDB with ConsistentRead=True** — PA/EC. AP during partition, EC for that specific read.
4. **Spanner** — PC/EC. CP during partition, EC during normal operation.
5. **Cassandra CL=ONE** — PA/EL. One replica, fast, possibly stale.
6. **Cassandra CL=QUORUM** — PC/EC. Quorum ensures strong consistency on healthy reads.
7. **Cosmos DB Eventual** — PA/EL.
8. **Cosmos DB Strong** — PC/EC.
9. **Zookeeper** — PC/EC. Majority quorum required, minority unavailable during partition.
10. **etcd** — PC/EC. Raft-based, requires majority for all operations.

### Drill 2: Design Scenario

**Scenario:** Design a global e-commerce platform with product catalog, shopping cart, checkout, and inventory systems.

**Step 1:** Identify per-system PACELC requirements.
- Product catalog: PA/EL (stale product descriptions and prices are acceptable for minutes).
- Shopping cart: PA/EL with session consistency (user must see their own adds).
- Checkout: PC/EC (payment must be strongly consistent, deduplicated).
- Inventory: PC/EC (overselling must be prevented; strong consistency is required).

**Step 2:** Choose data stores.
- Catalog: DynamoDB PA/EL, multi-region async replication.
- Cart: DynamoDB PA/EL, session affinity to region.
- Checkout: DynamoDB with ConsistentRead=True + external payment idempotency.
- Inventory: Spanner or CockroachDB for global strong consistency.

**Step 3:** Define partitioning strategy.
- Catalog partitioned by product ID, no cross-partition transactions needed.
- Cart partitioned by user ID, single-writer per user.
- Checkout needs cross-partition transactions (cart + payment + inventory). Use Spanner for this.

**Step 4:** Define failure handling.
- Partition in catalog region: serve from nearest healthy region (PA/EL).
- Partition in inventory: stop accepting new checkouts (PC/EC).
- Node failure in cart: transparent failover to another node (DynamoDB handles automatically).

### Drill 3: Failure Mode Identification

Given a system with the following characteristics, identify the PACELC failure mode:

1. Writes succeed but reads return data from 2 minutes ago: Replication lag cascade. Mitigate with CL=LOCAL_QUORUM for reads or reduce write throughput.
2. The checkout service returns 503 errors for 30 minutes during a network issue in one AZ: Quorum loss under partition. Minority side of a PC/EC system.
3. After a node comes back from a 4-hour outage, cluster write latency doubles: Read repair storm. The recovered node triggers repair on every read.
4. A user adds an item to cart in Tokyo, flies to London, and the cart shows the old version: Cross-region consistency window. Session affinity would prevent this.

## Interview Scripts

### Opening Response (All Levels)

Interviewer: "How do you handle consistency in a distributed system?"

Candidate: "I use PACELC to think about this. CAP Theorem tells us that during a network partition, we choose between availability and consistency. But partitions affect only about 3% of operating time. PACELC extends CAP to cover the other 97%: when the network is healthy, we choose between low latency and consistency. So for each subsystem, I define both behaviors — what happens during a partition and what happens in normal operation."

### Deep Dive (Mid-Level+)

Interviewer: "How would you design the data layer for a social media feed?"

Candidate: "The feed is a high-read, high-write system where stale data for a few seconds is acceptable. I would use PA/EL: DynamoDB with CL=ONE for reads and writes. During normal operation, each read returns the nearest replica's data in 1-3ms. During a partition, each side of the split continues serving. The feed might show inconsistent like counts or slightly old posts, but users get a response. For the user authentication system within the same platform, I would use PC/EC with CL=QUORUM, because authentication must be strongly consistent. The same infrastructure can serve both if I use tunable consistency."

### Staff-Level Nuance

Interviewer: "What are the limitations of PACELC?"

Candidate: "PACELC assumes the partition regime and the else regime are binary — either there is a partition or there is not. In reality, there are gray zones: high latency that is not quite a partition, intermittent packet loss, asymmetric network failures where one direction works but the other does not. PACELC also does not address operation ordering across regimes — what happens if a partition starts while a write is being processed under else-mode assumptions? Finally, PACELC does not specify how fast the system should switch between regimes. The detection delay matters: a slow detector keeps the system in else-mode during a partition, which causes invisible inconsistency. A too-fast detector causes false positives and unnecessary availability loss."
