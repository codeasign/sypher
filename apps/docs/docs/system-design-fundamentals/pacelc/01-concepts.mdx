# Understanding PACELC

Build the mental model that extends CAP Theorem into a practical framework for real-world distributed database design.

---

## The Problem Story

A team at a growing fintech startup built their transaction ledger on Cassandra. They had studied CAP Theorem carefully. Their reasoning was straightforward: partitions are unavoidable in distributed systems, so they must tolerate partitions (P). Between Consistency and Availability, they chose Availability (AP). Cassandra was famous for being AP. The decision felt correct.

The system launched. It handled 50,000 writes per second across three data centers. During a regional network outage, the system stayed up — exactly as CAP promised. Requests were served from the unaffected data centers. The team celebrated.

Then they noticed something puzzling. Even when the network was perfectly healthy — zero packet loss, sub-millisecond latency between nodes — read requests were returning stale data. A customer would deposit $500, check their balance 3 seconds later, and see $0. The write had completed on one node, but the read hit a different node that had not yet received the update. The replication lag was typically 50 to 200 milliseconds, but that gap was enough to create a terrible user experience.

The team ran the numbers. During normal operation, 97% of the time the network was healthy. Only 3% of the time was there any partition event. Yet their system's behavior was entirely governed by the partition case. They had optimized for a 3% scenario and accepted degraded behavior for the 97% scenario. That was the opposite of rational engineering.

They needed a framework that distinguished between partition-time behavior and normal-operation behavior. They needed PACELC.

---

## The Insight

PACELC is an acronym that extends CAP Theorem by asking a second question. CAP asks: during a partition, do you prefer Availability or Consistency? PACELC adds: and when there is no partition, do you prefer Low Latency or Consistency?

The full acronym breaks down as:

- **P**artition — if the network is split
- **A**vailability vs **C**onsistency — choose during the partition
- **E**lse — when the network is healthy (no partition)
- **L**atency vs **C**onsistency — choose during normal operation

This single addition transforms the framework. CAP gives you a binary choice that only applies during rare failure events. PACELC gives you a strategy for both failure and normal operation. The Else case is where your system spends 95% or more of its time. The trade-off you make there defines the day-to-day experience of every user.

### The Post Office Analogy

Think of a post office. Most days, the roads are clear, mail trucks run on schedule, and letters arrive at their destination within 24 hours. This is the Else case. The post office could deliver every letter immediately (low latency) by having each mail carrier deliver each letter as it arrives, but then letters to the same address might arrive out of order and the recipient would see inconsistent sequencing. Instead, the post office batches mail and sorts it — trading a few hours of latency for consistent delivery order.

Now imagine a snowstorm. Roads are blocked. Some neighborhoods are cut off entirely. This is the Partition case. The post office must decide: hold all mail for the snowed-in neighborhoods until roads clear (consistency — no partial delivery), or deliver what it can to accessible areas and queue the rest (availability — keep serving even if delivery is incomplete).

Normal operation (Else) and partition (P) require different strategies. PACELC says you must define both.

## Core Vocabulary

**PACELC** — An acronym that extends CAP Theorem: During a Partition, choose between Availability and Consistency; Else (no partition), choose between Latency and Consistency.

**Partition-tolerant** — The system continues to operate despite network failures that split the cluster into isolated subgroups. Every distributed system must tolerate partitions; the choice is what happens to requests during the split.

**Available (during partition)** — Every request receives a non-error response, even if some nodes cannot coordinate. The response may reflect only the data visible to the node handling the request.

**Consistent (during partition)** — The system may reject requests or become unavailable for some operations to ensure all nodes agree on the data before responding.

**Else (no partition)** — The dominant operational state of the system, typically 95% to 99.9% of operating hours. Network connectivity is intact between all nodes.

**Latency (during normal operation)** — The system favors fast responses over strict data agreement. A read may return slightly stale data if the alternative would require waiting for cross-node coordination.

**Consistency (during normal operation)** — The system waits for cross-node agreement before responding to a read or write, accepting higher latency in exchange for a guarantee that every read reflects the latest write.

**AP system in partition** — A system like DynamoDB or Cassandra that chooses Availability when the network splits. All reachable nodes accept reads and writes, but data across the partition may diverge temporarily.

**CP system in partition** — A system like Spanner or Zookeeper that chooses Consistency when the network splits. Some nodes become unavailable to prevent stale or conflicting data from being served.

**PA/EL** — The full PACELC designation for DynamoDB-style systems: Partition-tolerant, Available during partition, Else choose Low latency over Consistency. This is the most common configuration for web-scale systems.

**PC/EC** — The full PACELC designation for Spanner-style systems: Partition-tolerant, Consistent during partition, Else choose Consistency over Low latency. Used when correctness is paramount.

**Tunable consistency** — The ability to adjust the consistency-latency trade-off per operation, as supported by Cassandra and Cosmos DB. The same system can act PA/EL for some operations and PC/EC for others.

**Quorum** — The minimum number of replicas that must participate in a read or write for the operation to succeed. Write-all/read-one provides strong consistency. Write-one/read-all provides low latency.

**Hinted handoff** — A mechanism where a live node temporarily accepts writes destined for a downed replica and forwards them when the replica recovers. Reduces write unavailability without requiring full quorum.

**Read repair** — During a read, the coordinating node checks all replicas and updates any that hold stale data. Converges the system toward consistency without blocking writes.

## How It Actually Works

PACELC governs two distinct phases of every request. Understanding the transition between phases is the core of the framework.

### Step 1: Normal Operation — Request Arrives

A client sends a read or write request. The system's coordinator node (or load balancer) receives the request. The network is healthy. All nodes are reachable within normal latency bounds (typically under 5 milliseconds round-trip within a data center).

### Step 2: Normal Operation — Consistency Level Decision

The system applies its Else-regime policy. In a PA/EL system (DynamoDB default), the coordinator writes to one replica and immediately responds to the client. The write is propagated asynchronously to remaining replicas. In a PC/EC system (Spanner), the coordinator must obtain a lock, run two-phase commit across all participating replicas, and wait for a quorum acknowledgment before responding.

### Step 3: Normal Operation — Latency Impact

In PA/EL mode, p50 read latency is 1-3 milliseconds and p50 write latency is 1-5 milliseconds. In PC/EC mode, p50 read latency is 5-15 milliseconds and p50 write latency is 10-50 milliseconds. The difference comes from coordination overhead: locks, quorum waits, and distributed commit protocols.

### Step 4: Normal Operation — Read Behavior

For PA/EL reads, the coordinator returns data from the nearest replica without checking other replicas. The read may be stale by tens to hundreds of milliseconds depending on replication lag. For PC/EC reads, the coordinator must read from a quorum of replicas and return the latest timestamped version, providing strong consistency.

### Step 5: Partition Detection

The system continuously monitors node health using gossip protocols or heartbeat mechanisms. When acknowledgments from a node stop arriving within a configurable timeout (typically 3 to 10 seconds), the system suspects a partition. Once confirmed, the system switches from Else-regime behavior to Partition-regime behavior.

### Step 6: Partition — AP System Behavior

In an AP system during a partition, each side of the partition continues to accept reads and writes independently. Clients connected to either side get responses. Data diverges: the same key may have different values on each side of the partition. When the partition heals, conflict resolution mechanisms (last-write-wins, vector clocks, CRDTs) reconcile the differences.

### Step 7: Partition — CP System Behavior

In a CP system during a partition, the majority side (quorum side) continues to operate. The minority side rejects write requests and may reject reads or return errors. This prevents data divergence but reduces availability. Clients on the minority side cannot make progress until the partition heals.

### Step 8: Partition Recovery

When the network heals, data must be reconciled. In AP systems, hinted handoff replays writes that were queued for downed replicas. Read repair fixes stale data during client reads. Anti-entropy processes (Merkle tree comparisons) find and repair any remaining inconsistencies. In CP systems, the minority nodes catch up by replaying the write-ahead log from the majority's latest checkpoint.

## Variants and Flavors

### PA/EL — DynamoDB, Cassandra (default)

PA/EL is the dominant configuration for web-scale applications. It prioritizes low latency during normal operation and availability during partitions. This is the right choice when user-facing latency is critical and temporary inconsistency is acceptable.

**When to use:** User session stores, product catalogs, activity feeds, recommendation engines, shopping carts. Any system where a moment of stale data is better than a timeout or error.

**When NOT to use:** Financial ledgers, inventory systems with strict counting, any system where a stale read could cause an incorrect irreversible action. If a stale read can cause a plane to fly into a mountain, do not use PA/EL.

### PC/EC — Spanner, Zookeeper

PC/EC prioritizes consistency in both regimes. During normal operation, it waits for agreement. During partitions, it sacrifices availability to prevent divergence. This is the right choice when correctness cannot be compromised.

**When to use:** Financial transaction systems, distributed locks, leader election, configuration stores, any system where inconsistency causes irreversible harm.

**When NOT to use:** High-volume user-facing systems where 15ms+ write latency would create a poor user experience. If the system serves millions of reads per second for a social feed, PC/EC adds unnecessary overhead.

### Hybrid / Tunable — Cassandra with Adjustable Consistency

Cassandra allows per-operation consistency levels. A write can use `ANY` (fastest, weakest) or `ALL` (slowest, strongest). Reads can use `ONE` (fast, possibly stale) or `QUORUM` (slower, strong). This means the same cluster can operate PA/EL for most operations and switch to PC/EC for critical operations.

**When to use:** Systems that have a mix of consistency requirements. Payment processing needs strong consistency; product browsing can tolerate eventual consistency.

**When NOT to use:** When the complexity of managing per-operation consistency levels outweighs the benefit. If 95% of operations require strong consistency, just use PC/EC and simplify.

## Mental Model Check

**Question 1:** A shopping cart service uses DynamoDB. During a network partition, a customer adds an item to their cart on the East Coast while the same cart is updated on the West Coast. Both updates succeed. What happens when the partition heals?

**Answer:** DynamoDB uses last-write-wins (LWW) conflict resolution. Whichever write has the later timestamp wins. The earlier write is lost. This is acceptable for shopping carts where the total is recalculated from the current cart contents, but it means the customer may need to re-add the earlier item. Applications must be designed to tolerate this.

**Question 2:** Your database has p50 read latency of 2ms and p99 of 30ms. After switching from eventual to strong consistency, p50 rises to 12ms. Which PACELC regime changed, and why?

**Answer:** The Else-regime behavior changed from EL (low latency) to EC (consistency). The system previously returned data from the nearest replica immediately. With strong consistency, it must query a quorum of replicas and reconcile timestamps before responding. The extra network round-trips and coordination add 10ms to the median path.

**Question 3:** Spanner rejects writes from a minority partition during a network split. What PACELC quadrant does this represent?

**Answer:** PC/EC. During Partition, Spanner chooses Consistency over Availability (CP). During normal operation (Else), Spanner chooses Consistency over Latency (EC) through its TrueTime-based global commit protocol.

**Question 4:** Can a single database cluster operate in both PA/EL and PC/EC modes depending on the request?

**Answer:** Yes. Cassandra and Cosmos DB support per-request consistency levels. A user authentication check can use QUORUM consistency (PC/EC) for correctness, while product recommendations use ONE consistency (PA/EL) for speed. The PACELC designation describes the system's default or minimum guarantees, but tunable systems can shift within the quadrant.

**Question 5:** A team chooses PA/EL for a financial ledger system. What specific failure scenario should they be most concerned about?

**Answer:** Double-spend or lost transactions during partitions. If a customer withdraws $100 from an ATM connected to one partition and another $100 from their banking app connected to another partition, both writes may succeed independently. When the partition heals and LWW resolves the conflict, one withdrawal is lost. PA/EL in financial contexts requires additional safeguards like idempotency tokens, external consensus, or compensating transactions.
